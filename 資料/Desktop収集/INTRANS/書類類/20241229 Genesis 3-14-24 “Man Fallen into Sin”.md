# 20241229 Genesis 3-14-24 “Man Fallen into Sin”

20241229 Genesis 3:14-24 “Man Fallen into Sin”

Let us return to reading Genesis today, though it has been a while.

Man ate from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil. God asked, “Where are you?”

And man made excuses one by one.

From here, the text shifts into a poetic form, with many rhymes embedded.

Genesis 3:14

The Lord God said to the serpent,

“Because you have done this,

cursed are you above all livestock

and above all beasts of the field;

on your belly you shall go,

and dust you shall eat

all the days of your life.”

● The serpent is not questioned about why it acted as it did; judgment begins immediately.

In other words, while there is an explanation for why man fell into sin, there is no explanation given for why sin was placed in the world.

→ Judgment progresses in reverse order to the questioning: Adam, Eve, the serpent; then Eve, and back to Adam.

● If crawling on its belly is a curse, does this imply that the serpent once stood upright or had limbs? Instead, it is better understood as crawling being assigned a new, cursed significance.

(The serpent’s slithering on the ground and its tongue flicking out as if tasting the dust give the appearance of eating dirt.)

● Cleverness (arum), now cursed (arur).

● The serpent’s arrogant attitude of “knowing God” is replaced with one of crawling upon the ground.

This symbolizes forced submission to God.

Genesis 3:15

“I will put enmity between you and the woman,

and between your offspring and her offspring;

he shall bruise your head,

and you shall bruise his heel.”

● This passage is known as the Protoevangelium: It is the first instance where God reveals His plan for salvation from sin and evil. This “proto-gospel” prophesies the coming of Jesus Christ and His redemptive work.

The “he” in verse 15 refers to Jesus, and the “you” to Satan. This verse foretells Jesus crushing Satan’s head.

(1) Structural contrast

This passage poetically contrasts the relationship between the serpent (Satan) and the offspring of the woman (Christ):

• The serpent’s “head”: symbolizing the lethal, authoritative aspect of the serpent.

• Man’s “heel”: symbolizing human vulnerability and earthly suffering.

(2) Symbolic meaning

• “He shall bruise your head” → signifies Jesus’s ultimate victory. The head represents the serpent’s life and authority, and crushing it symbolizes nullifying its power at its root.

• “You shall bruise his heel” → represents pain and suffering, but not fatal harm. This is interpreted as symbolizing Christ’s suffering on the cross.

● The victory of the woman’s offspring is declared in advance, even before the prediction of the woman’s suffering.

● Victory is achieved, but not without damage → The righteous before God suffer attacks: Abel, Noah, and others.

● “I will put enmity” = This phrase signifies a severing of the relationship between the serpent and the woman. The woman is no longer associated with the serpent’s curse.

→ They are not judged as co-conspirators. This can be seen as a form of grace or mercy.

→ The prediction of conflict with evil and opposition to what defies God. This signifies walking alongside God.

● The beginning of the plan of salvation

While God judges sin, He also gives Eve hope. The phrase “I will put enmity” reflects God’s grace and mercy. The promise of Eve’s offspring overcoming Satan brought hope for salvation to humanity.

Romans 16:18-20

“For such persons do not serve our Lord Christ, but their own appetites, and by smooth talk and flattery they deceive the hearts of the naive.

For your obedience is known to all, so that I rejoice over you, but I want you to be wise as to what is good and innocent as to what is evil.

The God of peace will soon crush Satan under your feet.

The grace of our Lord Jesus Christ be with you.”

Revelation 12:13-17

“And when the dragon saw that he had been thrown down to the earth, he pursued the woman who had given birth to the male child.

But the woman was given the two wings of the great eagle so that she might fly from the serpent into the wilderness, to the place where she is to be nourished for a time, and times, and half a time.

The serpent poured water like a river out of his mouth after the woman, to sweep her away with a flood.

But the earth came to the help of the woman, and the earth opened its mouth and swallowed the river that the dragon had poured from his mouth.

Then the dragon became furious with the woman and went off to make war on the rest of her offspring, on those who keep the commandments of God and hold to the testimony of Jesus. And he stood on the sand of the sea.”

Isaiah 27:1

“In that day the Lord with his hard and great and strong sword will punish Leviathan the fleeing serpent, Leviathan the twisting serpent, and he will slay the dragon that is in the sea.”

Here, the offspring of the woman is associated with the church, which is the body of Christ.

Thus, while the woman in sin is given hope, she must still face consequences.

Genesis 3:16

To the woman he said,

“I will surely multiply your pain in childbearing;

in pain you shall bring forth children.

Your desire shall be contrary to your husband,

but he shall rule over you.”

● Only the woman does not have the phrase “because you have done this,” but as a result, the following things happen:

● Three things:

</w:r><w:r><w:t>1.</w:r><w:r><w:t>Pain in conception and the agony of childbirth → the suffering of motherhood.

</w:r><w:r><w:t>2.</w:r><w:r><w:t>A strong desire for her husband → “desire” carries a sexual nuance and the idea of wanting to control.

</w:r><w:r><w:t>3.</w:r><w:r><w:t>The husband will rule over her.

(1) The blessing and pain of childbirth and motherhood

Childbirth itself was instituted here. → Adam from the ground, Eve from a rib.

The pain of childbirth is a reality resulting from sin, but childbirth is also a privilege of bringing forth new life. This pain reflects the effects of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, incorporating part of God’s creative work within the woman.

● In faith, childbirth is a blessing. → 1 Timothy 2:15

1 Timothy 2:9-15

“Likewise also that women should adorn themselves in respectable apparel, with modesty and self-control, not with braided hair and gold or pearls or costly attire,

but with what is proper for women who profess godliness—with good works.

Let a woman learn quietly with all submissiveness.

I do not permit a woman to teach or to exercise authority over a man; rather, she is to remain quiet.

For Adam was formed first, then Eve;

and Adam was not deceived, but the woman was deceived and became a transgressor.

Yet she will be saved through childbearing—if they continue in faith and love and holiness, with self-control.”

(2) Changes in the marital relationship

The phrase “Your desire shall be contrary to your husband, but he shall rule over you” reflects the distortion of power dynamics as a result of sin.

“Desire” here does not merely refer to sexual attraction but encompasses a broader meaning of intense longing or a desire to dominate within the marital relationship. This desire, born from sin, may manifest as dependence on male leadership or competition.

Thus, “desire” symbolizes power struggles and tension in marriage. These distortions, however, are redeemed through the gospel, restoring the original “oneness” of the marital relationship.

The New Testament (Ephesians 5:22-33) teaches the restoration of marital relationships in Christ, emphasizing mutual love and respect.

Ephesians 5:21-28

“Submitting to one another out of reverence for Christ.

Wives, submit to your own husbands, as to the Lord.

For the husband is the head of the wife even as Christ is the head of the church, his body, and is himself its Savior.

Now as the church submits to Christ, so also wives should submit in everything to their husbands.

Husbands, love your wives, as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her,

that he might sanctify her, having cleansed her by the washing of water with the word,

so that he might present the church to himself in splendor, without spot or wrinkle or any such thing, that she might be holy and without blemish.

In the same way husbands should love their wives as their own bodies. He who loves his wife loves himself.”

Summarizing, the Lord pointed out from the woman’s perspective the impact of the knowledge of good and evil on what was originally the unity between man and woman.

Genesis 3:17

And to Adam he said,

“Because you have listened to the voice of your wife

and have eaten of the tree

of which I commanded you,

‘You shall not eat of it,’

cursed is the ground because of you;

in pain you shall eat of it all the days of your life;

● “Because of you”

Genesis 3:12

The man said, “The woman whom you gave to be with me, she gave me fruit of the tree, and I ate.”

It is the act of listening and eating for which Adam’s responsibility is called into question. The blame is neither on the wife nor God; it was Adam’s own fault that the world was cursed.

● The blessing of obedience to God is the opposite of this curse.

Deuteronomy 28:2-5

“And all these blessings shall come upon you and overtake you, if you obey the voice of the Lord your God.

Blessed shall you be in the city, and blessed shall you be in the field.

Blessed shall be the fruit of your womb and the fruit of your ground and the fruit of your cattle, the increase of your herds and the young of your flock.

Blessed shall be your basket and your kneading bowl.”

● These words are addressed to man, not as a husband or a male, but as Adam (humanity).

The Hebrew word for “man” here, Adam, reflects his creation from the ground (adamah). Adam’s sin has affected the very earth with which he was intimately connected.

Continued translation faithfully follows the original structure and content.

“cursed is the ground because of you” → reflects a broken relationship between man and the earth, which was once harmonious and productive.

“You shall eat of it in pain all the days of your life.”

● Labor was already commanded in Genesis 2:15:

Genesis 2:15

“The Lord God took the man and put him in the garden of Eden to work it and keep it.”

However, the nature of work changed—from joyful stewardship to painful toil.

● The ground is cursed.

→ While the serpent and the ground are cursed, man and woman are not cursed directly.

Instead, the environment, the ground in which man lives, bears the curse, continuing its impact even after Adam’s death.

Genesis 3:18

“thorns and thistles it shall bring forth for you;

and you shall eat the plants of the field.”

● The previous verse reflects an agricultural image; this verse shifts to a pastoral image.

● “For you” → conveys an antagonistic relationship between humanity and nature.

● Thorns and thistles → symbolize untamed and hostile nature throughout Scripture.

Before sin, childbirth and labor were purely blessings, untainted by suffering or hardship. These acts reflected collaboration with God in creating and nurturing life.

Childbirth brought joy and gratitude for new life, while labor was a creative and fulfilling expression of man’s role in God’s creation.

Sin disrupted these blessings, introducing pain and hardship into both processes.

However, the hope offered in Genesis points to restoration through God’s plan of salvation in Jesus Christ. These blessings will be fully restored in the future kingdom of God.

Romans 8:18-22

“For I consider that the sufferings of this present time are not worth comparing with the glory that is to be revealed to us.

For the creation waits with eager longing for the revealing of the sons of God.

For the creation was subjected to futility, not willingly, but because of him who subjected it, in hope

that the creation itself will be set free from its bondage to corruption and obtain the freedom of the glory of the children of God.

For we know that the whole creation has been groaning together in the pains of childbirth until now.”

The world itself longs to cease its current state of conflict and to exist as it was originally created—in harmony. Human sin has led to pollution, environmental degradation, and climatic instability.

Yet the gospel holds the power to begin repairing these relationships.

Genesis 3:19

“By the sweat of your face you shall eat bread,

till you return to the ground,

for out of it you were taken;

for you are dust,

and to dust you shall return.”

● This became a basis for social stratification in Europe, with clergy considered superior.

● “You will become like God” → Yet the conclusion was a reminder, “You are dust.”

What does it mean to return to dust?

Humankind, created from the dust of the ground and given the breath of life, was designed to reflect God’s image. Returning to dust symbolizes not only physical death but also the severance of intimate fellowship with God.

● Returning to the cursed ground highlights the tragedy of humanity’s rebellion.

● Death was decreed. While immediate death did not occur, the unmerited blessings originally given were now altered. Scripture promises their restoration to those who walk with God.

Romans 5:12

“Therefore, just as sin came into the world through one man, and death through sin, and so death spread to all men because all sinned—”

1 Corinthians 15:21

“For as by a man came death, by a man has come also the resurrection of the dead.”

Though we have fallen into sin, we must not forget that there is hope for restoration in all things.

Reflecting on the narrative of the Fall and its consequences, let us consider this:

Numbers 21:4-9

“From Mount Hor they set out by the way to the Red Sea, to go around the land of Edom. And the people became impatient on the way.

And the people spoke against God and against Moses, ‘Why have you brought us up out of Egypt to die in the wilderness? For there is no food and no water, and we loathe this worthless food.’

Then the Lord sent fiery serpents among the people, and they bit the people, so that many people of Israel died.

And the people came to Moses and said, ‘We have sinned, for we have spoken against the Lord and against you. Pray to the Lord, that he take away the serpents from us.’ So Moses prayed for the people.

And the Lord said to Moses, ‘Make a fiery serpent and set it on a pole, and everyone who is bitten, when he sees it, shall live.’

So Moses made a bronze serpent and set it on a pole. And if a serpent bit anyone, he would look at the bronze serpent and live.”

This connects to the passage:

“As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up, that whoever believes in him may have eternal life.”

Lifting up the serpent → Forces us to confront sin → Without recognizing sin, salvation’s significance cannot be understood → Leads to salvation

What do we see in the cross of Jesus Christ?

It is not about feeling pity for Jesus. Rather, we must see the weight of our own sins that put Him there.

The meaning of the Protoevangelium is that we are destined to have victory over sin, and therefore, we must seek out and confront it.

By confronting our sins and understanding our weaknesses, we can entrust everything to God.

There is no need to fear being weak. Let us believe in and entrust ourselves to the Lord, Jesus Christ.
