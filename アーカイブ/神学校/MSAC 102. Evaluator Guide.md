**Evaluator Expectations**

The expectations for teaching online classes differ from face-to-face classes, but the rigor of the course remains. Many students may be unfamiliar or uncomfortable with their first few online courses, so the Evaluator has an added responsibility to assist students to become confident in online learning. Evaluators are expected to give of themselves generously as they read through forum questions, respond to students, grade assignments, and post weekly announcements as these are activities in which you will be connecting with your students. Evaluators are responsible for reading any assigned texts, viewing assigned videos and reviewing the various websites and resources noted in the syllabus or course site. The following expectations are outlined to ensure that the online classroom runs smoothly and is student friendly.

**Value of Accelerated Response**

Accelerated courses provide a framework in which students must rapidly internalize learning experiences. Without critical reflection on Evaluator feedback, students may persist in previous patterns. Each assignment represents a brief window of time for transformative impact through an Evaluator's guidance. Therefore, the effectiveness of accelerated student work is highly dependent on equally accelerated availability of expert perspective.

**Pre-course Activity Timeline**

**Two Weeks Before Class**

***Check all Links***

Evaluators should check that all links in the course site and in the syllabus are up to date before the course begins. If a link for a resource is no longer available, the Evaluator should attempt to locate the current link and then contact the Ministry Studies office with the correct web addresses as soon as possible before the start of the class so that the course site can be updated if needed.

***Due Dates***

Note the due dates for this course. This is often a source of student concern. Being ready to help them know when things are due is critical.

**Three Days Before Class**

***Welcome to Class/E-mails***

Evaluators should post a welcome greeting in the Announcement Forum in the course site. Evaluators should also provide Evaluator information for students, providing contact information and indicating that a conference call or chat session with the student is available if needed. Evaluators should also e-mail this information to students, providing information about how to access the course assignments and forums, your course expectations, and the importance of MLA style.

**Mid-course Activity Timeline**

**First Week of Class**

***Introductions Forum***

Evaluators should respond to each student's introduction posting within 48 hours and provide encouragement and feedback. It is important to welcome students and let them know that they have a "face" in this course.

**Weekly**

***Weekly Announcements***

Your course is designed with pre-scheduled Weekly announcements which will go out to students each Monday. Feel free to add additional announcements or connections as it makes a difference for students knowing that their Evaluator is present and cares.

When Evaluators interact with a specific student in a discussion forum, use the student's name in a greeting and close using your own name.

***Course Attendance***

Evaluators are expected to be in the course site at least three times a week, with interaction spread throughout the week. Evaluators should respond to postings or course questions at this time, as well. Evaluators are expected to check e-mail daily.

***Responding to Discussion Posts***

The majority of the interaction with students in online classes is in the forums. Since this is where Evaluators "teach," they should respond to students by moving the discussion forward from their area of expertise. Evaluators should provide substantive feedback beyond just "Great work." It is advisable to begin public posts with a greeting by using the student's name and end with a closing that uses the Evaluator's name.

***Grading Discussion Questions***

Evaluators should enter students' forum grades within five days following the last day of the course week. The first day of the course week begins on Monday, the last day is Sunday and forum grades will be due five days later on a Friday. Students should receive an explanation for points lost according to the graduate discussion rubric on the course site. For example, Evaluators could respond, "Rhiannon, 35/40 your post answered the questions but you need to include information for one professional resource within the post like Smith (2008) reported ... and you did not post the complete citation for a resource after the post (-5). Thanks, Dr. Chara."

**Daily**

Evaluators are expected to check their e-mail and the course site daily. A 24-hour response time is recommended as a best practice.

**Weekly Content Guides**

The following suggestions aim to assist mid-week online instruction and community building in discussion, reflection forums, or news forum posts.

**Week 1: The Call of Christ**

In this first unit students will investigate the essential role that the call of Christ plays in discipleship. What will be revealed is that it is Christ who calls and Christ who qualifies us to be a disciple.

**Dietrich Bonhoeffer and Discipleship**

Students will be required to read a portion of Bonhoeffer's famous work *Discipleship* as part of their required reading for this week. Please be aware that this reading is a bit heavy and philoshopical when compared to the rest of their reading for this course. However, this portion (available in the course shell) introduces the topics that will be discussed in the videos. You may want to help your students know that this will be a "thinking" type of read.

**Your First Face to Face: Zoom Session 1**

This week you will have your very first Zoom session with your students. In preparation for this conversation you might want to prepare a simple PowerPoint presentation for use during the call. Primarily, you want to introduce yourself to the students and get the students to know each other a little bit better. You can use this time to disuss upcoming assignments, answer questions about the course, and give a general sense of ease to any anxious students. Also, use this time to give a general sense of the goal of this course by walking students through the course description and learning objectives listed on the syllabus. [A specific element that you will want to discuss with your students will be their Reflection Exercise 1 in Week 3. This is a two-part assignment requiring them to display creativity and critical thinking. This will need a thorough explaniation during your Zoom session. Please see below in Week 3 for a overview of this project.]{.underline}

**Reflection Exercise 1: Self- Study**

For this reflection exercise students will complete the first of six Ministry Development contracts. This is an exercise designed to allow students to develop in key areas of ministry poficency on their own terms. At the bottom of this document you will find a copy of the instructions for this exercise. Please be sure to review this tool thoroughly in order to answer questions for your students. At the end of week1 students are required to turn in a Ministry Contract Proposal. Your job will be to review, tweak and ultimately approve these plans. The ministry contract is then worked on over the next six weeks independently by the students. In week 6, students will submit a overview of their findings based on the work they have completed as part of the contract. Please keep in mind that this Ministry Development Contract is meant to help students development in the area of "Kingdom Mindedness" (see handout below).

**Week 2: The Goal of Discipleship**

This unit explores the telos of discipleship. As a part of this conversation sanctification will be investigated (both positional and progressive). The heart of this week is for students to walk away with the idea that, apart from the empowerment of the Holy Spirit, the goal of discipleship is an impossible achievement.

**Simpson and Sanctification**

**This week's discovery exercise has students interacting with the writings of Alliance founder AB Simpson. Students will read a selection from the book *Wholly Sancitified* and then respond with a one-two page paper which details their own experience with Christ our Sanctifier. This is a great place for you as en evaluator to not only grade but also shepherd. Be on the lookout for those who seem confused or lost by the idea and consider connecting with them one-to-one.**

**Tools for the Church**

**In this week's discovery exercise students will be tasked with building a handout for the local church. As much as possible challenge your students to developing this tool with the intent to actually use it. The more practical the assignment the more likely a higher investment will be put in on the part of the student. One good hint to give students is that if it doesn't have color and graphics it's not going to get a good grade.**

**Week 3: Understanding Spiritual Formation**\
This unit unpacks the concepts found within the field of Spiritual Formation. The fruit of the Spirit is investigated as a key indiciation of the work spiritual formation coming to fruition in the life of the believer.

**Reflection Exercise 1: Focused Study**

The work for this week is a little lighter. This is intention and is designed to give students focused time to work on their ministry development contract projects. Please be sure to remind them to use this time wisely.

**The Work of the Holy Spirit**

**Throughout their time in weeks 2-3 students will read through the book *Cultivating the Fruit of the Spirit* by Wright. This is a great little book that will help them understand that it's the development of the Fruit of the Spirit that ultimately leads to Christlikeness in the life of the believer. This is a point that is worth reiterating as you spend time engaging in discussioin with your students.**

**Week 4: Foundational Discplines**

This unit investigates hthe core spiritual disciplines of prayer and Bible reading. These two disciplines undergird many of the others that will be discussed throughout the ensuing weeks.

**Two Boundaries**

Students will be challenged to consider the double boundary of the Christian life. In the Christian life boundary one is to be found at our limit. Our outer limit represents the line we do not cross for to cross it will mean that we leave the life Christ has called us to. However, there is a second inner boundary that must be recognized. The inner boundary of the Christian life is the boundary that surrounds God himself. We cannot seek to replace God (our inner boundary) nor can we seek to ignore God (our outer boundary). These two boundaries will be discussed this week.

**Practicing the Disciplines**

Students will spend the week practicing a particular discipline from *The Spiritual Disciplines Handbook*. This is both personally practical as well as helpful for the cohort. Once students have completed their practice of the discipline they will be tasked with developing a one-page instruction guide for use within the local church. They are to submit this for grading but also submit this to the file marked "Spiritual Discipine Guidess". This folder then can become a repository for the rest of the cohort. Here others will be able to view and download these exericses for use within their own churches. Please encourage your students to share and take advantage of these peer resources. This exercise will be repeated in Week 5 as well.

**Week 5: The Two Boundaries of the Christian Life**

Having learned about the double boundary of the Christian life in this unit students will learn specific dispclines that will help them avoid crossing both the interior and exterior boundaries of the Christian faith. Two sessions will deal with disciplines of abstinence and two sessions will deal with the discipines of engagement. Both sets of disciplines are crucial for the Christian life.

**Finding God in My Everything**

During weeks 4-6 students will read through *God in My Everything* by Ken Shigematsu. Shigematus seeks to bring a level of relevant and practicality to the spiritual disciplines by asking how can they be practiced in our modern day hectic world. Keep revisiting this with your students. Life does not slow down simply because we desire to practice spiritual disciplines. Instead, we must change or adapt our rhythms in order to disocver God in our everything.

**Week 6: Developing Healthy Spiritual Rhythms**

This final unit discusses how students can practically begin to build helathy and sustainable rhythms in their lives. The key is the habitual practice of the spiritual habits which transform the soul.

**Our Identity in Christ**

The final project for this course requires students to reflect on their identity in Christ. This is another great place for you as an evaluator to shepherd your students toward transformation. As you read over their responses be sure to ask God to guide you to give feedback that is redemptive and uplifting as your students continue in their journey as children of God.

**Ministry Development Contract Check In**

Also, this final week students will be required to submit the results of the work they completed for their ministry development contract. What is required of each student will be a little different based on their own contract. Be sure to stay in touch with your students and ask for them to submit the appropriate material.

Ministry Development Contract
-----------------------------

Introduction
------------

> If you desire to build credibility as a ministry leader then you must seek to develop godly character and practical ministry skills. Those we lead and serve will continually draw conclusions about our character based on the attitudes and actions they observe.
>
> In the Office of Ministry Studies, we believe that it is essential for each of us who are training to be ministry leaders develop both the character and competency necessary to serve the Lord and a local congregation effectively. As author of Psalm 78 sums it up well:
>
> *And David shepherded them with integrity of heart; with skillful hands he led them (Psalm 78:72).*
>
> As part of your educational journey in the Ministry Studies program you will be required to complete several Proficiencies Learning Contracts.

Describing a Ministry Development Contract
------------------------------------------

> A learning contract is simply an agreement between student and professor of how you propose to strengthen a personal character or skill in your life. The contract will specify:

-   Which character quality/skill you intend to improve.

-   Why you believe this character/skill is vital in a ministry of shepherding others.

-   What activities you plan to use in your learning strategy.

-   How you will present evidence of improvement.

Writing a Ministry Development Contract
---------------------------------------

### STEP ONE: Review Your Assigned Distinctive

> Review APPENDIX A: DISTINCTIVES OF AN ALLIANCE WORKER. Read over the
>
> listing and familiarize yourself with the specific distinctive on which you will be working on through this contract.

### 

### STEP TWO: Specify Your Learning Objective

> After you have reviewed this your assigned distinctive for the learning contract, specify your learning objective. This is the heart of the contract. It describes what you want to become. Here are some examples:
>
> "I will increase my effective in sharing the Gospel with others."
>
> "I will become more self-aware of my personality style and its strengths and weaknesses."
>
> "I will develop my preaching skills."
>
> Be precise! Your learning objective should be stated in a single sentence.

### STEP THREE: Strategize Ways to Accomplish Your Learning Objective

> Indicate specific activities, resources and strategies you will utilize to reach your learning objective. The Learning Contract ***must*** include a biblical component to discover what God's Word teaches about the Distinctive you have selected.
>
> Possibilities include an exegetical study of a key passage, word studies, inductive studies, lessons learned from a person in scripture, etc. Be creative.
>
> Other worthwhile activities might include reading portions of books, journals and articles, blogs, watching DVD's, visiting websites, conducting interviews with those who model this character trait you seek to possess, focused prayer, journaling about your progress, listening to podcasts, watching teaching videos, small group discussion, attending a seminar or conference, a project, etc.
>
> The learning activities you choose are the roads you will travel to help you reach your destination. Plot your course carefully so that every task deliberately moves you toward achieving your learning objective of character development.
>
> The Learning Contract will have *three* learning tasks that must be completed by the end of week 6 (unless they require more time than 3 weeks in which case it should be noted in your summary report what progress has been made). Balance these activities between spiritual and practical pursuits. Remember to keep each task measurable, achievable and very specific. A sample Learning Contract can be found in APPENDIX B.
>
> Expect to spend 10-12 hours completing your learning objective.

Evaluating a Learning Contract
------------------------------

> Evaluating is the evidence of having achieved your learning objectives. The results of your self- evaluation will be reported in a 1-2 page Summary Report detailing evidence that learning has taken place. It will be comprised of a paragraph essay response to each of the following questions, along with submitting any summary papers or projects done in conjunction with the *individual objectives* of this Character Contract:

1.  Did I learn what I originally thought I would learn?

2.  On a scale of 1 (poor) to 10 (very well done), where did I begin and where did I finish?

3.  ![](media/image8.png){width="0.16333333333333333in" height="0.19999890638670167in"}What did I learn that took me by surprise?

4.  What activities, resources and strategies were most or least beneficial?

5.  What have I learned about myself?

6.  How could I cultivate this character trait/skill in the lives of those I will one day lead?

APPENDIX A

**Alliance leader distinctives defined & demonstrated**

Kingdom Minded

-   An Alliance Worker is someone who represents Jesus well by being invested in the local and global church community for the sake of demonstrating God's eternal and coming kingdom.

-   [Kingdom Mindedness is demonstrated through:]{.underline}

    -   An ability to articulate a salvation story.

    -   Submission to believers baptism in a local church.

    -   Active membership in a local church.

    -   Active service in the local church.

    -   A demonstrated ability to lead others to Christ.

    -   The active practice of Biblical stewardship including:

        -   Tithing

        -   Responsible debt management.

    -   An ability to work with people who are different than you.

    -   Sacrificial living for the sake of others.

Biblically Grounded

-   An Alliance Worker is someone who is engaged in the process of learning, understanding, and applying truth and information through the lens of a biblical worldview in order to gain greater leadership effectiveness.

-   [Biblical Grounding is demonstrated through:]{.underline}

    -   The conviction that the Bible is God's inerrant Word.

    -   An ability to articulate the core tenants of the historic Christian faith.

    -   The display of basic biblical literacy.

    -   An ability to articulate the unique theological distinctives of the C&MA.

    -   An ability to articulate a basic understanding of ministry calling and ministry offices (Eph 5).

    -   A demonstrated ability to read and adapt to specific cultures in order to effectively minister the Gospel of Jesus Christ.

    -   

Spirit Dependent

-   An Alliance Worker is someone who is engaged in the process of being conformed to the image of Christ through the power of and absolute dependency on the Holy Spirit for the sake of others.

-   [Spirit Dependency is demonstrated through:]{.underline}

    -   The practice of the spiritual disciplines.

    -   The ability to articulate a moment of spiritual crisis and resolution.

    -   The ability to hear, understand, and obey the voice of the Holy Spirit.

    -   A growing demonstration of Christ like character.

    -   The ability to live in the power of the Holy Spirit.

    -   The active display of humility.

Competently Skilled

-   An Alliance Worker is someone who has and is constantly developing a holistic set of skills that will catalyze a lifetime of leadership.

-   [Skill Competency is demonstrated through:]{.underline}

    -   A growing proficiency in basic pastoral skills (preaching, shepherding, etc).

    -   A growing proficiency in organizational leadership skills (budgeting, strategic planning, change management, etc.)

    -   A growing proficiency in basic ministry skills (evangelism disciple making, etc.).

    -   A growing proficiency in the soft skills of people management (conflict resolution, communication, etc.).

Self-Aware

-   An Alliance Worker is someone who is regularly seeking to grow in the understanding of self, their motivations, the impact of their past, and their natural abilities and liabilities in order to display a God-honoring life and engage in God-honoring ministry.

-   [Self-Awareness is demonstrated through:]{.underline}

    -   An ability to articulate one's spiritual gifts and their implementation for ministry.

    -   The increasing ability to understand and appropriately manage one's personality, motivations, and leadership style.

    -   An growing understanding of emotional intelligence.

    -   The ability to articulate one's personal life journey and its impact on one's current journey.

    -   An ability to demonstrate how one's identity is found in Christ.

    -   A growing awareness of one's blind spots.

    -   An ability to discuss the importance and role calling plays in ministry leadership.

    -   An ability to develop and understand the place of vision and values in ministry leadership.

Self-Disciplined

-   An Alliance Worker is someone who displays Kingdom-oriented rhythms which lead to greater Kingdom awareness, receptivity, and productivity.

-   [Self-Discipline is demonstrated through:]{.underline}

    -   A healthy work/life balance.

    -   Emotional well-being (the ability to grieve/celebrate/and have fun)

    -   The demonstration of biblically healthy personal relationships (marriage/single)

    -   The demonstration of covenant relationships (accountability)

    -   An appreciation for physical and mental health.

    -   The demonstration of a willingness to pursue personal restoration when necessary.
