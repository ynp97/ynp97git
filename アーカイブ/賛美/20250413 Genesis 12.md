**20250413 Genesis 12:1--9 "Abram's Departure"**

Let us continue reading the book of Genesis today.

Up until now in Genesis, great stories that concern the entire world, such as the creation of the heavens and the earth, the flood, and the Tower of Babel, have been depicted. However, from chapter 12, the stage suddenly narrows, and the story begins to revolve around one man---Abram (later Abraham).

God reveals His will to bless the whole world through Abram, the ancestor of Israel.

And the focus of the story from here on will be the figure of Abram, who believes that word and walks toward its fulfillment, though stumbling along the way.

Here are the words about this story as spoken by Stephen, the first martyr of the Christian church.

Acts 7:2 And Stephen said: "Brothers and fathers, hear me. The God of glory appeared to our father Abraham when he was in Mesopotamia, before he lived in Haran,

Acts 7:3 and said to him, 'Go out from your land and from your kindred and go into the land that I will show you.'

Acts 7:4 Then he went out from the land of the Chaldeans and lived in Haran. And after his father died, God removed him from there into this land in which you are now living.

Acts 7:5 Yet he gave him no inheritance in it, not even a foot's length, but promised to give it to him as a possession and to his offspring after him, though he had no child.

This is what we will be reading today.

Already at the end of chapter 11, it is recorded that Terah departed from Ur with Abram and others,

settled in Haran, and died there.

Gen. 11:27 Now these are the generations of Terah. Terah fathered Abram, Nahor, and Haran; and Haran fathered Lot.

Gen. 11:28 Haran died in the presence of his father Terah in the land of his kindred, in Ur of the Chaldeans.

Gen. 11:29 And Abram and Nahor took wives. The name of Abram's wife was Sarai, and the name of Nahor's wife, Milcah, the daughter of Haran the father of Milcah and Iscah.

Gen. 11:30 Now Sarai was barren; she had no child.

Gen. 11:31 Terah took Abram his son and Lot the son of Haran, his grandson, and Sarai his daughter-in-law, his son Abram's wife, and they went forth together from Ur of the Chaldeans to go into the land of Canaan, but when they came to Haran, they settled there.

Gen. 11:32 The days of Terah were 205 years, and Terah died in Haran.

As we can understand from Stephen's words, this story of Abraham

was a very important story for the Jews at that time, and we can see that it was accurately passed down.

Gen. 12:1 Now the Lord said to Abram,

"Go from your country

and your kindred and your father's house

to the land that I will show you.

There is a nuance in this sentence that is not translated.

In fact, in the word "go," there is a meaning of "for yourself."

If that is the case, it means that for your own sake, you must leave your country, your kindred,

and your father's house.

In other words, it means, for your own sake, "Leave all zones of safety and familiar communities,

abandon the economic foundation you have built so far, and throw everything away without being bound by your ancestors."

This is something that is also shared in the words of Jesus.

Matt. 8:21 Another of the disciples said to him, "Lord, let me first go and bury my father."

Matt. 8:22 And Jesus said to him, "Follow me, and leave the dead to bury their own dead."

Obedience to God often requires the abandonment of worldly security and customs,

and this is what was first required even of Abram, the father of faith.

In response to this, Abram must have decided to act according to the revelation of God. For he was a man of faith.

However, what is important here is that the destination was not shown.

Heb. 11:8 By faith Abraham obeyed when he was called to go out to a place that he was to receive as an inheritance. And he went out, not knowing where he was going.

Heb. 11:9 By faith he went to live in the land of promise, as in a foreign land, living in tents with Isaac and Jacob, heirs with him of the same promise.

This act of following the Lord without knowing the destination is not an easy thing.

However, if you believe in the Lord, then you ought to do so.

We may at times seek the destination.

And by thinking deeply and calculating, we might find a way to get there.

However, if one were able to do that, then the person might come to the conclusion in their heart that it was their own power,

and not the power of God.

This is something we must consider here.

Is God's purpose to accomplish something in this world? Do we believers live a life of faith in order to do something for God and produce benefit for Him?

That cannot be. Because God is not One who lacks anything.

If that is the case, then what we are to realize by obeying God

is that we ourselves, through that path of obedience, are to strengthen our faith in God---that is the purpose.

In other words, for us to grow in faith is the entirety of God's purpose.

If that is so, then how much more is it in accordance with God's purpose---and our purpose as believers---that His will is accomplished even though we do not know the destination or the method?

And furthermore, why does God desire to strengthen our faith? That is because it is the right thing,

and it allows that person to live their life in the truest sense.

And in the end, we are able to hold hope in the kingdom of heaven, in eternal life---

something we can only imagine, something beyond the grasp of human senses.

In other words, all of it is for the sake of that person, and the destination is not revealed.

That is exactly why the Lord says, "Go for yourself."

There is something we must remember.

That is, if the way forward is not visible, yet the Word of God resounds in our heart, then we ought to move forward.

Because that step is something that will truly be for your own sake.

This situation of Abram seems to bear much in common with living as one who believes in Jesus,

and being involved in Christianity, in today's Japan, where it is a difficult situation.

That is, living moderately in economic stability and a peaceful environment.

And those around do not believe in Christianity, but are traditionally Buddhist, having ancestral graves and various memorial services, and upholding those things is considered normal or a duty within the family and relatives. In a world that feels settled by carrying out those customs, to say "I believe in Jesus Christ" is a very difficult thing.

However, if your heart is seeking Jesus Christ, then you ought to move forward into that.

What lies ahead is only a future where your entire worldview up to now is thrown away.

What will happen---you cannot know, because you have not yet experienced it.

And yet, that is why the resolve to move forward is of utmost importance.

That very resolve to abandon what you had before is the first step into the wonderful, profound, and beautiful grace called faith.

That one step will transform you, and lead you to a good place.

That is why the Lord says: for your sake, take a step into the unseen.

I declare that step will surely be supported.

There is no need to hesitate in the life of believing in Jesus. Cast everything aside and go forth to the Lord.

Gen. 12:2 "And I will make of you a great nation,

and I will bless you

and make your name great,

so that you will be a blessing."

The term "great nation" (גּוֹי גָּדוֹל, goy gadol) is the promise that Abram's descendants will become numerous (such as in chapter 15, verse 5: "as the stars"). It can also be said to be a guarantee that the people of Israel will prosper.

Furthermore, this "great nation" will, through Christ, later extend even to all who believe.

Gal. 3:6 just as Abraham "believed God, and it was counted to him as righteousness"?

Gal. 3:7 Know then that it is those of faith who are the sons of Abraham.

Gal. 3:8 And the Scripture, foreseeing that God would justify the Gentiles by faith, preached the gospel beforehand to Abraham, saying, "In you shall all the nations be blessed."

Gal. 3:9 So then, those who are of faith are blessed along with Abraham, the man of faith.

"I will bless you" (אֲבָרְכֶךָ, avarekhekha), and "make your name great." From this pairing,

we can understand that the blessing from God includes both spiritual and material blessings---grace and prosperity.

And then, "so that you will be a blessing."

This is, literally, an imperative: "be a blessing." It commands the transition from "one who is passively blessed" to "a source who blesses others."

As we saw earlier in Galatians 3:8, Paul notes that "the gospel was proclaimed beforehand to Abraham."

This blessing becomes the universal salvation that reaches all humanity. In other words, it becomes the prototype of the gospel of Jesus Christ.

So what is blessing? To summarize, it includes such things as "a right relationship with God," "spiritual and material abundance," "protection from enemies," and "spreading grace to others."

And it means to become someone who distributes these things.

Express the grace from God through the way you live, and let those who come into contact with you receive it as blessing---receiving faith and spiritual awakening.

If one can become such a person, then it will be as follows.

Gen. 12:3 "I will bless those who bless you,

and him who dishonors you I will curse,

and in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed."

If Abram is to become a blessing, then it means that the Lord will deal appropriately with those who are involved with him.

And this recompense is not limited by ethnicity. It means that all of humanity is the target.

Truly, it can be said to be a forerunner of the gospel of Jesus Christ.

Those who believe in Jesus are saved, and those who do not believe end their lives under the curse of sin.

This structure was also present in the person of Abram at that time.

However, unlike Jesus, it was not accompanied by atonement, so it was not decisive.

Gen. 12:4 So Abram went, as the Lord had told him, and Lot went with him. Abram was seventy-five years old when he departed from Haran.

It reads as though Abram departed immediately, without hesitation or investigation.

As we saw earlier, at this point, the destination had not been revealed. What an obedient attitude this is.

And his age was seventy-five.

Although Genesis describes long lifespans, it is said that seventy-five at this point still counted as old age.

If that is the case, we can see just how extraordinary Abram's action was.

He never said the things we tend to say---like "I'm too old" or "I'm still too young."

Heb. 11:8 By faith Abraham obeyed when he was called to go out to a place that he was to receive as an inheritance. And he went out, not knowing where he was going.

Heb. 11:9 By faith he went to live in the land of promise, as in a foreign land, living in tents with Isaac and Jacob, heirs with him of the same promise.

• Extra-biblical traditions (such as in the Talmud) concerning Abram:

@@

The legend of "The Fiery Furnace with King Nimrod"

• A famous story mentioned in the Talmud (Bava Batra 91a), and in Bereshit Rabbah chapters 38--39, in which Abram rejects idolatry and is thrown into a fiery furnace, from which he is miraculously saved.

"The Destruction of Idols by Abram"

• Around Bereshit Rabbah 38:13, the story is told of Abram breaking his father Terah's idols and being reported to Nimrod. Some versions transition from here to the fiery furnace legend.

"Avodah Zarah 9a" mentions Abram's age and early recognition of the one true God

• Abram is said to have recognized the one true God at the age of three, or to have denied idols through the study of astronomy.

"Evangelistic work during stay in Haran," and "Departure after Terah's death"

• In the "Lech Lecha" section of Midrash Rabbah and Tanchuma, there are additions such as Abram and Sarai converting people during their stay in Haran, and departing for Canaan after Terah's funeral.

Gen. 12:5 And Abram took Sarai his wife, and Lot his brother's son, and all their possessions that they had gathered, and the people that they had acquired in Haran, and they set out to go to the land of Canaan. When they came to the land of Canaan,

Following chapter 11, Abram's family structure is again clearly stated. It has been mentioned in verse 30 that Sarai was barren.

Lot will later separate from Abraham, but he is a figure who appears in many stories, including the destruction of Sodom.

What should be noted here is the abundance of wealth and livestock.

As a nomad at the time, it is possible that Abram had gained considerable wealth through trade and the sale of livestock.

And Abram arrived at the entrance to the promised land.

At this point, he did not yet know whether this was the "land I will show you" as God had said.

Only after he arrived did the word of the Lord come, and only then did he know, "Ah, this is the place."

Gen. 12:6 Abram passed through the land to the place at Shechem, to the oak of Moreh. At that time the Canaanites were in the land.

Gen. 12:7 Then the Lord appeared to Abram and said, "To your offspring I will give this land." So he built there an altar to the Lord, who had appeared to him.

"Shechem" (שְׁכֶם, Shekhem) was an important city in the northern part of the land of Canaan and later becomes the setting of a brutal incident in Jacob's story (Genesis 34).

The oak of Moreh is thought to have been a great tree, possibly around ten meters tall.

When this was written, that tree may still have remained and been a kind of landmark for readers.

If this was the place where Abram built an altar, it is not difficult to imagine that people cherished it.

And here, it was revealed that Canaan was indeed the destination.

That is, the place God led him to was Canaan, a foreign land, and it is confirmed again that Canaanites, who were pagans, lived there.

And Abram deliberately entered that "land of the pagans."

In other words, Abram became a sojourner in a foreign land.

Moreover, what God promised Abram was, "To your offspring I will give this land."

Yes---that is to say, God clearly stated that it would not be given to Abram himself.

Acts 7:4 Then he went out from the land of the Chaldeans and lived in Haran. And after his father died, God removed him from there into this land in which you are now living.

Acts 7:5 Yet he gave him no inheritance in it, not even a foot's length, but promised to give it to him as a possession and to his offspring after him, though he had no child.

And as has already been clearly stated up to this point, his wife was unable to bear children.

That being the case, one could even say that, for Abram, this journey was one in which he gained nothing.

However, what did Abram do in this situation? He built an altar, rejoiced in God, and praised Him.

Gen. 12:8 From there he moved to the hill country on the east of Bethel and pitched his tent, with Bethel on the west and Ai on the east. And there he built an altar to the Lord and called upon the name of the Lord.

Bethel means "house of God," and it later becomes a holy place where Jacob has a dream (Genesis 28).

Ai is a city connected to the conquest of Canaan (Joshua chapters 7--8).

Abram "pitched his tent" (אֹהֶל, ohel), continuing the typical nomadic lifestyle.

This can also be said to serve as a motif for our way of life---living not settled in this world, but ultimately with the kingdom of heaven as our true homeland.

To "call upon the name of the Lord" (וַיִּקְרָא בְּשֵׁם יְהוָה, vayikra b'shem Yahweh)

means that he worshiped in order to walk with God, and welcomed the Lord as the center of his life.

Gen. 12:9 And Abram journeyed on, still going toward the Negeb.

The Negeb (הַנֶּגֶב, hanNegev) is the arid region in the south of Judah. It means "the dry land of the south."

Soon after, a famine occurs, which leads into the episode of Abram going down to Egypt (from verse 10 and onward), and this verse serves as a foreshadowing of that.

Now, here Abram's (Abraham's) faith comes clearly into view.

He gave up everything he had, left it behind, and set out on a journey solely by trusting in God's word. Yet, the destination had not been clearly revealed.

Moreover, the land he finally arrived at was not to become his own, but was said to be for his descendants.

At that time, Abram had no way to leave behind descendants. Based on the situation, there seemed to be no possibility.

In fact, the promise that "the land would be given to his descendants" would only be fulfilled 400 years later.

Furthermore, after those 400 years, the Israelites who came out of Egypt failed to enter Canaan due to fear,

and they spent another 40 years in the wilderness---making a total of 440 years.

What is important here is that, in a situation that from a realistic perspective seemed utterly impossible,

Abram uttered not a single complaint but obeyed the word of God. Rather, he worshiped and praised God and showed joy in the Lord. Truly, such was the person that Abram was.

Abram was by no means foolish or lacking in ability. In fact, it is evident that he had various economic abilities and also leadership to lead a people.

But why was Abraham able to continue such a walk?

It seems that a major reason lies in the fact that he believed in God and knew the beauty of living a life in trust of God.

Why is it beautiful? Because the path of walking with God is without sin or impurity.

Even if the destination is unknown or the conditions seem impossible, that is only from a human perspective.

Abraham understood this, and was able to preserve every aspect of his life in a pure, transparent beauty that surpassed the limits of human sight.

Then, why is that possible?

Because he was connected to God. When the holy God, the God who loves us, speaks the words "Follow me,"

they come from a heart of love desiring that we ourselves would grasp something wonderful in our relationship with Him.

In other words, for Abram, his relationship with God was the most important thing.

He could not find value in gaining something in this world or in fulfilling self-realization for the sake of achieving some goal.

At first glance, living with such values may seem difficult in this world. But in reality, it is equivalent to not being swayed by the things of this world.

Of course, even Abram was not perfect afterward. There were times he failed.

Nevertheless, the fact remains that by continuing to believe in God to the very end, he received something great.

However, more than the wealth or descendants he gained in his life, it is the moment-by-moment walk with God that was of greatest value to Abram.

That is the part we must read and understand from the story of Abram.

Let us reconsider whether our own lives are beautiful lives connected with God, like Abram's.

If we try to win God's favor merely to satisfy our own desires or ambitions,

we will not be able to attain that beauty.

When we truly trust God from our hearts and entrust ourselves to Him,

the sin and darkness surrounding us will be cleansed,

and we will begin to see the place beyond the darkness---a place invisible to the human eye.

And let us remember once again that what lies beyond is the gospel of Jesus Christ and the kingdom of heaven.

Heb. 11:14 For people who speak thus make it clear that they are seeking a homeland.

Heb. 11:15 If they had been thinking of that land from which they had gone out, they would have had opportunity to return.

Heb. 11:16 But as it is, they desire a better country, that is, a heavenly one. Therefore God is not ashamed to be called their God, for he has prepared for them a city.

We do not want to cling to this world, but rather, to be released from all its entanglements,

and walk straight on the path to our true homeland---the kingdom of heaven.
