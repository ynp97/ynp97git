Genesis 3:20-24 "Expulsion from Eden"

Genesis 3:20

*"The man called his wife's name Eve, because she was the mother of all living."*

Now, the fact that Adam named Eve carries various significant meanings.

The Authority and Meaning of Naming

In the Old Testament, the act of *naming* symbolized *authority* and was considered a way of recognizing and defining the essence of a particular being.

We should recall that when God created the world, He gave names to all things:

• He called the light "Day" and the darkness "Night" (Genesis 1:5).

• He called the expanse "Heaven" (Genesis 1:8).

• He called the dry land "Earth," and the gathered waters "Seas" (Genesis 1:10).

Thus, the act of *naming* signifies the essence of the entity and is performed by one who has authority over it.

Similarly, there is a scene where Adam names the animals:

Genesis 2:19-20

*"Now out of the ground the LORD God had formed every beast of the field and every bird of the heavens and brought them to the man to see what he would call them. And whatever the man called every living creature, that was its name."* (Genesis 2:19)

*"The man gave names to all livestock and to the birds of the heavens and to every beast of the field. But for Adam there was not found a helper fit for him."* (Genesis 2:20)

This represents the authority given to Adam over the creatures on earth, as seen in Genesis 1:28: *"Have dominion."*

The Name "Tiger" and Its Essence

The name "Tiger" has long been associated with characteristics such as *strength, courage, agility, solitude,* and *ferocity.* The very name reflects the essence of the tiger.

Etymology of "Tiger" (Linguistic Perspective)

The origin of the word *"tiger"* can be traced back to ancient India and Persia:

• Sanskrit: *व्याघ्र (Vyāghra)* → "One who runs like the wind"

• Persian: *tigrā* → "Sharp, fast"

• Greek: *τίγρις (Tigris)*

• Latin: *tigris*

• English: *tiger*

Even the name of the Tigris River (*Tigris River*) derives from the tiger, as its rapid currents were likened to the swiftness of a tiger.

From this etymology, it becomes clear that the name "tiger" conveys attributes such as *swiftness, sharpness,* and *power.*

So, how should we understand the naming of Eve?

Just as with the naming of animals, Adam naming Eve signifies an *act of understanding her essence and expressing it in words.* Particularly, the name *Eve* (*Havvah*) means *"mother of life,"* highlighting her essence as the mother of all humanity.

The Significance of the Name "Mother of Life" in Genesis 3

The name *Eve* appears at the moment just before Adam and Eve are expelled from the Garden of Eden. This holds profound theological significance.

1\. A Declaration of "Life" Amidst Sin and Death

Adam and Eve, having eaten from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, faced God's judgment, bringing death into the world (Genesis 3:19). However, immediately afterward, Adam named his wife *Eve,* a name imbued with a message of hope.

God had given the promise that the *offspring of the woman* would crush the head of the serpent (the *Protoevangelium* in Genesis 3:15). Furthermore, though humanity had fallen, *life would continue*---assuring that despite sin leading to death, life would still be passed down.

This points to God's redemptive plan for humanity, foreshadowing the birth of the Savior from Eve's lineage.

Though Eve was given the judgment of *"pain in childbearing"* (Genesis 3:16), this was not merely a punishment but also a mission---to bring forth life. This contrast highlights the simultaneous presence of suffering and grace within God's plan.

A Shift in Relationship Through Naming

1\. The Relationship Before the Fall (Genesis 2:23)

When Eve was first created, Adam declared:

*"This at last is bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh; she shall be called Woman, because she was taken out of Man."* (Genesis 2:23)

At this point, Adam did not give her a *name* but recognized her as *part of himself.* However, after the fall, Adam named her Eve, "Mother of Life," acknowledging her as an *independent being with a distinct role.*

This marked a shift:

• They were no longer one unified entity.

• Their roles were clearly separated.

• Eve was entrusted with the role of *carrying on life,* ultimately leading to the birth of the Messiah.

Eve in the New Testament: The Contrast with Mary

The New Testament highlights an important theological contrast:

From Eve to Humanity From Mary to Christ

Eve became the "mother of sin," bringing death to humanity. Mary, the "new Eve," bore Christ, bringing salvation.

This contrast forms a key theological theme in redemptive history.

Summary

The act of Adam naming Eve was not merely assigning a name but rather a declaration of hope and mission in human history.

1\. Naming signifies *authority*, and Adam recognized Eve's essence in naming her.

2\. The name "Mother of Life" symbolizes *God's redemptive plan*, pointing to the birth of the Messiah.

3\. The act reflects *a shift in relationships* due to sin, yet contains a message of hope.

4\. In the New Testament, the contrast between Eve and Christ and Eve and Mary further unfolds.

Thus, the name *Eve* carries a profound theological message: Even amidst sin and death, life continues by God's grace.

Genesis 3:21

*"And the LORD God made for Adam and for his wife garments of skins and clothed them."*

The *garments of skins* were literally clothing made from animal skins.

This means that in order to make these garments, an animal had to be killed.

This moment represents the first instance of death in human history.

The Theological Meaning of "Garments of Skins"

1\. The First "Sacrifice" to Cover Human Sin

Adam and Eve, after realizing their nakedness due to sin, made coverings for themselves using fig leaves (Genesis 3:7).

However, that was insufficient, and God Himself made garments of skins and clothed them.

This act signifies that:

• Human-made coverings (fig leaves) represent *human effort to cover sin* (*self-righteousness, legalism*).

• Garments of skins represent *God's provision of atonement* (*divine grace, substitutionary sacrifice*).

*"Indeed, under the law almost everything is purified with blood, and without the shedding of blood there is no forgiveness of sins."* (Hebrews 9:22)

Thus, the shedding of blood through the death of an animal was required to cover human sin.

This foreshadows:

• The later sacrificial system in the Old Testament (*Leviticus*).

• Ultimately, the sacrifice of Jesus Christ on the cross.

2\. The Contrast Between Fig Leaves and Garments of Skins

Fig Leaves (Human Effort) Garments of Skins (God's Provision)

Made by Adam and Eve Made by God

A temporary and insufficient covering A permanent and sufficient covering

Symbolizes human self-righteousness Symbolizes divine atonement

*"Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!"* (John 1:29)

The animal that was sacrificed to provide the garments of skins foreshadowed the atoning sacrifice of Christ.

3\. The Connection to Old Testament Sacrifices

Throughout the Old Testament, God required blood sacrifices for the atonement of sin.

Genesis 3:21 marks the beginning of the sacrificial system, later established under Mosaic Law:

*"For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it for you on the altar to make atonement for your souls, for it is the blood that makes atonement by the life."* (Leviticus 17:11)

However, these animal sacrifices were temporary, whereas Jesus' sacrifice was final and complete.

Genesis 3:22

*"Then the LORD God said, 'Behold, the man has become like one of us in knowing good and evil. Now, lest he reach out his hand and take also of the tree of life and eat, and live forever---'"*

This verse reveals God's response to humanity's changed state after the fall.

1\. "The Man Has Become Like One of Us" -- What Does This Mean?

\(1\) Humanity Gained Knowledge of Good and Evil

• The phrase *"like one of us"* may reflect God's plurality (*Trinity*).

• Adam and Eve gained knowledge of good and evil, but:

• Unlike God, they could not use it righteously.

• Their knowledge was now tainted by sin.

→ Their desire to *be like God* resulted in spiritual corruption instead of divine wisdom.

\(2\) A Shift in the Relationship with God

• Before the fall, humanity trusted and obeyed God.

• After eating the fruit, they sought independence, judging for themselves what was good and evil.

• The phrase *"like God"* directly echoes Satan's deception in Genesis 3:5:

*"For God knows that when you eat of it your eyes will be opened, and you will be like God, knowing good and evil."* (Genesis 3:5)

→ Humanity attempted to become like God, but instead, they became enslaved to sin.

Genesis 3:23

*"Therefore the LORD God sent him out from the garden of Eden to work the ground from which he was taken."*

*"Cursed is the ground because of you; in pain you shall eat of it all the days of your life."* (Genesis 3:17, SKY)

In Eden, work was a joyful act of cooperation with God.

After the fall, work became a painful struggle for survival.

However, this hardship also serves as a reminder of our need for God.

Genesis 3:24

*"He drove out the man, and at the east of the garden of Eden he placed the cherubim and a flaming sword that turned every way to guard the way to the tree of life."*

This verse describes the moment when humanity was expelled from Eden, and access to the tree of life was blocked.

1\. Who Are the Cherubim?

• *Cherubim* (כְּרוּבִים, *kerubim*) are heavenly beings who guard God's presence.

• In the Bible, they appear as guardians of divine holiness.

• In the Tabernacle and Temple, cherubim were depicted above the Ark of the Covenant, symbolizing God's throne (Exodus 25:18-22).

→ By placing cherubim at Eden's entrance, God ensured that sinful humans could not reenter His holy presence.

2\. What Is the "Flaming Sword That Turned Every Way"?

\(1\) The Sword as God's Judgment

• The *flaming sword* represents God's justice and holiness.

• The Hebrew word *הַמִּתְהַפֶּכֶת* (*hamithappeket*) means "constantly turning," indicating a dynamic, ever-present barrier.

\(2\) Humanity Can No Longer Enter God's Presence on Their Own

• The sword moves in all directions → *No way to re-enter Eden by human effort*.

• This symbolizes that sinful humanity cannot attain eternal life through their own means.

• Only God can reopen the way.

→ This foreshadows that eternal life will only be restored through Christ.

The Meaning of "East of Eden"

1\. East Symbolizes Separation from God

Event Meaning

Expulsion from Eden (Genesis 3:24) Separation from God's presence

Cain fled east of Eden (Genesis 4:16) Distance from God's face

Tower of Babel built in the east (Genesis 11:2) Rebellion against God

→ *"East of Eden"* symbolizes exile, distance from God, and the consequences of sin.

Why Did God Expel Humanity?

1\. To Prevent Them from Living Forever in Sin

• *"Lest he reach out his hand and take also of the tree of life and eat, and live forever---"*

• If Adam and Eve had eaten from the tree of life, they would have lived forever in their sinful state.

→ God, in His mercy, did not allow humanity to remain in eternal separation from Him.

2\. To Begin the Plan of Salvation

• By expelling them, God set in motion His plan for redemption.

• If they had lived forever in sin, salvation would have been impossible.

• Instead, God introduced "death" so that He could later bring "resurrection".

*"Therefore, just as sin came into the world through one man, and death through sin, and so death spread to all men because all sinned---"* (Romans 5:12)

Thus, the expulsion was not merely judgment but also grace, leading humanity toward redemption through Christ.

The Return of the Tree of Life in Revelation

The *tree of life*, which was forbidden in Genesis, reappears at the end of Scripture in Revelation.

This signifies that eternal life is ultimately restored when humanity is redeemed and returns to God.

*"Blessed are those who wash their robes, so that they may have the right to the tree of life and that they may enter the city by the gates."* (Revelation 22:14)

Thus, the tree of life is not forever lost---it will be granted again when redeemed humanity is reunited with God.

Summary of Genesis 3:24

When God expelled Adam and Eve, He placed cherubim and a flaming sword at the east of Eden to guard the way to the tree of life. This carries several key theological meanings:

1\. Preventing sinful humanity from living forever in a fallen state.

2\. Though the way to the tree of life was closed, it would be reopened through Christ.

3\. Cherubim symbolize God's holiness, and humans cannot enter His presence on their own.

4\. The flaming sword represents God's judgment, barring sinners from eternal life.

→ *The path to the tree of life was closed, but it would be reopened through Jesus Christ.*

God's Plan Even in the Expulsion from Eden

Through this study, we see that the expulsion from Eden, though seemingly a severe punishment, was actually part of God's plan.

This plan was meant to prepare humanity to once again live with God.

Humanity's first great judgment and the consequences of sin---all of it was due to our desire for self-centered knowledge and independence from God.

God knew that sin would bring suffering, so He did not allow humanity to live forever in that state and instead provided the redemption through Jesus Christ.

Seeing God's Plan in Our Own Lives

Even in our daily lives, though on a smaller scale, we experience similar things.

We may not understand why certain events happen, but God does not make mistakes.

Believing that God has a plan even in our struggles is crucial.

Through faith, we can reexamine our lives and find solutions in God's guidance.

Dying to Sin

One essential truth remains:

We must die to sin.

We cannot return to God's kingdom while still holding onto sin.

The question is: Will we completely renounce sin, or will we just try to hide it?

If we try to manage sin on our own---hiding it, justifying it, or merely reducing its outward signs---we will remain separated from God.

But if we confess our sins and seek Christ's redemption, then we will be restored to true life.

*"If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness."* (1 John 1:9)

Thus, repentance is not merely about eliminating sinful behavior but about a heart that desires to be transformed by God.

A Challenge to Our Faith

Faith is not just about believing in Jesus---it is about confronting the reality of our sin.

This process may be painful and difficult, requiring us to let go of deeply ingrained habits and reorient our hearts toward God.

But if we truly trust in Jesus, He will work in our struggles and lead us into greater joy than anything sin could offer.

Let us walk forward, trusting in the redemption that Christ has provided.

God's Patient Love for Humanity

Despite our repeated failures, betrayals, and weaknesses, God does not abandon us.

Instead, He waits for us with patience, offering grace and redemption through Christ.

Just as God clothed Adam and Eve with garments of skins, He now clothes us with the righteousness of Christ.

*"For as many of you as were baptized into Christ have put on Christ."* (Galatians 3:27)

Let us live as those who trust in God's love, walking in faith and obedience.

God has prepared a way for us to return to Him---let us not delay in taking it.
