2026-01-11 Luke 6:27--49 "Love Your Enemies" (Evening Service)

You probably know the phrase, "An eye for an eye, and a tooth for a tooth."

Hammurabi (reigning from 1792--1750 BC) was the sixth king of the First Babylonian Dynasty in ancient Mesopotamia, and one of the most notable rulers who built up the Babylonian Empire. He unified the main regions of Mesopotamia and brought political and economic prosperity.

The Code of Hammurabi, enacted by this Babylonian king Hammurabi in the 18th century BC, is one of the oldest written law codes in the world. It was inscribed in cuneiform on a stone stele and consists of 282 laws. Its contents cover all areas of social life, such as contracts, property, family relations, crimes, and their punishments.

The phrase, "an eye for an eye and a tooth for a tooth," is the most famous line in this code and expresses the principle of lex talionis, the law of retaliation. This principle seeks to maintain justice and order by imposing on the offender a punishment equal to the damage suffered by the victim. For example, one who injures another's eye will lose his own eye, balancing crime and punishment.

In those days, this "eye for eye, tooth for tooth" sounded very modern and excellent.

But in today's passage Jesus says we must go beyond that.

Put simply, He calls us to give up our own rights, and even to love the very one who wrongs us.

This is by no means easy, but doing so truly leads us into the deepest peace,

and Jesus strongly urges us---commands us---to aim for that.

Luke 6:27

"But I say to you who hear, Love your enemies, do good to those who hate you,

Luke 6:28

bless those who curse you, pray for those who abuse you.

Luke 6:29

To one who strikes you on the cheek, offer the other also, and from one who takes away your cloak do not withhold your tunic either.

Luke 6:30

Give to everyone who begs from you, and from one who takes away your goods do not demand them back.

Luke 6:31

And as you wish that others would do to you, do so to them."

Right from the start we are confronted with something very demanding.

We must love those who are hostile to us.

We must do good to those who come at us with hatred.

We must respond to cursing with blessing, to insults with prayer.

We are not to resist violence, but accept even further blows.

We are not to take back what is taken from us, but rather to give more.

This is far beyond "an eye for an eye."

These words are very hard to accept,

but they are connected to a teaching Jesus treasures deeply---this one:

Matt. 22:36

"Teacher, which is the great commandment in the Law?"

Matt. 22:37

And he said to him, "You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind.

Matt. 22:38

This is the great and first commandment.

Matt. 22:39

And a second is like it: You shall love your neighbor as yourself.

Matt. 22:40

On these two commandments depend all the Law and the Prophets."

This, as in Leviticus 19, is also the word of the Law.

Lev. 19:13--18

"You shall not oppress your neighbor or rob him. The wages of a hired worker shall not remain with you all night until the morning.

You shall not curse the deaf or put a stumbling block before the blind, but you shall fear your God: I am the LORD.

You shall do no injustice in court. You shall not be partial to the poor or defer to the great, but in righteousness shall you judge your neighbor.

You shall not go around as a slanderer among your people, and you shall not stand up against the life of your neighbor: I am the LORD.

You shall not hate your brother in your heart, but you shall reason frankly with your neighbor, lest you incur sin because of him.

You shall not take vengeance or bear a grudge against the sons of your own people, but you shall love your neighbor as yourself: I am the LORD."

In other words, Jesus is passing on these words as the foundational part of the Law---the Lord's teaching.

This is not something that those who have faith can just let pass by.

Let us look at it in more detail.

"Enemy" means someone who is a negative presence to you.

One who persecutes is already actively moving to harm you.

It is hard to accept this. Because they are enemies.

And yet, just as Jesus says, we Christians know that we ought to love our enemies.

At least, we know it now, having heard this.

How do we actually do this?

First, we need to sense God's perspective that every human being is someone He loves.

Every person is God's handiwork, and God loves each one.

This is easy to understand, so let us first take this in firmly.

But we also need to look a bit more closely at this way of thinking.

We should say it this way: in order to love others, we ought to give up our own rights.

Difficult, but let us think it through.

Why do we hate our enemies?

Because they invade our own domain, our own rights---

our land, our property, our family, our pride, and so on.

Or they invade the rights of someone we are trying to protect.

And defending that becomes our "right" too.

If you think about it a little, you realize that this idea of "rights" is born from a self-centered way of living.

That means: because everyone lives centered on themselves and insists on their own rights, conflicts of interest are created.

To traffic-control those competing wills, laws arise.

And through those laws, various rights are defined and generated.

When our rights are violated, a "right to retaliate" seems to arise.

"I was wronged, so I have every right to strike back."

Much of human law (not all, but a large portion) is built on that basic structure.

And the extreme form of that is war.

To protect, killing the enemy is declared to be "the right thing to do."

So, how then should Christians live in such a world?

That is what our passage today is speaking about.

"In this world, your rights, and all the rest---

those are no longer necessary at the core.

Jesus will guarantee something far better than all of that,

so there is no need to forcefully insist on your rights.

On the contrary, the rights defined by this world's system actually pull you away from the very heart of God's love,

so you should lay them down.

By doing so, you become able to welcome the Lord Jesus Christ

into the center of who you are.

And by the righteousness of this Jesus whom you have welcomed,

you will be filled with irreplaceable peace and joy.

In other words, you will obtain true life."

Mark 8:34

And calling the crowd to him with his disciples, he said to them, "If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow me.

Mark 8:35

For whoever would save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for my sake and the gospel's will save it.

Mark 8:36

For what does it profit a man to gain the whole world and forfeit his soul?"

It is obvious that this is not easy. Jesus knows that very well. That is why He repeats it.

Why is it so difficult?

As I said earlier, "Let go of your own rights."

"Yes, I've let them go."

Immediately we feel a fear that we will be torn apart by this world.

We will be used, deceived, taken advantage of.

Ordinarily, this is something we cannot do.

And yet, even so, Jesus says we must do it.

Luke 6:32

"If you love those who love you, what benefit is that to you? For even sinners love those who love them.

Luke 6:33

And if you do good to those who do good to you, what benefit is that to you? For even sinners do the same.

Luke 6:34

And if you lend to those from whom you expect to receive, what credit is that to you? Even sinners lend to sinners, to get back the same amount.

Luke 6:35

But love your enemies, and do good, and lend, expecting nothing in return, and your reward will be great, and you will be sons of the Most High, for he is kind to the ungrateful and the evil."

It is easy to read, but very hard to do.

We Christians are different from those who do not know God.

Becoming a Christian means that, through baptism, we have received the Holy Spirit within.

So we know which is right:

to prioritize "the self guaranteed by the rights of this world,"

or to abandon that and obey Christ even at the cost of our rights.

We know the answer.

And we also know that, though it is hard, as we go on in that way, our hearts and lives grow richer.

But of course, people of this world have not yet realized this.

They are desperate to protect the rights they have been given in this world,

and cannot reach the essential teaching of love that God gives.

Though they could receive an unimaginable assurance---

"You are loved by the One who has absolute power, so you are safe"---

they try to win security by their own strength, fail,

and let their days be dyed in anxiety, hostility, stress, jealousy, and other negative things.

We, however, have received the Holy Spirit and are taught this by the Word.

That is who we Christians are.

To renounce the self, to lay down the rights of this world and obey the Lord's teaching---

this is not something we can easily do either.

But we at least know where the true answer lies.

That is why Jesus commands us, as Christians,

to live in this world as those who aim for that answer.

He is not saying, "Do it, it's easy."

He is saying, "It is hard, but full of rich grace, and for that very reason, go forward."

Here we must note one important thing:

loving our enemies does not mean that our enemies are right.

We do not need to declare the enemy's position "correct."

Suppose there is a religious enemy---an outright heresy.

What do we do?

The answer is: we must love.

But loving does not mean endorsing their ideas.

Because we love them, we do not have to say,

"Well, that heretical idea is fine too; yes, that person really is a reincarnation of Jesus," and so on.

Rom. 12:17

Repay no one evil for evil, but give thought to do what is honorable in the sight of all.

Rom. 12:18

If possible, so far as it depends on you, live peaceably with all.

Rom. 12:19

Beloved, never avenge yourselves, but leave it to the wrath of God, for it is written, "Vengeance is mine, I will repay, says the Lord."

Rom. 12:20

To the contrary, "if your enemy is hungry, feed him; if he is thirsty, give him something to drink; for by so doing you will heap burning coals on his head."

Rom. 12:21

Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good.

Scripture is not telling us to call evil "good."

We must overcome evil.

We may sometimes get angry with our own family.

But even then, of course there is love, and we do not go beyond proper limits.

With that kind of sense, we are called to deal with people.

We do not love only after the enemy has repented.

We simply begin with this: every human being is God's creature,

and Jesus is right now longing for that person's salvation.

This must be our starting point.

What state the other person is in is secondary.

Fundamentally, we are to love all people.

Luke 6:36

"Be merciful, even as your Father is merciful.

Luke 6:37

Judge not, and you will not be judged; condemn not, and you will not be condemned; forgive, and you will be forgiven;

Luke 6:38

give, and it will be given to you. Good measure, pressed down, shaken together, running over, will be put into your lap. For with the measure you use it will be measured back to you."

In Matthew this is expressed like this:

Matt. 5:48

"You therefore must be perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect."

We are told to be perfect as God is perfect.

You can feel how weighty that is.

John 15:12

"This is my commandment, that you love one another as I have loved you."

As Jesus has loved us---that is, love of neighbor to the point of not shrinking back even from one's own death.

That is precisely what is demanded.

It is an extremely high demand.

And only Jesus Himself, as a human being, fulfilled it completely.

Yet this is actually the summary of today's words.

Luke 6:36

"Be merciful, even as your Father is merciful."

In other words, all that Jesus has been saying here---correcting Jewish understandings of the Law and teaching the heart of the Law---

all the points He has made come together in this:

"Become like your Father in heaven."

@@@

So what happens when we aim for this?

We come to realize just how high the wall is.

That is, we become aware of how deeply rooted our sin is.

All the true meaning of the commandments concerning anger, desire, and so on---

prohibitions even regarding what stirs at the bottom of the heart---

we realize we cannot suppress these.

That realization is exactly what Jesus aims for in teaching these things.

Rom. 7:7

What then shall we say? That the law is sin? By no means! Yet if it had not been for the law, I would not have known sin. For I would not have known what it is to covet if the law had not said, "You shall not covet."

Applied to today's part:

as long as we think, "It is my right to take revenge on my enemy,"

we do not feel sin in hurting someone as an act of revenge.

And where there is no knowledge of sin, there is no salvation.

Because the salvation Jesus brings is salvation from sin,

and it will not be received unless there is in us a heart that seeks that salvation.

If we think we have no sin, we feel no need for atonement.

In other words, we feel no need to be saved---and in that state, salvation is not received.

In Japan, under Buddhist influence, many people feel that unless they do something truly terrible,

they will not go to hell.

In other words, people basically think that humans are not really sinful.

So when they hear of "salvation" in Christianity,

they may vaguely think, "I guess it means things will become peaceful in this world,"

but they do not really grasp what we are saved from.

The Jews of that time thought differently:

they admitted sin, but believed that by circumcision and meticulous observance of regulations,

they could escape from sin by their own power.

Being a Jew, they thought, meant the problem was basically solved.

However, through the true meaning of the Law,

Jesus overturns both ways of thinking.

Our sin is not that simple.

If revenge and such things were not regulated by law,

this world would not function at all---

that is how impulsive and uncontrollable they are.

Even if we can keep up appearances outwardly,

in the realm of the heart that Jesus sees,

such things are almost impossible to control.

On that basis Jesus says:

We are all slaves of sin,

and are heading toward death because of it.

Know that first.

And when we know that,

we can clearly understand the meaning of the cross of Jesus Christ.

Jesus went to the cross for our sins.

As the only human being who could perfectly fulfill God's Law,

He died for all imperfect human beings.

It is heavy, but it is an important point.

Do you know your sin?

Do you know that you cannot do this by yourself?

If you are thinking, "This is depressing," you are misunderstanding.

Realizing that all people are sinners,

that they cannot solve that by themselves and are heading toward death---

that realization is at the same time realizing

that our sins have been atoned for by Jesus.

If you can look into your heart and say,

"Why can I not do this?

Why am I still ruled by desires, fears, and anger within?

Why can I not be like Jesus?"

then right there you can experience the salvation brought by Jesus' cross---

the forgiveness of sins.

This state is exactly what Jesus meant at the beginning of the Sermon on the Mount:

Matt. 5:3

"Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."

First, to know the existence of sin.

Then to realize how powerless we are before it.

That is the very doorway into the wonderful peace

that God wants to give us.

We can do nothing about our sin by our own power.

But becoming a Christian means being born again out of that condition.

Even as Christians, we will not become perfectly like God.

Yet by walking with the Holy Spirit who is already within us, we can move forward.

And that is very important.

From our original self-centered hearts,

if we try to keep Jesus' Law,

it will seem foolish, pointless, and wasteful.

But walking with the Holy Spirit is different.

When we willingly give up our own rights for the sake of others,

there is a sure joy there.

The key is love.

Jesus does not say, "Forgive your enemies." He says, "Love your enemies."

Let us look at that more closely.

When you love your enemies,

according to the rules of this world it looks like losing.

But for us Christians, that becomes our greatest joy.

Following Jesus, drawing nearer to true life,

living a life filled with love---that is our joy.

Loving enemies---this is God's command.

It is the commission given to Christians.

What happens when we obey it?

The kingdom of heaven appears locally here on earth.

What kind of place is the kingdom of heaven?

Is. 11:6--10

"The wolf shall dwell with the lamb,

and the leopard shall lie down with the young goat,

and the calf and the lion and the fattened calf together;

and a little child shall lead them.

The cow and the bear shall graze;

their young shall lie down together;

and the lion shall eat straw like the ox.

The nursing child shall play over the hole of the cobra,

and the weaned child shall put his hand on the adder's den.

They shall not hurt or destroy in all my holy mountain;

for the earth shall be full of the knowledge of the LORD

as the waters cover the sea.

In that day the root of Jesse,

who shall stand as a signal for the peoples---

of him shall the nations inquire,

and his resting place shall be glorious."

What is pictured here is a world where no one harms another.

A world that goes beyond the rules of this world, where creatures eat one another and close the cycle.

Human relationships too will be like this.

It is not a world where we must hurt someone to protect ourselves or to protect someone else.

It is a world where whenever we want to love someone,

that desire rises up without any obstacle.

And that love is answered with the same kind of loving desire.

This world will not be realized in this age,

because this world is still full of sin.

Yet Jesus says, "Love your enemies." Why?

To reveal this kingdom of heaven in this world.

And to give this world a basis for believing

that this kingdom truly is coming.

In other words, as each Christian prays and seeks to love their enemies,

the will of the Lord is revealed there.

In this world filled with hatred, suspicion, jealousy, and criticism,

our surroundings can become a foretaste of the kingdom of heaven.

This is what Jesus is seeking.

He wants us to become such people.

So, let us love our enemies.

It is difficult, but let us face it.

Indeed, there will often be times when we cannot love.

But let us try again and again.

Failure is allowed. He knows it is hard.

Because it is not easy, He encourages us like this in His Word.

And we must never forget:

when this command is carried out faithfully,

something unbelievably good is given to us.

When we receive negative treatment, accept it, and respond with good,

we often think of endurance, patience, and long-suffering.

But that is not quite right.

Because if that is all, we are not yet loving our enemies.

Here we are being asked to love.

What is love?

Simply put, it is to truly delight in the other person.

If all we are doing is putting on a good face and gritting our teeth,

we are not loving.

We have not yet reached what the Word calls us to.

So where is that goal?

It is this: whether they are enemies or anyone else,

we are commanded to come to truly like them.

And the heart that is in that state---

is always in a wonderful condition,

surrounded by people it "likes," no matter what is happening.

And when our worldview is like that,

we must remember that this is how Jesus Christ is shown to the world.

Let us grow in the power to love people.

That includes the power to get to know them,

the power to put ourselves in their place,

and the strength to open our hearts, let go of claiming our own rights, and receive others.

It is undoubtedly difficult.

But beyond that lies the wonderful privilege

of being free from the rules and perspectives of this world

and being able to truly like people as people.

This is not something we can do because the enemy has changed their heart,

nor because society has changed.

It is something we cannot do unless we ourselves are changed from the inside

by Jesus Christ.

Let us reveal the kingdom of heaven---filled only with wonderful love---

in the midst of this difficult world.
