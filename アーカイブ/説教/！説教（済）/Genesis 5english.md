**Genesis 5:1-32 \"Seth and His Descendants\"**
===============================================

Now, let us continue reading Genesis today. The passage we are covering today is the Book of the Generations of Adam. Such genealogies are also called \"the Book of Toledot (Book of History/Genealogy).\" In Genesis, this phrase \"the history (genealogy) of \~\" appears multiple times (e.g., Genesis 2:4, 6:9, 10:1, etc.), serving as markers that divide the narrative.

Here, in particular, the opening phrase \"the book of Adam\" introduces the journey of humanity, starting from Seth and Enosh---who, in his frailty, began to call upon the name of the Lord---and unfolding into the grand path that ultimately leads to Jesus. In a sense, we can say that a significant introduction has ended, and the main narrative of the Bible is beginning.

This genealogy stands in contrast to the genealogy of Cain that we examined last week. Whereas Cain's genealogy depicts a lineage that defied God, built cities, and, despite receiving the blessing of remarkable skills, grew arrogant and layered sin upon sin, the genealogy of Seth is the exact opposite. This genealogy leads to the great flood, where a world corrupted by sin is cleansed by water, and only Noah and his family, deemed righteous, survive along with their descendants.

Now, before delving into the main topic, let us address a significant question.

**1. Longevity**

The exceptionally long lifespans (around 900 years) in this passage have led to debates over whether these numbers should be taken literally or symbolically. The traditional interpretation, which takes them literally, holds that these were \"long lives during an era when the effects of sin had not yet fully manifested.\" The symbolic interpretation, on the other hand, sees these ages as referring to the duration of entire family lines.

However, at the beginning of the next chapter, human lifespan is redefined:

**Genesis 6:3** \"Then the Lord said, \'My Spirit shall not abide in man forever, for he is flesh: his days shall be 120 years.\'\"

From this, we understand that at this point, human lifespan was shortened. Considering that the maximum recorded human lifespan today aligns closely with this figure, it is not unreasonable to believe that people did indeed live for around 900 years before this decree.

A notable feature of today's passage is that after listing each name, the pattern of \"he fathered a son at a certain age, lived for additional years, had other sons and daughters, and his total lifespan was a certain number of years, and then he died\" is repeated, except in the case of Enoch. While long lifespans are emphasized, the greater focus is placed on \"death\"---repeatedly highlighting the consequence of sin, as stated in Genesis 2:17 and Romans 5:12.

**Main Text**

**Genesis 5:1** \"This is the book of the generations of Adam. When God created man, he made him in the likeness of God.\"**Genesis 5:2** \"Male and female he created them, and he blessed them and named them Man when they were created.\"

As mentioned earlier, this serves as a literary marker, signaling a new narrative section. A similar phrase is found in Genesis 2:

**Genesis 2:4** \"These are the generations of the heavens and the earth when they were created, in the day that the Lord God made the earth and the heavens.\"

The statement that \"man was created in God\'s likeness,\" that they were \"created male and female,\" and that \"God blessed them\" also appears in Genesis 1:

**Genesis 1:26** \"Then God said, \'Let us make man in our image, after our likeness. And let them have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over the livestock and over all the earth and over every creeping thing that creeps on the earth.\'\" **Genesis 1:27** \"So God created man in his own image, in the image of God he created him; male and female he created them.\" **Genesis 1:28** \"And God blessed them. And God said to them, \'Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth and subdue it, and have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over every living thing that moves on the earth.\'\"

According to John Calvin, \"the image of God\" refers to humanity sharing in reason, spirituality, and the essence of righteousness, or more broadly, being entrusted with the authority to steward creation (the cultural mandate).

Why is it necessary to reaffirm how humans were created? Because it serves as a contrast to the \"wrong way of being human\" as seen in Cain\'s genealogy.

For example, regarding the phrase \"male and female he created them,\" it affirms that both men and women were created in God\'s image, and that humanity, as \"Adam,\" exists as a unified entity of male and female.

Thus, Lamech\'s practice of polygamy---treating women as his possessions---was a departure from this fundamental order.

Incidentally, in modern Hebrew usage, general references to \"humanity\" commonly use words like \"Enoshut\" (humanity) or \"Bnei Adam\" (children of Adam). The singular **Adam** as a collective term for all humanity is less commonly used than in biblical times.

**Genesis 5:3** \"When Adam had lived 130 years, he fathered a son in his own likeness, after his image, and named him Seth.\"

The phrase \"in his own likeness (dəmûṯ)\" and \"after his image (ṣelem)\" mirrors the earlier statement that Adam was created in God\'s likeness. This affirms that \"because Adam bore God\'s image, that nature was also passed down to Seth.\"

**Seth (שֵׁת, šēt)** Genesis 4:25-26 already explains the name's origin: \"God has appointed for me another offspring instead of Abel\" (root: שִׁית, śît). As the child given by God in place of Abel, whom Cain had slain, Seth represents a new beginning---a continuation of the lineage that would ultimately lead to the Messiah, who would crush the serpent's head.

Thus, Seth becomes the starting point for the entire genealogy in Genesis 5.

**Genesis 4:25** \"And Adam knew his wife again, and she bore a son and called his name Seth, for she said, \'God has appointed for me another offspring instead of Abel, for Cain killed him.\'\"

Why is this naming recorded twice? And why is Seth named twice?

Genesis 4:25 focuses on \"Eve naming Seth\" within the narrative, while Genesis 5:3 presents a formal genealogy stating \"Adam fathered a son and named him Seth.\" This reflects a distinct style of biblical writing, where the same event is narrated in different ways for different purposes.

These two records do not contradict but complement each other:

The realistic and personal naming scene (Genesis 4:25): Eve\'s faith and expression of gratitude.

The official genealogical record (Genesis 5:3): The formal passing of the lineage from father to son.

By understanding these layers, we can appreciate how the biblical text conveys both personal faith and divine providence in the unfolding of human history.

Genesis 5:4 Adam lived 800 years after he fathered Seth, and he had other sons and daughters.

Genesis 5:5 Thus all the days that Adam lived were 930 years, and he died.

\(1\) The Life of Adam

It is stated that Adam lived 800 years after he fathered Seth and had other sons and daughters.

From this, the traditional explanation is derived that Adam and Eve had many other descendants, which answers the question, "Where did Cain's wife come from?"

His total lifespan was 930 years. And "he died" (וַיָּמֹת, vayāmōt).

The effect of original sin (Chapter 3) is shown to have actually manifested in death, extending even to Adam, the first man, who originally had eternal life.

Regarding the fact that Eve's death is not recorded in the biblical text, the classical interpretation simply states that "there was no need to write it down."

Genesis 5 is structured as a genealogical record focused on the paternal lineage, and the deaths of women are generally omitted.

Genesis 5:6 When Seth had lived 105 years, he fathered Enosh.

Genesis 5:7 Seth lived after he fathered Enosh 807 years and had other sons and daughters.

Genesis 5:8 Thus all the days of Seth were 912 years, and he died.

Now, Seth fathered Enosh (אֱנוֹשׁ, ʾĕnôš) at 105 years old, lived another 807 years, totaling 912 years, and then died.

The meaning of Enosh's name was examined last week:

[• אֱנוֹשׁ (]{dir="rtl"}ʾĕnôš) is the Hebrew word for "human (a weak being)," and in Psalms and the Prophets, it is used as a general noun referring to "man (a frail being)" (e.g., Psalm 8:4, "What is man (ʾĕnôš[)?").]{dir="rtl"}

Genesis 4:26 To Seth also a son was born, and he called his name Enosh. At that time people began to call upon the name of the Lord.

Last week, we examined how this part describes the contrast between the genealogy of Lamech, a "strong man" consumed by sin, and the genealogy of "weak and frail men," in which faith began to emerge.

However, in Chapter 5, this is not specifically mentioned. Perhaps this is because, as we will see later, the climax is in the birth of Enoch, a man who walked with God completely.

The "calling upon the name of the Lord" continued to flow naturally, even if it is not explicitly stated. This fact becomes clear here.

Genesis 5:9 When Enosh had lived 90 years, he fathered Kenan.

Genesis 5:10 Enosh lived after he fathered Kenan 815 years and had other sons and daughters.

Genesis 5:11 Thus all the days of Enosh were 905 years, and he died.

Enosh fathered Kenan (קֵינָן, Qênān) at 90 years old. Kenan is a different name from Cain (Qayin) in Chapter 4. Though they sound similar, they are entirely separate lineages. The meaning of Kenan's name has no established definitive explanation in Old Testament linguistic studies.

Enosh then lived 815 more years, totaling 905 years. The repetition of "he died" emphasizes the inevitability of human death.

Even those who "began to call upon the name of the Lord" ultimately die. Death had become so deeply ingrained in humanity.

One thing to remember is that, as seen in the resurrection of Lazarus,

when we face death, which appears as a dark finality from a human perspective,

it is precisely then that we can see the light of the Lord.

Genesis 5:12 When Kenan had lived 70 years, he fathered Mahalalel.

Genesis 5:13 Kenan lived after he fathered Mahalalel 840 years and had other sons and daughters.

Genesis 5:14 Thus all the days of Kenan were 910 years, and he died.

Kenan fathered Mahalalel (מַהֲלַלְאֵל, Mahalalʾēl) at 70 years old.

[• מַהֲלַלְאֵל]{dir="rtl"} is thought to mean "praise God" (from [הָלַל,]{dir="rtl"} hālal, "to praise[," + אֵל,]{dir="rtl"} ʾēl, "God[").]{dir="rtl"}

• Kenan lived 910 years in total.

Genesis 5:15 When Mahalalel had lived 65 years, he fathered Jared.

Genesis 5:16 Mahalalel lived after he fathered Jared 830 years and had other sons and daughters.

• At 65 years old, he fathered Jared (יֶרֶד, Yereḏ). Mahalalel lived 895 years.

The meaning of "Jared" is speculated to be "he descends (something descends)," but it is uncertain.

Genesis 5:17 Thus all the days of Mahalalel were 895 years, and he died.

Genesis 5:18 When Jared had lived 162 years, he fathered Enoch.

Genesis 5:19 Jared lived after he fathered Enoch 800 years and had other sons and daughters.

Genesis 5:20 Thus all the days of Jared were 962 years, and he died.

Now, Jared fathered Enoch (חֲנוֹךְ, Ḥănōḵ) at 162 years old. Jared lived a total of 962 years and then "he died." He was one of the longest-lived people in this genealogy (Methuselah lived even longer).

Now, we reach the climax of this genealogy---the life of Enoch.

Genesis 5:21 When Enoch had lived 65 years, he fathered Methuselah.

Genesis 5:22 Enoch walked with God after he fathered Methuselah 300 years and had other sons and daughters.

Genesis 5:23 Thus all the days of Enoch were 365 years.

"Enoch" (חֲנוֹךְ, Ḥănōḵ)

His name is considered to be related to the root חנך (ḥānakh), meaning "to dedicate," "to train," or "to consecrate."

There is also an Enoch in Cain's lineage (4:17), but the contexts are entirely different.

(The Enoch in Chapter 4 was the founder of a city, whereas the Enoch in Chapter 5 "walked with God" as a righteous man.)

So why is Enoch so special? It is because of the following statement:

Genesis 5:24 Enoch walked with God, and he was not, for God took him.

"Enoch walked with God" (וַיִּתְהַלֵּךְ חֲנוֹךְ אֶת־הָאֱלֹהִים, wayithalēḵ Ḥănōḵ ʾeṯ-hāʾĕlōhîm)

• "Walked (התהלך, hithalēḵ)" means "to live in continual fellowship" and "to have intimate communion with God" (e.g., Genesis 6:9, where Noah also "walked with God").

"For God took him, and he was not"

• The Hebrew phrase "לֹא נִמְצָא (was not found)" is reminiscent of Elijah being taken up to heaven (2 Kings 2:11).

Hebrews 11:5 states:

Hebrews 11:5 By faith Enoch was taken up so that he should not see death, and he was not found because God had taken him. Now before he was taken he was commended as having pleased God.

Enoch was the first human to be \"taken up into heaven without experiencing death.\" In the early stages of human history, it was demonstrated that merely calling upon the name of the Lord was not enough to escape death, but that through intimate fellowship with God, one could overcome death. That is, Enoch's deep communion with God showed that overcoming earthly death---being taken to God without experiencing death---was indeed possible.

This is portrayed as the pinnacle of Seth's genealogy, expressing the ultimate intimacy with God.

Let us consider Enoch's faith. The important point here is that his faith was accompanied by actions. Faith is a matter of the heart, but a righteous heart always results in righteous actions. Reflecting on Elijah makes this clearer. Though he endured hardship and wished to flee, he remained steadfast in obedience to God.

If we now desire to be upright before God like Enoch, then the way we should live will naturally become evident to us.

Now, within chapter 5, where lifespans of over 900 years are listed, only Enoch stands out with a notably shorter life. It has been suggested that his lifespan---365 years---symbolically represents the completeness of a solar year.

Genesis 5:25 When Methuselah had lived 187 years, he fathered Lamech.

Genesis 5:26 Methuselah lived after he fathered Lamech 782 years and had other sons and daughters.

Genesis 5:27 Thus all the days of Methuselah were 969 years, and he died.

\"Methuselah\" (מְתוּשֶׁלַח, Mᵊtūšeláḥ) • Enoch fathered Methuselah at 65. The name \"Methuselah\" has been interpreted through ancient Jewish tradition as a combination of מות (mût, \"to die\") and שלח (šālaḥ, \"to send\"), meaning \"when he dies, it shall be sent.\" Though not definitively proven, a rabbinic tradition holds that Methuselah\'s death coincided with the year of the flood.

• Methuselah fathered Lamech at 187, then lived 782 more years, totaling 969---the longest lifespan recorded in human history.

Jewish tradition suggests that \"the flood began the year Methuselah died,\" linking his death to God\'s judgment. Though not explicitly stated in Scripture, chronological calculations align with this interpretation.

Methuselah's unparalleled longevity is not explained directly in the text. However, rabbinic tradition suggests that his long life symbolized God's patience, delaying judgment to give humanity time to repent. This interpretation aligns with the present reality that God continues to delay the final judgment, demonstrating His mercy.

Genesis 5:28 When Lamech had lived 182 years, he fathered a son.

Genesis 5:29 And he called his name Noah, saying, \"Out of the ground that the Lord has cursed, this one shall bring us relief from our work and from the painful toil of our hands.\"

Genesis 5:30 Lamech lived after he fathered Noah 595 years and had other sons and daughters.

Genesis 5:31 Thus all the days of Lamech were 777 years, and he died.

Lamech (לֶמֶךְ, lemeḵ) A Lamech also appears in Cain's genealogy (Genesis 4:19--24) as a violent, boastful man who practiced polygamy. In contrast, Seth's Lamech fathered Noah (נֹחַ, nōaḥ), a man who remained faithful amidst a sinful world. Lamech lived 777 years. The number 7 often symbolizes completion in Scripture, contrasting with the 77-fold vengeance boasted by Cain's Lamech (4:24).

Noah Hebrew \"Noah\" (נֹחַ, nōaḥ) sounds similar to \"comfort\" (נָחַם, nāḥam) and \"rest\" (נוּחַ, nûaḥ). Lamech's words highlight Noah's role as a comforter amid the cursed toil of humanity. Indeed, Noah later becomes the central figure bringing order after the flood.

Genesis 5:32 After Noah was 500 years old, Noah fathered Shem, Ham, and Japheth.

At age 500, Noah fathered Shem, Ham, and Japheth, who later became the ancestors of all nations (Genesis 10).

Today's passage repeatedly states, \"and he died,\" affirming that even in the righteous lineage of Seth, death was inevitable. No matter how long one lived, no one could escape the consequences of sin.

However, two exceptional moments of \"hope\" stand out: Enoch's translation into heaven and Lamech's declaration of Noah as a source of comfort. These events interrupt the pattern of death and provide glimmers of redemption.

Enoch exemplifies that perfect righteousness can overcome death. Noah, representing comfort, foreshadows salvation through faith. While Enoch's path was rare---only Elijah and Jesus followed it---Noah's path is open to all who believe. By trusting in Jesus' atonement on the cross, we receive new life, escaping judgment.

Thus, even in the grim reality of sin and death, today's passage presents the gospel's foundation: \"Communion with God (Enoch)\" and \"Salvation and comfort from God (Noah).\"

Now, today, let us consider the more difficult of the two models, the model of Enoch.

What did Enoch do? It was that he \"walked with God.\" First, it is very important to understand that this involves a significant element of actual walking, that is, action.

It is true that faith in Jesus is not about works but about the heart. However, it is also true that a righteous heart leads to righteous actions. If we distort the words of Jesus to justify our own immature actions, that would be a great sin. It would be equivalent to the sin of Lamech, who used God\'s words as justification for his violence.

Therefore, we must examine our own actions. Thankfully, the Bible clearly tells us what we should do.

**Galatians 5:16-26 (ESV):**

\"But I say, walk by the Spirit, and you will not gratify the desires of the flesh. For the desires of the flesh are against the Spirit, and the desires of the Spirit are against the flesh, for these are opposed to each other, to keep you from doing the things you want to do. But if you are led by the Spirit, you are not under the law. Now the works of the flesh are evident: sexual immorality, impurity, sensuality, idolatry, sorcery, enmity, strife, jealousy, fits of anger, rivalries, dissensions, divisions, envy, drunkenness, orgies, and things like these. I warn you, as I warned you before, that those who do such things will not inherit the kingdom of God. But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self-control; against such things there is no law. And those who belong to Christ Jesus have crucified the flesh with its passions and desires. If we live by the Spirit, let us also keep in step with the Spirit. Let us not become conceited, provoking one another, envying one another.\"

This passage is very clear. Let us live in this way. Let us live in love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control. This is precisely the path that Enoch walked.

That being said, living according to this in a world filled with sin is extremely difficult.

\"In a world where the adult versions of the Precure protagonists, upon reaching adulthood, first engage in drunkenness,\" how can we declare that drunkenness is against God?

Factionalism and division---these are promoted as justice by the great figures of the world, even while they shout at each other with the Bible in their hands.

How can we, in a society where adults and leaders live in direct opposition to this Word, learn that such things are against God?

It is difficult. We cannot live like Enoch. See? I told you no one could. Let us just give up. Let us go with the flow of the world, indulging our desires, claiming that God sees only the heart, justifying ourselves by saying, \"That is what the Word says,\" while secretly---or rather, proudly and openly---engaging in the works of the flesh as if they are normal.

It's fine. After all, as long as we say we believe in Jesus in the end, all our fleshly deeds will be wiped away, right? So we can do whatever we want.

Let us live in this way, displaying the life of the flesh to the world, so that children grow up thinking that this is normal. Let us pass on the works of the flesh to our children.

Of course not. That is absolutely unthinkable. Do not give up. We must live by the Word. We must live righteously, distance ourselves from the works of the flesh, and earnestly desire that our children may walk in purity and beauty, like Enoch.

That being said, as we noted earlier, it remains difficult. It is about our mindset. We cannot instantly cut everything off. However, the important thing is not to approve of it. It is crucial that we do not resign ourselves to it.

When walking by the Spirit, let us try adding the phrase \"just a little\" to the things we must not do:

Just a little sexual immorality, just a little impurity, just a little sensuality, just a little idolatry, just a little sorcery, just a little enmity, just a little strife, just a little jealousy, just a little fits of anger, just a little rivalries, just a little dissensions, just a little divisions, just a little envy, just a little drunkenness, just a little orgies.

Can we truly say this in church?

\"Yesterday, I engaged in just a little idolatry. Today, I plan to engage in just a little sexual immorality.\"

Would we not be horrified to hear such things?

Why? Because within the phrase \"just a little,\" there is the implication that it is acceptable. The critical point here is that \"just a little\" carries an underlying approval.

Now, what happens when we apply \"just a little\" to the fruit of the Spirit?

Just a little love, just a little joy, just a little peace, just a little patience, just a little kindness, just a little goodness, just a little faithfulness, just a little gentleness, just a little self-control.

Here, we see a passive attitude toward the fruit of the Spirit.

Living out the fruit of the Spirit is not easy, and in a world twisted by sin, completely separating ourselves from the works of the flesh is not simple. However, the crucial question is: which direction are we facing? If we say, \"Just a little is fine,\" and secretly approve of it in our hearts, or if we are reluctant toward the fruit of the Spirit, then we have a serious problem in our orientation.

The issue is not about the specific deeds but whether we see them as good or as something that connects us to rebellion against God.

If that is the case, then as imperfect believers who cannot become like Enoch, the words we must add are:

\"Even a little.\"

Even a little less sexual immorality, even a little less impurity, even a little less sensuality, even a little less idolatry, even a little less sorcery, even a little less enmity, even a little less strife, even a little less jealousy, even a little less fits of anger, even a little less rivalries, even a little less dissensions, even a little less divisions, even a little less envy, even a little less drunkenness, even a little less orgies.

Let us move in the direction of doing even a little less. Here, there is a clear rejection of the works of the flesh. This is crucial.

And what happens when we apply \"even a little\" to the fruit of the Spirit?

Even a little more love, even a little more joy, even a little more peace, even a little more patience, even a little more kindness, even a little more goodness, even a little more faithfulness, even a little more gentleness, even a little more self-control.

Here, we see a clear will to move forward, however small, toward the fruit of the Spirit.

We cannot become like Enoch. However, even so, as believers, there is something we must do.

It is not to succumb to \"just a little\" of the works of the flesh, but to continue the fight with \"even a little.\"

We must continually ask ourselves which we are choosing in our time on this earth.

To walk with the Lord means to fight this battle. And the blessings of God are found precisely there.
