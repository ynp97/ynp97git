**October 13, 2024 - Luke 24:33-43 "Jesus Ate Fish"**

Today's passage describes the moment when the resurrected Jesus appeared to His disciples. After the resurrection, yet another mysterious event occurred.

The situation is this: Jesus was crucified by the hands of the Jews and Romans and was buried by Nicodemus and Joseph of Arim　athea. Three days later, when Mary went to the tomb, she found it empty. Peter and John ran to the tomb to confirm that the body was gone, but the linen cloths were left behind. After they returned, Jesus first appeared to Mary in His resurrected body and then to two disciples walking on the road to Emmaus, as we read last week.

**Luke 24:33**

"They got up at once and returned to Jerusalem. There they found the Eleven and those with them, assembled together and saying, 'It is true! The Lord has risen and has appeared to Simon.'"

We often reference this scripture in our study:

**1 Corinthians 15:3-7**

"For what I received I passed on to you as of first importance: that Christ died for our sins according to the Scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day according to the Scriptures, and that he appeared to Cephas, and then to the Twelve. After that, he appeared to more than five hundred of the brothers and sisters at the same time, most of whom are still living, though some have fallen asleep. Then he appeared to James, then to all the apostles."

This part is somewhat difficult, as the Bible does not explicitly mention the exact timing of Jesus appearing first to Peter. It could relate to the scene where Peter receives three declarations of forgiveness, though that occurred later.

**John 21:14**

"This was now the third time Jesus appeared to his disciples after he was raised from the dead."

So, the appearance to Simon remains somewhat of a mystery. However, Peter is among the Eleven mentioned in today's passage. Despite witnessing the risen Lord, the disciples were still overwhelmed with fear, thinking they had seen a ghost. This suggests that Peter may have seen Jesus, but perhaps had not fully understood the significance of the resurrection.

**Luke 24:35**

"Then the two told what had happened on the way, and how Jesus was recognized by them when he broke the bread."

This is what we read last week---the two disciples who encountered Jesus on the road to Emmaus shared their story. They likely conveyed the extraordinary theological lessons they had received directly from Jesus with great excitement.

**Luke 24:36**

"While they were still talking about this, Jesus himself stood among them and said to them, 'Peace be with you.'"

John provides additional details of this event:

**John 20:19**

"On the evening of that first day of the week, when the disciples were together, with the doors locked for fear of the Jewish leaders, Jesus came and stood among them and said, 'Peace be with you.'"

That day refers to the third day after Jesus was crucified---the day He had predicted He would rise. It was the same day Mary first saw Him, so the appearance to Simon must also have occurred on that eventful day.

**John 20:17-18**

"Jesus said, 'Do not hold on to me, for I have not yet ascended to the Father. Go instead to my brothers and tell them, "I am ascending to my Father and your Father, to my God and your God."' Mary Magdalene went to the disciples with the news: 'I have seen the Lord!' And she told them that he had said these things to her."

Even though Mary had informed them of Jesus' resurrection, the disciples remained locked away, still afraid and unsure of what was happening.

Why were they afraid of the Jews? Perhaps they feared being handed over to the Romans for treason, or being ostracized by the Jewish community for following Jesus, whom the leaders had condemned. Despite hearing the resurrection news from Mary, the disciples were still not free from their fears.

When people panic, they often focus only on the negative. However, Jesus is always in the midst of us. Fear and anxiety can blind us to what's truly important or cause us to cling to things that don't really matter.

This is true for believers as well. In moments of crisis---when we should be turning to God in prayer---we often turn instead to worldly ways of coping. When we should trust God, we sometimes find ourselves doubting Him. We may tell others to trust in God, yet fail to do so ourselves when we are the ones in need.

The reality is that God is always in our midst, working to organize everything according to His perfect plan, even when we fail to see it. We know this from Scripture, yet we still often ignore Jesus in the center of it all, looking instead in the wrong direction.

Then suddenly, we realize---"Wait! Jesus has been here all along. Everything is going to be okay." It's not that Jesus suddenly appeared, but that we finally opened our eyes to see that He has been there all along.

This truth---that Jesus is always in the center---is something we must hold onto regardless of our circumstances. The more difficult our situation, the more we need to remember that He is with us.

Still, don't we often close our hearts out of fear of the world? When we are afraid to open the doors of our hearts, let's remember that Jesus is already there, right in the center, protecting and guiding us. Let's open our hearts to Him---our fears, struggles, and burdens---because He already knows them all and stands with us. It may take courage, but the Lord will always answer our prayers.

In today's passage, the disciples were overwhelmed by fear, but Jesus appeared in their midst. The doors were locked, yet Jesus was able to enter, perhaps by passing through the walls. Jesus' physical presence is emphasized later when He eats with them.

But maybe it wasn't about physical barriers. Just as on the road to Emmaus, Jesus may have already been there, unnoticed among them. When their eyes were opened, they suddenly realized, "Oh! Jesus has been here all along!"

The first words Jesus spoke were, "Peace be with you." In Hebrew, this is the common greeting, "Shalom," much like saying "Hello" today. So, Jesus stood in the middle of them and simply said, "Hello."

Luke 24:37: "They were startled and frightened, thinking they saw a ghost."

Luke 24:38: "And He said to them, 'Why are you troubled, and why do doubts rise in your hearts?'"

Luke 24:39: "Look at My hands and My feet. It is I Myself! Touch Me and see; a ghost does not have flesh and bones, as you see I have."

Luke 24:40: "When He had said this, He showed them His hands and feet."

Now, why did Jesus show His hands and side?

When Jesus was crucified, nails were driven into His hands. If this truly was Jesus, there would be marks from the nails in His hands. Additionally, when a Roman soldier, using the spear famously referred to as the "Spear of Longinus," pierced Jesus' side to confirm His death, there would be a wound there as well. Showing these marks served as evidence that it was indeed the risen Jesus. These visible wounds were proof that Jesus was the same person who had been crucified and that He had risen from the dead.

Regardless of how His appearance might have changed after the resurrection, these wounds provided clear and undeniable proof that it was Jesus. The disciples could recognize Him through the very marks of His suffering.

However, these wounds carried more significance than simply identifying Jesus. They also symbolized the suffering He endured and the victory He achieved over death. His wounds were a testimony to the agony He had undergone for our sake and the triumph He secured through His resurrection.

Jesus' resurrection was not a painless or simple event. Some might think that He somehow escaped suffering or avoided the worst, but that was not the case. The marks in His hands---likely large gaping wounds---were the result of nails driven into His flesh that held Him to the cross. His side, pierced by the spear, bled a mixture of blood and water, indicating a deep and grievous wound. These signs demonstrated that Jesus did not avoid pain, fear, or death, but fully experienced them in His humanity.

Yet, even after experiencing all of this, Jesus stood before them, alive and victorious over death. This must have filled the disciples with immense courage. The One who had died was now in their midst, and He had conquered death itself. How much comfort and peace that must have given them!

The peace Jesus brought erased the fears and doubts they had struggled with. They had feared that perhaps Jesus was not truly the Messiah, especially since He had died. They also must have been anxious about their own future: How would they survive? Would they be hunted down and killed like their Master?

But now, seeing Jesus alive, they knew that He had indeed conquered death. He was the true Messiah, and this gave them a deep sense of peace, replacing all the uncertainty and fear they had felt before.

John's Gospel gives us a follow-up to this event:

John 20:24: "Now Thomas (also known as Didymus), one of the Twelve, was not with the disciples when Jesus came."

John 20:25: "So the other disciples told him, 'We have seen the Lord!' But he said to them, 'Unless I see the nail marks in His hands and put my finger where the nails were, and put my hand into His side, I will not believe.'"

If Thomas had been with the other disciples during the event we read today, he might have believed right away. But unfortunately, he was not there. As a result, he had only the testimony of his fellow disciples to go on.

The other disciples told him, "We have seen the Lord!" They must have shared how Jesus had shown them His hands and His side---the physical proof of His resurrection. But even with this testimony, Thomas refused to believe, insisting that he would need to see and touch the wounds himself.

This sets the stage for the encounter that would change Thomas' doubt into a profound statement of faith.

John 20:26: "A week later His disciples were in the house again, and Thomas was with them. Though the doors were locked, Jesus came and stood among them and said, 'Peace be with you!'"

John 20:27: "Then He said to Thomas, 'Put your finger here; see My hands. Reach out your hand and put it into My side. Stop doubting and believe.'"

Jesus appeared to the disciples again, just as Thomas had demanded. He spoke directly to Thomas, repeating the exact conditions that Thomas had set for his belief. This shows that Jesus came specifically for Thomas, to help him believe. He approached Thomas with kindness, not rebuke, offering him the very proof Thomas had requested.

Jesus didn't dismiss Thomas' doubts or scold him for his lack of faith. Instead, He met Thomas where he was, addressing his specific need for physical evidence. Jesus invited Thomas to examine His hands and His side---the very proof of His suffering and resurrection.

John 20:28: "Thomas said to Him, 'My Lord and my God!'"

This is one of the strongest declarations of faith in the New Testament. Thomas, who had doubted, now confesses Jesus as both Lord and God. This is the moment when Thomas fully understood what Jesus had been teaching all along---that Jesus was not only the Messiah but also God Himself.

Thomas' confession echoes the Old Testament way of addressing God. For example, in Isaiah 25:1, it says:

Isaiah 25:1: "Lord, You are my God; I will exalt You and praise Your name, for in perfect faithfulness You have done wonderful things, things planned long ago."

Thomas' words here reflect the fulfillment of everything Jesus had taught about His divine nature. It is a complete recognition of Jesus as both Lord and God, something Thomas had not fully grasped until this moment.

It's interesting to note that Thomas, often labeled as "Doubting Thomas," is the first disciple to make such a bold and complete declaration of faith. It shows that even those who struggle with doubt can, through an encounter with the risen Christ, arrive at the deepest understanding of who He is.

What's more, this passage demonstrates that the timing of one's faith doesn't matter. Thomas was the last of the disciples to believe in the resurrection, yet his confession of faith was profound and true. It reminds us that faith is not about how quickly one comes to believe but about the depth of understanding that follows.

Even someone who is skeptical or slow to believe, like Thomas, can be led to a deep and lasting faith. This should encourage us not to give up on those who seem resistant to the gospel. Thomas shows us that doubt, when honestly faced, can lead to a stronger, more profound faith.

Let's think more about Thomas. What kind of person was he? In one word, we might say "skeptical." But we could also say he was "serious" and "sincere."

Thomas didn't hide his doubts. He didn't pretend to believe something he wasn't convinced of. He was honest about his uncertainty and his need for evidence. While always expressing doubt may not be the best approach, there's something admirable about Thomas' honesty.

What was he honest about? He was honest about his lack of certainty. Yet, even though he doubted, he stayed within the community of believers. This shows that deep down, Thomas wanted to believe. Even though he declared, "I will not believe," he must have had a desire to see the risen Christ.

After all, this is the same Thomas who once said he would go and die with Jesus. He was likely thinking, "If Jesus really is alive, that would be wonderful. But it just doesn't seem possible." So, he remained in that state of doubt---unable to experience peace.

Thomas thoroughly doubted. He questioned everything, but he did so seriously. He wasn't content with superficial faith. He wanted to believe fully, but only if it was true.

Seeing Thomas' heart, Jesus appeared specifically for him, offering him the evidence he had asked for. This must have been a piercing moment for Thomas---a direct response to his doubts.

I can personally relate to Thomas' attitude. I've also had moments of doubt, and I've come to appreciate the seriousness with which Thomas approached his faith. Rather than feeling irritated by someone who doubts Jesus, I find myself understanding their need for certainty.

"Stop doubting and believe." That's a clear and right command. But when faith begins with doubt, when someone wrestles with difficult questions and works through their skepticism, the faith that results can be unshakeable.

Doubt is not necessarily a bad thing. To doubt is to wrestle with the question of truth. It's asking, "Is this real? Is Jesus truly who He says He is?"

Of course, no one likes to be doubted. If someone doubted me, constantly watching to see if I was trustworthy, it would be uncomfortable. They might say, "Oh, look, he made a mistake!" That would be frustrating because I'm human and imperfect, and my flaws would quickly show.

But Jesus is not like that. He can withstand scrutiny because He is perfect. Doubting Jesus---carefully considering whether He is truly the way, the truth, and the life---will ultimately lead us to the conclusion that He is indeed all these things. The more we investigate, the more we will find that Jesus is trustworthy.

There are many things done in the name of Christianity that are wrong---wars, exploitation of the weak, and other injustices. But those are human failings, not the work of Jesus. When we read Jesus' words, it becomes clear that those actions do not reflect His truth. In fact, the Bible warns against such things. The more we investigate Jesus, the more His truth stands out.

It's like the story of Carl Sagan and his daughter...

There is a well-known story about Carl Sagan, who was once asked by his daughter about life after death. In his gentle manner, he responded: "It's dangerous to believe something just because we want it to be true. If we stop questioning, whether ourselves or others---especially those in authority---we may be deceived. The truth can withstand any amount of scrutiny."

I often think of this statement when studying the Bible. The Bible is indeed a book of true truth.

"The truth can withstand any amount of scrutiny."

This is precisely what Jesus embodies---the absolute truth. The truth is strong, enduring all questioning and examination.

As followers of Christ, we must continue to fix our eyes on the truth of Jesus Christ.

But where do we see this truth revealed?

Luke 24:41 says, "While they still did not believe it because of joy and amazement, He asked them, 'Do you have anything here to eat?'"

Luke 24:42 says, "They gave Him a piece of broiled fish,"

Luke 24:43 says, "and He took it and ate it in their presence."

Jesus demonstrated His bodily resurrection by eating with the disciples. There is also a scene with Peter involving a meal.

In John 21:12, Jesus says, "Come and have breakfast." None of the disciples dared ask Him, 'Who are You?' They knew it was the Lord."

John 21:13 says, "Jesus came, took the bread and gave it to them, and did the same with the fish."

John 21:14 says, "This was now the third time Jesus appeared to His disciples after He was raised from the dead."

John 21:15 says, "When they had finished eating, Jesus said to Simon Peter, 'Simon, son of John, do you love Me more than these?' 'Yes, Lord,' he said, 'You know that I love You.' Jesus said, 'Feed My lambs.'"

This scene isn't marked by spectacular miracles. There are no splitting skies or bursts of fire. It is simply a quiet meal shared with the disciples, which demonstrates the certainty of Jesus' resurrection. The greatest miracle of Jesus, His resurrection, is confirmed in such a peaceful and everyday act---eating.

As Christians, we should treasure this image. Many religions emphasize dramatic displays of power and supernatural events. But true miracles, like the one shown here, are often gentle and quiet.

Jesus ate food with His disciples, and we should consider why He did so. Eating is an essential act of living, something we do daily to sustain life. It is basic yet vitally important.

The truth of Jesus is revealed in these ordinary moments. It's not always in grand gestures, but in the simple, quiet rhythm of daily life. Recognizing this teaches us to see the grace God has given in the day-to-day routines of life.

We are alive because of the Lord, and everything we have comes from Him. Recognizing this allows us to see the truth of who God is---the One who knows every detail of our lives, even the number of hairs on our heads. He is the Creator and Sustainer of all, watching over every breath we take. This is our Lord Jesus Christ, the One who is intimately involved in our lives.

Sometimes, we may fall into thinking that we are responsible for our success and that our strength moves things forward. We might feel that God isn't involved. We may even act as if we are the center of our own world, leaving little room for God's presence.

But we must remember that God is always at the center of everything. By grasping this truth in our daily lives, we can walk on the right path.

And what is that right path?

It is the path of true peace, found in Christ.

On the other hand, living in a world where we do not believe God is present---where we think we are the center---leads us to a life of fear. We may close ourselves off, worrying about what we might lose or miss out on. Fear and anxiety become our companions as we look out at the world with suspicion.

However, when we live with the assurance that God is always at the center, our lives are fully protected by His care.

When we can trust in this truth without wavering, we begin to understand God's grace and the invitation He extends to us. Jesus wants us to rest in His peace, knowing that He is always with us. His desire is that we trust Him and live in the peace that only He can provide.

As more people come to experience this peace, the world will change. The peace of Christ will spread, and with it, true peace will reign on earth. This is the world that the Lord desires to see.

Let us give thanks to God in every moment of our daily lives. He has already given us so much.

We do not need to wait for the Lord to come into our lives someday in the future. He is already here, at the center of our lives. When we believe this and live closely with Him at the center, the beauty of God's truth will emerge and be revealed.
