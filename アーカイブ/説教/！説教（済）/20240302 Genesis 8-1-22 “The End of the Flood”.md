**20240302 Genesis 8:1-22 "The End of the Flood"**

Genesis 8 describes how those who were in the ark with Noah were saved according to God's plan. It also depicts the process in which the world is recreated and order is restored after the flood.

**Gen. 8:1**

But God remembered Noah and all the beasts and all the livestock that were with him in the ark. And God made a wind blow over the earth, and the waters subsided.

The Hebrew word **"זָכַר (zakar)"**, which corresponds to "remembered," carries a nuance beyond mere memory, implying **"consideration"** and **"faithful recollection."**

Throughout the Old Testament, when God "remembers" His covenants and promises, it signifies salvation and the fulfillment of His word.

**Ex. 2:24**

And God heard their groaning, and God remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac, and with Jacob.

Moreover, the **"wind"** that blew (**"רוּחַ (ruach)"**) is not merely a physical movement of air but is also used as a symbol of **life and spirit**, representing God's presence and intervention.

**Gen. 1:2**

The earth was without form and void, and darkness was over the face of the deep. And the Spirit of God was hovering over the face of the waters.

(*The word for "Spirit" is the same as in Genesis 8:1.*)

By God's authority, an **invisible power** was at work, and finally, the world transitioned from **negative to positive.**

**Gen. 8:2**

The fountains of the deep and the windows of the heavens were closed, the rain from the heavens was restrained,

**Gen. 8:3**

and the waters receded from the earth continually. At the end of 150 days the waters had abated.

In the **ancient Near Eastern cosmology**, the sources of water were expressed as **"the fountains of the deep"** and **"the windows of the heavens."**

These represent **the water that wells up from the ground, like underground springs**, and **the water that falls from the sky, like rain.**

Now, by God's power, these were **stopped.**

The key point is that **it did not cease naturally** but was **under God's sovereignty.**

The number **"150"** can be understood as **30 days per month,** with **10 months (a complete number) making 300 days**---and **150 is exactly half of that.**

There are various interpretations, but in the act of God's **re-creation of the world,** this can be seen as a **turning point where the negative phase of destruction ends.**

**Gen. 8:4**

And in the seventh month, on the seventeenth day of the month, the ark came to rest on the mountains of Ararat.

**"Ararat" (Hebrew: "אֲרָרָט")** has traditionally been identified with a mountainous region in **eastern Anatolia.**

It is located at the **eastern edge of Turkey, near the borders of Iran and Armenia**, within the **Armenian Highlands.**

In **Turkish,** it is called **Ağrı Dağı.**

The mountain has a **diameter of 40 km** and consists of two peaks:

• **Greater Ararat** stands at **5,165 meters**, making it the highest peak in Turkey, and is covered in glaciers year-round.

• **Lesser Ararat** is **3,896 meters high.**

For **Armenians,** Mount Ararat holds deep **historical and ethnic identity** and is frequently depicted in their poetry, paintings, and literature as **a symbol of their homeland.**

※ The dominant religion in Armenia is historically **Christianity, particularly the Armenian Apostolic Church,** which has been central to the national identity.

On the other hand, some interpretations suggest that **"Ararat"** may not refer to a single mountain but rather **a broader region,** possibly the ancient **Urartu kingdom,** or that it holds a **symbolic meaning.**

**The seventeenth day of the seventh month**

• **Seventh month:** Represents **completeness,** symbolizing that everything is proceeding according to God's plan.

• **Seventeenth day:** According to **Gematria (Hebrew numerology):**

The Hebrew word **"טוֹב (tov)"** meaning **"good"** has letters with the numerical values **ט = 9, ו = 6, ב = 2**, which sum to **17.**

In ancient Jewish numerology, **17** is associated with **"goodness" and "God's benevolence."**

→ ※ An intriguing interpretation, though not definitively proven.

**Gen. 8:5**

And the waters continued to abate until the tenth month; in the tenth month, on the first day of the month, the tops of the mountains were seen.

From the start of the flood to the **first day of the tenth month** (when the mountain tops appeared), **224 days had passed.**

This signifies that **the waters receded to the point where the mountain peaks became visible,** symbolizing the gradual restoration of the earth.

**Gen. 8:6**

At the end of forty days, Noah opened the window of the ark that he had made

**Gen. 8:7**

and sent forth a raven. It went to and fro until the waters were dried up from the earth.

The process of the **world's purification** was carried out over **forty days,** a **divine period of time.**

The **window** was **not opened by God, who had closed the ark, but by Noah himself.**

After **God had shut the ark's door and executed judgment,** Noah's **act of opening the window** symbolizes a **transition** from **God's direct intervention** to **human participation in the new order.**

It represents a **cooperative relationship between God and man**, in which **humans confirm signs of new hope and restoration.**

The phrase **"at the end of forty days"** is traditionally interpreted as occurring **40 days after the ark rested on Ararat (on the 150th day),** meaning **the 190th day from the flood's beginning.**

Thus, **Noah's actions are described retrospectively**, before **verse 5.**

The **"raven"** (Hebrew: **"עֹרָב (orev)"**) is known for its **high intelligence** and **adaptability to diverse environments.**

It actively **explores new food sources and favorable survival conditions, even in desolate or unknown regions.**

This is why Noah **first sent out the raven.**

In **the ancient Near Eastern context,** ravens were often depicted as **symbols of desolation.**

Thus, sending a **raven** after the flood, a **harsh judgment,** serves as a **symbolic depiction of the still-devastated world.**

**Gen. 8:8**

Then he sent forth a dove from him, to see if the waters had subsided from the face of the ground.

**Gen. 8:9**

But the dove found no place to set her foot, and she returned to him to the ark, for the waters were still on the face of the whole earth. So he put out his hand and took her and brought her into the ark with him.

**Gen. 8:10**

He waited another seven days, and again he sent forth the dove out of the ark.

**Gen. 8:11**

And the dove came back to him in the evening, and behold, in her mouth was a freshly plucked olive leaf. So Noah knew that the waters had subsided from the earth.

**Gen. 8:12**

Then he waited another seven days and sent forth the dove, and she did not return to him anymore.

The **dove** (Hebrew: **"יונה (yonah)"**) is a symbol of **peace and purity** in the Old Testament and **the Holy Spirit** in the New Testament (e.g., **Matthew 3:16**).

Doves have a **strong homing instinct** and tend to **return to a secure place when the environment is uncertain.**

However, when **the dove did not return,** it signified that it had found a **better dwelling,** symbolizing that **God had provided a new and restored world.**

The **"olive leaf"** represents **the rebirth of life and the arrival of peace,** a widely recognized symbol of renewal in the ancient Mediterranean world.

The repetition of **the number "seven,"** a biblical symbol of **completeness and divine order,** demonstrates that everything is unfolding **according to God's perfect plan.**

In the **Epic of Gilgamesh**, a similar use of birds (**a dove**) for exploration can be observed.

In the most well-known **"Standard Babylonian Version,"** Utnapishtim releases birds to check the state of the land after the flood in the following sequence:

1\. **Releasing the dove**

• Utnapishtim first opens the window and sends out a **dove.**

• The dove returns to the boat, indicating that the land is still covered in water.

2\. **Releasing the swallow**

• Next, a **swallow** is released.

• The swallow also temporarily returns, suggesting that the floodwaters have not yet fully receded.

3\. **Releasing the raven**

• Finally, a **raven** is released.

• The raven **does not return,** which is interpreted as a sign that dry land has appeared and that the earth's condition has recovered.

**Gen. 8:13**

In the six hundred and first year, in the first month, the first day of the month, the waters were dried from off the earth. And Noah removed the covering of the ark and looked, and behold, the face of the ground was dry.

**Gen. 8:14**

In the second month, on the twenty-seventh day of the month, the earth had dried out.

The expressions **"dried"** and **"dried out"** here resonate with **Genesis 1,** particularly the passage **"Let there be light."**

Just as order emerged from chaos, separating **heaven and earth, sea and land**, and creating a **habitable place for humanity**, the same event is happening again here.

God restoring the **chaos of nature** into an **ordered state** is a **consistent theme** in the history of salvation, demonstrating His **absolute sovereignty.**

Furthermore, Noah's **act of removing the ark's covering and looking out** follows the sequence of **opening the window and sending out birds.**

This highlights **his proactive role** in observing and confirming the world's restoration.

• **The first day of the first month of the six hundred and first year** → **The 314th day**

• **The twenty-seventh day of the second month** → **The 370th day**

**Gen. 8:15**

Then God said to Noah,

**Gen. 8:16**

"Go out from the ark, you and your wife, and your sons and your sons' wives with you.

**Gen. 8:17**

Bring out with you every living thing that is with you of all flesh---birds and livestock and every creeping thing that creeps on the earth---that they may swarm on the earth, and be fruitful and multiply on the earth."

Now, God gives Noah a **command**: **"Go out from the ark."**

The imperative **"Go out"** (Hebrew: **"צֵא (tze)"**) does not merely mean **physically exiting** the ark.

It signifies **the resumption of the original role given to creation** by God.

**Gen. 1:26**

Then God said, "Let us make man in our image, after our likeness. And let them have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over the livestock and over all the earth and over every creeping thing that creeps on the earth."

**Gen. 1:27**

So God created man in his own image, in the image of God he created him; male and female he created them.

**Gen. 1:28**

And God blessed them. And God said to them, "Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth and subdue it, and have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over every living thing that moves on the earth."

**Gen. 1:29**

And God said, "Behold, I have given you every plant yielding seed that is on the face of all the earth, and every tree with seed in its fruit. You shall have them for food.

**Gen. 1:30**

And to every beast of the earth and to every bird of the heavens and to everything that creeps on the earth, everything that has the breath of life, I have given every green plant for food." And it was so.

**Gen. 1:31**

And God saw everything that he had made, and behold, it was very good. And there was evening and there was morning, the sixth day.

As seen in this passage, **Genesis 8:15--17** strongly parallels the **creation mandate** in Genesis 1.

This means that **the world is undergoing a re-creation.**

Furthermore, **mankind's original purpose** is to **rule over all creation on earth**, meaning not **domination,** but **stewardship in love.**

**Gen. 8:18**

So Noah went out, and his sons and his wife and his sons' wives with him.

**Gen. 8:19**

Every beast, every creeping thing, and every bird, everything that moves on the earth, went out by families from the ark.

The phrase **"by families"** (Hebrew: **"מִמִּין (mi-min)"**) emphasizes that **God has established order within creation**, assigning each **"kind"** its **specific place** within His divine order.

Noah **obeyed God's command** and stepped outside the ark.

And what did he do next?

**Gen. 8:20**

Then Noah built an altar to the Lord and took some of every clean animal and some of every clean bird and offered burnt offerings on the altar.

The phrase **"built an altar"** (Hebrew: **"מִזְבֵּחַ (mizbeach)"**) signifies an act of **worship** and **thanksgiving** to God.

Additionally, the **"burnt offering"** (Hebrew: **"עֹלָה (olah)"**) symbolizes **complete devotion** to God.

Noah offered **some of the clean animals and birds he had brought for worship**, offering **a heartfelt sacrifice** to God.

Noah **did what was right before God**, and **the Lord responded.**

**Gen. 8:21**

And when the Lord smelled the pleasing aroma, the Lord said in his heart,

"I will never again curse the ground because of man, for the intention of man's heart is evil from his youth. Neither will I ever again strike down every living creature as I have done."

The phrase **"pleasing aroma"** (Hebrew: **"רֵיחַ נִסָּג (reach nichoach)"**) is a **typical expression** in the Bible for **a sacrifice that is accepted by God.**

This phrase is later used frequently in **Leviticus**, but **this is the first occurrence in the Bible.**

※ **"The Lord said in His heart"** → How do we know this? → **The author was inspired by God.**

**Verse 21** may be somewhat difficult to understand.

• **God declares that He will never curse the ground again.**

• **Yet, He acknowledges that man's heart is evil from youth.**

Ordinarily, one might expect God to say:

• **"Since man's heart is evil from youth, he deserves destruction!"**

• Or, **"Since Noah was righteous, perhaps there are other righteous people, so I will not destroy humanity."**

In summary, **God saw Noah's righteous character as the ideal standard for humanity.**

This is **what all people should be.**

Naturally, since **God created mankind**, it is expected that He would desire this.

However, **God already knew human limitations.**

Unfortunately, **most people would not live like Noah.**

Yet, **God loved humanity, knowing that there was potential for some to follow Noah's example.**

Thus, instead of **destroying and recreating humanity repeatedly**, God **chose to deal with us with mercy and love.**

This declaration from God **directly connects to the covenant of the rainbow,** which we will read next week.

Ultimately, it leads **directly to the redemption through Jesus Christ.**

Gen. 8:22

"As long as the earth remains,

 sowing and reaping, cold and heat,

 summer and winter, day and night, shall not cease."

The Lord made this promise here. It is that He would support humanity with mercy. For that purpose, He would keep the rhythm of the seasons so that there would be no shortage of food. As human beings, in order to sustain life, He first promised to maintain the rhythm of the world.

Upon reading this, some may think, "But with global warming, it seems there is only summer!" However, that is not because God has broken His promise, but because man has overstepped His bounds.

Various covenants appear in the Bible, and if it seems that they are being broken, the problem lies on the human side. God's righteousness never wavers.

The Lord sees into the very depths of the human heart and, even though He knows that the human heart harbors evil from youth, He gives us the most essential things for daily life. In other words, He sustains us.

Today's passage, which begins with last week's episode of God shutting the door, presents two alternating actions: Noah's self-initiated actions and his actions in obedience to God's command. This indicates that it is not enough merely to obey God's commands; man must also use wisdom within his given circumstances to find the most optimal situation.

The major point for today is that, within the exercise of free will and personal action, the act of worship was the first offered.

As can be seen from the text, there is no command from God here. Just as when Noah opened the window on his own and used the birds to survey the condition of the earth, he acted according to his own initiative.

It can be said that the act of worship performed with free will was the most admirable characteristic of Noah as he was led to be a model.

This act of worship expresses gratitude. In other words, in the very moment of stepping out of the ark, he felt, deep in his bones, the awesome power of God, revered Him, and was grateful for being allowed to stand on the earth once again. This is the very image of Noah.

Now, is this something that should be taken for granted? Let us consider what we tend to do in such situations.

In our daily lives we encounter various difficulties. Of course, God knows everything---in other words, everything is within His plan, and He leads us in it and even prepares an escape route for us. In our daily lives, even though the scale is different, everything that occurred in Noah's experience is also happening. But when things finally come to rest, do we worship God as Noah did? Instead of receiving what has been given as something natural and being grateful, do we immediately complain that something is lacking here or there?

Let us look once more at Noah's situation. Indeed, Noah was saved. It was a wonderful circumstance that only his family was saved, but if you think about it, what was given to Noah was a land where nothing remained. He was left all alone on a completely desolate land, and yet God commanded him to manage everything. If we were placed in such a situation, we might be so overcome with despair that we would lose the will to live. How, then, would we provide for our family? Although God certainly provided for that, one can imagine that the hardship of establishing a new life would not have been simple.

In such circumstances, Noah was grateful to God. And that gratitude toward God is what constitutes the proper relationship with Him---in other words, being righteous before God.

What we must never forget is that God never fails. If that is so, then our current situation is part of God's plan, and it is meant for each one of us.

This means that no matter how difficult our situation may be, God's solution is already provided. And those very difficult circumstances are the trials necessary for each of us, and by overcoming them we are given the sustenance for spiritual growth.

Of course, difficult circumstances are, by their very nature, difficult. It is only natural that we cannot simply accept them easily.

I often think of this when teaching children. The children say, "This situation is the worst. It's hell to be given such worksheets." Yet from where I stand now, I can enjoy not having to work and being able to devote all my time solely to improving my abilities. How enviable that abundance is! But to be honest, when I was in school, I never recognized that, and I found no value in studying at all.

How do we feel about our lives at this very moment? We need to look at that once again, carefully.

How we view our lives must be seen within our relationship with God. If we mistakenly view it solely in terms of worldly values, we tend to think that something is missing or that our situation is miserable.

However, if by worldly standards we can truly feel that we have enough, then for the person who feels that way, it is already sufficient. And that feeling of sufficiency will bring peace to the heart.

Again, God never errs. If that is so, then above us there is now God's wonderful plan. If we believe in this God, why should we ever feel a lack in our circumstances?

As believers, we must nurture this sense, for it is here that God's grace resides. God is not here to heed our selfish desires. He is the One who provides us with our most necessary things in the most fitting way.

Yet we cannot fully know this, so within our limited understanding we feel lack, discontent, and continue to ask for more---even when it sometimes harms us.

God's grace is given when we align our own axis with His and live uprightly. And what is needed to align that axis is gratitude. In other words, to be thankful is to accept what God has given us and to regard it as good.

And that gratitude becomes our worship and praise. We must learn to offer this worshipful gratitude to God from the heart, and when we are able to do so, we can walk in God's way---leading to eternal life, a life of peace in our hearts, and the grace of God.

What was Noah's situation like?

He was isolated from the world in order to obey God, given an extremely difficult task, and though his life was saved, he was suddenly thrown out onto a land of nothingness---a situation in which, if it were to happen to us, we might be so overwhelmed with despair that we would lose the will to live. Why? Because we believed in God.

Yet, even in such circumstances, Noah was grateful to God. And that gratitude toward God arose from Noah's own initiative. In other words, it clearly shows that he was truly grateful from the heart.

Likewise, if we too can become like Noah, then we too will be able to overcome any hardship in this world with a positive spirit of gratitude.

For this reason, God has said:

**1Th. 5:16** Rejoice always.

**1Th. 5:17** Pray without ceasing.

**1Th. 5:18** Give thanks in all circumstances; for this is the will of God in Christ Jesus for you.

It is necessary for us to be able to live this way.

However, it is certainly not an easy thing to do. So how can we achieve this?

One way is to practice giving thanks for the small things in our daily lives.

First, give thanks for the fact that you are alive today.

Give thanks that you woke up this morning.

Give thanks that you were able to eat a meal.

Give thanks that you made it to work.

There are many things we can be thankful for.

We must practice expressing gratitude for each of these things.

And as we do, we will begin to see something.

That is, the undeniable truth that **God is at work in each and every detail of our lives.**

When we come to recognize this, our hearts will **naturally be drawn into a spirit of worship.**

Worship is the act of **believing in God, loving God, and receiving the grace of being able to give thanks for everything within Him.**

Let us believe in the Lord.

And let us live out our lives---lives given and loved by God---with **gratitude and joy.**
