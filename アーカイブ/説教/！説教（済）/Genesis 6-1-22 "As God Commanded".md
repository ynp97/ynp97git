Genesis 6:1-22 \"As God Commanded\"

Today\'s passage records the state of the world before the great flood, showing how humanity, though increasing under the blessing of God\'s creation, gradually fell into moral corruption and strayed from God\'s will. This has relevance to our modern world as well. Let us consider God\'s will in this situation and the life of Noah, a righteous man.

Gen. 6:1 \"When man began to multiply on the face of the land and daughters were born to them,\"

The Hebrew word \"בְּהִתְרָבְבוֹ\" (behitrāḇbō), meaning \"to multiply,\" conveys a process of continuous internal growth, emphasizing a sustained and powerful increase over time. This implies continuity with God\'s command to \"be fruitful and multiply,\" showing that humanity received the gift of prosperity. Over the past few weeks, we have studied the genealogies of Cain and Seth, which demonstrate two contrasting ways of life in relation to God, both of which continued to expand.

Gen. 6:2 \"the sons of God saw that the daughters of man were attractive. And they took as their wives any they chose.\"

Verse 2 states, \"the sons of God saw that the daughters of man were attractive. And they took as their wives any they chose.\" The Hebrew phrase \"בְּנֵי הָאֱלֹהִים\" (benê hāʾĕlōhîm), literally meaning \"sons of God\" or \"members of God\'s tribe,\" has been the subject of much debate.

One interpretation takes this literally, referring to supernatural beings such as angels or fallen angels. This interpretation is supported by 2 Peter 2:4:

2Pet. 2:4 \"For if God did not spare angels when they sinned, but cast them into hell and committed them to chains of gloomy darkness to be kept until the judgment;\"

The book of Job also provides insight into the nature of the \"sons of God\":

Job 1:6 \"Now there was a day when the sons of God came to present themselves before the LORD, and Satan also came among them.\" Job 1:7 \"The LORD said to Satan, \'From where have you come?\' Satan answered the LORD and said, \'From going to and fro on the earth, and from walking up and down on it.\'\"

Here, the \"sons of God\" are depicted as part of a heavenly assembly, serving as divine council members who mark the distinction between human and spiritual realms.

On the other hand, the traditional Reformed interpretation understands \"sons of God\" as referring to the godly descendants of Seth, who began intermarrying with the morally corrupt descendants of Cain.

The word \"attractive\" (טוֹב, tōv) not only refers to physical beauty but also to good character traits, suggesting that these daughters possessed charming qualities in every aspect.

Regardless of the interpretation, this union represents a disruption of God\'s ordained order. Essentially, those who were supposed to be pure indulged in improper desire and formed unions contrary to God\'s will. Naturally, God regarded this gravely.

Gen. 6:3 \"Then the LORD said, \'My Spirit shall not abide in man forever, for he is flesh: his days shall be 120 years.\'\"

The phrase \"My Spirit\" (רוּחִי, rūḥî) in Hebrew means \"spirit\" or \"breath,\" symbolizing God\'s life-giving and sovereign presence:

Gen. 2:7 \"Then the LORD God formed the man of dust from the ground and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life, and the man became a living creature.\"

However, due to sin, the Spirit that had been granted to humanity became finite, destined to depart upon bodily death. Now, even that duration was shortened.

The \"120 years\" can be interpreted in two ways: as the new lifespan limit for humanity or as the period of divine patience before the flood. If the former, we see that, despite medical advancements, human lifespans today still generally do not exceed this limit.

Ultimately, God declared, \"I will no longer contend with humanity indefinitely.\" If mankind continued to misuse the gift of life, God would not extend His Spirit's presence among them.

Gen. 6:4 \"The Nephilim were on the earth in those days, and also afterward, when the sons of God came in to the daughters of man and they bore children to them. These were the mighty men who were of old, the men of renown.\"

Genesis 6:4 introduces the \"Nephilim,\" described as present both before and after the union of the \"sons of God\" and \"daughters of man.\" The Hebrew term \"נְפִילִים\" (nəfîlîm) is derived from the root meaning \"to fall,\" leading some scholars to translate it as \"giants,\" \"heroes,\" or \"fallen ones.\" This term aligns with various ancient myths, including the Epic of Gilgamesh and Middle Eastern giant legends.

The apocryphal Book of Enoch also elaborates on these beings, describing them as the offspring of fallen angels, reinforcing the supernatural interpretation.

The Nephilim are mentioned again in Numbers:

Num. 13:30 \"But Caleb quieted the people before Moses and said, \'Let us go up at once and occupy it, for we are well able to overcome it.\'\" Num. 13:31 \"Then the men who had gone up with him said, \'We are not able to go up against the people, for they are stronger than we are.\'\" Num. 13:32 \"So they brought to the people of Israel a bad report of the land that they had spied out, saying, \'The land, through which we have gone to spy it out, is a land that devours its inhabitants, and all the people that we saw in it are of great height.\'\" Num. 13:33 \"And there we saw the Nephilim (the sons of Anak, who come from the Nephilim), and we seemed to ourselves like grasshoppers, and so we seemed to them.\" Num. 14:1 \"Then all the congregation raised a loud cry, and the people wept that night.\"

However, since this passage describes the Israelite spies\' reaction of fear, it is likely that they exaggerated what they saw. While this account does not confirm the existence of giants, it does show that traditions about them persisted long after the flood. These beings were recognized as overwhelmingly powerful, striking fear into the hearts of those who encountered them.

In summary, Genesis 6:1-4 describes the period leading up to the flood, a time when human morality had deteriorated and supernatural forces or corrupted lineages had emerged to further disrupt God\'s creation. These passages set the stage for God\'s decision to bring judgment upon the world while preserving Noah, the one righteous man.

Gen. 6:5 The LORD saw that the wickedness of man was great in the earth, and that every intention of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually.

The Hebrew word for \"wickedness\" here does not merely mean \"evil\" but rather a comprehensive moral decay and corruption. It emphasizes that all thoughts, intentions, and desires of the heart were consistently inclined toward evil.

It is truly tragic that humanity, being foolish, continually inclines every intention of their heart toward evil. This reality, seen in the pre-flood era of Noah, continues even in our present day. Regrettably, the world today appears to be inclined toward evil. While individual conversations may not always reveal this, when viewed from a macro perspective of humanity, the sheer foolishness and inclination toward evil are undeniable. If fulfilling one\'s desires and asserting one\'s rights are considered justice in this world, then it is inevitable that people's hearts will be fundamentally based on evil.

It is essential to deeply examine what serves as the standard for one\'s heart.

Gen. 6:6 And the LORD regretted that he had made man on the earth, and it grieved him to his heart. Gen. 6:7 So the LORD said, \"I will blot out man whom I have created from the face of the land, man and animals and creeping things and birds of the heavens, for I am sorry that I have made them.\"

How should we understand God\'s \"regret\"?

The Hebrew word \"נחם (nacham)\" does not merely mean \"regret\" but also conveys meanings such as \"grieve,\" \"seek consolation,\" or \"determine a change in course.\" Therefore, when it is written that God \"regretted,\" it signifies His sorrow over the depth of human sin, the disruption of order it caused, and His resolve to correct the situation.

Now, a simple question arises: Did God fail in creating humanity?

The answer is that God does not fail. Then why did He regret?

As mentioned earlier, \"regret\" here is better understood as \"sorrow.\" This indicates that God sympathized with the suffering of humanity, which had lost sight of justice and was drowning in sin.

Essentially, God had entrusted humanity with free will and had left them to their own choices. However, the time had come when He had no choice but to reset the world. It was a realization that this moment had arrived---a sorrowful yet inevitable decision.

Then, does this mean failure?

God\'s plan for the flood was already within His foresight as a foreshadowing of Jesus Christ. It was an event necessary for humanity to learn from. Yet, even so, there was sorrow.

If we think this way, it follows that God will also experience this sorrow during the final judgment. The Judge, Jesus Christ, will carry out His judgment with immense grief. The fact that God has such emotions in judgment reveals the depth of His love for us.

God is not a dispassionate judge indifferent to human emotions; He is merciful. However, He is also a just God who will judge in accordance with righteousness. This is something we must learn from this passage.

Gen. 6:8 But Noah found favor in the eyes of the LORD.

In an era of moral corruption and Nephilim walking the earth, light still shone through. That light was Noah. His name itself, \"Noah\" (נוח, nōaḥ), contains the meaning \"to rest\" or \"to bring comfort,\" symbolizing God\'s mercy and salvation.

Gen. 6:9 These are the generations of Noah. → Here, the term used is \"sepher\" rather than \"toledot.\" Noah was a righteous man, blameless in his generation. Noah walked with God.

The words \"righteous\" (צַדִּיק, ṣaddīq) and \"blameless\" (תָּמִים, tamîm) do not merely refer to outward moral actions but rather to a profound relationship with God and a life of complete faithfulness.

Even in times of moral decline and societal confusion, those who remain faithful to God will always partake in His salvation. Noah, despite being surrounded by a world that dismissed righteousness as meaningless, continuously chose what was right before God, discerning it in his heart and following it unwaveringly.

Gen. 6:10 And Noah had three sons, Shem, Ham, and Japheth.

Noah's three sons---Shem, Ham, and Japheth---are mentioned here. Their genealogy will later play a crucial role in God's covenant and the development of nations.

Gen. 6:11 Now the earth was corrupt in God's sight, and the earth was filled with violence. Gen. 6:12 And God saw the earth, and behold, it was corrupt, for all flesh had corrupted their way on the earth. Gen. 6:13 And God said to Noah, \"I have determined to make an end of all flesh, for the earth is filled with violence through them. Behold, I will destroy them with the earth.

Here, the state of the world is reiterated. Due to the evil inclinations of human hearts, what was happening in society? \"The earth was corrupt in God's sight and was filled with violence.\" It is stated that \"all flesh had corrupted their way on the earth.\"

The phrase \"the end of all flesh\" (כֵּל מִכָּל בְּשָׂר) declares the judgment that is about to come upon all of creation, emphasizing the precision of God\'s justice.

This passage reveals that sin is not merely an individual matter of the heart but has severe consequences that affect all of society. A world that has lost sight of God\'s righteousness results in the corruption of all creation. Since humans were entrusted with the management of creation, such corruption was inevitable.

Let us reflect on what it means to \"corrupt one\'s way.\"

What is \"one's way\"? Is it merely a self-centered path dictated by personal desires? No, it is not.

Rather, \"one's way\" refers to the best path that God has prepared for each individual. God, knowing everything, created us with a unique path in mind for each of us. Alongside this, He also granted us free will.

The best and most righteous path we can walk is to willingly follow the way God has prepared for us, using our free will. When we do this, our lives truly become blessed. Our talents are utilized, relationships are harmonious, and our hearts are so rich that we do not need to covet anything.

However, unfortunately, humans frequently go astray.

They refuse to trust God\'s word, thinking they possess greater wisdom than Him, and redefine everything according to their own understanding. They deviate from the path God has given them, choosing instead their own paths.

Certainly, such choices may provide temporary pleasure and satisfaction. This often deceives people into believing that those things are necessary for their lives.

However, as Scripture states, such a way of life is sinful and can never be truly good. As a result, their lives are filled with a sense of insufficiency, criticism of others, and dissatisfaction over things that do not go as they wish.

A person who continually inclines their heart toward evil, ignoring God\'s word and pursuing sin, ultimately makes their own life suffocating.

While the way of surrendering to God brings peace and freedom, people willingly stray from that path, complaining about restrictions, lamenting their lack of rights, resenting others, and yearning for what they do not have.

Then, God declares to Noah, \"I will blot out man whom I have created from the face of the land,\" pronouncing His judgment on all of creation.

This means that God, seeing humanity continuing to self-destruct daily through sinful living, determines to reset the world. The root of this judgment lies in His love for humanity, desiring to give them a better way to live. This is something we must always remember.

Gen. 6:14 Make yourself an ark of gopher wood. Make rooms in the ark and cover it inside and out with pitch. Gen. 6:15 This is how you are to make it: the length of the ark 300 cubits, its breadth 50 cubits, and its height 30 cubits. Gen. 6:16 Make a roof for the ark, and finish it to a cubit above, and set the door of the ark in its side. Make it with lower, second, and third decks.

Here, God commands Noah to build an ark. God instructs him to use \"gopher wood\" and to cover it inside and out with pitch, providing specific dimensions and structure details.

\"Gopher wood\" (עֲצֵי גוֹפֶר, \'ăṣēy gopher): The exact identification of this wood is unknown. Traditional interpretations suggest it could be cypress or another durable, preservative wood.

The specified measurements (300 cubits, 50 cubits, 30 cubits) symbolize order and completeness in the ancient Near Eastern worldview.

For instance, the number \"300\" is often used in ancient texts to signify grandeur, completeness, and the fulfillment of divine order. The length of the ark being 300 cubits reflects God\'s perfect order and intention in restructuring the world through His salvation plan.

Additionally, \"50\" represents cyclical renewal and liberation, as seen in Leviticus 25, where the Jubilee year occurred every 50 years, symbolizing release and restoration. The ark's width of 50 cubits may indicate God's plan for a new beginning and deliverance.

The number \"30\" in the Bible frequently symbolizes maturity, order, and discipline. The ark's height of 30 cubits represents a structured, harmonious salvation plan.

If we approximate one cubit as 0.45 meters:

Length: 300 cubits = about 135 meters

Width: 50 cubits = about 22.5 meters

Height: 30 cubits = about 13.5 meters

The window in the ark served a functional purpose for ventilation but also had a symbolic significance, allowing light to enter the enclosed space. This light represents God\'s revelation and presence, signifying that the ark was not just a refuge but also a place of divine communion and new beginnings.

The door positioned on the side of the ark symbolizes the singular entrance into salvation. In the New Testament, Jesus declares, \"I am the door\" (John 10), drawing a parallel to the exclusive path to salvation. The door's placement underscores the necessity of divine election and order in the salvation process.

Thus, the ark serves not only as a physical refuge but also as a symbol of God's covenant and salvation, foreshadowing the rainbow covenant in Genesis 9 and ultimately pointing to the final judgment and salvation in Christ.

Gen. 6:17 For behold, I will bring a flood of waters upon the earth to destroy all flesh in which is the breath of life under heaven. Everything that is on the earth shall die. Gen. 6:18 But I will establish my covenant with you, and you shall come into the ark, you, your sons, your wife, and your sons' wives with you. Gen. 6:19 And of every living thing of all flesh, you shall bring two of every sort into the ark to keep them alive with you. They shall be male and female. Gen. 6:20 Of the birds according to their kinds, and of the animals according to their kinds, of every creeping thing of the ground, according to its kind, two of every sort shall come in to you to keep them alive.

God declares His judgment through the flood, eliminating \"all flesh in which is the breath of life,\" yet He establishes a covenant with Noah. This moment marks the beginning of explicit covenant theology in Scripture.

The instruction for pairs of animals, male and female, signifies God's intent to preserve life and sustain creation beyond the flood. The restoration of species aligns with His original declaration that creation was \"very good.\" (Gen. 1:31)

This passage emphasizes the sanctity of creation and the importance of stewardship. As believers, we must recognize that God values all living things and that we have a responsibility to care for the earth.

Gen. 6:21 Also take with you every sort of food that is eaten, and store it up. It shall serve as food for you and for them.

Here, we see that God's plan is not reckless but well-prepared. He provides for Noah and the creatures, ensuring their survival. This reflects a broader biblical theme: When God calls His people to a mission, He also supplies their needs.

This principle applies to the Christian walk: When we follow God's calling, He prepares the way and provides all that is necessary for the journey.

Gen. 6:22 Noah did this; he did all that God commanded him.

Finally, verse 22 highlights Noah's unwavering obedience. This phrase encapsulates Noah's faith and commitment.

Noah's obedience was not a one-time event but a lifestyle.

Gen. 6:9 states: \"Noah was a righteous man, blameless in his generation. Noah walked with God.\"

Noah\'s \"walking with God\" signifies a continuous, faithful relationship rather than occasional acts of devotion.

Consider the world in which Noah lived---one where \"the wickedness of man was great in the earth\" (Gen. 6:5) and where \"all flesh had corrupted their way on the earth\" (Gen. 6:12). Despite this, Noah chose to live righteously.

This is relevant for us today. We cannot blame the world, society, or others for our choices. Each person must decide how to live. Like Noah, we are called to make righteous choices regardless of the surrounding moral decay.

Ultimately, Noah's life teaches us that faith is expressed through obedience. His actions demonstrate that true belief manifests in faithful living. May we also seek to do all that God commands, walking faithfully with Him every day.

Gen. 5:19 Now the works of the flesh are evident: sexual immorality, impurity, sensuality, Gen. 5:20 idolatry, sorcery, enmity, strife, jealousy, fits of anger, rivalries, dissensions, divisions, Gen. 5:21 envy, drunkenness, orgies, and things like these. I warn you, as I warned you before, that those who do such things will not inherit the kingdom of God.

Indeed, the world is filled with such things. It is a matter of sorrow.

Gen. 5:22 But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, Gen. 5:23 gentleness, self-control; against such things there is no law. Gen. 5:24 And those who belong to Christ Jesus have crucified the flesh with its passions and desires. Gen. 5:25 If we live by the Spirit, let us also keep in step with the Spirit. Gen. 5:26 Let us not become conceited, provoking one another, envying one another.

Can we truly live in this way? Can we crucify our flesh along with its passions and desires?

It is indeed difficult, but even so, the importance of asking God at every step, \"Is this truly the right way to go?\" If we seek to be righteous, we must continue to ask this question. Continuing to ask this question itself is righteousness, and it is walking in God's path.

And another aspect of Noah's absolute obedience: It was not about doing what he could do. It was not about doing something because it was within his ability.

What was Noah commanded to do?

It was to build an ark of an enormous size that was practically impossible for a single person to construct, and to believe in a prophecy of an unimaginable scale---the coming of the flood and the destruction of the world.

Yet, Noah did it.

Noah heard the word, believed it, and began the work. Despite the immense difficulties---physically, economically, and mentally---he completed it. What enabled him to do this was his faith, his unwavering belief that God had commanded him to do so.

One thing we must remember here: Noah did not suddenly receive faith through a dramatic event. Rather, through the accumulation of small, daily acts of faith, he had cultivated his belief. That is why Noah was a righteous and blameless man. It was through his lifelong walk of faith that he was entrusted with such a great task.

Through this journey, Noah must have learned many lessons. He must have experienced again and again that God does not make mistakes, that His plans always bring blessings to those who trust and follow Him, and that along the way, unseen preparations had already been made. Through his everyday experiences, Noah had come to fully trust that when he followed God's way, solutions far beyond human understanding were provided.

That is why such a great task was entrusted to him.

For us as well, if we sense God's will in our lives, we should step forward and act upon it.

It is rarely something easy or convenient. Most often, we feel, \"This is beyond me.\" Why?

Because through such experiences, we come to know the true nature of God. This is the path God has prepared for us.

If, in our fear, we pray to God and receive His words, \"Do not fear,\" then rather, that is exactly when we should move forward.

Being young, being old, being weak, having no money---none of these matter in God\'s plans. The Bible makes that clear.

For example, in the story of Noah's ark, what seems most difficult is not even the construction, but the gathering of the animals. How was that even possible?

We might think this way, reasoning that since we cannot figure out a way, then it must be impossible. And so, we decide not to act.

But that is not the right answer.

If we do not understand, then first, we should trust and start by building the rooms. That is the answer.

In the place of righteousness, God works and fulfills. We must believe this completely.

To believe in God's word and put it into practice---that is how we come to know the certainty of God's plan. Through that experience, our faith deepens even further. This process is the very journey we are meant to walk, the true path given to us, the good and righteous way.

Gen. 6:22 Noah did this; he did all that God commanded him. Gen. 6:9 Noah was a righteous man, blameless in his generation. Noah walked with God.

Can we believe like Noah?

First, we must believe that when we trust in God and walk in obedience to Him, our path is established, and everything becomes \"good.\"

Let us live each day in obedience to God.
