Genesis 2:18-25 "It Is Not Good That the Man Should Be Alone."

Up to last week, we explored how the creation of the world was depicted as good from God's perspective and then shifted to being portrayed from the human perspective. We learned that humans were formed like clay vessels, carefully and lovingly crafted by God's hands.

Today, we will look at how the distinction between male and female was made from the human. This is a passage that has been the subject of much discussion, so we will examine it carefully.

To start, let us briefly consider the nature of the debates surrounding this passage. These debates center on whether men and women are equal. Specifically, they question whether this equality means that men are in a position of superiority. Those who argue for male superiority often point to the following passage as definitive:

1 Timothy 2:8-15

"I desire then that in every place the men should pray, lifting holy hands without anger or quarreling; likewise also that women should adorn themselves in respectable apparel, with modesty and self-control, not with braided hair and gold or pearls or costly attire, but with what is proper for women who profess godliness---with good works. Let a woman learn quietly with all submissiveness. I do not permit a woman to teach or to exercise authority over a man; rather, she is to remain quiet. For Adam was formed first, then Eve; and Adam was not deceived, but the woman was deceived and became a transgressor. Yet she will be saved through childbearing---if they continue in faith and love and holiness, with self-control."

Certainly, this passage can be understood as affirming male superiority. Moreover, it is important to recognize that these words were spoken by Paul, who is not thought to have expressed theological error in his teaching.

Theological Positions on Gender Roles

Here is a summary of the theological positions regarding the roles of men and women in the church and family, which stem from differing interpretations of the Bible and cultural contexts:

1\. Complementarianism (Traditional/Conservative):

• Belief: Men and women have complementary roles. Men should assume leadership in the home and church.

• Key Scriptures: Ephesians 5:22-23 ("Wives, submit to your own husbands"), 1 Timothy 2:12 ("I do not permit a woman to teach or to exercise authority over a man").

2\. Egalitarianism (Feminist Theology):

• Belief: Men and women are equal in creation and should share equal roles in life and ministry. Supports women pastors.

• Key Scriptures: Galatians 3:28 ("There is neither male nor female"), Genesis 1:27 ("So God created man in his own image, male and female he created them").

3\. Moderate Position:

• Belief: Acknowledges the differences between men and women while allowing flexible roles in various contexts. Policies may differ between churches.

• Key Scriptures: 1 Corinthians 11:11-12 ("Nevertheless, in the Lord woman is not independent of man nor man of woman"), Philippians 4:3 (Paul commends women for their ministry roles).

4\. Pneumatological View (Spirit-led):

• Belief: Emphasizes the gifts and calling of the Holy Spirit over gender distinctions. Many churches in this view permit female pastors.

• Key Scriptures: Joel 2:28-29 ("I will pour out my Spirit on all flesh"), Galatians 3:28.

5\. Ascetic Perspective:

• Belief: Downplays gender and marriage, viewing them as temporary. Prioritizes spiritual completeness.

• Key Scriptures: Matthew 22:30 ("In the resurrection they neither marry nor are given in marriage"), 1 Corinthians 7:7-8 (Paul advocates for singleness).

6\. Congregational Autonomy:

• Belief: Individual churches decide roles independently. Traditions range from traditional to progressive.

• Key Scriptures: Romans 14:5 ("Each one should be fully convinced in his own mind"), 1 Corinthians 9:22 ("I have become all things to all people").

7\. Cultural Adaptation:

• Belief: Interprets Scripture in its cultural context and applies it flexibly to modern situations.

• Key Scriptures: 1 Corinthians 11:16 ("We have no such practice, nor do the churches of God").

8\. Pragmatic View:

• Belief: Focuses on addressing contemporary church and family issues. Redefines roles as needed.

• Key Scriptures: Acts 6:3 ("Choose seven men of good repute, full of the Spirit").

Summary of Positions:

• Complementarianism: Emphasizes distinct and hierarchical roles, especially in the church and home.

• Egalitarianism: Emphasizes equality in roles and relationships.

• Moderate View: Allows for flexibility depending on context.

• Other Views: Include gender minimization, cultural adaptation, and pragmatic approaches.

Each position is rooted in differing interpretations of Scripture and provides important perspectives for theological discussion.

Genesis 2:18

"Then the Lord God said, 'It is not good that the man should be alone; I will make him a helper fit for him.'"

Let us examine the original meaning of the phrase "a helper fit for him."

1\. Linguistic Interpretation

• Ezer (עֵזֶר): This Hebrew term means "help" or "helper." It is often used to describe God as a powerful helper to humanity (e.g., Psalm 121:2).

• Kenegdo (כְּנֶגְדּוֹ): Composed of the preposition ke (כְּ) ("like" or "for"), the noun neged (נֶגֶד) ("opposite" or "corresponding to"), and the possessive suffix -o (וֹ) ("his"). This indicates "corresponding to him" or "opposite to him."

Interpretation:

The phrase "helper fit for him" does not imply a subordinate or servant-like role but rather a counterpart or complementary partner, reflecting equality and mutual support.

Genesis 2:19-20

"Now out of the ground the Lord God had formed every beast of the field and every bird of the heavens and brought them to the man to see what he would call them. And whatever the man called every living creature, that was its name. The man gave names to all livestock and to the birds of the heavens and to every beast of the field. But for Adam, there was not found a helper fit for him."

• The idea of a helper is of one who complements and balances the other, suggesting equality.

• However, the act of naming, qara (קָרָא), implies authority and dominion, as naming something reflects a position of governance or stewardship.

• The word qara ("to call") appears three times here, emphasizing Adam's active role in categorizing creation, as God had done in Genesis 1:26-27.

Animals, like humans, were made from the ground, but they did not possess the unique qualities of being a suitable helper. The process highlights Adam's authority to name and organize, a task entrusted by God. This authority mirrors God's own act of creating order from chaos.

Genesis 2:21-22

"So the Lord God caused a deep sleep to fall upon the man, and while he slept took one of his ribs and closed up its place with flesh. And the rib that the Lord God had taken from the man he made into a woman and brought her to the man."

Deep Sleep

The phrase "deep sleep" indicates a state where human awareness is completely suspended, allowing God's sovereign will to be carried out without human interference.

• Genesis 15:12: "As the sun was going down, a deep sleep fell on Abram. And behold, dreadful and great darkness fell upon him."

This sleep also symbolizes God's exclusive role in revealing His will and purpose.

The Rib

The creation of Eve from Adam's rib has sparked much debate. Unlike Adam, who was formed from the dust of the ground, Eve was made from an existing part of Adam.

• The Word for Rib:

The Hebrew word tsela (צֵלָע) often translates as "side" or "beam" in other contexts (e.g., Exodus 26:20, referring to the sides of the Tabernacle). This suggests structural or supportive connotations.

• 1 Kings 6:8-11:

In these passages, tsela is used to describe chambers or supports within the Temple. This usage implies strength, stability, and a crucial supporting role.

Eve's creation involved precision and intentionality. The Hebrew word used for her creation, banah (בָּנָה), means "to build" or "construct." This contrasts with Adam's formation, yatsar (יָצַר), which is more akin to shaping or molding clay. Eve's construction suggests careful assembly and design for a specific purpose, highlighting God's intricate craftsmanship.

Genesis 2:23

"Then the man said, 'This at last is bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh; she shall be called Woman because she was taken out of Man.'"

Adam names Eve, emphasizing her unity with him---"bone of my bones, flesh of my flesh." Yet, the act of naming also implies authority and responsibility. The Hebrew ishah (אִשָּׁה), "woman," is derived from ish (אִישׁ), "man," reflecting their shared origin and mutual dependence.

Reflections on God's Actions

God's deep involvement in creating Eve, from Adam's rib, reveals His purpose and plan for human relationships. The partnership between man and woman is not one of hierarchy but of complementary roles that reflect the unity and diversity within God's creation.

God's design for human relationships is intentional, filled with care, and centered on mutual support. Let us remember that the unity symbolized in the creation of man and woman mirrors God's divine order and His perfect will.

• Naming: Does this imply male authority?

• Bone of my bone and flesh of my flesh: In the context of close relationships, this is the highest expression.

The expression "bone of my bone, flesh of my flesh" reflects joy and acknowledges an intimate connection. However, as mentioned earlier, it raises significant issues. The fact that the man names the woman has often been linked to patriarchal thought. As previously noted regarding the naming of animals, naming conveys authority.

This naming has contributed to male-dominated ideologies in many religions, including Christianity, which draw from the Old Testament.

Let us examine the original Hebrew of the names given:

"'She shall be called Woman (Ishah), because she was taken out of \*\*Man (Ish).'"

The words "Ish" (man) and "Ishah" (woman) share the same root in Hebrew. This linguistic connection underscores the deep relationship and mutual dependence between men and women, emphasizing their unity. Naming could also signify the recognition of mutual necessity rather than domination.

Furthermore, Adam was initially a unified being (encompassing both male and female). In this moment, Adam not only names Eve but also becomes distinctly male.

Genesis 2:24

"Therefore a man shall leave his father and his mother and hold fast to his wife, and they shall become one flesh."

• Verses 24-25 summarize:

These verses are foundational, as Jesus Himself references them:

Matthew 19:3-6

"And Pharisees came up to him and tested him by asking, 'Is it lawful to divorce one's wife for any cause?' He answered, 'Have you not read that he who created them from the beginning made them male and female, and said, "Therefore a man shall leave his father and his mother and hold fast to his wife, and the two shall become one flesh"? So they are no longer two but one flesh. What therefore God has joined together, let not man separate.'"

• Key points:

• Biological vs. Social Gender: The focus here is not biological sex but the societal and relational roles of men and women.

• Ancient Context: Ancient Israel practiced polygamy, and women typically left their family to join their husband's household. Yet this verse reverses expectations by stating that a man shall leave his father and mother, emphasizing the reordering of relationships.

• "Leave" as Sacrificial Commitment: The Hebrew word for "leave" also appears in phrases like "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Psalm 22:1; Matthew 27:46). It conveys deep commitment and sacrifice.

• Unity as Completion:

This foundational statement reflects God's intent for human relationships: mutual dependence and completion.

• "One Flesh" as Existential Unity:

The phrase "one flesh" goes beyond physical union to encompass existential unity, emphasizing shared life and purpose.

Genesis 2:25

"And the man and his wife were both naked and were not ashamed."

In the Bible, it is emphasized that man and woman were originally one and united as a single entity.

At this point, neither Adam nor Eve felt shame about themselves. This demonstrates that neither was in a position of superiority over the other. This is because it is explicitly stated by God that they were created as suitable helpers for one another, in a relationship of mutual complementarity.

Today's passage touches on issues that, when viewed through the lens of human rights, can be difficult to reconcile. However, what we must first remember is that in Jesus' teachings, asserting one's rights is not encouraged.

"Recognizing that there are multiple perspectives is crucial.

Do not criticize one another; instead, accept your own position."

As we reflect on these issues, we should respect the fact that Adam and Eve initially felt no shame. A relationship where both parties bring and share their strengths to complement one another is enough to form a meaningful bond.

However, after the fall into sin, the very first act they performed was to cover their nakedness. This reflects a desire to dominate, to assert rights, and to protect oneself. Addressing such inclinations with love and gentleness is vital.

Being able to manage these tendencies is, in itself, the way to live righteously before God.

In marriage and gender relationships, if both parties can complement one another, their bond will become something truly good and fulfilling. This is because God created us to function in this way. If we live in accordance with how God designed us, our relationships will be truly united and wonderful. If, however, this unity and fulfillment are absent, we must return to the Word and learn from it.

We are called to treasure the first human relationship ever established---between man and woman---even before the parent-child relationship was instituted.

Gender relationships vary depending on each individual's circumstances---some may be married, others single, and so on. Additionally, the force of sexual energy within humans is significant and must be controlled with care.
