Today's passage is the well-known story of the Tower of Babel. This section tells the story of a time when the whole earth was unified by one language and culture, and of humanity's prideful attempt to build a tower reaching to heaven by their own strength in pursuit of eternal fame. In response to this prideful endeavor, God directly intervened, confused their language, and scattered them over the whole earth.

Gen. 11:1

Now the whole earth had one language and the same words.

So first, let us resolve one issue.

That is, the consistency with the following verses:

Gen. 10:5

From these the coastland peoples spread in their lands, each with his own language, by their clans, in their nations. → Descendants of Japheth

Gen. 10:20

These are the sons of Ham, by their clans, their languages, their lands, and their nations.

Gen. 10:31

These are the sons of Shem, by their clans, their languages, their lands, and their nations.

If that is the case, then why does it say here that there was only one language?

How should we understand this? Temporally, today's passage comes first.

In other words, at first, there was only one language. However, through the incident of the Tower of Babel, division occurred.

Seen from the perspective after that division, it can be understood that each nation had its own language.

To put it more precisely, this shows the state after the flood, when all humanity still shared a common language and culture.

This unified state itself became the driving force for people to unite and build a tower reaching to heaven.

However, this cooperation enabled by a common language promoted human pride---the ambition of "let us make a name for ourselves"---and God intervened against that pride by confusing their language.

As a result, they were scattered over the face of all the earth, and it was then that the different nations actually began to speak different languages, and cultural and national diversity became a reality.

And the manner of that scattering happened exactly as the Word of God says: "each with his own language, by their clans, in their nations," showing the intentionality of God's plan.

This phrase "the whole earth had one language and the same words" uses the Hebrew word **"שָׂפָה (sāfāh)"**, and it is important to pay attention to its meaning.

This word signifies more than just a means of communication---it also symbolizes **community unity and cultural cohesion**.

If that is the case, then the state of the world before the Tower of Babel can, in a sense, be considered an ideal image of the world.

It was like the Garden of Eden---how the world ultimately ought to be.

In other words, the re-creation of the world after the flood was functioning properly up to this point.

However, in the end, it was human sin that distorted it.

In ancient Near Eastern traditions outside of the Bible---for example, the Enuma Elish or the Atrahasis Epic---there is a strong emphasis on the gods restoring cosmic order and bringing harmony back to the created world during the post-flood reconstruction period.

In other words, those texts emphasize the part where the world becomes an ideal state again.

In contrast, the Bible is a wonderful book because it speaks to us even today.

It explains why the world is the way it is now, what the cause was (human pride), and how we ought to live in response (the words of Jesus Christ).

Gen. 11:2

And as people migrated from the east, they found a plain in the land of Shinar and settled there.

This location is not precisely identified, and we can only speculate where it was, but Shinar is believed to refer to a plain region located in southern Iraq or eastern Syria today.

It is said to be connected to the flourishing of the ancient Sumerian and Akkadian cultures.

Indeed, archaeological evidence of the formation of city-states around 4000--3000 BC has been found in this area, and it is a fact that there are points of resonance between biblical accounts and archaeological facts.

Gen. 11:3

And they said to one another, "Come, let us make bricks, and burn them thoroughly." And they had brick for stone, and bitumen for mortar.

This shows the scene where a technological innovation took place on the plain in the land of Shinar.

The Hebrew word **"לְבֵנָה (leḇēnāh)"** refers to "bricks" that are artificially made as opposed to natural stone.

And **"חֵמָר (ḥēmār)"** refers to **bitumen**, used for its waterproofing and adhesive properties.

In ancient Mesopotamia, bricks and bitumen were widely used in the construction of **ziggurats (temple towers)** and became the foundation for the development of urban civilization.

※ A **ziggurat** is a step pyramid-shaped temple built of sun-dried bricks in ancient Mesopotamia. The word means "temple tower."

It is believed to have originated from **"Kur worship"**---a belief in sacred mountains (Kur = mountain).

Originating in Sumer (in Sumerian, the word for ziggurat is **"é-u-nir"**, meaning "House of Awe"), ziggurats were generally attached to ground-level temples or temple complexes, and they also had temples on their summits.

They were constructed as artificial mountains for the gods to visit in the cities of Mesopotamia.

Gen. 11:4

Then they said, "Come, let us build ourselves a city and a tower with its top in the heavens, and let us make a name for ourselves, lest we be dispersed over the face of the whole earth."

The Hebrew word **"עִיר (ʿir)"** simply means "city," but it is used here as a symbol of communal unity and shared culture and faith.

On the other hand, **"מִגְדָּל (migdal)"** means "tower," and it suggests a boastful structure so tall that it reaches the heavens, expressing a challenge to God's domain---a manifestation of human pride seeking to exalt their own glory.

Also, **"שֵׁם (shem)"** means "name" or "honor," and here it is understood as more than a mere label, indicating the desire for lasting recognition.

In other words, at this point, humanity had achieved a technological revolution, built a city, and under a unified language and will, constructed a great temple. This technological revolution, as a result, led to overconfidence in their own strength and a disregard for God.

Perhaps at first, the idea was centered on drawing closer to God in worship, even by just a little.

However, in the end, it came to be used solely as a means to prove one's own ability in a self-centered way.

In a sense, this is something that may also reflect our own attitude of faith.

If behind what appears to be passionate devotion lies mere self-assertion, or a sense of superiority within the social context of the church, then that becomes a kind of tower-building.

And under the name of drawing near to God, one may in fact be showing disregard for Him.

The phrase "lest we be dispersed over the face of the whole earth" can be understood as meaning, "We don't want to return to a powerless state."

In reality, we might consider a situation like this: what if we were told today that the internet would cease to function entirely? That would be a very severe condition for us.

So, in order to avoid such a state, we seek to reach ever more advanced things---gathering, organizing, and analyzing information. If we go even higher, we may obtain wisdom like that of God. That is the trajectory.

But in reality, there is no end to this pursuit. Because, fundamentally, God is not someone who can be reached by human effort.

We forget this most basic truth because development in various forms is so alluring, even addictive.

Gen. 11:5

And the Lord came down to see the city and the tower, which the children of man had built.

The phrase "came down" clearly expresses God's direct intervention in this event.

This action recalls manifestations of God's presence in the Old Testament (e.g., the cloud on Mount Sinai, the visions in Ezekiel), suggesting the reality of His authority appearing.

Gen. 11:6

And the Lord said, "Behold, they are one people, and they have all one language, and this is only the beginning of what they will do. And nothing that they propose to do will now be impossible for them."

Here, God saw the power humanity possessed and perceived the danger. He says, "Nothing that they propose to do will now be impossible for them."

From this phrase, it may appear that God is afraid that humanity could become like God.

However, reading closely, there is a qualifier: "that they propose to do."

In other words, if they had fully understood God, they might have been able to draw near to Him.

But the fundamental truth is that people cannot understand God. He is a being far beyond human wisdom.

A limited intellect that tries to categorize God anthropologically only reduces Him to a human level and has no real meaning.

And it was likely this lack of understanding that God perceived as dangerous.

Indeed, trying to draw near to God from a state of ignorance leads only to increased misunderstanding.

※ When we say "danger" or "fear," it is not that God is harmed, but rather that **humanity** is in danger.

Ultimately, with our limited wisdom, we end up approaching God in a way that emphasizes only those aspects of Him that we can comprehend---His authority, His power over the world.

And in doing so, we create a distorted image of God that is easier for humans to grasp.

This is something that misleads people about the true God and, in fact, causes humanity to fall into confusion and the misery of self-centeredness.

Gen. 11:7

Come, let us go down and there confuse their language, so that they may not understand one another's speech.

This represents God's decisive intervention to deliberately sever human collaboration and break their pride.

Reading this, it seems that humanity at that time was doing quite well.

Perhaps there was a powerful dictatorship forcing people into labor.

Even so, as a whole, they were making progress.

Or perhaps, they were unified culturally, living intelligently and in balanced ways.

However, what was definitely present there was the prideful desire to draw near to God.

And if we return to the fundamental truth that God cannot be understood, then this appearance of success itself is, in fact, humanity's misunderstanding.

Gen. 11:8

So the Lord dispersed them from there over the face of all the earth, and they left off building the city.

Gen. 11:9

Therefore its name was called Babel, because there the Lord confused the language of all the earth. And from there the Lord dispersed them over the face of all the earth.

**Babel** means "to confuse."

Here, God dismantled the prideful civilization.

In truth, it is not bad for the whole earth to have one language and a common speech.

Gal. 3:28

There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is no male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus.

When this is fulfilled, truly humanity becomes one under God.

However, the direction of their development at Babel was fundamentally wrong, and so they could not be allowed to proceed any further.

Therefore, they were scattered. They were returned to the state of dispersed power they had not desired.

Prov. 16:16

How much better to get wisdom than gold!

To get understanding is to be chosen rather than silver.

Prov. 16:17

The highway of the upright turns aside from evil;

whoever guards his way preserves his life.

Prov. 16:18

Pride goes before destruction,

and a haughty spirit before a fall.

Prov. 16:19

It is better to be of a lowly spirit with the poor

than to divide the spoil with the proud.

Prov. 16:20

Whoever gives thought to the word will discover good,

and blessed is he who trusts in the Lord.

Prov. 16:21

The wise of heart is called discerning,

and sweetness of speech increases persuasiveness.

According to these words, the proud are in fact those who lack true wisdom, and they are heading toward destruction.

Even in today's world, there are many who are seen as successful.

But no matter how successful someone may be, if they are not right before God, there is still a hole in their heart.

No matter what they obtain, satisfaction flows out from that hole, and new dissatisfaction flows in.

No matter how high a tower that person may build, the very fact that they are unable to be satisfied unless they build it already shows they are perishing.

That is why the Lord once again reset everything, so that true, heartfelt satisfaction could be gained

---even without building a tower---starting again from a place of weakness.

Now, let us look at an event in the New Testament.

Acts 2:1

When the day of Pentecost arrived, they were all together in one place.

Acts 2:2

And suddenly there came from heaven a sound like a mighty rushing wind, and it filled the entire house where they were sitting.

Acts 2:3

And divided tongues as of fire appeared to them and rested on each one of them.

Acts 2:4

And they were all filled with the Holy Spirit and began to speak in other tongues as the Spirit gave them utterance.

Acts 2:5

Now there were dwelling in Jerusalem Jews, devout men from every nation under heaven.

Acts 2:6

And at this sound the multitude came together, and they were bewildered, because each one was hearing them speak in his own language.

Acts 2:7

And they were amazed and astonished, saying, "Are not all these who are speaking Galileans?

Acts 2:8

And how is it that we hear, each of us in his own native language?

Acts 2:9

Parthians and Medes and Elamites and residents of Mesopotamia, Judea and Cappadocia, Pontus and Asia,

Acts 2:10

Phrygia and Pamphylia, Egypt and the parts of Libya belonging to Cyrene, and visitors from Rome,

Acts 2:11

both Jews and proselytes, Cretans and Arabians---we hear them telling in our own tongues the mighty works of God."

Acts 2:12

And all were amazed and perplexed, saying to one another, "What does this mean?"

This, as you know, is the event of Pentecost.

What happened here was that people---who had become "nations by their clans, in their lands, each with his own language"---

now understood "one language and the same words."

It is as if the event of the Tower of Babel was brought to a proper resolution here.

And this very image is the foundation of the leading of Jesus Christ.

Though they spoke different languages, they were able to understand the Word of God---the truth.

There are two important matters here:

"That mutual acceptance itself is the fulfillment of love."

"Each has meaning as they are."

Why did God scatter the people?

It was because He wanted them to draw near to Him in the right way.

That is, He truly wanted them to become like Him in the true sense.

Then, what kind of being is God?

1 John 4:8

Anyone who does not love does not know God, because God is love.

God scattered the people. And they each began to speak different languages.

If one cannot understand what another is saying, it is naturally difficult to understand that person.

And if one cannot understand another person, can they truly love them?

It is not just about language. As nations and regions become independent and assert their respective rights,

it becomes very difficult to find love in the midst of such conflicting claims.

That difficulty is visible in its clearest form in the reality of war around the world,

but it is also something that happens daily in the personal realms of our lives.

We see ourselves clearly---because we are ourselves.

However, we do not truly see others clearly. We may know what is happening,

but what we cannot know is how that person is feeling in that situation, from their own position.

For example, even among married couples who are always together, this is a common story:

the husband works outside the home and, from his position, it may seem that the wife, who stays at home, is not working.

There may indeed be no economic income from her.

But from the wife's position, she is doing housework and childcare---tasks that, if outsourced, would yield significant monetary value---and by doing them unpaid, she is certainly contributing economic benefit.

If both continue to insist on their own positions, there is no resolution---they run parallel.

Because both are completely right from their own standpoint.

If this is to be resolved, the only way is for each to consider the other's position.

Rather than trying to make the other understand how we feel, the true love is to try to understand how the other feels.

And Jesus said this:

John 15:12

"This is my commandment, that you love one another as I have loved you."

To persist in trying to make someone understand how we feel in an unyielding back-and-forth is love directed toward oneself.

But Jesus lived for the sake of love toward others, even sacrificing Himself.

In other words, to try to understand a person's situation---something unknowable from our own standpoint---by stepping beyond our own perspective, is true love in its fullest sense.

It was to bring forth such true love that the Lord scattered people.

God scattered people throughout the world so that we would come to know the difficulty of this task.

And through that difficulty, to learn and draw near to the true image of God.

And why does He go to such lengths?

This is something very important:

because being the one who **loves** is far more blessed than being the one who is **loved.**

Rather than saying something sharp that stabs the other's heart just to make ourselves understood,

to receive the other's words, gently reshape ourselves according to them, and find joy in that act---

this is true love. And when we can do that, we become conformed to the image of God and possess true peace.

The one we must contend with in the name of love is not the one who does not accept us,

but **ourselves**, when we cannot accept the other---

ourselves, who hold out a weapon to force the other to accept us.

To be "loved" is inevitably tied to **expectation**, or in other words, **desire**.

But desire always has a hole---it can never be completely satisfied.

However, **to love** is something we can do freely, with all our heart.

There are no restrictions on loving. That means it fills the heart.

Yet of course, it is not easy.

If left alone, people tend to become self-centered and full of self-love.

And eventually, they move toward trying to justify their own actions.

When someone with power does this, it becomes dictatorship, violence, and the attempt to unify others by force.

From this, we can understand the danger in the direction of wanting others to accept us.

Therefore, we must be deeply aware that such movements of the heart dwell within us.

For this very reason, God separated us. The event at the Tower of Babel was not God's failure.

It was a trial God gave us in order for us to know the right direction.

And for humanity---who fails to learn from this---

a way was provided to bring an end to the inevitable clash of self-centered claims.

That way is Jesus Christ.

Jesus clearly embodied the model of love.

By coming to know the joy that lies at the end of Jesus's self-sacrificing love,

we can come to understand one another.

Even if we cannot do it easily, we can know the **right direction.**

And the one who points us in that direction is none other than the **Holy Spirit** who dwells within us.

Let us live according to the voice of the Spirit.

And finally, one more thing: "Each has meaning as they are."

If mutual understanding is the fulfillment of love,

then the more distinct each person is, the greater the fulfillment of love can become.

Jesus Christ, our Lord, does not wish for everyone to be the same

or to blame one another for being different.

It's okay to be different. It's okay not to be as skilled as someone else. It's okay not to have something others have.

There is no need to compare yourself to anyone. You were created and loved by God **as you are.**

You must receive that truth firmly.

And as that person whom God created, you are to love others---

in other words, to love people who are different from you.

That very heart to love others is the gift from God.

Acts 20:35

In all things I have shown you that by working hard in this way we must help the weak and remember the words of the Lord Jesus, how he himself said, "It is more blessed to give than to receive."

If, upon reading these words, you thought, "I am in a difficult situation, I am weak, so I must receive love," then you have misunderstood this vital part: "It is more blessed to give than to receive."

True weakness is not about the situation you are placed in.

It is revealed in whether you have entrusted everything to God, or whether you are still struggling in self-centeredness.

If that is the case, then we can become strong at any time.

And with that heart, it is possible to accept the weak and give love.

If we truly love someone from the heart, then every issue in our relationship with that person will be resolved.

That is because we are able to accept and rejoice in them.

If we think we love, and yet things remain unresolved, then perhaps we must examine the self-centeredness that lies within us.

The Lord surely sees the courage it takes to face that.

And if this joy of giving love spreads, how rich we will become.

That richness is not a richness with holes in it---it is the true richness that fills the very center of the heart.

To reach that richness is to draw near to God; it is, in the truest sense, what it means to have faith in God.

Let us not build a great tower, but rather, let us draw near to God with hearts that love others.
