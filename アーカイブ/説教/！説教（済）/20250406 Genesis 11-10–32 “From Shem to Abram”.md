**20250406 Genesis 11:10--32 "From Shem to Abram"**

Let us continue reading Genesis today. Once again, we come to a genealogy.

This passage records in detail the genealogy of Shem's descendants as the beginning of a new history after the flood.

Shem's genealogy is significant because it eventually leads to David, and then to Jesus.

The names and lifespans of the individuals mentioned here are not merely records;

they are understood to symbolize various circumstances and to reflect God's divine plan.

This does not mean that the account was rewritten later with meaning added,

but rather that these people truly lived as described,

and when looking back from a broader perspective, God's plan becomes visible within it.

Gen. 11:10

These are the generations of Shem.

When Shem was 100 years old, he fathered Arpachshad two years after the flood.

The name **"Shem (שֵׁם, Shem)"** includes the meanings of "name" and "origin," and as the chosen of God,

he represents the foundation of the salvation history leading from Abraham to Jesus.

The name "Arpachshad (אַרְפַּךְשָׁד, Arphaxad)" is uncertain in meaning,

but is often symbolically interpreted as "blessing spreads" or something similar.

The Hebrew word **"מֵאָה (me'ah)"**, meaning "100," often symbolizes maturity or completeness in ancient Israel.

That is, after the devastating event of the flood,

a new era begins to be built upon a sure foundation, demonstrating God's work.

Furthermore, the number "two years after the flood" indicates a very early moment,

and it is also considered to mark God's timing for restoration.

Hosea 6:1

Come, let us return to the Lord;

for he has torn us, that he may heal us;

he has struck us down, and he will bind us up.

Hosea 6:2

After two days he will revive us;

on the third day he will raise us up,

that we may live before him.

In other words, after great tribulation, a reconstruction initiated by God begins through the genealogy of Shem.

Gen. 11:11

And Shem lived after he fathered Arpachshad 500 years and had other sons and daughters.

The number **500** signifies, in ancient thought, "a great length of time,"

indicating the abundance of God's blessing and the secure transmission of His salvation plan to the next generation.

In other words, this 500 is not merely a reference to a lifespan,

but contains the message that through the genealogy of Seth,

God's continuous grace and the line of faith is being preserved without interruption.

Incidentally, based on the calculation that Abraham died 467 years after the flood,

and Shem died 502 years after the flood,

we can conclude that at the time of Shem's death, Abraham had already passed away (35 years earlier).

Gen. 11:12

When Arpachshad had lived 35 years, he fathered Shelah.

The name **"Shelah (שֶׁלֶף, Shelah)"** is said to carry the meaning "to go forward" or "to transmit,"

suggesting the role of passing faith on to the next generation.

No special significance is found in the number **35**,

but considering that this was a relatively young age to bear children,

it gives the impression of a rhythm of life being carried forward swiftly and steadily into the next generation.

From Arpachshad ("blessing spreads") to Shelah ("go forward" or "transmit"),

the image is of God's plan advancing smoothly and purposefully.

Gen. 11:13

And Arpachshad lived after he fathered Shelah 403 years and had other sons and daughters.

The number **403** appears again in later generations,

indicating that God's plan was precisely recorded and that continuity and completeness were intentionally emphasized in each generation.

This is structured as the great number **400** plus the final digit **3**.

In ancient times, 400 often represented an entire generation or a completed era,

and 3 often suggests God's perfect work (e.g., the symbol of the Trinity).

This combination emphasizes that individual lives were not mere happenstance

but time periods meticulously planned by God's providence.

And because the same number appears again later in Shelah's account,

it reinforces the certainty of God's plan.

Gen. 11:14

When Shelah had lived 30 years, he fathered Eber.

In the progression of God's plan, the decisive figure **Eber** is born.

The name **"Eber (עֵבֶר, Eber)"** is later associated with the word **"Hebrew (עִבְרִי, Ivri)"**.

This word is thought to contain meanings such as "the one who crosses over" or "boundary,"

and it suggests that the Hebrew people came across boundaries of peoples and lands.

They are the chosen people who crossed the Red Sea and the Jordan River---

a people who embody God's plan of salvation.

The number **30** is seen throughout the Bible as a turning point in God's plan,

and is regarded as a marker of spiritual maturity.

For example, in the New Testament, it was the age at which Jesus Christ began His public ministry.

Thus, **30 years** symbolizes a significant transition point in the journey of faith,

where an individual's calling becomes clear, and the grace is passed on to the next generation.

In other words, a spiritual turning point has come within the genealogy of Seth.

Gen. 11:15

And Shelah lived after he fathered Eber 403 years and had other sons and daughters.

With the number **403** appearing again, continuity with the previous verse is emphasized,

showing that God's plan is progressing steadily within the flow of actual time.

Gen. 11:16

When Eber had lived 34 years, he fathered Peleg.

The name **Peleg (פֶּלֶג, Peleg)** is derived from a root meaning "to divide" or "to split."

Indeed, in **Genesis 10:25**, Peleg is mentioned as the son of Eber, and it says,

"for in his days the earth was divided," clearly tying his name to the Tower of Babel incident---

when languages were confused and the peoples were scattered.

Gen. 10:25

To Eber were born two sons: the name of the one was Peleg, for in his days the earth was divided, and his brother's name was Joktan.

Though Eber's era was one of spiritual strength, it's possible that such spiritual zeal to draw near to God

may have also led some to arrogance, causing the division.

The number **34**, just a little more than the milestone of 30,

can be interpreted as a "preparation period" before reaching the next stage of completeness.

That is, it may indicate a stage just before the transmission to the next generation,

revealing the human imperfection that must be covered by God's grace.

This may be the result that leads to division---in other words,

in order to rise to greater heights, they needed to go through the trial of Babel.

Gen. 11:17

And Eber lived after he fathered Peleg 430 years and had other sons and daughters.

Eber is recorded to have lived **430 years** after fathering Peleg.

This number combines the great number **400** with the milestone **30**,

and is interpreted as symbolizing a "complete period" or "a time full of blessing" in the history of God's salvation.

Also, in other Old Testament references---such as the length of Israel's stay in Egypt---**430 years** appears,

placing this number as a key cycle in God's promises and redemptive history.

Exodus 12:40

The time that the people of Israel lived in Egypt was 430 years.

Exodus 12:41

At the end of 430 years, on that very day, all the hosts of the Lord went out from the land of Egypt.

Gen. 11:18

When Peleg had lived 30 years, he fathered Reu.

The name **Reu (לֵו, Reu)** has uncertain etymology,

but is traditionally interpreted as meaning "friendship" or "faithfulness."

It relates to the covenant relationship with God found throughout Scripture,

and to the realization of God's promise of salvation through mutual love (friendship).

Peleg, at age **30**, gave birth to Reu, who bears such a good name.

Though Peleg is associated with division, in producing a good descendant, this was a praiseworthy thing.

Gen. 11:19

And Peleg lived after he fathered Reu 209 years and had other sons and daughters.

The number **209** combines the rounded base of 200 with the addition of **9**.

In Mesopotamian and Egyptian literature, **9** is sometimes associated with peak ripeness or the climax of harvest.

It is linked to the conclusion of a cycle and the abundance of maturity.

However, it can also be interpreted the other way: as nearly reaching a full cycle,

but just shy of perfection---perhaps signaling an impending new turn.

Gen. 11:20

When Reu had lived 32 years, he fathered Serug.

The name **Serug (שְׂרוּג, Serug)** is understood to carry the meaning "noble" or "exalted,"

and has a very pleasant tone.

The number **32**, when compared with the earlier appearance of **30**, is just a small addition---

but this "+2" can be taken symbolically as a slight adjustment during generational transitions,

or as a sign that God's plan works individually and uniquely in each person.

In other words, something a little extra was present---just before complete transformation.

Gen. 11:21

And Reu lived after he fathered Serug 207 years and had other sons and daughters.

The number **207** adds **7** to the foundational 200,

and **7** in Scripture symbolizes completion, divine perfection, and sanctification.

This number emphasizes the completeness of God's providence.

Previously we noted that the "+2" before full transformation could feel excessive,

but now, after Serug was born, all was perfect.

Gen. 11:22

When Serug had lived 30 years, he fathered Nahor.

**"Nahor (נָחוֹר, Nahor)"** is considered in ancient language to signify "nobility" or "exaltedness."

Considering that from him the people of Israel, God's chosen nation, would arise, his name is fitting and appropriate.

As previously mentioned, the number **30** functions as a pivotal point of transition,

marking an important turning point in an individual's life.

Here, it symbolizes the maturation of one phase and the beginning of the next.

Gen. 11:23

And Serug lived after he fathered Nahor 200 years and had other sons and daughters.

The clean, round number **200** is considered to symbolize a completed era or a time of generational stability,

giving the impression that God's plan of salvation is being fulfilled within His divine order.

Now, at last, the time of Abram is drawing near.

Gen. 11:24

When Nahor had lived 29 years, he fathered Terah.

The name **Terah** is thought to suggest "traveler" or "nomad,"

which matches the fact that his family led a nomadic lifestyle.

It is also sometimes interpreted to mean "delay" or "waiting,"

reflecting the reality that God's promises are not always fulfilled immediately.

The number **29** is very close to 30, and is often interpreted as representing a state just before full maturity.

In other words, it's a little short---implying incompleteness.

Indeed, the fact that Terah was not Abram (Abraham) underscores this point.

Gen. 11:25

And Nahor lived after he fathered Terah 119 years and had other sons and daughters.

The number **119** appears irregular compared to other figures.

However, within Scripture, 119 is known as a significant number, particularly in the Psalms---

as the longest chapter in the Bible, Psalm 119 symbolizes the perfection of God's law and the depth of faith.

In that way, this number may also be interpreted as representing a delicate balance at a turning point in history---

the space between human limitation and God's plan.

Gen. 11:26

When Terah had lived 70 years, he fathered Abram, Nahor, and Haran.

And so, at last, Abram appears.

It goes without saying that he is the most significant figure in this genealogy.

The number **70** is frequently used throughout the Old Testament

to symbolize a "complete period" or a "whole nation/people."

Exodus 24:9

Then Moses and Aaron, Nadab, and Abihu, and seventy of the elders of Israel went up.

The age of Terah and the birth of his sons is regarded as a historical turning point

at which God's promise is passed down not only to the next generation but beyond the family line---

sometimes referred to as the "beginning of a new covenant."

Now for a quiz: At what point in this genealogy did Noah die?

Here's a hint:

**Gen. 9:28** Noah lived 350 years after the flood.

**Gen. 9:29** All the days of Noah were 950 years, and he died.

**Gen. 11:10** These are the generations of Shem.

When Shem was 100 years old, he fathered Arpachshad two years after the flood.

The answer: "At this point, Noah was still alive."

Gen. 11:27

Now these are the generations of Terah.

Terah fathered Abram, Nahor, and Haran; and Haran fathered Lot.

From this verse, the focus of the genealogy shifts to Terah.

As mentioned earlier, the appearance of Abram (later Abraham) makes this section especially important.

The resolution of the narrative becomes clearer than that of the genealogy of Seth.

1\. Nahor (נָחוֹר, Nahor)

Some scholars translate "Nahor" as "snorer,"

but more symbolically it is often emphasized as representing "noble" or "dignified."

2\. Abram (אַבְרָם, Abram)

The name "Abram" means "exalted father,"

and it foreshadows his destiny to later enter into a covenant with God and become "Abraham,"

the "father of many nations."

3\. Haran (חָרָן, Haran)

Though the exact etymology is unclear, Haran's name is sometimes interpreted as "to shout" or "strong."

He died before his father Terah, and his early death in the family is often seen as symbolic.

4\. Lot (לוֹט, Lot)

The name "Lot" is understood to mean "covering" or "concealment,"

and is often interpreted as a symbol of God's protection or providential concealment.

Lot later plays a significant role in relation to Abraham and his relationship with God,

occupying an important position within the entire genealogy.

Gen. 11:28

Haran died in the presence of his father Terah in the land of his kindred, in Ur of the Chaldeans.

Here, suddenly, we are told that Haran died before his father. The place of his death is called **Ur**.

**"Ur (עֻר)"** was one of the principal cities of ancient Mesopotamia, located specifically in the region controlled by the Chaldeans.

According to archaeological studies, Ur was home to a grand **Ziggurat (temple tower)** and prospered as a cultural and economic center.

In other words, this city symbolized ancient wisdom and civilization.

Haran's death signified, for the family, the end of an old lifestyle and culture.

It serves as a foreshadowing of what will come in Genesis chapter 12, when God calls Abram (later Abraham) to "Go from your country and your kindred and your father's house."

Thus, **Ur** becomes a symbol of past glory and sinfulness, and its ending signals the beginning of a new covenant.

Gen. 11:29

And Abram and Nahor took wives. The name of Abram's wife was Sarai, and the name of Nahor's wife, Milcah, the daughter of Haran the father of Milcah and Iscah.

Sarai (שָׂרַי, Sarai)

Etymology/Meaning: Sarai means "my princess," "my noblewoman," or "my lady."

Later, God changes her name to **Sarah (שָׂרָה)**.

By removing the possessive suffix "-ַי (ai)" from "Sarai," the emphasis shifts from private nobility to public motherhood, highlighting her role as the mother of nations.

Milcah (מִלְכָּה, Milcah)

Milcah means "queen" or "ruler," symbolizing authority and dignity.

Grandmother of Rebekah

When Abraham became old and sought a wife for his son Isaac, he sent his servant to his homeland.

Rebekah's willingness to act generously when asked, "Please let me drink," (Genesis 24:15--20) reveals her kindness and initiative.

Later, the servant learns that she is the daughter of Bethuel, a relative of Abraham, affirming God's guidance in the choice.

Iscah (יִסְכָּה, Iscah)

The exact meaning of Iscah is debated, but it is often interpreted as "to see" or "to observe,"

implying insight or the ability to receive divine revelation.

She is mentioned only here.

Gen. 11:30

Now Sarai was barren; she had no child.

In that society, producing offspring was the foundation of family continuity and inheritance.

Barren women often suffered great sorrow and shame.

This verse highlights the deep trial that Sarai and Abram faced.

However, within this trial, the great power of God was also at work.

This detail is placed here to teach that all things are part of God's provision.

Gen. 11:31

Terah took Abram his son and Lot the son of Haran, his grandson, and Sarai his daughter-in-law, his son Abram's wife,

and they went forth together from Ur of the Chaldeans to go into the land of Canaan,

but when they came to Haran, they settled there.

Terah, along with Abram, Lot, and Sarai, sets out from Ur toward Canaan,

but they stop and settle in Haran.

Regarding **Canaan**, it lay along trade routes connecting Egypt, Mesopotamia, and Anatolia,

making it not only an economically rich land but also a land "flowing with milk and honey,"

suitable for agriculture and livestock.

However, it was also marked by droughts and conflict with pagan cultures,

giving it a dual character: a land of **blessing and trial**.

Furthermore, because of its richness, it became a contested region full of idolatry and cultural pluralism.

The patriarchs of Israel, including Abraham, lived in this land,

and through battles and interactions with foreign peoples,

they deepened their covenantal relationship with God.

In this way, "journeying" itself becomes a test of faith and obedience within God's plan.

**"Haran (חָרָן, Haran)"** was a trade city in the northwestern region of Mesopotamia.

It is noted as the place where Nahor's family settled,

but here it functions as an **intermediate stop**, falling short of reaching Canaan.

That is, while it seems similar to Canaan, it was closer and a more **convenient compromise** for the journey.

Thus, stopping here implies a **season of waiting**.

And most importantly, since Abram himself would later set out for Canaan by his own decision,

this pause in Haran must also be seen as **a form of divine guidance**.

Gen. 11:32

The days of Terah were 205 years, and Terah died in Haran.

It is recorded that Terah lived **205 years**.

The combination of the large base number 200 plus 5, or the sum 2+0+5 = 7,

can be interpreted symbolically as representing **completion** or **a new beginning**.

Thus, with this, the genealogy comes to a close,

and the relationship between God and humanity will now be depicted in full through the life of Abraham.

Here we are reminded of the grandeur and continuity of God's plan.

The salvation that was built up through the lives of so many people finds its culmination in the cross of Jesus Christ.

It is indeed necessary for us to reflect on the truth that this is the kind of plan that God establishes.

This means, for example, considering the idea that what is happening in our lives now

may be something that will lead into something greater in the future.

Even if things are difficult now, it might be through those difficulties that our children are saved.

If that is the case, then the question becomes: How should I respond in this current situation?

Even though problems are arising, will I still try to remain in places like Ur or Haran, where things are comfortable?

Or will I choose to break free and once again set my sights on Canaan?

This is not an easy thing in any sense.

There are times when, even if we know we will perish if we stay,

people remain trapped in their pasts, imprisoned by their own thoughts, unable to move.

But God intervenes in our daily lives to shake the fixed ideas hardened by our sin.

He says: "Where are you?"

We must constantly examine ourselves through Jesus Christ.

If we do not, we will be crushed by the age, by time, by the world, and by our environment, and our eyes will be closed.

So first, let us think upon the vastness of God's plan.

Everything has meaning. If that is so, then let us entrust the present to God's hand,

live by faith in His will, and believe that we can walk forward.

Even if our own thoughts fail to show us the way ahead,

when we live according to what is written in the Bible---

that is, the more we let go of ourselves, the more we will come to understand God's will,

and we will gain the experience of seeing clearly the right path forward, as our vision is cleared.

If God's plan is so grand and is working in every life,

then even the time when we were not living as believers was also part of His plan.

If there is anyone who has not yet made that decision, please take the step to believe.

Then your life will begin to turn in the right direction.

And within that right cycle, may you discover your true worth, the meaning of life, and eternal life itself.
