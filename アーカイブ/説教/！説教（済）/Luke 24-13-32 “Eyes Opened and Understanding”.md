Today, let's continue reflecting on the resurrection. Let's start by revisiting Paul's words.

**1 Corinthians 15:3** - "For what I received I passed on to you as of first importance: that Christ died for our sins according to the Scriptures,

**1 Corinthians 15:4** - that He was buried, that He was raised on the third day according to the Scriptures,

**1 Corinthians 15:5** - and that He appeared to Cephas, and then to the Twelve."

Here, Paul clearly emphasizes the resurrection of Jesus as the most important truth he shared with the believers.

Whoever believes in the name of Jesus Christ will be saved. What this means is that Jesus, though innocent, took upon Himself the burden of our sins, which we were born into and could not escape. He bore those sins on the cross and died with them. Three days later, He rose again with a new, glorified body. And this promise of a resurrected body, one that has defeated death, is also given to us---an eternal life in victory over death.

This resurrection represents the core of our salvation in Jesus Christ. It's something we as believers should rejoice in and celebrate with all our hearts.

Today's Scripture passage describes one of the encounters the risen Jesus had with His disciples. It's a mysterious and powerful story.

Let's briefly recap the story: After Jesus' crucifixion, two disciples were walking back to Emmaus, feeling discouraged. Jesus approaches them and asks, "What are you talking about?" The disciples, not recognizing Him, tell Him about how Jesus had been crucified and buried, but now His body was missing from the tomb. Jesus then explains the Scriptures to them, showing how all of it pointed to His resurrection. Still, they didn't realize it was Him. It wasn't until later, when He broke bread with them, that their eyes were opened and they recognized Him. And just as they did, Jesus vanished from their sight.

The most striking thing here is: why didn't they recognize Him? The answer isn't explicitly given---it remains a mystery. But this mystery helps reveal the truth behind the words of Jesus: "Blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed."

Let's walk through the passage together.

**Luke 24:13** - "Now that same day two of them were going to a village called Emmaus, about seven miles from Jerusalem.

**Luke 24:14** - They were talking with each other about everything that had happened."

This day refers to the third day after the crucifixion---the day when Jesus rose from the dead. It's the same day when His body was found missing from the tomb.

These two disciples are not part of the Twelve. One of them is named Cleopas.

**John 19:25** mentions Cleopas' wife standing at the foot of the cross, which leads some to believe the two disciples might have been husband and wife, though we cannot know for sure. Some even speculate that the other disciple could have been Luke himself.

As for Emmaus, while the exact location isn't known, the distance is about 11 kilometers, or a three to four-hour walk from Jerusalem.

These disciples were heartbroken. They had followed Jesus, hoping He was the promised Messiah. But now, with His crucifixion, it seemed like everything had fallen apart. They feared they had followed a false Messiah, and as His disciples, they were now in danger.

**Luke 24:15** - "As they talked and discussed these things with each other, Jesus Himself came up and walked along with them.

**Luke 24:16** - But they were kept from recognizing Him."

Here's one of the mysteries in this story---their eyes were kept from recognizing Him. Even though they were discussing Jesus, they didn't realize that He was right there with them.

Why? The text says their eyes were "kept" from recognizing Him. Some theologians believe this could be due to their grief or the evening light, but others suggest Jesus appeared in a slightly different form after His resurrection.

**Mark 16:10-14** also recounts how Jesus appeared in a different form and wasn't recognized at first. Jesus later even appeared suddenly in locked rooms.

The key point here isn't how Jesus appeared, but that their eyes were "kept" from seeing the truth. Their understanding was clouded.

What does this mean for us? It highlights how, often, our own spiritual eyes can be clouded. We might know the facts about Jesus, but our hearts remain closed to truly understanding His presence and power.

Now, let's think about Jesus' resurrected body. What does it mean to be resurrected? Resurrection is not the same as resuscitation. In Scripture, there are other examples of people being brought back to life---Elisha raised the dead, and in the New Testament, Jesus raised Lazarus and others. But they eventually died again. Jesus' resurrection, however, was different---His body was transformed, made new, and He would never die again.

**1 Corinthians 15:42-44**: "So will it be with the resurrection of the dead. The body that is sown is perishable, it is raised imperishable; it is sown in dishonor, it is raised in glory; it is sown in weakness, it is raised in power; it is sown a natural body, it is raised a spiritual body. If there is a natural body, there is also a spiritual body."

This passage assures us, as Christians, of the resurrection of the body. It tells us that just as Christ was raised with a spiritual body, so too will we be raised with a body that is imperishable, glorious, and full of power.

But what exactly is a spiritual body? This is a difficult question, and it's likely something we won't fully understand until we experience it ourselves. Trying to comprehend it while still in our earthly, perishable bodies is difficult, and perhaps beyond our current understanding. (Personally, I look forward to that day with great anticipation.)

Jesus' body at this time, in this post-resurrection encounter, was in this glorified state. Therefore, the mysteries and strange aspects of this passage must remain mysteries for now.

**Luke 24:17** - "He asked them, 'What are you discussing together as you walk along?' They stood still, their faces downcast.

**Luke 24:18** - One of them, named Cleopas, asked Him, 'Are you the only one visiting Jerusalem who does not know the things that have happened there in these days?'"

From Cleopas' question, we understand how significant the crucifixion of Jesus was. It wasn't just a small event---everyone in Jerusalem knew about it.

It seems almost rude to ask Jesus, "Are you the only one who doesn't know?" when, in fact, Jesus knew more than anyone else. This highlights a common human error: sometimes, in our limited understanding, we believe we know more than God.

**Luke 24:19-24** records the disciples' account of what had happened: "About Jesus of Nazareth," they said, recounting that He was a powerful prophet in word and deed, but had been handed over to be crucified. They had hoped He was the one to redeem Israel, but now it had been three days, and although some women had reported the tomb was empty, they didn't believe He had risen.

Their understanding was incomplete. They saw Jesus as a prophet, but failed to fully grasp that He was not only a prophet---He was the Messiah, the spiritual Savior who had come to bring redemption not just to Israel, but to all of humanity.

They were hoping for a political Messiah who would free Israel from Roman rule. They thought Jesus would be a mighty leader who would restore Israel's national independence. But because He had died, they assumed He wasn't the Messiah after all.

However, the truth is that Jesus' death on the cross was for the atonement of sins, not just for Israel, but for all people. By believing in Jesus, we become part of the spiritual Israel, the people of God.

Their hopes had been dashed because they misunderstood Jesus' mission. Verse 21 shows this with the past tense---"We had hoped He was the one to redeem Israel." In their minds, everything was over.

Moreover, even though Jesus had repeatedly told them He would rise on the third day, they couldn't grasp the reality when the body was missing. Instead of celebrating His resurrection, they were confused and doubtful, thinking it was perhaps a mistake or a trick.

Even though the women reported seeing angels and hearing that Jesus was alive, the disciples couldn't believe it. "It sounded like nonsense," they thought.

**Luke 24:25-27**: Jesus responds by gently rebuking them for their slowness to believe the prophets' words. He explains that the Messiah had to suffer and then enter His glory. Starting with Moses and the prophets, Jesus explains how the Scriptures pointed to Him. This must have been an incredible moment, as Jesus Himself walked them through the Old Testament, revealing how it all pointed to His life, death, and resurrection.

Jesus' teaching here is the ultimate theological lesson, as He ties the entire Bible together, showing how the promises and prophecies of the Old Testament were fulfilled in Him.

**Luke 24:28-32** records how, as they approached their destination, the disciples urged Jesus to stay with them. At the table, Jesus took bread, gave thanks, broke it, and began to give it to them. At that moment, their eyes were opened, and they recognized Him---but then He disappeared from their sight. They immediately realized that their hearts had been burning within them as He had spoken to them and explained the Scriptures.

Their "eyes were opened," a moment of revelation where they truly recognized Jesus for who He was. We can imagine their astonishment and joy---everything they had failed to see suddenly became clear.

This scene recalls another famous moment of eye-opening revelation: Paul's conversion on the road to Damascus. In Acts 9:3-19, we read how Paul (then Saul) was struck blind by a heavenly light and heard Jesus' voice. After three days, something like scales fell from his eyes, and he could see again, both physically and spiritually.

In Luke 24:16, we read that the disciples' eyes were "kept from recognizing Him," and in verse 31, their eyes were opened. This mirrors Paul's experience of spiritual blindness and then having his eyes opened to recognize Jesus as Lord.

Jesus' resurrection is a powerful truth, and often, like the disciples, our eyes can be slow to recognize Him. But when our eyes are opened, we can see the glory of Christ and understand the fullness of His plan.

They had their eyes opened, yet they couldn't see. In reality, their vision was still obstructed. But when the scales fell away, they were able to truly see. This imagery of being blinded and then having that obstruction removed is a common thread in the biblical narrative.

Seeing, in this context, means understanding what is true. It signifies grasping the truth that was previously hidden.

Now, there is another striking element in today's passage: the moment their eyes are opened, they recognize Jesus, and at that very moment, He vanishes from their sight.

This leads to a significant theme in Scripture: **believing without seeing**.

Let's turn to a scene in the Gospel of John that speaks to this idea of faith in the resurrection.

**John 20:26-29** tells us about Thomas, the disciple who doubted Jesus' resurrection until he saw and touched the wounds in Jesus' hands and side. Jesus says to Thomas: "Because you have seen Me, you have believed; blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed."

In today's story from Luke, as soon as the disciples realize it's Jesus, He disappears. At the very moment they fully understand who He is, He vanishes. In a sense, they are being invited to move from **seeing to believing without sight**.

It's interesting to ponder---why didn't Jesus stay longer with them? He could have remained with them on earth forever after His resurrection. But instead, He stayed only for 40 days before returning to heaven.

If Jesus had stayed physically present, we humans---weak as we are---might have only seen Him as a man performing miracles, a sort of divine superhero. This could have distracted the disciples and kept them focused on their earthly expectations, such as the political freedom of Israel, rather than on the deeper spiritual truth.

The same can be true for us. Many of us have had experiences where God intervenes in miraculous ways during difficult times, and we are filled with awe and gratitude. Those moments strengthen our faith, reminding us that God is working.

But what about the times when nothing spectacular happens? Can we still believe then? What if circumstances go from bad to worse---will our faith endure?

God has a plan for each of us, even when we can't see it. That's why **believing without seeing** becomes crucial. When life is tough, when everything seems to be going wrong, it's easy to think, "Maybe God isn't really there. Maybe I was mistaken." But the absence of visible signs doesn't mean God is absent. In fact, faith is often required precisely when we can't see what God is doing.

We need to trust that God is guiding us, even when His plans are hidden. In those moments, faith is essential.

Even though Jesus is not physically here on earth now, what does that mean for us?

The resurrection of Christ is perhaps the most challenging part of Christianity for many to believe. People today can accept many things about Jesus, but for some, the resurrection remains a stumbling block---an event too miraculous to believe.

It's true that no one has been able to scientifically or logically prove the resurrection in the past 2000 years. Yet, millions of people across the world believe in this truth.

Why? Because of the witness and testimony of the early disciples who, even after Jesus vanished from their sight, continued to proclaim the resurrection. Their unwavering testimony has led countless others to faith in Jesus.

For those who do not believe, the resurrection is the hardest part to accept. But there is only one way to prove the reality of the resurrection---through the faith of each believer.

When we internalize the teachings of Jesus, when His words become flesh within us, we bear witness to the truth of the resurrection. Our faith is the testimony that proves Jesus is alive.

Therefore, it is through our faith, through the church, that we reveal the invisible Christ to the world. We are called to lead others to the kind of faith that does not rely on sight.

As Jesus said in **Luke 24:46-48**: "Thus it is written, that the Christ should suffer and on the third day rise from the dead, and that repentance for the forgiveness of sins should be proclaimed in His name to all nations, beginning from Jerusalem. You are witnesses of these things."

Let us pray that we may fulfill our mission, to be witnesses of the resurrection and to spread its meaning to the world.

※There is a God who surpasses human understanding, and there may be something stirring in your heart, even if you can't fully grasp it yet. I encourage you to open yourself to seeing it, to seeking it. My prayer is that your eyes will be opened, and that you will come to know the truth in your own journey.

God is not far from you---He is waiting to reveal Himself when your heart is ready to receive Him.
