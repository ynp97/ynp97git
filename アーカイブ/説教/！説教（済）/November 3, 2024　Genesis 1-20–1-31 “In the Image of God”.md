**November 3, 2024　Genesis 1:20--1:31 "In the Image of God"**

Last week, we began reading Genesis, the very first book of the Bible. As a book of beginnings, it makes clear that everything began with God's plan. Unlike other gods, who were believed to be born within the world, it's stated plainly here that our God created the world itself.

In the midst of chaos and lifeless material, God brought order, distinguished things from one another, and provided light. We learned that this act of creation is deeply connected to the Gospel of Jesus. Without the light of Jesus, our lives would simply be about pursuing desires, like soft machines following a set program. But being illuminated by His light enables us to walk in a truly meaningful life. This is the Good News.

Now, we are in a world entangled once again in chaos and darkness due to sin. This world is filled with people who live according to its rules and desires. Those who do not accept Jesus will remain ensnared by this world's cravings and face judgment while still dwelling in darkness.

Today, we'll continue reading from Genesis as we reach the momentous creation of humankind. Humans were created with a purpose: to govern this world. God prepared everything in advance, distinguishing and arranging everything so that humanity could fulfill this purpose effectively.

Reading Genesis, we see that the original world was indeed good for humanity---a world we would want to live in. Yet today, the world is undeniably filled with envy, hatred, sorrow, and anger.

Today, let us explore why everything goes well when we follow God. God made this world for humans, and if we live rightly, it becomes a wonderful place. To understand this, let's revisit our love for and faith in God.

**Scripture Reading**

**Genesis 1:20**

"And God said, 'Let the waters swarm with swarms of living creatures, and let birds fly above the earth across the expanse of the heavens.'"

At this point in creation, God's work shifts from stillness to movement. For the first four days, God created spaces---sky, sea, and land. Now, on the fifth day, He creates moving, living creatures. In ancient understanding, life was defined as anything that breathes. Fish, by this definition, narrowly qualify through their breathing process.

Interestingly, with plants, God commanded the earth to produce them. But here, He does not address water or sky directly. The reason for this difference is unclear but intriguing.

**Genesis 1:21**

"So God created the great sea creatures and every living creature that moves, with which the waters swarm, according to their kinds, and every winged bird according to its kind. And God saw that it was good."

*Sea creatures* here translates to *taninim*, often associated with the mythical *Leviathan*:

1\. A mythical dragon-like sea creature (Isaiah 27:1; 51:9)

2\. A serpent (Exodus 7:9, where Moses' staff turns into a serpent)

3\. A crocodile (Ezekiel 29:3)

In the Bible, *taninim* is often seen as something ominous. So why does it appear here?

**Possible Explanations:**

1\. Even the creatures of myth are part of God's creation.

2\. God sees even ominous creatures as "good," demonstrating His perspective transcends human views of good and evil.

On this fifth day, God's approach to creation is slightly different. Previously, He spoke, and it happened. But here, we see the Hebrew word *bara* (to create) used, showing a special act of creation. Furthermore:

**Genesis 1:22**

"And God blessed them, saying, 'Be fruitful and multiply and fill the waters in the seas, and let birds multiply on the earth.'"

God blesses these creatures with the ability to "be fruitful and multiply." The blessing includes reproductive power---permission to create new life. This could explain why God didn't address water or sky; instead, He gave the creatures the ability to fill these spaces on their own.

**Genesis 1:24--25**

"And God said, 'Let the earth bring forth living creatures according to their kinds---livestock and creeping things and beasts of the earth according to their kinds.' And it was so. And God made the beasts of the earth according to their kinds and the livestock according to their kinds, and everything that creeps on the ground according to its kind. And God saw that it was good."

Here, the earth itself is commanded to produce animals according to their kinds. Unlike the living creatures of the sea and sky, they are not blessed. Like the plants, they are classified into kinds: livestock, creeping things, and beasts of the earth.

• *Livestock* and *beasts of the earth* refer to mammals, while *creeping things* includes small animals and insects. At this time, humanity had not yet begun eating meat, so livestock were not considered food. Later, livestock would generally be deemed edible, while wild beasts were considered unclean.

• *Creeping things* or small animals, often insects, are declared unclean in Leviticus 11:29--31.

Interestingly, in this account, livestock are created before humans, which may seem odd. However, in Genesis 2:19, they are created after humans. This difference shows that Genesis 1 and 2 describe the same creation from different perspectives.

**Genesis 1:26--27**

"Then God said, 'Let us make man in our image, after our likeness. And let them have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over the livestock and over all the earth and over every creeping thing that creeps on the earth.' So God created man in his own image, in the image of God he created him; male and female he created them."

**"Let Us": A Difficult Passage**

The phrase "Let us" here is challenging and significant. It raises the question: Is this like the gods of Roman mythology, or the many gods of ancient beliefs? Why does the one Creator God speak in the plural?

This is often viewed as the first revelation of the Trinity---the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. Humanity was created in the image of the Triune God. The phrase "Let us" reflects the unity of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.

Of course, there are other interpretations, and I will briefly outline a few:

1\. **A Consultation with Angels:**

Some suggest that "us" signifies God consulting with the angels. In this view, God's words here involve the angels symbolically, though only God Himself is involved in the act of creation.

2\. **The Royal "We" or Majestic Plural:**

In Hebrew, the plural form can express majesty or authority. The use of "we" here could emphasize God's greatness and power.

3\. **Ancient Near Eastern Influence:**

Some scholars believe that the plural form may reflect the polytheistic culture of the ancient Near East, where gods often collaborated in creation myths. In this view, the plural language may have cultural origins but ultimately reveals the one true God's divine authority.

**The Image of God**

Another complex question is what it means to be created in God's image. Does it mean that God has two arms, two legs, and no tail?

The word *image* (*tselem*) originally referred to a physical representation or likeness. In ancient Egypt, Pharaohs were described as "the image of God on earth." Similarly, humanity is given the task of ruling, as God rules.

However, Scripture offers further insights:

**Ephesians 4:24**

"...and to put on the new self, created after the likeness of God in true righteousness and holiness."

**Colossians 3:10**

"...and have put on the new self, which is being renewed in knowledge after the image of its creator."

These verses suggest that the image of God refers not to physical appearance, but to spiritual and moral qualities. Humanity, marred by sin, has lost the image of God, yet through inner renewal, we are transformed back into His likeness.

● "Similar" does not mean "the same." The degree of similarity is unknown.

● "Man" refers to Adam, who, in chapter 2, is formed from clay. Incidentally, the Hebrew word for clay, *adamah*, shares a root meaning of "emptiness."

● The phrase "created" appears three times, emphasizing the uniqueness of humanity.

God created man in His own image, male and female He created them.

● Humanity is not divided into types, and thus racial discrimination is unnecessary.

● Here, male and female are created simultaneously, with "them" having dominion.

In this passage, God is distinctly differentiated. This distinction can be perceived as either God's grace or as a mystery of God. It is an area worthy of deep discussion.

**Genesis 1:28**

And God blessed them. And God said to them, "Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth and subdue it, and have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over every living thing that moves on the earth."

● This is not a denial of sexuality; rather, it is a strong affirmation.

● Humanity's superiority is highlighted, while in Buddhism, for instance, all living beings are considered essentially the same.

**Genesis 1:29**

And God said, "Behold, I have given you every plant yielding seed that is on the face of all the earth, and every tree with seed in its fruit. You shall have them for food."

**Genesis 1:30**

"And to every beast of the earth and to every bird of the heavens and to everything that creeps on the earth, everything that has the breath of life, I have given every green plant for food." And it was so.

Here, the provision of food is established:

● Every plant yielding seed (grains).

● Originally, all creatures were herbivores. It wasn't until after the Flood that meat consumption began.

● Vegetarianism represents an absolute image of peace. In the original order, no blood was shed.

**Isaiah 11:6-8**

The wolf shall dwell with the lamb, and the leopard shall lie down with the young goat, and the calf and the lion and the fattened calf together; and a little child shall lead them.

The cow and the bear shall graze; their young shall lie down together; and the lion shall eat straw like the ox.

The nursing child shall play over the hole of the cobra, and the weaned child shall put his hand on the adder's den.

Incidentally, meat-eating was permitted only after Noah's Ark, after the Flood.

**Genesis 9:1**

And God blessed Noah and his sons and said to them, "Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth.

**Genesis 9:2**

The fear of you and the dread of you shall be upon every beast of the earth and upon every bird of the heavens, upon everything that creeps on the ground and all the fish of the sea. Into your hand they are delivered.

**Genesis 9:3**

Every moving thing that lives shall be food for you. And as I gave you the green plants, I give you everything.

**Genesis 9:4**

But you shall not eat flesh with its life, that is, its blood.

**Genesis 1:31**

And God saw everything that He had made, and behold, it was very good. And there was evening and there was morning, the sixth day.

● "Behold!" is a strong call to attention.

● "Very" is added, indicating a unique quality of "goodness" here. As mentioned before, the term "good" in Genesis refers to whether something aligns with God's order.

Now, with the completion of humanity, the world was deemed very good, signifying the establishment of order. Harmony was brought about by the completion of humanity.

The order and harmony in the world are tied to the mission given to humanity: "Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth and subdue it, and have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over every living thing that moves on the earth." Thus, humanity's responsibility is to manage this world.

Humanity is blessed, yet also bears great responsibility: to bring peace to this world God has created. This is humanity's original mission.

This mission may seem challenging. However, as we have seen from a careful reading of Genesis, this world was created with humanity at the center. God meticulously formed the world from chaos, distinguishing between sky, sea, and land, creating plants as food, and establishing seasons. Everything was prepared for humanity, and then humanity was created.

This means that the management of creation is indeed within humanity's capacity. When humanity was completed, and God declared it "very good," it implied that a perfect environment had been established, allowing humanity to fulfill its mission completely.

Now then, are we humans truly able to properly manage this world?

I believe most would agree that we are not. Not only are we failing to do so, but we are also not even trying to stop. We keep finding reasons to distort the world, and I don't think I'm the only one who feels this way. This is something we must reflect on deeply.

Why is this so? Why has our society become like this? It is because the world, which was originally deemed good, has been corrupted by sin. Humanity, the appointed manager, is in a sinful state, which has brought a curse upon the world. If humans could live according to God's rules, free from sin, then harmony would naturally return to the world.

Modern society cannot be described as harmonious by any means. All around the world, people cling to their rights, their positions, and their opinions. In a way, it's as if people are consumed with maintaining their own status within society.

Because people refuse to let go of their own sense of self, they are unable to follow God's rules. Even if they appear to be following God, it often becomes conditional---"I'll follow in this way" or "only in these circumstances." From God's perspective, it might just seem like an attempt to use Him for one's own benefit.

I recently had a chance to look at a dollar bill. On American currency, there is a pyramid, an eye symbolizing God's eye, standing over material wealth. Yet, it doesn't appear that America is walking in a way that is pleasing in the eyes of God. The money that was supposedly entrusted to God seems to have taken control in its own way.

But even within such a society, we Christians must strive to live as those made in the image of God, as God originally intended. Not "I" or "this society," but "Jesus"---this is the fundamental principle of being a Christian.

Why? Because, though distorted by sin, this world was originally created by God for humanity. When we walk in trust with God, we can experience the most stable, blessed path, as this is how the world was designed.

However, "walking in trust with God" is not always easy, because God does not always lead us down paths we know. People, by nature, find comfort in familiar methods. When we've done something before, the sense of security is vastly different. So, it's understandable that we often want to stick to the paths of this world.

In that case, it's good to start by practicing walking God's path in a simple way. Begin with small steps of trusting God and taking that step forward. Sometimes, Jesus may even prepare opportunities for us to take those steps. Take courage, trust in the Lord, and take a step. Even amid worries and uncertainties, hold onto the assurance that, because Jesus is with us, everything will be okay. After a week, or perhaps a year, look back and see that things truly turned out alright. Repeating this process will gradually shape our hearts to trust and surrender to God.

Additionally, Christians should live with gratitude for what they have now. To live by entrusting everything to God with gratitude is very important. Even if we find it hard to do so easily, we must continue to believe that this is our goal. And each day, we should practice it, starting with small things---breathing in gratitude. This is also a way of affirming the truth that God created this world for us.

Today, as we read the Bible, we learned that the world was created to revolve around humanity. In other words, if we prepare ourselves to live by trusting and entrusting ourselves to God, we can feel the presence of the wonderful Kingdom of God within our lives.

And we should remember that each of our attitudes towards this goal helps move the world toward restoration. Our everyday lives as Christians reflect God's heart.

God has commanded us to rule over the world. To fulfill this command, each of us must first learn to trust in God. We need to reflect on what our intentions are as we engage with society and what we hold dear. So, what should we cherish?

In fact, Genesis 2 says the following:

**Genesis 2:15**

The LORD God took the man and put him in the garden of Eden to work it and keep it.

This is God's command to humanity in chapter 2: to work. The original word for "work" here also carries the meaning of "to serve." In other words, we are to serve, protect, and carefully manage this perfect world God has entrusted to us, maintaining the excellent state God created. This reveals that the essence of "having dominion" in chapter 1 is about "serving."

Earlier, I said that the focus is not on "I" or "this society," but on "Jesus," which is foundational for Christians. But that doesn't mean we should abandon society, become outcasts, or distance ourselves from the world.

The correct path is to serve within this sinful world, leading people to Jesus by our actions, and supporting this world. We must strive to make even one step toward the restoration of the perfect world that once existed.

**Luke 17:21**

"Nor will they say, 'Look, here it is!' or 'There!' for behold, the kingdom of God is in the midst of you."

Indeed, this world is overflowing with desires and greed. We live within it---not turning a blind eye or merely criticizing it---but serving, supporting, and encouraging those who remain lost in endless suffering. Because the Kingdom of God to be realized is in the midst of us.

Our goal is that people can come to accept God, Jesus Christ. So that they may step out of the dark shadow of self-centeredness and surrender their lives to the bright light of Jesus.

And as we strive to serve society as those created in the image of God, we hope to see God's Kingdom revealed in our midst.

**Prayer**: We too are suffering. But please grant us the strength to fulfill the original mission given to humanity.
