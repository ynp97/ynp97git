**Genesis 1:1-19: "In the Beginning, Created and Separated"**

This week and next, we will explore the first chapter of Genesis.

The Old Testament is a collection of myths, legends, faith teachings, regulations for worshiping God, rules for daily life, history, and religious literature of the Jewish people. It was shaped over approximately 1,200 years, through writing, additions, and edits, and it became a formal canon around 90 AD during the "Council of Jamnia" led by Jewish rabbis.

In ancient Israel, Genesis was referred to as the "Book of Beginnings." It covers themes like God, the world, humanity, sin, society, civilization, destruction, and salvation up to Chapter 11, which acts as a temporal and theological starting point. Starting from Chapter 12, we encounter the patriarchal narratives, beginning with Abraham. Moses is traditionally regarded as its author:

*Exodus 17:14*: "Then the Lord said to Moses, 'Write this as a memorial in a book and recite it in the ears of Joshua, that I will utterly blot out the memory of Amalek from under heaven.'"

*Exodus 24:4*: "And Moses wrote down all the words of the Lord."

*Deuteronomy 31:9*: "Then Moses wrote this law and gave it to the priests..."

*Numbers 33:2*: "Moses wrote down their starting places, stage by stage, by command of the Lord."

Genesis 1 describes God's acts within His divine domain, later recorded from Israel's perspective. Notably, Chapter 2 repeats the story of creation from a human-centered viewpoint.

**Genesis 1:1 - "In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth."**

This opening verse asserts a central truth: God created the universe. This statement is a key feature of Genesis, emphasizing God as the Creator.

Interestingly, Japan's *Kojiki* shares similarities with this creation story but has a key difference: in it, gods emerge from chaos. However, the Bible presents God as existing before creation and bringing the universe into being.

Genesis 1:2 - "The earth was without form and void, and darkness was over the face of the deep. And the Spirit of God was hovering over the face of the waters."

The phrase "without form and void" is a Hebrew expression, *tohu vavohu*, seen in Deuteronomy 32:10 and Isaiah 24:10, often describing emptiness or chaos. The "Spirit of God" (Hebrew: *ruach*) can also mean "breath" or "wind," as seen in Genesis 2:7 when God breathes life into Adam. This verse sets the scene for God's shaping hand to bring order from chaos.

Genesis 1:3 - "And God said, 'Let there be light,' and there was light."

God creates through His spoken word alone. In Chapter 1, God speaks, and creation obeys. In Chapter 2, however, He molds living creatures from the earth, showing His direct involvement.

There are various meanings for "good," including aesthetic appeal and moral righteousness. However, in this context, the word is *tov*, used to signify something that is rational, in line with structure, or orderly.

In this case, God creating light and calling it "good" means that He brought forth an orderly, structured entity from a state of *tohu vavohu*---darkness, chaos, and disorder. As we continue reading, we see that God engages in a process of separation and division in His creative acts.

This process of dividing is also seen in the end times at judgment: whether one lives according to God's way or in worldly chaos, each will be divided into light or darkness. With Jesus at the center, paths are divided to the right and left, marking which direction each will walk.

Since God has created the world with this order from the beginning, we too, as people in His world, should understand what is good and what is bad.

1.*Leviticus 20:25*: "You shall therefore separate the clean beast from the unclean, and the unclean bird from the clean; you shall not make yourselves detestable by animal or by bird or by anything with which the ground teems, which I have set apart for you to hold unclean."

2.*Philippians 1:9-10*: "And it is my prayer that your love may abound more and more, with knowledge and all discernment, so that you may approve what is excellent, and so be pure and blameless for the day of Christ."

We are called to seek wisdom and understanding to discern as God does. This is something to remember.

This concludes the first day. However, the term "day" here does not definitively mean a 24-hour period. While Christians generally interpret it literally, there is no certainty that the concept of "day" back then was the same as now. Understandably, ancient cultural and modern scientific perceptions differ.

Incidentally, the expression "evening and morning" is typical in Israel. While day begins with morning in Japan, in Israel, day begins in the evening.

**Genesis 1:6-8** records God saying, "Let there be an expanse in the midst of the waters, and let it separate the waters from the waters." God creates the firmament, separating the waters above from those below.

The word *raqia* refers to a firmament or dome. It comes from a root meaning to hammer out, like thin sheets of gold or silver. Hence, it suggests an image of something hammered above, dividing waters. This separated water likely refers to clouds and rain. Later, in Noah's time, we see a reversal, with all the upper waters falling to earth---a judgment closing off human living space, which God later promised would not happen again.

The Creator names the firmament "heaven," darkness "night," and light "day," showing dominion over what He creates. Naming something at that time implied authority, so by naming these elements, God brought them under His dominion.

**Genesis 1:9-10** continues the division, with waters gathered to one place so that dry land, called "Earth," appears. The gathered waters are named "Seas." God saw that this, too, was good.

Here, God's power over water is further emphasized---a theme seen later in Exodus and when Jesus calms the stormy lake. Water is something massive and uncontrollable yet subject to divine authority, reminding us of God's immense power.

**Genesis 1:11-13** tells how God commanded the earth to produce vegetation---plants with seeds and fruit-bearing trees. The earth obeyed, bringing forth plants according to their kind, with each containing its own seed. This provides a reproductive system so that life can continue through its own means.

Distinct separation is again evident. Trees are made for human consumption and grass for animals, each according to its kind, with purpose and variety clearly defined. These distinctions emphasize that God created everything with intention and structure, which some take as opposition to evolutionary theory.

The land, separated from the seas, now produces vegetation in orderly response to God's command. Notably, plants were not considered living things at the time; they were seen as more like inanimate objects.

This concludes the third day.

The following section involves the creation of heavenly bodies:

**Genesis 1:14-19** records God creating "lights in the expanse of the heavens" to divide day from night, and to serve as signs for seasons, days, and years.

Their roles are to distinguish day and night, serve as signs marking time, and illuminate the earth. One might think, "Wait, wasn't day and night established on the first day?"

The distinction on the first day was a fundamental separation---*light* as the visible, shining phenomenon and *darkness* as the unseen. Here on the fourth day, the distinction is temporal: the hours of daylight are when the sun shines, and night begins with the moon's appearance. Thus, the celestial bodies are commanded to govern time. This governance is to maintain temporal boundaries.

It's suggested that the lack of specific references to "sun" and "moon" here may be intentional, as astrology was widespread in the ancient Near East.

However, for those who revered sun gods, moon goddesses, or astrology, this passage declares that all these are merely creations, moving under God's commands.

This concludes the work of creation up to the fourth day. Next week, we will continue with the second half.

So, what can we learn from today's passage?

First, we recognize that God created everything. He was there from the very beginning, existing in a realm beyond human understanding, before the world came into being. This is a key point: accepting God as the Creator.

Rather than God emerging from the world, God made the world itself. This world we live in was crafted by the Creator. This world is not something that exists within God or flows from Him, nor is it a place where God gains enlightenment to become God within an energy field larger than Himself. Instead, God is the source of everything.

And this world God created was made with a clear purpose.

To understand this purpose, consider what God is doing during creation. What does God do? He is distinguishing, creating order. From the chaos and darkness, He creates light. He separates the waters vertically and horizontally, forming land and sea, and allows plants to grow according to their kinds. He establishes time's rhythms, creating seasons and the day-night cycle.

God first created this world according to His own order, with only His rules governing it.

Reading further, we find that this order was intended for humanity. God prepared the world to help humans thrive, yet humans harbored a serpent of greed in their hearts, leading them into sin.

Through sin, humanity veered from God's order, attempting to govern by its own rules. As a result, the order of the world became distorted, and a world filled with strife, jealousy, anger, and sorrow emerged.

It's as if we've returned to the initial chaos.

Jesus Christ, though compassionate, does not abandon His role as Judge. God sent Jesus into this world, bringing light into the chaos. Humanity, illuminated by this light, will now be judged---a division of light and darkness.

Believers in Jesus Christ are distinguished as those able to move forward into a new world of light, while those who do not believe are left behind in chaos, unable to escape hatred, sorrow, envy, and unquenchable hunger.

When we read Genesis, the book of beginnings, we sense a connection between creation and the judgment at the end of times. If so, judgment seems less about condemning evil and more about choosing and refining the good. The essence of judgment is to separate what is good.

So, what is good? It is what God calls "good." As I mentioned before, in this book of beginnings, "good" refers to alignment with God's order---this is the key point.

The way we determine if we are good is by considering if we are aligned with God's order. We should carefully examine our lives to ensure our steps align with God's order.

Are we prioritizing our own order over God's, causing us to stray from His path and making our lives more difficult? Are we unable to approach God because of it? This is something we need to evaluate continually.

After all, God created this world in a way that allows us to live in comfort when we follow His rules. When we trust, accept, and surrender to this, our lives are made new. This is the message found in the Bible.

Light was good. In other words, light aligns with God's order. Darkness, on the other hand, evokes an image of unrefined chaos. This transformation from the chaos of disorder to the order represented by light and darkness reflects God's creative process.

In this sense, we can see the creation story in Genesis as a model of the Gospel of Jesus Christ. Darkness represents a state of sin, while light symbolizes walking in alignment with God's order. Christians undergo a renewal through water, symbolizing baptism, and are thus set apart from those who remain in darkness. At the end of the world is judgment, whose essence is not so much about condemning evil as it is about distinguishing and choosing what is good.

Today's reading in Genesis reveals this paradigm.

Darkness calls to mind the image of sin. In the creation of the world, there was no need for light before life was born; darkness alone sufficed for the heavens and earth, an inorganic world. Inorganic matter, such as simple earth and water, requires no light. It is inorganic. It is lifeless.

When we read Genesis and understand sin as something similar to this lifeless state, it becomes easier to grasp. The human body, we might say, is like a soft machine. It has pumps for circulation, electrical currents in the brain, and mechanisms that enable sight and scent. When observed solely in terms of function, it may indeed appear almost inorganic.

Among these mechanical aspects of human nature are our instinctual desires. The drive to preserve our species, for example, may compel us to seek out stronger mates to ensure survival. The instinct for survival can drive us to assert strength, even at the expense of others.

We all experience these natural desires, yet these are the mechanical, inorganic aspects of human nature. These are essential; desires for survival and procreation are basic needs given to us by God. They are foundational, perhaps like the operating system and programming language of a computer.

However, if these desires become our only pursuit, we remain lifeless. We are like soft machines, little different from the earth itself. In such a state, we are in sin, in darkness, dwelling in chaos.

"Of course, not many people live solely by instinct, so this might be a rather extreme description. However, looking at the state of the world, it's undeniable that various instances stem from instinctual self-preservation---whether through criticizing others or business pursuits driven by desire. Sadly, we see instances where some would deceive even children to fulfill their greed. The present world is filled with darkness from sin, justified by human desires as if they were right.

Amid this pervasive darkness of sin throughout the world, there exists something known as 'light.' Light represents the opposite of inorganic, instinctual desires.

And what does this light produce? It brings forth 'life.'

In the darkness of chaos, God creates light, reflecting His order. He separates water above and below, shapes land and sea, and classifies plants by kind to grow. He also sets the times and seasons necessary for plants to flourish, establishing the rhythm of day and night.

In this way, the 'light' created to overcome the darkness of chaos forms a stage for new life to grow. Thus, light becomes the starting point for creating life, which is the story of Genesis. This directly mirrors the Gospel of Jesus Christ.

John 1:1-5 (ESV)

1 In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.

2 He was in the beginning with God.

3 All things were made through him, and without him was not any thing made that was made.

4 In him was life, and the life was the light of men.

5 The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it.

In this chaotic world, swirling with human instinct, inorganic desires, and darkness, Jesus, through His cross, "lit" the "life" as the "light" for us. Jesus offered His life for each of us and became the light of the world. Illuminated by that light, we can become aware of the chaos within us, recognize our sin, and repent, thus advancing to a new stage of life.

The light of Jesus shines into the darkness of our hearts, revealing even the hidden ugliness we either ignored or deliberately overlooked. Many people, even if confronted with these aspects, might still cover them up, justifying them with their own reasoning, making excuses like "I had no choice" or "It's someone else's fault," because they cannot remove them by their own power. This is the stance of one who does not repent.

However, those who believe in Jesus Christ understand that when they recognize sin, they know it is forgiven. They learn through God's Word how to address it. This means there is no need to hide sins; rather, the more we expose our sins to the light of Jesus, the more life is sorted, naturally aligning with God's order, leading to a richer way of life.

This is the new life we receive. This new life is not driven by inorganic desires. The "life" that Jesus speaks of consists of faith, hope, and love---something beyond mere mechanical desires. Such things are clearly separated and placed under God's control.

Naturally, those with new life still retain basic instincts, as they are necessary for living. However, these instincts are rightly utilized within the light of Jesus rather than being hidden in darkness.

Looking from another perspective, "life" requires light. Without light, it would revert to inorganic matter, mere dust. "Life" in darkness cannot truly find its path.

Therefore, we want to express gratitude for obtaining "life" by believing in Jesus as the light.

We want to remain in the "light" to avoid returning to insatiable cravings and suffering from unfulfillment. We desire to walk the path to eternal life, guided by the light of Jesus.

Genesis 1:3-4 "And God said, 'Let there be light,' and there was light. And God saw that the light was good, and He separated the light from the darkness."

John 1:4-5 "In Him was life, and the life was the light of men. The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it."

We are truly grateful for the blessing of living a life in the light.
