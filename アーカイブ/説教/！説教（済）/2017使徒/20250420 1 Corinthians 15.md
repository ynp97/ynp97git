20250420 1 Corinthians 15:50-58 "Victory through the Resurrection."

Today is Easter, the day we celebrate and commemorate the resurrection of Jesus Christ.

Jesus Christ died around noon on Friday after enduring suffering on the cross for six hours.

The following day, Saturday, was the Sabbath when activities were strictly limited,

so on that very day, His body was laid in the private tomb of Joseph of Arimathea, a member of the Jewish council.

Then, on the third day, Sunday morning, women who had anxiously awaited dawn hurried to the tomb.

However, to their astonishment, the tomb was empty.

Mark 16:1 When the Sabbath was past, Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of James, and Salome bought spices, so that they might go and anoint him.

Mark 16:2 And very early on the first day of the week, when the sun had risen, they went to the tomb.

Mark 16:3 And they were saying to one another, "Who will roll away the stone for us from the entrance of the tomb?"

Mark 16:4 And looking up, they saw that the stone had been rolled back---it was very large.

Mark 16:5 And entering the tomb, they saw a young man sitting on the right side, dressed in a white robe, and they were alarmed.

Mark 16:6 And he said to them, "Do not be alarmed. You seek Jesus of Nazareth, who was crucified. He has risen; he is not here. See the place where they laid him.

Mark 16:7 But go, tell his disciples and Peter that he is going before you to Galilee. There you will see him, just as he told you."

Mark 16:8 And they went out and fled from the tomb, for trembling and astonishment had seized them, and they said nothing to anyone, for they were afraid.

Jesus Christ died on the cross in our place for our sins.

And on the third day, He rose again, conquering death.

Actually, while there are Gospels that do not describe the birth of Jesus, there are no Gospels that omit His resurrection.

In other words, the resurrection is the central theme of the gospel.

When we speak of the resurrection, those who are not Christians might think, "Impossible! I cannot follow that."

However, according to the Bible, at least more than 500 people met the resurrected Jesus Christ.

Therefore, the resurrection of Jesus is an undeniable fact.

This resurrection of Jesus, a historical reality, became the foundation of the church and the basis of our faith.

Let's consider this in more detail.

Every human being will face "death" at the end of their earthly life.

However, through the resurrection of Jesus Christ (His rising from "death" into "new life"),

"death" is no longer the end of everything. Beyond what had been only darkness, we now have a definite hope: "new life."

The resurrection of Christ is the victory given to all humanity over "sin" and "death."

If we believe in Christ as our Savior, we too can live eternally in new life, just as Christ did.

John 11:25 Jesus said to her, "I am the resurrection and the life. Whoever believes in me, though he die, yet shall he live,

John 11:26 and everyone who lives and believes in me shall never die. Do you believe this?"

The resurrection and the separation from scientific reasoning:

The resurrection is indeed a difficult thing to comprehend. The idea of a dead person coming back to life is considered impossible according to modern science.

Furthermore, regarding the resurrection of Jesus, it is true that apart from the Bible, there are no ways to prove it. Some may say, "Where is the evidence?" or claim it to be fabricated.

However, to speak plainly, seeking historical proof or scientific evidence for the resurrection of Jesus Christ is meaningless. All these events occurred in God's domain. It is natural that no such proof exists. Rather, the fact that, despite having only the Bible as evidence, Christians continue to be born even today, serves as proof of the resurrection, and can be called the greatest miracle of Jesus.

Let's first briefly look at chapter 15.

Paul is speaking to the church in Corinth, a church that had many problems.

Actually, this entire chapter 15 is a theological discussion about the resurrection.

It describes how the resurrection is related to us, among other things.

1 Corinthians 15:3 For I delivered to you as of first importance what I also received: that Christ died for our sins in accordance with the Scriptures,

1 Corinthians 15:4 that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day in accordance with the Scriptures,

1 Corinthians 15:5 and that he appeared to Cephas, then to the twelve.

At the beginning of chapter 15, Paul declares that the resurrection itself is the gospel. The gospel means "good news."

He emphasizes that it is the most important thing. In other words, the resurrection of Jesus is the best news.

1 Cor. 15:12 Now if Christ is proclaimed as raised from the dead, how can some of you say that there is no resurrection of the dead?

Despite being the most wonderful news, the problem arose in the Corinthian church that some people claimed there was no resurrection of the dead.

It is written in Romans as follows:

Rom. 8:11 If the Spirit of him who raised Jesus from the dead dwells in you, he who raised Christ Jesus from the dead will also give life to your mortal bodies through his Spirit who dwells in you.

The key point of this verse is not about Christ's resurrection itself, but rather the issue of whether believers, meaning we ourselves, will be resurrected or not. Paul emphatically declares this as certain, clearly stating that Christ himself rose to raise the dead. In other words, our resurrection is explicitly declared in Scripture.

1 Cor. 15:16 For if the dead are not raised, not even Christ has been raised.

1 Cor. 15:17 And if Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile and you are still in your sins.

1 Cor. 15:18 Then those also who have fallen asleep in Christ have perished.

1 Cor. 15:19 If in Christ we have hope in this life only, we are of all people most to be pitied.

I think verse 19 is particularly important. It is filled with immense hope.

It declares that we may have hope beyond everything in this world. If that is the case, even if this life feels stifling and difficult, we can hold onto hope that everything will be completely renewed one day. Through faith, the word "despair" disappears entirely.

When we believe in this resurrection, we transcend all good and bad things in this world, and it is Christ's will that we walk with that hope in peaceful hearts, leading blessed lives.

1 Cor. 15:30 Why are we in danger every hour?

1 Cor. 15:31 I protest, brothers, by my pride in you, which I have in Christ Jesus our Lord, I die every day!

1 Cor. 15:32 What do I gain if, humanly speaking, I fought with beasts at Ephesus? If the dead are not raised, "Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die."

1 Cor. 15:33 Do not be deceived: "Bad company ruins good morals."

1 Cor. 15:34 Wake up from your drunken stupor, as is right, and do not go on sinning. For some have no knowledge of God. I say this to your shame.

Here Paul explains what happens to believers if they do not believe in the resurrection:

They will be unable to endure persecution.

They will say, "We will die tomorrow anyway! Let's do whatever we like."

Rather, he says that if you believe, "You yourself will also be resurrected," you will be able to live correctly. A correct way of life, meaning living filled with the hope of resurrection, will guide you along a peaceful and clear path. Through this clear vision, you will no longer be drawn into sin.

Next, Paul explains what kind of body we will have at the resurrection.

1 Cor. 15:42 So is it with the resurrection of the dead. What is sown is perishable; what is raised is imperishable.

1 Cor. 15:43 It is sown in dishonor; it is raised in glory. It is sown in weakness; it is raised in power.

1 Cor. 15:44 It is sown a natural body; it is raised a spiritual body. If there is a natural body, there is also a spiritual body.

We see clearly here that the resurrected body is not the same as our current bodies. The resurrected body of Jesus passed through doors and appeared simultaneously in various places. Perhaps our resurrected bodies will be like that. Also, when Christ appeared to Paul, He appeared as a light brighter than the sun.

However, it also says this:

1 Cor. 15:35 But someone will ask, "How are the dead raised? With what kind of body do they come?"

1 Cor. 15:36 You foolish person! What you sow does not come to life unless it dies.

1 Cor. 15:37 And what you sow is not the body that is to be, but a bare kernel, perhaps of wheat or of some other grain.

1 Cor. 15:38 But God gives it a body as he has chosen, and to each kind of seed its own body.

What this means is that our current bodies are the seeds to be sown, and God will give each one of us a resurrected body perfectly suited to who we are.

Indeed, each of us is already a perfect creation by God, and He respects the individuality we originally possess.

Moreover, our resurrected bodies will be extensions of that individuality. In other words, we will not become completely different beings. Rather, we will enter God's kingdom truly as ourselves.

1 Cor. 15:45 Thus it is written, "The first man Adam became a living being"; the last Adam became a life-giving spirit.

1 Cor. 15:46 But it is not the spiritual that is first but the natural, and then the spiritual.

1 Cor. 15:47 The first man was from the earth, a man of dust; the second man is from heaven.

1 Cor. 15:48 As was the man of dust, so also are those who are of the dust, and as is the man of heaven, so also are those who are of heaven.

1 Cor. 15:49 Just as we have borne the image of the man of dust, we shall also bear the image of the man of heaven.

When we experience the resurrection, we will receive a new body while maintaining our individual personalities and memories. Moreover, this body will also bear a heavenly form, which means it will also have characteristics like the image of God.

In reality, we don't know exactly what will happen at that time, but it will certainly be something truly wonderful.

We will receive this marvelous gift as the culmination of the gospel of resurrection.

Now, let's briefly summarize what we have covered so far.

The resurrection is very important.

There definitely is a resurrection of the dead! Jesus was raised precisely for that purpose!

If you don't believe that, you cannot firmly stand as a Christian!

As for the type of body, it will be connected to our current body but will also resemble God's own!

Now, let's move into today's passage.

1 Cor. 15:50 I tell you this, brothers: flesh and blood cannot inherit the kingdom of God, nor does the perishable inherit the imperishable.

1 Cor. 15:51 Behold! I tell you a mystery. We shall not all sleep, but we shall all be changed,

1 Cor. 15:52 in a moment, in the twinkling of an eye, at the last trumpet. For the trumpet will sound, and the dead will be raised imperishable, and we shall be changed.

Here, we encounter the mention of a "trumpet," which in the Old Testament is often described as a "horn." This is a common metaphor frequently found in Jewish apocalyptic literature. The trumpet is depicted as some sort of signal from God.

Rev. 11:15 Then the seventh angel blew his trumpet, and there were loud voices in heaven, saying, "The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of his Christ, and he shall reign forever and ever."

We don't know exactly what form this signal will take when it actually happens.

1 Cor. 15:53 For this perishable body must put on the imperishable, and this mortal body must put on immortality.

1 Cor. 15:54 When the perishable puts on the imperishable, and the mortal puts on immortality, then shall come to pass the saying that is written: "Death is swallowed up in victory."

Here we see the expression of putting on imperishability and immortality, and from this we understand that the resurrected body has some connection with our current bodies. We will be transformed, yet we will retain our current identity and put on immortality.

Now, a question for you: as Christians, we are said to have been reborn when we came to know Jesus Christ. The Bible indeed states this, but how about you?

Have you experienced being born again? Some may feel very clearly that they have experienced it, while others might think, "Hmm, I'm not sure." Indeed, those who grew up in Christian homes may find it difficult to sense a clear difference. Even those who came to faith later in life may initially have felt a clear change, but over time might find themselves preoccupied with worldly matters.

Theologically speaking, we are currently in a stage called sanctification, gradually becoming holy. But the final transformation that we are discussing today is absolute. The "change" mentioned here is what we might call our ultimate form, incomparable to the gradual changes we experience now.

And Jesus was resurrected ahead of us, precisely to lead us toward that final transformation.

This same Jesus, having received an imperishable body, is now living eternally in the kingdom of heaven.

1 Cor. 15:55 "O death, where is your victory?

O death, where is your sting?"

1 Cor. 15:56 The sting of death is sin, and the power of sin is the law.

1 Cor. 15:57 But thanks be to God, who gives us the victory through our Lord Jesus Christ.

This is a very difficult passage to understand.

First of all, what does it mean that "the sting of death is sin"?

It may be easier to understand this by thinking, "Actually, death itself is not something to be feared." If, right now, we clearly understand the truth of resurrection and once again face "death," how would we see it? If we believe in Jesus, we realize that even if we die, we can be resurrected while keeping our own identity. If that is the case, then "death" itself becomes much less frightening.

In other words, "death" is the wonderful moment when we lay aside this body tainted with sin and receive from God a new body.

Thus, if we feel the pain and fear of the sting regarding "death," it means we are not trusting God. It indicates we have doubts about God's words, that we have drifted away from God.

In Christianity, "sin" means being separated from God.

"The sting of death is sin" has this kind of meaning.

We will return to this later.

Regarding "the power of sin is the law," this is explained in detail in Romans chapter 5 (although it is difficult), and I encourage you to read it. To put it simply, the law itself was given by God, and it is good. But since all humans are inherently sinful, no one can perfectly keep the law. Therefore, the presence of the law causes sin to be revealed.

1 Cor. 15:55

"O death, where is your victory?

O death, where is your sting?"

Now, death's sting can no longer hurt us. This is a declaration that our sins have been redeemed by the death on the cross. Jesus redeemed our sins according to the law.

Jesus Christ died on the cross as a substitute for our sins, and on the third day He broke the power of death and rose again. Christ's resurrection is the victory given to all humankind, defeating both "sin" and "death."

Now, returning to "the sting of death is sin," some may say, "Even so, I'm still afraid of dying. Does this mean I don't truly believe in Jesus? Am I still living in sin? Is the sting of death still stuck in me?"

It is not easy to escape the fear of death. Moreover, it's not necessarily wrong to fear death or want to avoid it. After all, there must be things God has called us to do.

But ultimately, I believe Christians can entrust themselves to God at the very last moment.

If someone suddenly says, "You must not fear death! Ah! You just felt afraid now, didn't you?"---indeed, everyone would feel fear in such a moment.

Yet, even in our weakness, precisely because we are Christians, we can entrust ourselves to Jesus in the end. There might indeed be suffering, but we will be able to think that beyond it is not darkness, but light. At the very end, we have been given the power to hold onto such hope, from the very moment we first declared, "I believe in Jesus."

This means that we have been born anew. Believers in Jesus Christ are redeemed from sin, and this connects directly with no longer fearing the sting of death.

Currently, in everyday life, we might find it difficult to fully entrust ourselves to God. Facing various struggles, we pray, "God, help me!" But during such prayers, we might vaguely sense God saying, "Let it go!" Even so, we hesitate. We feel anxious and afraid. We might pray, "God, please do something---but not Your way, my way!" Perhaps this repeats itself frequently. We might say we believe in Jesus, but perhaps we are only thinking of our own convenience. We may be imperfect believers.

Yet, even if we remain in such a state, when finally facing death---the thing humans naturally fear most---we will surely entrust ourselves to Jesus. In that moment when there is truly nothing else we can do, we will trust and entrust ourselves to the Lord. We must first believe that we have been transformed into such people. That is the gospel, the good news.

1 Cor. 15:58

Therefore, my beloved brothers, be steadfast, immovable, always abounding in the work of the Lord, knowing that in the Lord your labor is not in vain.

The victory given through Jesus Christ is victory over death. For humans, death is the greatest enemy; we naturally fear death because we do not know what comes afterward. The resurrection of Jesus our Lord removes precisely that fear.

And just as Jesus was actually resurrected, we too will truly, tangibly overcome death with new physical bodies. Please, firmly recall this truth once again.

This "resurrection" is precisely the central message that the thick Bible is trying to convey to us.

Whoever believes in Jesus Christ overcomes death.

Whoever believes in Jesus Christ overcomes death.

Please, always keep this truth firmly in your mind. In everyday life, you may stumble in many areas. However, as long as you firmly grasp this core point---that our sins have already been redeemed and we have already conquered death---then other matters will gradually improve. Do not hurry; rather, slowly, carefully, improve step by step.

At the end of the Bible, a book describing the end of the world is included. It says:

Rev. 21:3 And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, "Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man. He will dwell with them, and they will be his people, and God himself will be with them as their God.

Rev. 21:4 He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away."

Our ultimate destination is precisely such a place: a world where there is no death, no mourning, no crying, no pain, where we live together with God. Jesus was resurrected in order to teach us this truth. There is no worldly evidence for this. Nevertheless, we have been granted the privilege of simply believing it. Today, on Easter, let us hold fast to this marvelous grace and wholeheartedly give thanks to the Lord.
