20250309 Genesis 9:1-29 "The Covenant of the Rainbow"

Genesis 9 describes the scene where, after the great flood marks the beginning of a new history, God bestows a new blessing and command upon Noah and his descendants and establishes a universal covenant (Hebrew: בְּרִית, berit) with all creation.

→ Noah and his family give thanks to God and offer worship.

The Meaning of the Flood as a Re-Creation

This chapter can be seen as a re-enactment of creation in Genesis 1, with the command "Be fruitful and multiply" (Genesis 1:28). Furthermore, in this new world, God's order is restored, and humanity is given a renewed dominion over all creation.

List of Covenants in the Bible

1\. The Covenant with Adam (The Original Covenant / The Order of the Covenant)

• Genesis 2:16--17

Here, God commanded Adam in the Garden of Eden that he could freely eat from any tree except the tree of the knowledge of good and evil. This was God's first command to Adam and is regarded as a "Covenant of Works" in a certain sense.

• Genesis 3

As a result of Adam and Eve disobeying God's command, God punished them, and the consequences affected all of humanity. This is sometimes interpreted as a covenantal element within God's relationship with humanity, involving both promises and declarations (what is often called a "curse").

2\. The Covenant with Noah

• After the flood, God made an unconditional promise to all creation: that He would never again destroy the world by a flood. The sign of this covenant is the rainbow.

3\. The Covenant with Abraham

• God's promise to Abraham included descendants, land, and blessings. Circumcision was given as a sign of the covenant, and Abraham was designated as the source of blessings for all nations.

4\. The Mosaic Covenant (Sinai Covenant)

• Through the giving of the Law, Israel was called to a conditional relationship where obedience would bring blessings, and disobedience would bring curses.

5\. The Covenant with David

• God promised the everlasting continuity of David's throne, including a Messianic hope. This covenant signified the eternal reign of the royal line and the fulfillment of God's kingdom.

6\. The New Covenant

• A covenant in which God writes His law upon human hearts and forgives sins, establishing an intimate relationship with believers. Fulfilled through the atoning work of Jesus Christ, it forms the foundation for eternal salvation and a new spiritual life.

Genesis 9:1

*"And God blessed Noah and his sons and said to them, 'Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth.'"*

Here begins the blessing upon Noah and his family, who survived the flood and worshiped God.

First, let us clarify the concept of blessing, as it differs significantly from how it is often understood in modern Japan.

In Japan, "blessing" is often associated with phrases like "Good things happened to you! Congratulations!" However, this biblical blessing is quite different.

• First, it is not spoken in past tense, as in "That was great."

• If we carefully read today's passage, we see that the content is actually a command, saying, "Do this."

The Hebrew root of the word blessing (בָּרַךְ, barak) originally carries the meaning of "to kneel" or "to worship." It signifies an attitude of humility and reverence before God.

From this background, the concept of God's blessing arises as an extension of worship and praise toward Him.

The Meaning of בְּרָכָה (berakhah) -- Blessing:

Blessing is not merely a wish for good things to happen, but rather an act of God in which He pours out grace, provides protection, grants prosperity and righteousness, and bestows mission and covenantal power. It signifies divine favor and mission given directly by God to individuals or nations, serving as a mark of God's covenant.

In other words, it is not simply a promise of good things happening; rather, it is an exhortation saying, "God has shown you the right path---now walk in it."

The joy lies in the fact that one is in a state where one can hear these words directly from God.

Thus, in this passage, standing upon the barren earth, Noah and his family are called to fulfill the mission of being fruitful, multiplying, and filling the earth, thereby actualizing God's blessing.

At the same time, this signifies the beginning of the world's re-creation through Noah and his descendants.

*"And God blessed them. And God said to them, 'Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth and subdue it.'"*

Genesis 1:28

*"...and have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over every living thing that moves on the earth."*

Genesis 1:29

*"And God said, 'Behold, I have given you every plant yielding seed that is on the face of all the earth, and every tree with seed in its fruit. You shall have them for food.'"*

Genesis 1:30

*"And to every beast of the earth and to every bird of the heavens and to everything that creeps on the earth, everything that has the breath of life, I have given every green plant for food.' And it was so."*

These words from the creation account are now redeclared as a blessing in a world renewed by the flood.

Genesis 9:2

*"The fear of you and the dread of you shall be upon every beast of the earth and upon every bird of the heavens, upon everything that creeps on the ground and all the fish of the sea. Into your hand they are delivered."*

Next, within the blessing, the righteous way before God is revealed:

→ To have dominion over all the beasts of the earth, the birds of the heavens, everything that moves on the ground, and the fish of the sea (1:28).

The phrase "fear and dread" does not merely imply terror but also carries an aspect of reverence and divine awe.

Indeed, in Psalms and the Prophets, "the fear of the Lord" is a key concept that expresses a correct relationship with God.

→ "Fear the Lord."

Thus, the animals instinctively recognize that humanity is given dominion by God, and they regard humans as rulers, experiencing fear and trembling before them.

Within this divine order of respect from the animal kingdom, humanity is commanded to face all living creatures properly and manage them with righteousness.

Genesis 9:3

*"Every moving thing that lives shall be food for you. And as I gave you the green plants, I give you everything."*

Here, a new food regulation is established. The original regulation was stated earlier in Genesis 1:29.

This means that plants were the entirety of food---not only for humans but also for animals.

This food regulation was revised after the flood, allowing all living, moving creatures to be food.

In other words, predation began to occur in the animal world.

Why was this change in food regulations (permission for meat consumption) made?

There are three perspectives:

1\. Practical Necessity

• As an immediate consideration, this was a provision for a world without crops or cultivated food.

• Additionally, as human population growth and lifestyle changes progressed, meat became a crucial nutritional resource.

• God acknowledged this necessity and permitted meat consumption.

2\. The Dual Aspect of Human Depravity and Divine Grace

• Ideally, in the original creation, a plant-based diet was established.

• However, after the fall, humans began raising animals, hunting, and domesticating livestock for food.

• The fact that Abel offered livestock as a sacrifice reflects this early reality.

• The Bible does not record any specific group strictly adhering to vegetarianism.

• In fact, given the cursed ground and the difficulty of agriculture after the fall, meat consumption likely became prevalent as an easier source of sustenance.

• Humanity turned away from God and stopped listening to His commands.

Thus, this change in food regulations can be understood as God acknowledging human depravity---not to endorse it but to lower a hurdle, making life more manageable while giving people another opportunity to walk the righteous path.

3\. Expansion of Authority and Awareness of Responsibility Toward Creation

• The permission to use animals for food also brought with it the responsibility to manage living creatures appropriately.

• This came with moral and spiritual awareness regarding the value of life.

This leads directly to the next passage:

Genesis 9:4

*"But you shall not eat flesh with its life, that is, its blood."*

The Hebrew word "דָּם (dam)" refers to more than just the physical fluid of blood; it symbolizes life itself (נֶפֶשׁ, nephesh).

This prohibition is closely related to later dietary laws found in Leviticus 7 and Deuteronomy 12, forming the ethical foundation that blood, as the source of life, must be treated with solemn respect.

→ In Leviticus, this later shifts to a more detailed regulation regarding what can and cannot be eaten.

God permits the use of animals for food, but He strictly forbids the consumption of blood, the symbol of life.

This rule balances the dominion granted to humanity with the responsibility to respect the sanctity of life.

By doing so, people recognize that life is a precious gift from God, while also being able to meet practical nutritional needs.

This declaration that "blood is life" leads directly to the next regulation.

Genesis 9:5

*"And for your lifeblood I will require a reckoning: from every beast I will require it and from man. From his fellow man I will require a reckoning for the life of man."*

The Required "Price" of Blood

• "I will require" means that any act of taking another's life will necessarily incur a just consequence.

• This is God's declaration of justice.

• The shedding of blood is always a grave offense, and God will always demand the price of blood (i.e., life itself) as justice.

This applies not only to humans but also to animals.

• Even if an animal kills a human, the principle of accountability remains.

• This establishes the universal standard that all life is valuable and must be protected.

Furthermore, this means that even among family and close relationships, the same strict justice will be applied.

Genesis 4:23

*"Lamech said to his wives:*

*'Adah and Zillah, hear my voice;*

*you wives of Lamech, listen to what I say:*

*I have killed a man for wounding me,*

*a young man for striking me."*

The pre-flood world was filled with people who viewed human life as disposable.

This regulation ensures that such attitudes do not continue in the re-created world.

Genesis 9:6

*"Whoever sheds the blood of man,*

*by man shall his blood be shed,*

*for God made man in his own image."*

This is not an instruction for personal revenge but an establishment of divine justice.

This verse must be understood within a judicial framework, where society, under God's ordained justice, enforces consequences for sin.

Romans 13:1-3

*"Let every person be subject to the governing authorities. For there is no authority except from God, and those that exist have been instituted by God.*

*Therefore whoever resists the authorities resists what God has appointed, and those who resist will incur judgment.*

*For rulers are not a terror to good conduct, but to bad. Would you have no fear of the one who is in authority? Then do what is good, and you will receive his approval."*

This means that senseless murders like Lamech's will be judged by the law.

And the judicial system that enforces this law is an authority established by God.

Of course, human justice systems have many flaws.

But God's principles are clear and just.

If justice fails, it is due to human failure---not God's law.

Genesis 9:6

*"For God made man in his own image."*

We must always remember this truth: "For God made man in his own image."

With this in mind, let us recall the words of Jesus.

Matthew 5:21

*"You have heard that it was said to those of old, 'You shall not murder; and whoever murders will be liable to judgment.'"*

Matthew 5:22

*"But I say to you that everyone who is angry with his brother will be liable to judgment; whoever insults his brother will be liable to the council; and whoever says, 'You fool!' will be liable to the hell of fire."*

This means that the regulation established in Noah's blessing was expanded beyond physical murder to include even anger and insults within the heart.

If that is the case, then Genesis 9:5-6 is not just about others---it must be understood as a personal matter of the heart.

Genesis 9:7

*"And you, be fruitful and multiply, increase greatly on the earth and multiply in it."*

Here again, the command "Be fruitful and multiply" is given, but an additional phrase is included: "increase greatly (swarm)."

The expression "swarm" does not merely indicate individual reproduction but can be interpreted as a command for the reconstruction of social order and the reestablishment of communities.

This implies that within this social structure, laws should be established and a society should be formed where people manage one another.

This is a divine command to build a functioning civilization.

Now, let us look at the scene where God establishes a new covenant (Hebrew: בְּרִית, berith) with Noah, his sons, and all living creatures that came out of the ark.

Key Aspects of the Covenant

1\. Universal Scope:

• The covenant is not only with humanity but also includes birds, livestock, wild animals, and even fish.

• This shows that all creation is included in God's covenant.

2\. Unchangeable Nature of the Covenant:

• The promise "Never again shall there be a flood to destroy the earth" demonstrates God's mercy and absolute faithfulness.

• Compared to ancient Near Eastern treaty documents, this covenant is unique because God Himself serves as the guarantor.

• Since the eternal God is the guarantor, this covenant is also eternal.

Genesis 9:8

*"Then God said to Noah and to his sons with him,"*

By including Noah's entire family, this covenant is established not just with individuals but with entire communities.

This suggests that God's grace of salvation extends beyond individuals to families, nations, and even all creation.

Genesis 9:9

*"Behold, I establish my covenant with you and your offspring after you,"*

A covenant includes the assurance that it will be fulfilled.

This applies not only to Noah and his sons but also to all their descendants---who are the sole survivors of the world.

In other words, this covenant extends to us today as their descendants, and it will also be fulfilled in the generations to come.

Amazingly, this covenant is unconditional.

Humanity does not need to fulfill any requirements---God unilaterally establishes this covenant.

Normally, covenants require guarantors, but here, no external guarantor is needed.

Genesis 9:10

*"And with every living creature that is with you, the birds, the livestock, and every beast of the earth with you, as many as came out of the ark; it is for every beast of the earth."*

Previously, the universal aspect of the covenant was emphasized for all humanity.

However, this passage clarifies that the covenant extends beyond humans to include all animals as well.

This demonstrates that God's covenant is of an incredible scale, encompassing all living beings.

Content of the Covenant

Genesis 9:11

*"I establish my covenant with you, that never again shall all flesh be cut off by the waters of the flood, and never again shall there be a flood to destroy the earth."*

Here, God promises that He will never again wipe out the entire world with a flood.

This means that God has pledged His love and protection over humanity.

This provides a guarantee that:

• No matter how severe disasters or calamities (earthquakes, locust plagues, diseases) may be,

• They will never again be a single, all-consuming catastrophe that erases the entire world.

Even if disasters occur, they should be understood as regional or temporary hardships and warnings within God's mercy and divine plan.

They may serve as temporary judgments against sin and corruption or operate within the natural order established by God.

In other words, while individual disasters have meaning within God's plan,

the covenant of the rainbow guarantees that complete destruction will never again occur until the final judgment.

Thus, this world will be preserved until the final judgment.

But won't the world be destroyed at the final judgment?

The final judgment is an event where God evaluates each person's deeds and determines their eternal destiny.

It is focused on individuals, not the entire world.

Thus, the flood was a one-time, universal judgment,

and God promised never to repeat it.

This means that at the final judgment, each person will be judged individually according to their deeds.

In this way, the judgment of the flood and the final judgment do not contradict each other.

They serve different purposes and methods, demonstrating how God's mercy and justice are balanced within His divine plan.

Genesis 9:12

*"And God said, 'This is the sign of the covenant that I make between me and you and every living creature that is with you, for all future generations.'"*

Genesis 9:13

*"I have set my bow in the cloud, and it shall be a sign of the covenant between me and the earth."*

The Hebrew word for "bow" originally refers to a weapon of war, but here, it is used as a symbol of peace.

Thus, it represents both God's wrath and His mercy.

Genesis 9:14

*"When I bring clouds over the earth and the bow is seen in the clouds,"*

Genesis 9:15

*"I will remember my covenant that is between me and you and every living creature of all flesh. And the waters shall never again become a flood to destroy all flesh."*

Genesis 9:16

*"When the bow is in the clouds, I will see it and remember the everlasting covenant between God and every living creature of all flesh that is on the earth."*

Genesis 9:17

*"God said to Noah, 'This is the sign of the covenant that I have established between me and all flesh that is on the earth.'"*

The Meaning of the "Sign"

Here, the "sign" functions as a visual marker that permanently confirms God's promise.

This means that whenever one sees it, they can clearly remember the covenant.

And it is not only for humans---God says that He Himself will also remember it.

If we take the meaning more broadly, this signifies that:

• God loves humanity.

• God loves all living creatures.

• God loves the world He created.

The Covenant of the Rainbow is Not "Invalid" Today

This covenant is still fully upheld today as an eternal and unconditional promise given by God to all creation.

We should be grateful that we live in a world loved by God.

What Lies at the End of This Covenant?

Although this covenant promises that there will never again be a worldwide judgment,

as we saw earlier, this does not mean that judgment has been abolished.

To rephrase it, there will no longer be collective judgment,

which means that judgment becomes a matter of individual responsibility.

Each person's judgment depends on their own life.

As long as God remains just, judgment will never disappear.

However, the fact that judgment depends on how we live is an incredible blessing.

Why?

Because those who love God have already received the assurance that they will be counted as righteous and enter the Kingdom of God.

But Life is Not Easy

At times, we may wander, stumble, and stray from the path.

In such moments, we might think:

• "Perhaps I am rebelling against God."

• "Maybe I will not be recognized as righteous at the final judgment."

These doubts may arise.

But even in those moments,

we can correct our steps, one by one, and draw closer to God.

This is a wonderful hope.

• We do not need to rely on others.

• Even in this world filled with sin, we can approach God by our own choices.

Just looking at the state of the world today,

it is truly amazing that this opportunity remains open to us.

So, let us keep pressing forward.

When It Gets Difficult, Look Up at the Sky

If it becomes too difficult,

we must lift our eyes to the sky.

Surely, a rainbow will appear.

We do not know exactly what form that rainbow will take,

but we can trust that God sees our hearts and gives us a sign.

When Do Rainbows Appear?

Rainbows usually appear when it rains.

Now, imagine what rain meant for Noah.

It likely brought fear and anxiety.

• "What if the world is destroyed again?"

• "This time, will I be swept away too?"

It is not unreasonable to think that he felt such fears.

Thus, at the moment Noah's heart was most troubled,

God gave him a clear sign: "It is okay."

That is why this is called the Covenant of the Rainbow.

It is as if God is saying, "See? Everything is fine."

Once We Know We Are Safe, Rain Becomes a Blessing

For us, too, the Covenant of the Rainbow is valid.

We will never again be destroyed as part of a collective judgment.

No matter how corrupt this world becomes,

as long as we desire to draw near to God, we can.

Today's passage clearly reveals this truth.

So do not give up, do not be swayed.

Believe wholeheartedly

that we are loved and protected by God.

Let us live today as those who receive His blessing.
