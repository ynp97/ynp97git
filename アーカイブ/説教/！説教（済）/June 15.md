June 15, 2025 -- Genesis 16:1--7 "Sarai and Hagar"

Now then, let us read from Genesis again today. Since we've been away from it for a while, let's briefly summarize chapter 15.

Abram was growing old without having a child, and he asked God, "Is Eliezer of Damascus to be my heir?"

But God promised that one who would come from his own body would be his heir, and He showed him the stars in the night sky and said, "So shall your offspring be." Abram believed this promise, and it was counted to him as righteousness.

After that, God had Abram prepare a sacrifice for making a covenant.

In the covenant rituals of the ancient Near East, where animals were cut in two and arranged opposite each other,

both parties would pass between the pieces to signify that they would bear the curse if they broke the covenant.

However, in this case, Abram fell into a deep sleep,

and only "a smoking fire pot and a flaming torch" (symbols of God) passed between the pieces.

This meant that God alone would bear the responsibility of the covenant unilaterally,

and that the land would be given to Abram and his descendants not based on human deeds,

but based on God's sovereignty and grace.

Furthermore, in a vision, God told Abram that his descendants would suffer for four hundred years in a foreign land (Egypt),

and afterward they would be delivered and return to this land. This included a prophecy of the Exodus,

showing that God's plan of salvation was inherent in this covenant.

Then, the boundaries of the promised land were listed, and today's passage takes place ten years after that.

Let me say at the beginning---every character in today's passage is wrong.

If you find it hard to empathize with any of them, then you're right. Everyone is mistaken.

Let us read.

Genesis 16:1 Now Sarai, Abram's wife, had borne him no children. She had a female Egyptian servant whose name was Hagar.

Let us first confirm the meaning of the names.

Sarai, as previously mentioned, means "noblewoman," but "noble" also implies one in authority, and carries with it a dominant image. It could also be translated as "mistress."

And that title is fitting for the situation in today's scene.

Next, Hagar---her name evokes the meaning of "fugitive" or "sojourner,"

and this foreshadows the later development where she flees into the wilderness.

And her status as "an Egyptian female servant"

aligns remarkably with the imagery that later appears in the Exodus.

And, of course, it is clear that she was a foreign woman, of different blood and status from Sarai.

As for faith, she was from Egypt (a land of idolatry).

Socially, she was of slave status, and it is highly likely that both her personal freedom and freedom of faith were restricted.

She was regarded as Sarai's property and was neither a subject of inheritance of faith nor of spiritual instruction.

In other words, although she was within a cultural sphere that acknowledged Yahweh,

she herself was likely not a believer.

A barren wife---being part of the lineage of God's promise and yet unable to bear children---

this situation continues throughout the Abraham narrative: from Sarah to Rebekah to Rachel.

This is a stage set for human beings to learn the message that "children, blessings, and promises are given not by human effort but by the grace and sovereignty of God."

Genesis 16:2 And Sarai said to Abram, "Behold now, the Lord has prevented me from bearing children. Go in to my servant; it may be that I shall obtain children by her." And Abram listened to the voice of Sarai.

To summarize what Sarai is saying here: since we, as a couple, cannot have children,

please have a child with my servant.

That is, the barren wife gives her slave to her husband to bear a child as a surrogate mother.

If we, living today---especially Christians with strong ethical standards regarding sexuality---receive this from our modern perspective,

it may seem like a very impure or unnatural idea.

However, considering the social and cultural background of that time, this was by no means an outrageous thought.

For the people of that time, having children to continue the household was one of the most important matters in life.

To avoid the extinction of one's lineage and name was a means by which one's existence would continue even after death.

Therefore, in order to accomplish that, they would take various measures,

and there was a deeply rooted value system that, no matter what, the house must be passed down to one who carries the bloodline.

Reflecting such a social context, the concept of surrogate motherhood was regarded as "formal procedure"

in ancient Near Eastern legal documents, such as the Code of Hammurabi.

Even though it may be shocking to us now, at that time it was a common contract to preserve the household.

In other words, I said at the beginning that the actions of every character in today's passage are wrong,

but that does not mean they are wrong from the standpoint of sexual impurity or ethical judgment.

So then, what was the issue?

It was that they could not endure God's silence, and tried to solve the problem by human means.

Genesis 15:3 And Abram said, "Behold, you have given me no offspring, and a member of my household will be my heir."

Genesis 15:4 And behold, the word of the Lord came to him: "This man shall not be your heir; your very own son shall be your heir."

Genesis 15:5 And he brought him outside and said, "Look toward heaven, and number the stars, if you are able to number them." Then he said to him, "So shall your offspring be."

Genesis 15:6 And he believed the Lord, and he counted it to him as righteousness.

If he was counted righteous because he believed, then he should have waited.

However, he could not. That is the major issue in today's passage.

With that in mind, let us look again at Sarai's words.

"The Lord has prevented me from bearing children."

Certainly, she acknowledges God's sovereignty. But the protagonist is "me."

At the core of this is, ultimately, a criticism of God for not doing things the way she wants.

"That I shall obtain children by her."

"That I shall obtain children" = "I shall be built up" (אִבָּנֶה / 'ibbāneh), passive form (Niphal).

This means "I myself shall be built up as a house,"

showing that "obtaining a child" was equivalent to "building a house."

In ancient times, "building a house = gaining an heir"

represented a woman's honor within the family system.

Thus, the verb here reflects Sarai's desire to recover her social standing.

Why could she not wait for God's promise?

Because it came from self-centeredness, such as the desire to prove her own worth.

With such motives, what Sarai did was to hand Hagar over to Abram.

In fact, this resembles the structure of another passage in the Bible.

Genesis 3:6 So when the woman saw that the tree was good for food, and that it was a delight to the eyes, and that the tree was to be desired to make one wise, she took of its fruit and ate, and she also gave some to her husband who was with her, and he ate.

Just like in Genesis 3, Sarai based her judgment on the present situation before her---on what she could see---

and determined her actions accordingly. And without waiting for the grace of God, which was not yet visible,

she advanced toward what seemed better in her own eyes, according to her own reasoning.

And another major point shared with chapter 3 is that it drags another person into the sin.

Abram is silent, but he must have longed for a child more than anyone else.

His heart, which believed that it would be given by God, was shaken by Sarai's words.

Human weakness is shared. In other words, the weaker the point, the more it resonates.

In this passage too, Abram was pulled into Sarai's idea and became involved in betrayal against God.

The anxiety deep within---whether merely continuing to wait is truly enough---

must have surfaced here.

We too must consider carefully whether our words and actions are dragging our fellow believers down into corruption,

or drawing our families away from God. We must live with this awareness.

Genesis 16:3 So, after Abram had lived ten years in the land of Canaan, Sarai, Abram's wife, took Hagar the Egyptian, her servant, and gave her to Abram her husband as a wife.

Just like giving the fruit of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, Sarai gave Hagar to Abram.

And Abram accepted it.

Now here, in addition to the information from verse 2, another detail is added:

that ten years had passed.

Ten years is a long time. He was eighty-five years old.

In the context of childbirth, ten years is not merely long in duration,

but the longer it goes, the less likely it becomes to come true.

The "ten years," a complete number, is not just a numerical value---it symbolizes a period during which faith is tested under God's silence.

During this time, the believer is questioned: will you continue to trust in God, or will you make your own path?

Isaiah 40:25 "To whom then will you compare me, that I should be like him?" says the Holy One.

Isaiah 40:26 Lift up your eyes on high and see: who created these?

He who brings out their host by number, calling them all by name,

by the greatness of his might, and because he is strong in power not one is missing.

Isaiah 40:27 Why do you say, O Jacob, and speak, O Israel,

"My way is hidden from the Lord, and my right is disregarded by my God"?

Isaiah 40:28 Have you not known? Have you not heard?

The Lord is the everlasting God, the Creator of the ends of the earth.

He does not faint or grow weary; his understanding is unsearchable.

Isaiah 40:29 He gives power to the faint, and to him who has no might he increases strength.

Isaiah 40:30 Even youths shall faint and be weary, and young men shall fall exhausted;

Isaiah 40:31 but they who wait for the Lord shall renew their strength;

they shall mount up with wings like eagles;

they shall run and not be weary; they shall walk and not faint.

Even in our present-day life of faith, how we respond during periods of silence---like this "ten years"---is a time when the depth of our faith is tested. When it seems that our prayers are not being answered, that is precisely when faith is required to remember "what kind of God He is."

Genesis 16:4 And he went in to Hagar, and she conceived. And when she saw that she had conceived, she looked with contempt on her mistress.

Abram had relations with Hagar, and she became pregnant immediately.

In one sense, the fact that Hagar became pregnant right away can be seen as "the human plan succeeded."

Sarai's plan was a complete success.

But this success was not the fulfillment of God's promise, but rather the result of actions not grounded in faith.

And this would go on to fundamentally shake Sarai's position.

What happened as a result of Hagar obtaining a "child"---which Sarai had not been able to obtain even after ten years?

The reality that Sarai lacked the ability to bear children as a woman became all the more clear.

From Hagar's perspective, she began to feel a sense of "superiority" from the fact that she could conceive.

In other words, she started to feel that she was "superior to Sarai as a woman."

As a result, she began to look down on her mistress Sarai.

As we saw earlier, Sarai's goal was to obtain a child and regain her honor.

However, because she did not wait for God's grace, and disregarded God,

and insisted on doing things her own way, what happened was the very opposite of her original goal.

What was Hagar's position like in that time?

Certainly, surrogate birth was a socially accepted method.

However, even in such cases, loyalty to the mistress (wife) was expected.

That is, Hagar got carried away and crossed the boundary.

In response to this, Sarai, filled with anger, spoke to Abram.

Genesis 16:5 And Sarai said to Abram, "May the wrong done to me be on you! I gave my servant to your embrace, and when she saw that she had conceived, she looked on me with contempt. May the Lord judge between you and me!"

There were various emotions in these words, and many factors likely fueled her anger.

Because the reality that "she could not bear children" was exposed,

because of the psychological inferiority complex that came from that,

because of the undeniable fact that all of this began with her own initiative,

and above all, because Hagar now carried the child who could be the heir of Abram,

She could not correct Hagar's attitude.

How strong was Hagar's defiance?

Literally, the phrase means, "May the wrong/violence/injustice done to me fall on you,"

which suggests that her behavior was quite severe.

In summary, Sarai was wrong, but so was Hagar!

Hagar's fault was that she got carried away and became arrogant.

It was not a spiritual wrongdoing but rather a social and ethical one.

However, the theme of the Bible here does not focus on Hagar's wrongdoing,

but rather on Sarai's.

Why? Because Sarai is a believer in the Lord.

Precisely because she believes, there is an attitude she ought to take.

The fact that she failed to do so is what is being questioned.

With that in mind, let us read it once more.

Genesis 16:5 And Sarai said to Abram, "May the wrong done to me be on you! I gave my servant to your embrace, and when she saw that she had conceived, she looked on me with contempt. May the Lord judge between you and me!"

Within these words lies Sarai's deep psychological state: a self-positioning as the victim.

Sarai portrays herself as "the victim" and "the one who has been betrayed" in response to Hagar's contempt toward her.

Being the victim means she is not at fault---this includes an element of self-justification.

At the heart of this tone---"I am not to blame"---lies the following logic:

"God made me barren." → "The Lord has prevented me from bearing children."

"Therefore, I had no choice but to do this."

"And yet she (Hagar) and you (Abram) have hurt me."

This is a form of self-justification that places the cause of the situation on God.

And self-justification stirs up blame-shifting.

The targets of that blame are Abram---and God.

"May the Lord judge between you and me."

At first glance, this may seem like she is entrusting everything to God,

but at its core, it means she is demanding that God take responsibility.

This moment of shifting blame echoes the words of Adam.

Genesis 3:12 The man said, "The woman whom you gave to be with me, she gave me fruit of the tree, and I ate."

While they themselves ignored the word of God, they shifted the responsibility onto God.

This is not just something Adam and Eve or Sarai did---we, too, do the same.

Genesis 16:6 But Abram said to Sarai, "Behold, your servant is in your power; do to her as you please." Then Sarai dealt harshly with her, and she fled from her.

Now, looking at this verse, if we translate it word for word, it is as follows:

"Abram said to Sarai, 'Behold (הִנֵּה), your female servant (שִׁפְחָתֵךְ) is in your hand (בְּיָדֵךְ). Do to her what is good in your eyes (הַטּוֹב בְּעֵינָיִךְ), do to her (עֲשִׂי־לָהּ).'"

In Genesis 16, Abram does not speak a single word directly to Hagar.

Here, again, the roles are reversed: even though she is now pregnant, she is mistreated by her mistress Sarai,

yet Abram says to Sarai, "Do to her as you please" (16:6), and refuses to get involved.

This verse highlights Abram's indifference---his posture of not looking.

He did not see Hagar's suffering. He showed no concern for the fact that his first child had been conceived.

As a result, she was cast out into the wilderness and became someone "erased from human sight."

And this indifference of Abram is contrasted with God's care for the forsaken in the next week's passage.

Genesis 16:13 So she called the name of the Lord who spoke to her, "You are a God of seeing," for she said, "Truly here I have seen him who looks after me."

There is a contrast between seeing and not seeing.

Now then, what was Abram's fault in today's passage?

It was that Abram, who was the spiritual leader in the family of faith, faced this matter without prayer.

Ten years had passed since he last received a word from God.

During that time, Abram gradually lost the purity of his faith,

grew weary of waiting, and allowed human thinking to spread within his heart.

Human reasoning is built on what is convenient for people, and thus it is indeed comfortable.

That is why, naturally, his heart was swayed, and he became unable to walk the right path---the path of righteousness---

in other words, the path of trusting God wholeheartedly.

Here is one issue we must reflect upon:

this does not mean we should simply do nothing and remain still.

Understanding, accepting, and waiting through God's silence is sometimes important.

However, that does not contradict taking action through prayer.

For example, what Abram should have done at this time

was to pray and encourage Sarai.

But he did not do that; instead, he simply accepted what was offered to him

and chose an easy way out.

If only he had prayed beforehand, a proper solution or way of avoidance would have been shown.

Before Sarai was pushed to this point of despair,

if they had together strengthened their faith as a family

and truly prepared to receive God's silence as hope,

then we could say Abram was walking the right path by faith.

Abram's fault was that he failed to do so.

We, too, often feel that many things are not going well. Matters do not resolve within the time span we imagined. "Even though I prayed so much," "even though I did all this," "I thought I had heard the word of God." Yet, it is not at all uncommon for God to remain silent in our lives.

Then why do such things happen? The answer is hinted at in today's passage.

That is to say---it is precisely that silence that becomes the trial of our faith.

Abram and Sarai, in the end, must have come to understand that their plan had failed.

And that very realization became nourishment for their growth.

This attempt at surrogacy ultimately brought discord into the family.

Sarai's plan, from a human standpoint, was completely successful.

But we, too, should realize that human success does not necessarily lead to true blessing.

In other words, people often mistake their true goal.

And without realizing it, they set goals accordingly.

Though our true goal is "to be blessed,"

we end up making our goal something secondary---like "to obtain money."

What God tells us to pursue is true blessing.

It is the Lord Jesus Christ who truly desires to give us what is good.

However, because our perspective is narrow, we tend to focus only on the problems before our eyes.

Certainly, having money may at times help us avoid misfortune.

But money alone cannot bring true happiness.

And it is also not true that we cannot be happy without money.

In today's passage as well, Sarai tried to gain a child through a humanly legitimate method.

It was socially accepted, and her plan seemed to succeed.

However, as a result, Sarai---who should have been a vessel of blessing---ended up wounding and hating others.

Chaos ensued.

God had not commanded her to do that.

The absence of true goodness in it had already been spoken through God's silence.

What we need is to live a life of prayer.

And to pray is not to impose our desires on God. It is to entrust them to Him.

Prayer is the act of aligning our heart with the will of God.

"What is God trying to make me do through this situation?" "What should I do?"

To ask this of God---that is true prayer.

We must learn to pray in this correct way.

If we continue to bring only our demands before God and try to make Him obey,

those prayers will not be answered.

Because the one praying such prayers will not be able to find true happiness.

God does not give us what is wrong.

That is precisely why He sometimes remains silent.

Then what should we do when God is silent?

It is to bring order to our inner selves so that we can perceive His will.

For that reason, we read the Bible.

Let us be made whole---both in spirit and in body.

If that is so, then we should receive even that "silence" with joy and thanksgiving.

God is waiting patiently for us to mature and to truly find what is right.

If we can find hope and offer thanks even within that silence,

then a wonderful relationship with God will be built there.

It is precisely "believing in God"---that is, "faith counted as righteousness"---that will appear in such moments.

If we pray with an understanding of what God considers good and evil, then resolution will surely be given.

That may not be in a way we can understand.

Even so, we must trust the Lord and walk in that way.

We must become those who do what has been shown to us.

What we should pray is, "Please teach me Your will."

This is the most important thing.

Let us prepare ourselves so that we can pray in this way.

And then, ahead of that, let us receive the abundant blessing

that will come upon even those around us, including our families.
