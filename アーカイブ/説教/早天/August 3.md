**August 3, 2025　Genesis 18:16--22 "A Place That Deserves to Perish"**

The central figure of today's story is Lot. How has Lot appeared in the Bible until now?

Lot was Abram's nephew and accompanied Abram in his journey.

However, as they both came to possess wealth, conflict arose between them, and so they decided to part ways.

When the time came to choose land, Lot made the decision to go toward a place that "seemed likely to make a lot of money"---this is what the Bible says. That place was the city of Sodom.

Basically, he was not a bad person, but his behavior as one who makes worldly decisions gives us spiritual insight through comparison with Abraham, who makes spiritually righteous decisions.

In the previous passage, we saw the LORD speak to Abraham about the coming destruction of Sodom, and Abraham began to intercede. It is beyond doubt that Abraham had Lot, who lived in that city of Sodom, in mind during this intercession.

What kind of place was Sodom?

It was a place where industry could easily flourish, and by producing asphalt, they were able to gain considerable profit from Egypt and other regions. Religiously, the specifics are unclear, but based on the surrounding context of that era, it is said that there is a high possibility it was polytheistic.

This is the biblical evaluation of Sodom:

2 Peter 2:3--14 (ESV):

"And in their greed they will exploit you with false words. Their condemnation from long ago is not idle, and their destruction is not asleep.

For if God did not spare angels when they sinned, but cast them into hell and committed them to chains of gloomy darkness to be kept until the judgment;

if he did not spare the ancient world, but preserved Noah, a herald of righteousness, with seven others, when he brought a flood upon the world of the ungodly;

Genesis 19:1 (ESV):

"The two angels came to Sodom in the evening, and Lot was sitting in the gate of Sodom. When Lot saw them, he rose to meet them and bowed himself with his face to the earth."

From this passage, we can observe several things.

First, let us consider the expression, "Lot was sitting in the gate of Sodom."

At that time, a "gate" was not merely a passageway, but the place where the elders of the city gathered to conduct public affairs, such as judgments and business discussions.

From this, it is likely that Lot held a certain "official position" in the city.

In fact, in verse 9, there is a scene where people say to Lot, "This fellow came to sojourn, and he has become the judge!"

These words show that the residents of Sodom regarded Lot as someone "in a position to judge,"

and so it is highly probable that he was involved in a public role.

Next, we should pay attention to the contrast with Abraham.

Gen. 18:1 The LORD appeared to Abraham by the oaks of Mamre, as he sat at the door of his tent in the heat of the day.

Gen. 18:2 He lifted up his eyes and looked, and behold, three men were standing in front of him. When he saw them, he ran from the tent door to meet them and bowed himself to the earth

Gen. 18:3 and said, "O Lord, if I have found favor in your sight, do not pass by your servant.

Gen. 18:4 Let a little water be brought, and wash your feet, and rest yourselves under the tree,

Gen. 18:5 while I bring a morsel of bread, that you may refresh yourselves, and after that you may pass on---since you have come to your servant." So they said, "Do as you have said."

Abraham also showed a very respectful attitude toward the visitors,

but there is a difference in the manner of his action.

Abraham ran to meet the guests from a distance and bowed himself to the ground.

In contrast, Lot responded by "standing up" from a state of "sitting."

In other words, while Lot "stood up as one already present there,"

Abraham "ran to the ones coming from afar."

This difference can be understood as a difference in attitude toward the angels of God---

namely, the attitude of one who is passively waiting versus one who actively runs out to meet.

That said, the fact that Lot "stood up to meet" and

"bowed with his face to the ground" is by no means a disrespectful attitude.

It is clear that he also treated them with reverence.

In that respect, although there is a difference from Abraham, Lot's posture

can also be seen as maintaining a certain sincerity.

Gen. 19:2 and said, "My lords, please turn aside to your servant's house and spend the night and wash your feet. Then you may rise up early and go on your way." They said, "No; we will spend the night in the town square."

Gen. 19:3 But he pressed them strongly; so they turned aside to him and entered his house. And he made them a feast and baked unleavened bread, and they ate.

Here too, a difference from Abraham can be seen.

Gen. 18:6 And Abraham went quickly into the tent to Sarah and said, "Quick! Three seahs of fine flour! Knead it, and make cakes."

Gen. 18:7 And Abraham ran to the herd and took a calf, tender and good, and gave it to a young man, who prepared it quickly.

Gen. 18:8 Then he took curds and milk and the calf that he had prepared, and set it before them. And he stood by them under the tree while they ate.

Lot's attitude here was by no means disrespectful.

However, there is certainly a significant difference in depth.

Abraham ran himself, chose the best, commanded his wife, and had a servant prepare it.

He moved several people, took multiple steps, and offered hospitality with sacrifice.

But in Lot's case, he immediately brought them home and baked unleavened bread himself.

This scene shows that he prepared everything simply with what was already at hand.

Compared to Abraham, one cannot deny the difference in the scale of preparation and degree of sacrifice.

What Lot did was respond "in the best way possible with what was presently in his hands,"

meaning he did not move from his current place---this is the image.

On the other hand, in Abraham's case, he moved himself from where he was,

sacrificed personally, and served.

Here we can see a model of the spiritual difference between the attitude of

"I will do what I can, but what I can't do is impossible," and

the attitude of "I will move beyond what I can do, and make it possible."

And this becomes a very important theme as a difference in attitude toward God.

That is, the difference in attitude concerning how much one will do to serve God.

There is a clear difference between faith that offers what is left over

and faith that offers what one does not have, trusting in God.

This directly connects to the fundamental theme of what we think God is like,

and it is a theme that each of us should ask ourselves regarding the purity of our faith.

We, too, would do well to reevaluate from the theme of "what shall I do to serve God"

as a guidepost for measuring our own heart toward God.

As a side note, this unleavened bread is also significant, being made during the Exodus,

and we can say that the image of Lot fleeing from the perishing Sodom is overlaid with the image of the Exodus.

Now here, the angels sent from heaven declare, "We will spend the night in the square."

This statement can be considered to have meaning beyond the mere choice of a place to stay.

The square is a place where the people of the city regularly pass by, gather, and exchange information.

It is, in a sense, the "face" of the city, and it is also the place where the values and spirituality of that society are most clearly reflected.

Therefore, to remain in the square can be read as an intention to experience and discern the essence of that city firsthand.

In other words, the angels were trying to directly observe the people of the city and confirm the reality of their corruption before destroying it.

And the fact that this takes place at night is also significant.

The night square---that is, a kind of "touchstone."

Especially in ancient cities of the time, where there were no streetlights, the night was truly "dark."

In situations where no one could recognize each other or even see each other's faces, people would sometimes act with their true nature fully exposed.

That is, in the darkness where responsibility becomes ambiguous, human conscience is tested.

Modern-day internet lynching is a good example of this: malice in full bloom under the cover of anonymity.

If one desires to harm, it is possible to do so without anyone knowing.

When placed in such an "invisible situation," what will a person choose, as a truly free being?

That is something we can witness.

In other words, this proposal to "spend the night in the square" can be seen as an extremely important spiritual test---one that evaluates the essence of the town, the true character of its residents, and the spiritual health of the entire society.

However, in reality, it becomes clear very quickly just how severe the corruption of this town is, without even needing to stay in the square.

Lot desperately tried to bring them into his house and prevent them from staying in the square,

because he already knew very well how dangerous that place was.

(Here, we also see the theme of "Lot, who did not try to leave from there.")

The following is their response:

Gen. 19:4

But before they lay down, the men of the city, the men of Sodom, both young and old, all the people to the last man, surrounded the house.

Gen. 19:5

And they called to Lot, "Where are the men who came to you tonight? Bring them out to us, that we may know them."

In the end, the two angels ended up staying at Lot's house.

However, at the same time, the true nature of the town sprang up in the night square and quickly bloomed.

That collective behavior, as if a manifestation of the town's shared will in the square where no one had to show their true selves,

was stirred up right here.

And it was the desire of the whole town to commit sexual assault against the two angels.

A deeply offensive proposal---yet presented as the consensus of the city of Sodom. That is what is evident here.

※ "Both young and old": A hyperbolic Old Testament expression (merism) indicating "total civic agreement."

※ "Merism" is a rhetorical device that expresses the whole by mentioning two contrasting parts.

Let us now look at the parallel section in the Book of Judges.

Judges chapter 19 describes a story from a period of Israel's moral decline. A Levite, traveling with his concubine, lodges in Gibeah of the tribe of Benjamin. The men of the town gather to abuse him, but instead his concubine is offered in his place, and she is raped all night and dies. The Levite cuts her body into twelve pieces and sends them to the tribes of Israel, denouncing the evil committed in Gibeah. This event shocks all Israel and triggers a civil war. It symbolizes the deep moral collapse of a people who have lost sight of God.

It is a terrifying passage. It may be the most brutal in the entire Bible.

And it is something that is being spoken of in parallel with today's account of Sodom.

In other words, it means that Israel is becoming like Sodom.

Gen. 19:6 Lot went out to the men at the entrance, shut the door after him,

In Judges 19, the man himself also goes inside, so here, Lot may be seen a little more favorably.

The issue lies in the next part.

Gen. 19:7 and said, "I beg you, my brothers, do not act so wickedly.

Gen. 19:8 Behold, I have two daughters who have not known any man. Let me bring them out to you, and do to them as you please. Only do nothing to these men, for they have come under the shelter of my roof."

It is an overwhelmingly shocking and difficult-to-interpret part,

but generally speaking, this is almost universally evaluated negatively by commentators (Calvin, Luther, Wenham, Waltke) as a perverse misuse of the norm of prioritizing hospitality.

Incidentally, in Judges 19, the virgin daughter is not handed over, only the concubine is, and as a result, it leads to the division of Israel---in other words, "no good result comes from it."

Gen. 19:9 But they said, "Stand back!" And they said, "This fellow came to sojourn, and he has become the judge! Now we will deal worse with you than with them." Then they pressed hard against the man Lot, and drew near to break the door down.

Gen. 19:10 But the men reached out their hands and brought Lot into the house with them and shut the door.

Gen. 19:11 And they struck with blindness the men who were at the entrance of the house, both small and great, so that they wore themselves out groping for the door.

What is happening here is that God intervened in this situation.

The time of the Judges can be described as a period when God intentionally kept His distance,

but this time is different---in this era, God was near His people, carefully ordaining each matter,

and thus, we can understand that such an intervention took place.

As a result, Lot was saved from being violated himself,

and though not by his own strength, he was also able to protect the angels.

This method of "blinding" is also highly symbolic.

In Genesis, the sequence of "seeing," "desiring," and "eating"---the flow of "seeing" leading to "desiring,"

which leads into "coveting"---is now reversed here as "seeing" is destroyed.

So then, although today's passage is quite difficult, I would like us to learn from the figure of the people of Sodom.

It is a matter of: what do we think about when we are in the public square at night?

If we describe their state simply, it would be:

They are running completely contrary to the teachings of God, and yet they do not even realize it.

When we look at the people of Sodom now, we can truly say, "This is the worst." And we think, "I would never do anything like that."

However, the important point is that even the people of Sodom at that time,

in their own way, thought they were "doing the right thing."

The "rightness" spoken of here does not refer to a moral standard of good or bad deeds,

but rather, to the idea that "one has no doubt that one's own judgment is the most effective."

In other words, even if one does something bad, if it seems beneficial to oneself, it is judged as "right." Conversely, even if one tries to do good, if it seems disadvantageous to oneself, it is judged as "not right"---this kind of attitude of never doubting that "I am right" was present.

And what we must be careful about is that when such thoughts are shared "in the square,"

the society itself can come to recognize evil as "right."

If we are to give the clearest example of this, it would be "war."

Everyone knows that killing people is wrong. And yet,

under the noble causes of "defending the country," "protecting national interests," and "eliminating threats to ourselves,"

people come to believe that killing others is the right thing to do---this is the true nature of war.

This is a mistake on a very large scale,

but such things can actually happen quite frequently in our daily lives.

Furthermore, in the present day, we must not forget that this "square" has become something unbounded in space due to the advent of the internet.

In the past, the sharing of consciousness occurred within somewhat limited communities such as towns, regions, workplaces, or schools. But now, with almost no restrictions, if there is a mistaken hatred or way of thinking,

it can easily spread, and it may even be accepted as something "right."

Just like the Tower of Babel, where by having a common language, people united in a direction toward evil---

we must not forget that such a spiral is something that can also happen in our time.

On a smaller scale---for example, even the place right in front of us called "the church" is the same.

Within the church, if a negative topic is shared, and it gets amplified, and that negative opinion is shared with the whole church as something "right," if such a thing happens, it is something that truly requires caution.

Moreover, it is not at all a rare occurrence; rather, it can happen quite easily.

Someone begins to denounce a person, saying, "That person is wrong," and the people around go along with it.

Then someone adds a plausible reason, saying, "God is saying this."

But that is wrong.

Because God does not forsake even a single person.

So why would the church forsake that person?

This is something we must never forget.

And originally, we should be able to notice this. We can notice it.

However, as we spend time in the "square," we may fall into the trap of that square.

Between people, there are times when we simply don't get along, or when personalities clash.

However, to accept that, and to take time to repair the relationship---

that is precisely the mission that the church ought to fulfill.

Nevertheless, if someone in a strong position expresses an opinion against a negative idea,

and everyone conforms to that person and becomes aggressive,

if such things occur, we must keep it in our hearts that this is something very wrong.

Let us be shaped so that we do not become like the people of Sodom.

To do that, we need to keep our eyes firmly fixed on Jesus Christ.

Because Jesus never fell into such traps of the square,

and always held perfect, true "rightness."

We tend to determine what is "right" based on our own judgment of "good" and "evil."

But Jesus is perfectly righteous and perfectly good,

and in that righteousness, He truly loves each and every one of us.

By looking up to that Jesus Christ and striving to live like Him,

we will not fall into this "trap of the square." We will be able to stand firm.

And such a life will become one in which we love people from the heart like Jesus,

and live as those who rejoice in sacrificing ourselves for others from the heart.

So that we may gain that wonderful way of living---

I would like us to apply today's study of the Bible to our lives going forward.
