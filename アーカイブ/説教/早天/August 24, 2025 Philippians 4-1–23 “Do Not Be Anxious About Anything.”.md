**August 24, 2025 Philippians 4:1--23 "Do Not Be Anxious About Anything."**

Today, let us read Philippians---the final chapter.

To summarize what has been written so far:

There has been a battle against self-centeredness.

Philippians 2:3 "Do nothing from selfish ambition or conceit, but in humility count others more significant than yourselves."

Philippians 2:4 "Let each of you look not only to his own interests, but also to the interests of others."

*Do not see others as the same as yourself, but consider them superior to yourself.*

To win this battle, it is necessary to die to your current self once.

That is, you must listen to the words of Jesus, discover the self-centeredness within yourself,

and offer it up to the Lord.

And you need to be reborn as someone worthy of the new world that is to come.

For that, you must die to your current self---that is, you must stop living according to the ways of this world.

That means becoming like Jesus in His death.

And just like Jesus, be born again as something new.

Naturally, this is a very difficult thing.

Philippians 3:10 "That I may know him and the power of his resurrection, and may share his sufferings, becoming like him in his death,"

Philippians 3:11 "that by any means possible I may attain the resurrection from the dead."

However, this difficult challenge is not about completing the transformation into the likeness of Christ as the goal.

The very act of starting the challenge to become like Christ is the goal.

In other words, following the words of Jesus, and beginning---and continuing---a life that seeks not the things of this world,

but righteousness before God, is the goal.

Completion will be achieved in the kingdom of heaven. This will certainly come.

And until then, every challenge we take on amid difficulties---no matter how small---will be evaluated by the Lord.

Moreover, all the failures we make as we continue this challenge are already redeemed by the cross of Jesus.

Therefore, let us continue to be transformed step by step, without giving up.

Paul encourages this to the people of the church in Philippi, and to us.

Today's message is a continuation from that. And it is also the conclusion.

Philippians 4:1 "Therefore, my brothers, whom I love and long for, my joy and crown, stand firm thus in the Lord, my beloved."

There are believers who are living just as Paul encouraged and who continue walking on the path of the faith challenge.

That is the very reward for Paul, who, despite facing many hardships, continued to proclaim Jesus.

For a pastor, the good thing is not that something of his own is praised.

Rather, it is purely the strengthening of the believers' faith that is the greatest reward a pastor can receive.

Philippians 4:2 "I entreat Euodia and I entreat Syntyche to agree in the Lord."

Philippians 4:3 "Yes, I ask you also, true companion, help these women, who have labored side by side with me in the gospel together with Clement and the rest of my fellow workers, whose names are in the book of life."

Here, personal names appear. We do not know exactly who these people were.

Most likely, they were individuals within the church in Philippi who had differing opinions,

and Paul probably heard about this from Epaphroditus.

Therefore, at the end of this letter, he once again encourages them to mend the rift between them.

To help with that, a person called the "true companion" is being appointed.

There is debate over whether this is a real name or a nickname for someone.

In any case, there is no doubt that he is someone who understands well how a Christian ought to be.

And it is revealed here that Euodia and Syntyche had a past of being greatly used for the Lord's work.

They were likely friends who fought alongside Paul in the difficulties of founding the church for the sake of the gospel.

Paul hopes that they will again be united and work powerfully together.

Christians are still human. Sometimes there will be differences of opinion.

And sometimes the problems will grow large.

The church must always overcome such things.

How to do that---we must learn from the words of Jesus.

**The first thing we must remember in the church is this:**

What is "right" for me is not everything.

Certainly, some things may be logically correct. There may be times when someone else appears to be clearly wrong.

But what we must ask ourselves in such moments is this: **Is being right really what matters most?**

We need to begin by placing ourselves in the other person's shoes.

My position and theirs are always different.

We must examine ourselves and ask whether we truly understand that.

And then, we must discern whether the "rightness" we hold is really worth using as a weapon to attack or hurt someone else.

**Furthermore, if we still feel an urge to criticize someone, we should examine our own hearts.**

Why is it that we cannot accept this person?

Perhaps the root of it is anger that stems from knowing our own mistake.

Or perhaps it is an attack meant to hide our own weakness.

If so, then the problem lies on our side.

Let us begin by restoring and resolving our own hearts.

There are, of course, times when the other person is clearly wrong.

So then, what should we do in those moments?

That is precisely when we should learn from Jesus.

**Jesus, though perfectly righteous, was crucified as if He were a sinner.**

And what happened afterward?

He was exalted as the One who revealed the love of God to all humankind.

In other words, it was made clear that He was not wrong at all, but absolutely blameless.

It is in these difficult moments in our relationships that we must follow Christ.

That is, we must approach others with **love, gentleness, and humility**, not hatred---understanding their weaknesses, and embracing them.

**In the value system of Jesus, where everything is reversed**, winning means very little.

Rather than swinging the sword of justice and harming someone, it is far more valuable to lose and receive the blow.

**What is wrong will certainly be corrected** by the Lord, who is perfectly righteous and knows everything.

If that is the case, then we should not exhaust ourselves in judging good and evil,

but instead, focus on **Jesus' command to love others.**

If we live that way, our life will surely be rewarded by the Lord.

Because **that** is what is truly right---**what is right before God.**

When we trust in that reward, we will naturally release our grip on the sword,

and be able to resolve the situation with the weapons of **love and gentleness** in hand.

**Philippians 4:4**

Rejoice in the Lord always; again I will say, rejoice.

Paul repeats it because it is important.

But what does it mean to rejoice?

It is not about rejoicing **because something good happened**.

It is about rejoicing **always**, that is, **in everything**.

**1 Thessalonians 5:16--18**

Rejoice always,

pray without ceasing,

give thanks in all circumstances;

for this is the will of God in Christ Jesus for you.

This is the very heart of Christianity.

But then, what does it mean to rejoice?

We often misunderstand it as a matter of **endurance**---"I must rejoice!"---convincing ourselves that something bad is actually good.

But if we act that way, it becomes no different from the kind of external performance that Jesus despised.

Such pretense might fool others,

but not Jesus, who commands us to "Rejoice!"---He would say, "That's not it."

Of course, this isn't easy.

Even if Paul repeats it twice, it doesn't make it any easier.

**So what should we do?**

The way forward is to **accept everything**.

What does that mean?

It means believing that this world---created by the Creator---is known by the Lord and is completely under His care.

It means trusting that the perfect and flawless plan of the Almighty God is already at work in our lives.

If we truly believe that, then we know that the end will be glorious.

No matter what difficulties we face along the way,

they are merely stepping stones toward something better.

In fact, it is precisely **because we go through those difficulties** that we come to know something even better.

We can consider them a **gracious blessing**.

So, if something good happens, of course we rejoice.

And even if something bad happens, we can still rejoice---

because we are expecting the next good step.

Phil. 4:5 Let your reasonableness be known to everyone. The Lord is at hand.

To accept everything, to have a heart that always rejoices, a heart that gives thanks in everything---that is a gentle heart.

Paul says to show that to people.

In other words, those who believe in Jesus must let it be known to people that "we are living by believing that God always does things for us to rejoice in."

Because the day of judgment is near.

There is no more time to be thinking that the way of life of believing in Jesus is "not joyful," and being satisfied surrounded by the things of this world.

The fact that Jesus has redeemed us is synonymous with the beginning of the end times.

To save people from this world that gets closer to the final time with each second,

first, we must let it be known that we can rejoice no matter what happens,

that the way of life of believing in Jesus is always filled with great joy.

Because we must let people understand that this is something wonderful,

different from the worldly way of life that can only rejoice when good things happen,

and falls into despair immediately when something happens.

If that is so, then we must express our Christian way of life in relationships with others.

It is not enough to live in a way worthy of God only when we are alone.

How we relate to others when we interact with them---that is extremely important.

And the more problems there are, the more it can be said to be a chance to show our trust in God.

In other words, to be gentle and always rejoicing in the midst of troubles with others

is very important for the salvation of people's souls.

Let us remember this when we have trouble with someone.

It is difficult. It becomes trouble because it is difficult.

But the Lord knows what is right. If that is so, then we entrust the rightness of the issue to the Lord,

and in order to express the grace of God, we face the problem of relationships with others

with love and gentleness, and seek resolution.

Phil. 4:6 Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God.

To forgive, accept, and live in love and gentleness at all times is, in reality, truly difficult.

Because this world is full of things that "want to hurt someone."

However, the words Paul said in the famous Philippians 4:6--7 encourage us.

What is written here is that we *can* "always rejoice."

"Rejoice always." We don't say "That's impossible," but rather, "It's possible." But how?

It is achieved by not being anxious.

In other words, it is a question of whether we believe the Word of Philippians 4:6.

Do we believe that, because of Jesus Christ, everything will be okay no matter what happens?

This does not mean "Let us give thanks because something good happened."

Even when there is nothing, we first give thanks for "being able to believe in the Lord."

We give thanks that "the Lord Jesus Christ is worthy to be believed."

From the standards of this world, this is baseless and irrational.

That is why we must believe.

No matter what problems we have, we believe without a doubt that they will surely be resolved,

and we lift those problems up to the Lord in prayer.

Then what will happen?

Phil. 4:7 And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus.

What is here is the peace of God which surpasses all understanding.

Unfortunately, this cannot be explained. Because it surpasses all understanding.

But we can understand only this---that what we are given is peace.

Surely it is not a small peace that is hard to understand, but a great peace that surpasses understanding.

Earlier I said unfortunately it cannot be explained,

but it is not unfortunate at all. Rather, it is so wonderful that it *cannot* be explained.

Why can you rejoice at such times? On what basis can you rejoice?

From the perspective of others, an incomprehensible great peace appears in that person's heart and mind.

If we read this carefully, it does not say that the problem disappears.

And furthermore, it does not promise that physical or material things will be fulfilled.

Only that the heart and mind will be guarded in Christ Jesus.

Things will one day break, and we will one day die.

Beyond all of those things, the heart will be filled with the peace of the Lord.

Then, no matter what happens, it will be okay.

Because the heart can accept all of those things.

But this is extremely difficult. That is why Paul gives a hint in the next part.

**Philippians 4:8--16 -- Living Without Anxiety**

Phil. 4:8

Finally, brothers and sisters, whatever is true, whatever is honorable, whatever is right, whatever is pure, whatever is lovely, whatever is commendable---if there is any excellence and if anything worthy of praise---think about these things.

Phil. 4:9

Practice the things you have learned and received and heard and seen in me, and the God of peace will be with you.

How can we live without being anxious about anything? Here we find a hint:

It is to always fix our thoughts on what is good.

Let us do what is right. We live in this world, and therefore we are called to walk in line with what is deemed right and good in this world.

Though our freedom is guaranteed in Christ, let us use that freedom to do what is good.

This is actually very simple. Furthermore, even if we are not able to carry it out perfectly, it is still okay.

What matters is to "fix our thoughts"---to think seriously and intentionally about what is right and good, and to live accordingly.

And what happens when we do that? We can say this: "I am striving to live rightly before God."

That simple fact---the practice of faith, the living testimony that we follow Jesus---reveals to this world a portion of what is invisible and beyond human understanding.

In our everyday lives, by simply fixing our thoughts on what is good and doing what we can, we will experience the works of God manifest. And our faith will be deepened through that experience.

As we repeat such experiences, we gradually grow out of anxious living.

For God surely rewards. That is to say, if I trust in Him and stand right before Him, everything will be resolved.

We arrive at that assurance.

And that faith guards our hearts and minds in Christ Jesus.

Paul teaches us that such things were actually happening in the church at Philippi.

Phil. 4:10

But I rejoiced in the Lord greatly, that now at last you have revived your concern for me; indeed, you were concerned before, but you lacked opportunity to show it.

Phil. 4:11

Not that I speak from need, for I have learned to be content in whatever circumstances I am.

Phil. 4:12

I know how to get along with little, and I also know how to live in prosperity. In any and every circumstance I have learned the secret of being filled and going hungry, of having abundance and suffering need.

Phil. 4:13

I can do all things through Him who strengthens me.

Phil. 4:14

Nevertheless, you have done well to share with me in my difficulty.

Phil. 4:15

You yourselves also know, Philippians, that at the first preaching of the gospel, after I left Macedonia, no church shared with me in the matter of giving and receiving except you alone.

Phil. 4:16

For even in Thessalonica you sent gifts for my needs more than once.

In simple terms, the church at Philippi supported Paul's ministry through their financial offerings.

That, too, is the "doing of good."

It is the practical outworking of righteousness before God.

Because of their support, Paul was able to write letters and continue to encourage the churches. That actually happened.

Their financial contribution---far from being an easy act---was used by the Lord as a righteous deed before God.

Phil. 4:17

Not that I seek the gift itself, but I seek the profit which increases to your account.

The sense of accomplishment they gained through giving made them spiritually stronger.

Paul's message is this: doing what is right and good before God is not only for the benefit of others---it brings great spiritual gain to the one who gives, as well.

When we "care for others," when we choose not to draw the sword in conflict but live in love and gentleness in our relationships---

yes, at times that may feel like a one-sided burden.

Moreover, giving financially, serving others---these involve offering up our precious money and time. It is no small matter.

But in the end, such actions meet the needs of others and bring joy to them.

And at the same time, the one who gives will experience the works of God being done through them,

and they will be enriched spiritually.

If someone says they don't need to give---especially with a negative attitude---it may simply mean they have not yet tasted the joy of giving.

Philippians 4:18 I have received full payment, and more. I am well supplied, having received from Epaphroditus the gifts you sent, a fragrant offering, a sacrifice acceptable and pleasing to God.

Philippians 4:19 And my God will supply every need of yours according to his riches in glory in Christ Jesus.

Philippians 4:20 To our God and Father be glory forever and ever. Amen.

Philippians 4:21 Greet every saint in Christ Jesus. The brothers who are with me greet you.

Philippians 4:22 All the saints greet you, especially those of Caesar's household.

Philippians 4:23 The grace of the Lord Jesus Christ be with your spirit.

Paul says he is well supplied and rejoicing.

Paul received a generous offering. Now, did he buy a nice car with it?

No, he is sending it to the early church, which is fighting under persecution from Judaism.

Moreover, the church he is sending it to is one that is written to be not so cooperative with Paul's approach.

And Paul himself is still a prisoner on the verge of being sentenced to death.

Yet even so, Paul is able to accept all of it and rejoice.

And he is expecting the people of Philippi---and furthermore, all Christians---to hold that same kind of joy.

That is the nature of what is good in the name of God. It is something that sanctifies the hearts of those who do it.

Let us look once more at this Word.

Philippians 4:6 Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God.

Philippians 4:7 And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus.

To not be anxious about anything is not a simple thing.

Anxieties come one after another, and we cannot stop them.

But what we must remember is this: we are already being protected by God.

We are already saved and forgiven.

That is not "someday," but right now---this very moment---it is upon us.

The world is in a difficult state. Even personally, there may be many things we cannot see.

I believe anxieties inevitably arise.

But God speaks to us through the Bible:

You are okay. You are under My protection. I, the Almighty God, know everything, and the perfect plan I have established is already advancing over you without a single error. Do not be anxious.

Let us believe this.

The only basis is simply that it is written in the Bible.

It is written in this letter to the Philippians that we are reading now. That is why we believe this incredible truth.

This is the privilege given to us Christians.

People of the world, in this ever-changing society, will surely be tossed around and anxious about the "right answers" that quickly shift with unreliable information.

But we are able to grasp and believe in an unchanging truth.

Let us make full use of this privilege, and live in this world with great peace that surpasses understanding.

This very thing will become a manifestation of God's glory.

By this revealed glory of God, let us lead our beloved ones to the Lord and store up our treasures in heaven.
