**20250427 Genesis 12:10-20 "Abram Goes Down to Egypt"**

Now, let us continue reading Genesis today.

Gen. 12:10 Now there was a famine in the land. So Abram went down to Egypt to sojourn there, for the famine was severe in the land.

Now, Abram had been called by the LORD God, who said, "Go from your country and your kindred and your father's house to the land that I will show you." At that time, Abram was seventy-five years old.

It was certainly not easy for an elderly patriarch of a nomadic tribe to abandon his familiar cultural environment and head toward an unknown land, yet Abram took that step forward. We must not forget that when he took that step, the exact land had not yet been shown to him.

Then, when he arrived at the land of Canaan, he was informed that this was indeed the promised land that the LORD had indicated.

Today's passage records what happened immediately afterward.

So, after finally arriving in Canaan following great faith, what awaited him there?

Immediately after Abram's arrival in Canaan, what met him was neither a "great harvest" nor "peace," but famine---indicated by the Hebrew word רָעָב (ra'ab), meaning severe hunger.

This word appears fourteen times in Genesis and over one hundred times throughout the Old Testament, always linked with trials through which God disciplines His covenant people. Indeed, archeological research has indicated that famines were common at that time.

What is important here is the early dismissal of the simplistic notion that a "blessed land equals a comfortable land."

The Bible does not recklessly romanticize faith. It juxtaposes blessings and adversity, teaching the reader that "blessing does not equal ease." The true essence of God's blessing is not "environmental comfort," but rather "the presence of God." This stage is set precisely so that Abram---and we---might realize this truth.

Moreover, this famine, a physical crisis, also prepares Abram for the next trial. Trials follow one after another. As a result, it becomes a pattern foreshadowing the Exodus, far into the future. This reveals an intricately complex and carefully woven divine plan.

Incidentally, this pattern of famine leading to refuge in Egypt repeats itself with Abraham (chapter 12), Isaac (26:1), and Jacob (chapters 46-47), culminating dramatically in the Joseph narrative. Thus, the rhythm of "trial and salvation" is woven deeply into the Patriarchal narratives of Genesis.

Now, since it is a "trial," we must ask: what exactly was being tested?

Firstly, whether Abram should indeed go to Egypt.

Up until this point, Abram's movements were guided explicitly by the word of the LORD. However, here, the LORD God remains silent. Even though Abram had been told that the promised land was here, when actual inconvenience arose, he left that land to move elsewhere. This would demonstrate weakness in his faith toward God.

Incidentally, Isaac was explicitly commanded, "Do not go down to Egypt; dwell in the land of which I shall tell you" (26:2). Under similar famine conditions, explicit instructions were given to remain.

Ultimately, Abram decided to take his family, livestock, and servants, heading toward the Nile region "to sojourn there for a while."

The Hebrew verb לָגוּר (lagur) conveys meanings like "to temporarily dwell as a foreigner" and "not claiming rights to the land," emphasizing a temporary stay aimed at crisis avoidance rather than permanent settlement.

Certainly, the Nile Delta was known at that time as the "breadbasket" of the Orient, due to regular flooding replenishing the fertile land. Thus, Abram's choice to temporarily relocate there was rational, aimed simply at maintaining life and livestock.

While the Bible does not explicitly label this decision a sin, through the silence of God and the bitter outcome, it portrays Abram's decision in contrast to a perfect expression of faith. Remaining in Canaan, even amid famine, and trusting in God's provision (possibly experiencing miraculous provision like manna in later generations), is suggested as the path of true faith.

Thus, Abram could take bold steps in peaceful times, such as abandoning the old ways. However, he exhibited weakness when confronted with a crisis. This sequence of events was allowed by the LORD precisely to reveal this weakness in Abram.

Gen. 12:11 When he was about to enter Egypt, he said to Sarai his wife, "I know that you are a woman beautiful in appearance."

If we look closely, verses 11--13 repeatedly use verbs related to "seeing" (Abram saw → Egyptians will see → they will see her as beautiful). This persistent emphasis on vision contrasts strikingly with God's invisible intervention later (verse 17, where He afflicts Pharaoh), highlighting a theme of "human sight versus the unseen hand of God."

Abram's words to Sarai here, literally translated, are "See now, please," combining the strong imperative "hinneh," meaning "look," with the gentle request "nāʾ," conveying earnestness.

Thus, Sarai was exceedingly beautiful. But wait---when Abram departed, he was seventy-five years old. How old was Sarai then? Sarai was around sixty-five years old at this time.

There are several theories regarding this, but ancient records and artistic materials indicate that royal women and high priests' wives maintained a "queenly elegance" using perfumed oils, makeup, and wigs. Jewish tradition further idealizes Sarai, claiming she maintained "the beauty of a twenty-year-old even at a hundred." The Patriarchal narratives repeatedly highlight "age-defying beauty," suggesting perhaps a special attractiveness bestowed by God rather than physical conditions alone.

1 Pet. 3:3 Do not let your adorning be external---the braiding of hair and the putting on of gold jewelry, or the clothing you wear---

1 Pet. 3:4 but let your adorning be the hidden person of the heart with the imperishable beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit, which in God's sight is very precious.

1 Pet. 3:5 For this is how the holy women who hoped in God used to adorn themselves, by submitting to their own husbands,

1 Pet. 3:6 as Sarah obeyed Abraham, calling him lord. And you are her children, if you do good and do not fear anything that is frightening.

Though we cannot know for certain, it seems evident that Sarai was indeed an exceptional woman.

Gen. 12:12 and when the Egyptians see you, they will say, "This is his wife." Then they will kill me, but they will let you live.

Gen. 12:13 Say you are my sister, that it may go well with me because of you, and that my life may be spared for your sake.

In ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia, it appears that kings and powerful men sometimes removed the husbands of women they desired, so this was indeed something to fear. Nobody wants to be killed.

These words of Abram seem at first glance to reflect concern for Sarai's safety, but in reality, they put his own preservation first. In the original Hebrew nuance, the contrast "I will be killed, and you will live" strongly emphasizes Abram's fear of being killed. Prioritizing self-preservation to the extent of sacrificing another cannot be said to align with the will of the Lord. The key point here is that Abram seems to improve in the subsequent scene involving Lot, indicating that this trial had its intended effect.

However, to defend Abram slightly, it is possible that his proposal was not entirely deceitful, but rather a "half-truth," reflecting a social practice shown in Nuzi documents (around the 15th century B.C.)---calling a wife "sister" at the time of marriage to provide extended legal protection. Moreover, Abram himself says in Genesis 20:12, "She is indeed my sister, the daughter of my father though not the daughter of my mother," thus making it partly true in terms of blood relationship.

But a deeper question is: why did such difficulties arise in the first place?

Why, upon entering the promised land, did hardships immediately pile up?

This, too, was God's plan.

Here God placed Abram under two forms of pressure: a threat from nature (famine) and a threat from political power (the risk of being killed). Abram was an excellent man of faith, but perhaps his faithfulness was partly due to a stable environment.

Thus, precisely at the moment Abram reached the promised land, God shook his circumstances to further strengthen Abram's faith, so that he would continually look up to the Lord and find peace regardless of the situation.

Given this, Abram ought to have trusted the Lord even here. However, he did not look to God, but instead kept moving according to what was visible, rational, and his own reasoning.

Observing this behavior, Martin Luther said that no human can be righteous before God. Certainly, in this passage, Abram does not reflect the image of an ideal righteous believer.

Gen. 12:14 When Abram entered Egypt, the Egyptians saw that the woman was very beautiful.

Gen. 12:15 And when the princes of Pharaoh saw her, they praised her to Pharaoh. And the woman was taken into Pharaoh's house.

In verse 14, three Hebrew verbs appear consecutively: first ראו "they saw," next הללו "they praised," and finally ותֻקַּח "she was taken." In Hebrew, these verbs give the impression of events accelerating like an avalanche. Moreover, the third verb is in the passive voice, highlighting the structure of Sarai being overwhelmed by forces beyond her control. Thus, we experience in a breathless manner the irreversible flow: "seen, praised, and taken."

"Pharaoh's house" (בַּיִת) was not merely a residence but a complex encompassing royal family, harem, and administration. Women belonging to the harem were guaranteed food, clothing, and precious goods like metals and perfumes. However, they had no freedom and were politically utilized in diplomatic marriages with foreign kings or rewarded to high officials.

While it is deeply troubling that women were treated as mere possessions, it is also true that Sarai's position provided her with high status, exquisite adornments, and honor---things many people in this world desire.

Thus, for women who were inevitably viewed as subordinate at the time, the "praise" and "entrance into the royal court" given to Sarai represented the pinnacle of external success.

In this situation, Sarai became a pawn in the dealings between Pharaoh and Abram.

Gen. 12:16 And for her sake he dealt well with Abram; and he had sheep, oxen, male donkeys, male servants, female servants, female donkeys, and camels.

Sarai was accepted as a candidate for Pharaoh's harem, and Abram was presented with seven kinds of goods---sheep, oxen, male donkeys, male and female servants, female donkeys, and camels. Among these, camels have long been controversial for chronological reasons, but radiocarbon dating has confirmed domestic camel use as far back as the 19th century B.C., lending limited support to patriarchal-period descriptions. At the time, camels were not merely livestock but symbols of technological superiority and trade networks brought by Egypt.

At first glance, Abram appears to gain immense wealth and enjoy smooth sailing. However, the reality is that this prosperity is merely a by-product of deception driven by fear and represents dealings that God does not endorse. The number seven symbolizes completeness or fullness in the Bible, but here, Abram's self-preservation is completely filled with material things obtained from distrust of God and disregard for his wife.

Gen. 12:17  But the LORD afflicted Pharaoh and his house with great plagues because of Sarai, Abram's wife.

Immediately after Sarai was brought into Pharaoh's palace, the narrative takes a sudden turn.

The verb "afflict" נָגַע (naga') used here means "to touch and cause harm," or "to suddenly strike," and is a keyword repeatedly employed in Exodus chapters 7--12, describing the ten plagues. Thus, it can be seen that this event foreshadows the future national-scale deliverance event of the Exodus. From this, it becomes clear that the flow of events here serves as a prototype for the Exodus that Israel will later experience.

Regarding the nature of the plague, rabbinic tradition suggests "skin disease," the Septuagint generally describes it as "a general affliction," and the recent Egyptologist Hoffmeier proposes "infertility throughout the harem." The infertility interpretation parallels the later account in Genesis 20, where God closes the wombs in Abimelech's household.

However, the specific form of the affliction is not of primary importance here. The essential point is that God intervened before Sarai could have sexual contact with Pharaoh. God thus protected Abram, who had taken a misstep in his trial and exhibited "imperfect obedience," preserving the genealogy indispensable to the history of salvation.

In fact, this wife-sister motif is repeated three times. Such repeated use of the "sexual danger toward wife or mother---immediate divine intervention" motif within the patriarchal narratives is because it dramatically and directly visualizes the theological tension involved in protecting the promised "offspring (zeraʿ)," which lies at the core of salvation history.

In other words, this scene clearly reveals Abram's neglect of the divine promise regarding the crucial lineage, the family line connected ultimately to Jesus Christ.

Gen. 12:18  So Pharaoh called Abram and said, "What is this you have done to me? Why did you not tell me that she was your wife?

Gen. 12:19  Why did you say, 'She is my sister,' so that I took her for my wife? Now then, here is your wife; take her, and go."

Having confirmed the connection between the plague and Sarai, Pharaoh investigates her identity and discovers she was married. He immediately summons Abram to the palace. Pharaoh rapidly confronts him with three pointed questions, almost like a judicial interrogation: "What is this you have done to me?" "Why did you not tell me she was your wife?" "Why did you say she is your sister?" Abram, however, cannot utter a single word in response.

It is the moment when the chosen man of faith, Abram, is morally rebuked by a pagan who does not know God. Here, we can see God questioning the faith of a believer through a non-believer.

We also must pay attention to the words of others and continually re-examine our faith. As we go out into society as "salt of the earth," we need to consider what we might be asked by others:

"Why are you dishonest?"

or,

"How can you be so honest?"

It is obvious which of these represents our appropriate image. Therefore, we ought to live in this world, in society, and especially among our beloved families, in a manner resembling Jesus Christ.

Gen. 12:20  And Pharaoh gave men orders concerning him, and they sent him away with his wife and all that he had.

Though this verse is short, two significant verbs conclude the narrative. The first verb, "gave orders" (צִוָּה, tsivvah), shows Pharaoh's final royal judgment. The second verb, "sent away" (וַיְשַׁלְּחוּ, vayeshalechu), shares the root (שלח, shalach) famously used in Exodus, where Moses demands and Pharaoh reluctantly utters, "Let my people go" (šallaḥ ʿammî). From this passage, we can sense the foreshadowing of "expulsion/liberation," reminiscent of the Exodus.

Furthermore, the phrase "with his wife and all that belonged to him" (עִמּוֹ כָּל־אֲשֶׁר־לוֹ) parallels the depiction of Israel leaving Egypt after plundering Egyptian wealth (Exodus 12:36).

This wealth can symbolize "prosperity tainted by sin," and we can interpret it as foreshadowing Israel's temptation in the wilderness, where they crafted the golden calf. Even after the covenant people are freed, their hearts can again become enslaved by unredeemed possessions. As clearly seen later with the Israelites in the wilderness, escaping desires and greed is extremely difficult.

Abram, thus, faces another trial immediately after this one---acquiring wealth.

Indeed, later in chapter 14, Abram fights and wins a battle. At that time, the king of Sodom says to him:

Gen. 14:21  And the king of Sodom said to Abram, "Give me the persons, but take the goods for yourself."

Gen. 14:22  But Abram said to the king of Sodom, "I have lifted my hand to the LORD, God Most High, Possessor of heaven and earth,

Gen. 14:23  that I would not take a thread or a sandal strap or anything that is yours, lest you should say, 'I have made Abram rich.'"

Abram's ability to refuse wealth from the worldly desires of Sodom at this later point can be seen as a result of his training through the trials he faced here.

Gen. 14:21  And the king of Sodom said to Abram, "Give me the persons, but take the goods for yourself."

Gen. 14:22  But Abram said to the king of Sodom, "I have lifted my hand to the LORD, God Most High, Possessor of heaven and earth,

Gen. 14:23  that I would not take a thread or a sandal strap or anything that is yours, lest you should say, 'I have made Abram rich.'"

It is said that Abram's ability to refuse the wealth offered by Sodom, which was covered with worldly desires, was a fruit of the training he had undergone at this time.

Here we too must firmly understand in our hearts that the gifts Abram received were never blessings themselves.

When can we truly say we are richest?

Is it when we have wealth beyond measure, buy good things, eat good food, and are envied by others?

Certainly, the world may call that "being rich."

However, this is merely the world's perspective, which is based only on what is visible.

What the unseen One---God---recommends to us as the way to truly feel rich is to be able to feel abundant even if we possess nothing at all.

This is the ultimate state.

Many people feel satisfied when they have one hundred things.

But if we already feel satisfied at the point of having nothing, then our fulfillment is limitless. It is the ultimate satisfaction.

We must never forget that Jesus is always guiding us to give us this ultimate wealth.

In today's passage, when Sarai was taken into Pharaoh's palace, many poor women likely thought, "I too want to live luxuriously next to the king."

When Abram left Egypt with wonderful gifts, some people may have thought, "Why do they get such good things?"

However, we must remember clearly that all those things were merely shown by God temporarily as training, only to be taken away again. They were never true riches.

Any wealth obtained by improper means is never genuine abundance.

We must thoroughly internalize this way of thinking.

Has our life become centered on "possessing things"?

Has our goal become to look good in the eyes of people?

As long as our goals are mistaken, our lives will never move forward in a straight line, because God seeks to bring us back to the right path.

Many people pursue wealth while offering various explanations and excuses, saying things like, "I can't attend Sunday worship, but the reason is financial."

Indeed, suppose they have accumulated a great fortune.

But is that truly being wealthy?

Even though "the most important thing is peace with God," they lose sight of this and instead cling desperately to worldly wealth, which will eventually collapse. Such is the state they have fallen into.

Certainly, earthly wealth may temporarily appear good.

However, it is extremely fragile.

We must become able to recognize this fragility.

And we must walk with the true strength of following God---"walking together with God."

This is not easy.

Because worldly temptations are extremely intense.

But God guides us.

This guidance might resemble Abram's trial in today's passage. At times, we may feel fear, humiliation, and thus do wrong things.

Yet, all these things connect us to the right path when we repent.

And the key point is "not to be caught up only in what is visible."

Whether something seems reasonable or beautiful, people tend to assume visible things are correct.

However, what is truly right cannot be seen with our eyes, because it belongs to God's realm.

Can God's work be seen by our eyes?

No, it cannot.

Therefore, what we believe will always happen beyond what is visible.

What matters here is whether we can "believe completely" or not.

When Abram reached the promised land, today's events awaited him. They were trials themselves.

However, we must never forget that beyond these trials was God's very love, seeking to guide Abram onto the correct path.

Please believe that God's invisible power is always working upon us, no matter the circumstances.

Let us not be deceived by what is visible.

May we pray to attain the true grace beyond---

not the state of "needing something to feel satisfied,"

but the ultimate peace of "being content under God, even if we possess absolutely nothing."
