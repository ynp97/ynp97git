Genesis 24:29--67 "Rebekah's Decision"

Let us continue reading Genesis today.

What happened up to last week can be seen within the text itself, so let us enter directly into today's passage.

Gen. 24:29 Rebekah had a brother whose name was Laban. Laban ran out toward the man, to the spring.

Gen. 24:30 As soon as he saw the ring and the bracelets on his sister's arms, and heard the words of Rebekah his sister, "Thus the man spoke to me," he went to the man. And behold, he was standing by the camels at the spring.

Gen. 24:31 He said, "Come in, O blessed of the LORD. Why do you stand outside? For I have prepared the house and a place for the camels."

In this scene, Rebekah's brother Laban quickly goes out and brings the guest into the house.

Seeing the ornaments and bracelets on his sister, Laban must have judged that the visitor was not merely a traveler but someone with a significant background.

The expression "O blessed of the LORD" is both a polite greeting and a signal that he recognizes the guest's status and the gravity of the matter.

Offering even a place for the animals shows hospitality and indicates his readiness to formally receive the discussion that is about to take place.

Gen. 24:32 So the man came to the house, and the camels were unloaded. And they gave straw and fodder to the camels, and there was water to wash his feet and the feet of the men who were with him.

Gen. 24:33 Then food was set before him to eat. But he said, "I will not eat until I have said what I have to say." He said, "Speak on."

Entering the house, the camels are unloaded, given straw and fodder, and even water is prepared to wash his feet.

This is "complete hospitality" with attention extending even to the servants who accompanied him.

It is an attitude that respects the other, and through it trust is built.

Meanwhile, the servant says that before receiving the offered food, "I will first tell you my errand."

This is not a breach of courtesy but a deliberate reordering of priorities, making clear from the start that everything is an assignment entrusted by God.

By showing who is truly directing this whole matter, he creates a foundation for focused conversation, preventing the discussion from being carried away only by the atmosphere of hospitality.

Gen. 24:34 He said, "I am Abraham's servant.

Gen. 24:35 The LORD has greatly blessed my master, and he has become great. He has given him flocks and herds, silver and gold, male servants and female servants, camels and donkeys.

Gen. 24:36 And Sarah my master's wife bore a son to my master when she was old, and he has given to him all that he has.

Gen. 24:37 My master made me swear, saying, 'You shall not take a wife for my son from the daughters of the Canaanites, in whose land I dwell,

Gen. 24:38 but you shall go to my father's house and to my clan and take a wife for my son.'

Gen. 24:39 I said to my master, 'Perhaps the woman will not follow me.'

Gen. 24:40 But he said to me, 'The LORD, before whom I have walked, will send his angel with you and prosper your way. You shall take a wife for my son from my clan and from my father's house.

Gen. 24:41 Then you will be free from my oath, when you come to my clan. And if they will not give her to you, you will be free from my oath.'"

The servant identifies himself by saying, "I am Abraham's servant," and lists in detail the blessings that his master received from God.

Flocks and herds, silver and gold, male and female servants, camels and donkeys---he clearly communicates that the foundation of life is more than sufficient.

Furthermore, he states that Isaac, born to Sarah in their old age, is the one to whom the entire inheritance will be given, thus presenting reassurance that the matter of succession is already firmly established.

He then respectfully explains the oath not to take a wife from the daughters of the Canaanites, but to obtain one from Abraham's own clan,

the faith-based reason that the LORD would send His angel to make the journey successful,

and even the release clause that if they refuse, the oath is no longer binding.

Throughout, there is a consistent posture of wishing to proceed according to the LORD's ordered promise and trust.

Gen. 24:42 "I came today to the spring and said, 'O LORD, the God of my master Abraham, if now you are prospering the way that I go,

Gen. 24:43 behold, I am standing by the spring of water. Let the virgin who comes out to draw water, to whom I shall say, "Please give me a little water from your jar to drink,"

Gen. 24:44 and who will say to me, "Drink, and I will draw for your camels also," let her be the woman whom the LORD has appointed for my master's son.'

Gen. 24:45 Before I had finished speaking in my heart, behold, Rebekah came out with her water jar on her shoulder, and she went down to the spring and drew water. I said to her, 'Please let me drink.'

Gen. 24:46 She quickly let down her jar from her shoulder and said, 'Drink, and I will give your camels drink also.' So I drank, and she gave the camels drink also.

Gen. 24:47 Then I asked her, 'Whose daughter are you?' She said, 'The daughter of Bethuel, Nahor's son, whom Milcah bore to him.' So I put the ring on her nose and the bracelets on her arms.

Gen. 24:48 Then I bowed my head and worshiped the LORD and blessed the LORD, the God of my master Abraham, who had led me by the right way to take the daughter of my master's kinsman for his son.

Gen. 24:49 Now then, if you are going to show steadfast love and faithfulness to my master, tell me; and if not, tell me, that I may turn to the right hand or to the left."

The servant recounts the prayer he prayed at the spring just as it was.

"If the young woman not only gives me water to drink but also draws water for the camels,"

then she would be the one appointed by the LORD.

Since watering camels requires considerable labor, this was not leaving things to chance but a realistic sign that reveals consideration, diligence, and generosity through action.

Before he finished praying, Rebekah appeared and acted exactly according to the condition,

and then, upon confirming her lineage, it became clear she was from Abraham's relatives.

All conditions were met, and a wonderful result was shown in answer to prayer.

With this result in view, the servant requests a definite answer---"to the right or to the left."

Indeed, human beings, even when shown a clear result by God, tend to reinterpret it over time and walk in mistaken directions,

so making a firm decision while the matter is still fresh is entirely the right choice.

Gen. 24:50 Then Laban and Bethuel answered, "The thing has come from the LORD; we cannot speak to you bad or good.

Gen. 24:51 Behold, Rebekah is before you; take her and go, and let her be the wife of your master's son, as the LORD has spoken."

Gen. 24:52 When Abraham's servant heard their words, he bowed himself to the earth before the LORD.

Gen. 24:53 And the servant brought out jewelry of silver and of gold, and garments, and gave them to Rebekah. He also gave to her brother and to her mother costly ornaments.

Laban and her father Bethuel acknowledge, "This has come from the LORD," and proceed to consent.

By placing the basis of agreement in the will of God, their words carry a religious weight that restrains any later reversal.

The servant immediately bows to the ground and worships, attributing success not to his own achievement but to God's grace.

Then he gives ornaments and garments to Rebekah, and precious items to her brother and mother.

This consists of both the primary gifts to the bride herself (a form of bridal wealth) and customary gifts to the family.

It expresses sincerity that includes both heart and expense, providing a warm foundation for the relationship.

Gen. 24:54 And he and the men who were with him ate and drank, and they spent the night there. When they arose in the morning, he said, "Send me away to my master."

Gen. 24:55 Her brother and her mother said, "Let the young woman remain with us a while, at least ten days; after that she may go."

Gen. 24:56 But he said to them, "Do not delay me, since the LORD has prospered my way. Send me away that I may go to my master."

Gen. 24:57 They said, "Let us call the young woman and ask her."

Gen. 24:58 And they called Rebekah and said to her, "Will you go with this man?" She said, "I will go."

The group stays for the night, and in the morning the servant requests immediate departure.

The family asks that she remain "about ten days," but the servant responds,

"Since the LORD has prospered my way, do not delay me."

The more one waits after a successful event, the more room emerges for changing terms or wavering hearts; therefore, he presses forward now.

Here the final decision is entrusted to Rebekah herself.

When asked, "Will you go with this man?" Rebekah answers, "I will go."

The family's consent and her own voluntary choice align, showing that this is not forced but a free and willing decision.

It depicts divine guidance and human free response not in tension, but in cooperation.

Gen. 24:59 So they sent away Rebekah their sister and her nurse, and Abraham's servant and his men.

Gen. 24:60 And they blessed Rebekah and said to her,

"Our sister, may you become

thousands of ten thousands,

and may your offspring possess

the gate of those who hate him!"

Gen. 24:61 Then Rebekah and her young women arose and rode on the camels and followed the man. Thus the servant took Rebekah and went his way.

Rebekah, along with her nurse, is sent away.

A nurse accompanying her indicates high status and readiness to establish a household immediately in the new land.

Their blessing---"may your offspring possess the gate of those who hate him"---echoes the Abrahamic oath,

publicly affirming Rebekah's entrance into the covenant story.

The caravan heads south.

This is not merely a relocation.

It is the theological movement of "the bride entering the promised land," embodied in an actual geographical journey.

Gen. 24:62 Now Isaac had returned from Beer-lahai-roi and was dwelling in the Negeb.

Gen. 24:63 And Isaac went out to meditate in the field toward evening. And he lifted up his eyes and saw, and behold, there were camels coming.

The scene shifts to Canaan. Isaac was living in the region of Beer-lahai-roi in the Negeb.

Gen. 16:13 So she called the name of the LORD who spoke to her, "You are a God of seeing," for she said, "Truly here I have seen him who looks after me."

Gen. 16:14 Therefore the well was called Beer-lahai-roi; it lies between Kadesh and Bered.

Gen. 16:15 And Hagar bore Abram a son, and Abram called the name of his son, whom Hagar bore, Ishmael.

Gen. 16:16 Abram was eighty-six years old when Hagar bore Ishmael to Abram.

The meaning of this place is that God is shown to be watching over this marriage,

and that this marriage---in which the bloodline prepared by God, leading ultimately to Jesus Christ, is being established---

will in the end be connected even to the salvation of those who appear forsaken, like Hagar.

It suggests an inclusio.

In the evening Isaac goes into the field peacefully.

Thus we see that the heir Isaac "does not return" to his original homeland, just as Abraham commanded.

It is the servant and the bride who move; the center of the household remains in the promised land.

This pattern is fully preserved.

Human preparation (waiting) and God's timing (the arrival of the caravan) converge,

showing that things are being arranged within divine providence.

Gen. 24:64 And Rebekah lifted up her eyes, and when she saw Isaac, she dismounted from the camel

Gen. 24:65 and said to the servant, "Who is that man, walking in the field to meet us?" The servant said, "It is my master." So she took her veil and covered herself.

Gen. 24:66 And the servant told Isaac all the things that he had done.

When Rebekah sees the caravan approaching, she dismounts and asks the servant, "Who is that man?"

Learning that the man is her bridegroom Isaac, she covers herself with a veil.

This act expresses modesty before marriage and the acceptance of her new status.

The servant reports everything he has done to Isaac.

Everything happened according to the promise, the sign of the prayer was fulfilled, and both the family and the woman had given consent.

Sharing this experience in full would have strengthened the new couple's trust in God.

Gen. 24:67 Then Isaac brought her into the tent of Sarah his mother and took Rebekah, and she became his wife, and he loved her. So Isaac was comforted after his mother's death.

The passage ends with a warm picture. Isaac brings Rebekah into the tent of his mother Sarah, takes her as his wife, and loves her.

The empty tent is filled again, and the center of the household begins to be restored.

"Isaac was comforted after his mother's death."

Just as the cave of Machpelah established physically the "staking of the land,"

here the rebuilding of the household begins in the heart.

With both pillars---child (lineage) and land (settlement)---established, the story is passed to the next chapter.

Overall, God prepares the way first, and humans respond freely.

The story moves forward not by force but by promise, trust, and careful steps, inviting the reader to walk likewise.

So today, I would like to look at this theme, "The Decision of Rebekah."

Rebekah makes an immediate decision. And she acts immediately.

This is the pattern taken by many people in Scripture.

For example, Peter and the others immediately left their nets---the means of income in this world---and followed Him.

Mary also was able to say to the angel, "Behold, I am the servant of the Lord; let it be to me according to your word," offering herself.

This form of immediate action appears many times.

Abraham, who is at the center of this story, is the same,

and we may say that this becomes the basic pattern of the actions of the people who appear in the Bible.

Then why is this "immediate decision" emphasized in Scripture?

It is because the root of this immediate action is precisely **faith**.

And "to believe" is the very target point Scripture leads us to.

Why is it that those who believe can decide immediately?

It is because they believe that there is no mistake in the direction God points.

They believe this fully.

And because walking in that direction leads to blessing.

They have that as their lived experience.

We also have such experiences.

When we are troubled about "What should I do about the problem before me now?",

God shows us the answer through the Holy Spirit.

Through Scripture He tells us, "God thinks this way. Jesus Christ thinks this way.

Then where should I go?"

We must have felt that before.

And if we feel that, then we should immediately go in that direction.

When we come into conflict with someone, should we focus even more on the faults of that person?

Should we keep fighting and cling to victory?

Or should we make ourselves flexible and draw near to the other?

Which is right is obvious.

If that is so, then we must do that.

Because that is the path that leads to the best place.

But people are weak, and we tend to walk toward the answer that suits our convenience.

"I know God says this, but God doesn't really know my situation. This other way is better..."

Even though Scripture speaks truth, we end up making decisions that---indirectly---say,

"Jesus doesn't really know about me."

That is the human heart.

Unless we completely believe, we cannot do it.

And we must walk in the direction God points.

Sometimes we cannot see ahead on that road and we become anxious.

Indeed, to those who do not believe in God, the future is a darkness they cannot see.

But if we believe in God, then at any time we must surely be able to feel

that there is a place where the sunlight shines beautifully,

a place filled with abundant grace.

Because the God who loves us leads us there precisely because He loves us.

If we truly believe that Jesus loves us, everything becomes necessary---inevitable.

God's desire is to lead us to what is good; therefore we have this present situation.

This is what we must understand.

Scripture does not describe what kind of faith Rebekah personally had,

but through all that happened, she must have known that the LORD was working.

And at that moment, believing in the power of the LORD and obeying His word,

she made the completely correct decision.

That is the decision of Rebekah.

One more important matter in today's passage.

This marriage resulted in a wonderful outcome. We can see that it is filled with happiness and blessing.

And this result all began with "obeying the Lord."

Because they obeyed the word of the Lord with the best they could offer as human beings,

a conclusion far beyond human imagination,

the best possible result, was given.

This is something we must also apply to our own lives.

When we do something in this world, there are many choices.

There are "good things" written in good books,

and "wonderful things" said on YouTube.

So many things surround us today.

Sometimes that information feels as sweet as honey.

"It certainly feels like this would be better."

This modern world is filled with such thoughts.

In contrast, the method Scripture shows is sometimes bitter, heavy, and a choice that opposes this world.

And from the viewpoint of family members who are still on the world's side,

it may even look like something wrong.

But if we are given the conviction that this is "right before God,"

and we walk in that way,

then there will be a wonderful result.

We have a conclusion we desire.

The method God points to may look like walking in the opposite direction.

But if we obey God with all our heart,

then in fact we are given a blessing far greater than the conclusion we wanted---

a higher, superior blessing.

We should believe this.

For example, the matter of raising children.

Parents want to raise their children well. That is natural.

And for that, they must live properly in society.

Then, through school events and such,

they must adapt their children to this world.

"If we do that, then happiness will come to the children," we think.

And so, we send our children into the things of this world.

But if we ignore the words of Jesus,

"Let the children come to Me,"

then in fact we are distancing them from our very desire---

our desire to give them true happiness.

We may think we are walking straight,

but we may be running at full speed in the opposite direction---

while holding the children's hands.

But if we believe the word of God

and continue to bring our children to the Lord,

then in their hearts,

they are given the most important things for living in this society:

"a heart that considers others,"

"a heart to live with love,"

"a heart to have hope for all of life."

In fact, that gives far more real advantage for living in the world.

Because the values the world teaches---

trying to become average, trying to become more advantageous than someone else,

joy over earning one point but ruled by negativity over not earning one hundred---

children are dyed into those values.

Before their hearts are colored by a society that continually teaches such methods,

children who already know:

"I am truly loved,"

"I am wonderful as I am,"

"Love is the foundation of this world, and it creates everything,"

and therefore,

"Before forcing myself upon others, I can understand others."

Which of these children will be loved?

Which of these will receive true blessing?

It is obvious.

In other words, as a result of obeying Scripture,

the very thing that had originally been our goal---

good social outcomes---

is also given.

**Matthew 6:33 (ESV)**

"But seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness, and all these things will be added to you."

The way of Scripture may look steep.

It may often oppose the ways of this world.

But the direction is actually the same.

The conclusion we desire---

"to be loved," "to be happy," "to live a good life"---

is given in a better way on the path of following the Lord.

We must remember this.

God loves us,

and because He desires to lead us and our families to a wonderful place,

He does all things.

We must first believe this and follow.

And we must walk with hope in every moment.

What God gives is wonderful.

As we see in today's passage,

there were other ways this marriage could have been arranged---

perhaps a way that looked more economically prosperous,

perhaps a way of marriage that would make living in the region easier.

But by following God,

their home became fully established with trust in God at its center,

and a wonderful family was formed.

There is no happiness greater than this.

Let us receive Jesus Christ as the center of our lives,

and let us be formed into a people who build households that worship the Lord.
