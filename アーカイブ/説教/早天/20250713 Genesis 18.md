20250713 Genesis 18:1--18:15 "Sarah Laughed."

Let us read Genesis again today.

Abram obeyed the word of the Lord and set out from his long-familiar homeland, without being told where he was going. This was an exceedingly great step, symbolizing his faith and obedience.

Over the course of more than twenty-five years, in chapter 12, he was promised, "I will make of you a great nation," and in chapter 15, he was made to look up at the stars and was sworn to, "So shall your offspring be."

Moreover, through the ancient covenant ceremony of cutting animals in two, the Lord Himself, in the form of a smoking fire pot, passed through and gave a unilateral and certain guarantee.

However, even after ten more years, the couple still had no child. In chapter 16, Sarah, using the surrogate custom that was common at the time, had her maidservant Hagar bear a child, and Ishmael was born.

This human decision brought tension into the household, but the Lord's plan remained unchanged. In chapter 17, Abram was renamed Abraham, meaning "father of a multitude of nations," and Sarai was renamed Sarah, who would become "mother of kings of peoples." Circumcision was imposed as a sign of the everlasting covenant.

Then, Abraham and his household showed their obedience by receiving circumcision on that very day.

What follows is today's passage.

Gen. 18:1 And the Lord appeared to him by the oaks of Mamre, as he sat at the door of his tent in the heat of the day.

There are three views regarding the timing of the Lord's appearance.

Jewish oral tradition tells the "third day" theory, saying the Lord came to comfort Abraham when the pain of circumcision reached its peak on the third day.

Scholars who read the narrative as a continuous story adopt the "same day" theory, seeing the appearance of the Lord immediately following the circumcision passage, and find in Abraham's hospitality, even amid pain, a model of faith and love for neighbor.

In either case, immediately after the completion of the blood covenant of circumcision, the Lord came in the form of three guests and shared a meal with Abraham. This resembles the "covenant meal" that follows the conclusion of a treaty.

In the ancient Near East, the parties confirmed reconciliation and loyalty by eating the same food together.

Therefore, the depiction of the Lord taking milk and meat like a human is a sign that the Lord Himself descends into the life of the covenant partner and guarantees blessing.

The promise of Isaac's birth is also declared in the midst of this intimate meal, where covenant, fellowship, and promise are united.

As for the location, the oaks of Mamre (Hebrew: 'êlônê Mamrē) has no established site, but there are three candidate locations north of Hebron. Among them, the great tree of Harni Mamra is called "Abraham's Oak," and is a pilgrimage site for Jews, Christians, and Muslims.

It seems that there was a custom in Canaanite times of viewing evergreen trees as sacred.

Thus, there is a possibility that the Bible portrays this scene as the personal God revealing Himself amid the scenery of an ancient culture that worshiped natural objects.

Abraham welcomed the three travelers in the heat of the day, and later it is revealed that they were the Lord and His angels.

The idea of "protecting and welcoming strangers" was shared not only by Israel but also by surrounding nomadic peoples, and continues today in Bedouin society as "Diyafa."

The Jewish law also commands, "Love the sojourner," and the New Testament book of Hebrews chapter 13 says that some "have entertained angels unawares" by welcoming guests.

Matt. 25:34 Then the King will say to those on his right, "Come, you who are blessed by my Father, inherit the kingdom prepared for you from the foundation of the world.

Matt. 25:35 For I was hungry and you gave me food, I was thirsty and you gave me drink, I was a stranger and you welcomed me,

Matt. 25:36 I was naked and you clothed me, I was sick and you visited me, I was in prison and you came to me."

Matt. 25:37 Then the righteous will answer him, saying, "Lord, when did we see you hungry and feed you, or thirsty and give you drink?

Matt. 25:38 And when did we see you a stranger and welcome you, or naked and clothe you?

Matt. 25:39 And when did we see you sick or in prison and visit you?"

Matt. 25:40 And the King will answer them, "Truly, I say to you, as you did it to one of the least of these my brothers, you did it to me."

We are called to reflect the loving nature of God as love for our neighbor.

"He was sitting at the door of his tent in the heat of the day." → Theophany in the midst of weakness

This is interpreted as the time shortly after Abraham and his household received circumcision, around noon, when the pain was most intense.

In other words, he was in physical distress---both in pain and in heat---during the time of day when he was at his weakest, and yet, though the reason is not stated, he sat at the entrance of the tent, in the sun.

This shows that it was a time of great difficulty.

Here, a pattern is presented: "The Lord visits in the midst of human helplessness."

This forms a prototype for what Paul later says in 2 Corinthians 12:9, "For my power is made perfect in weakness."

Furthermore, this passage can be seen as emphasizing revelation through contrast in time.

Noon is not typically a time associated with visions; God's appearances and dreams are usually connected with the night.

By specifically stating "in the heat of the day," the text highlights that God appeared in the most unexpected time, in a situation thought most impossible for visions or theophany.

This leads into the central message that follows: "Is anything too hard for the Lord?"

Gen. 18:2 He lifted up his eyes and looked, and behold, three men were standing in front of him. When he saw them, he ran from the tent door to meet them and bowed himself to the earth.

Gen. 18:3 and said, "O Lord, if I have found favor in your sight, do not pass by your servant."

Whether Abraham recognized at the moment of greeting that these three were divine has been a topic of long-standing debate.

Those who affirm it argue that the term used in verse 3, "O Lord" ('ădōnāy), is the pronunciation form used for the divine name, and they also point to the shift in the conversation to singular in verse 10 as evidence that Abraham recognized Him as the Lord from the beginning.

Rabbinic tradition also interprets this as Abraham discerning angels through spiritual insight.

On the other hand, those who deny it highlight the plain description in verse 2, "three men," and emphasize that offering water for foot washing and a meal were customary courtesies toward ordinary guests, arguing that Abraham showed hospitality according to custom, not knowing they were divine.

Hebrews 13:2 is cited to support this view, saying some "have entertained angels unawares."

Some commentators take a middle position: Abraham first saw them as travelers, but came to realize they were divine beings through the dignity of their words or prophetic speech.

Moreover, interpretations throughout history diverge on how to understand these three men.

Ancient Christian Church Fathers, such as Augustine, saw this as a foreshadowing of the Trinity, interpreting the three men as one God in three persons.

In contrast, rabbinic literature explains that the three had separate missions and were angels, while God Himself was only one.

Contemporary Jewish apologists also strongly argue that viewing all three as divine contradicts the biblical premise of monotheism.

Thus, the same text yields significantly different readings depending on the faith tradition---whether it points to the Trinity or simply to angels.

The important point is that Scripture intentionally leaves ambiguity, and ultimately emphasizes the theological message that unconditional hospitality brings about an encounter with God.

Gen. 18:4 Let a little water be brought, and wash your feet, and rest yourselves under the tree,

Gen. 18:5 while I bring a morsel of bread, that you may refresh yourselves, and after that you may pass on---since you have come to your servant." So they said, "Do as you have said."

In verses 3 through 5, Abraham offers first water, then "a morsel of bread" (פַּת־לֶחֶם morsel of bread), but in actuality, the hospitality unfolds into a lavish feast.

This "under-promise---over-deliver" is a rhetorical humility also seen in ancient Assyrian hospitality texts:

"In the eyes of someone like you, even this will be too little."

This scene may also be interpreted theologically as illustrating the idea that "the giver has already been given to"---that one is merely returning to God what has already been received.

Gen. 18:6 And Abraham went quickly into the tent to Sarah and said, "Quick! Three seahs of fine flour! Knead it, and make cakes."

Gen. 18:7 And Abraham ran to the herd and took a calf, tender and good, and gave it to a young man, who prepared it quickly.

Gen. 18:8 Then he took curds and milk and the calf that he had prepared, and set it before them. And he stood by them under the tree while they ate.

Abraham's hospitality to his guests lavishly reflects the hospitality culture of the ancient Near East.

The "three seahs" of flour would yield over twenty liters---enough to feed more than ten people in an era when a single portion was around 0.3 seah---signifying a feast-level provision rather than a light snack.

Moreover, it is specified as "fine flour," indicating the best reserves.

He then selects a "tender calf" from the herd and has a young servant prepare it quickly. Beef was a valuable asset to nomads, and this menu shows the guests were treated as royalty.

Adding curds and milk makes the meal a complete set that offers both nourishment and refreshment in the desert climate.

The combination of dairy and meat reflects pre-kashrut customs, before later prohibitions based on "You shall not boil a young goat in its mother's milk."

As oral law became codified, strict separation of dairy and meat---including the use of separate cookware---became established as normative Jewish practice.

The scene in which Abraham himself serves under the tree shows that by serving on the front lines, the master gives his guests complete assurance.

When the head of the household personally brings food and stands by the guest, it is the highest expression of welcome that says, "I fully guarantee the safety of this place."

As a result, the guest can lay down their weapons and relax both in body and mind. This is a model of nomadic hospitality.

Gen. 18:9 They said to him, "Where is Sarah your wife?" And he said, "She is in the tent."

Gen. 18:10 The Lord said, "I will surely return to you about this time next year, and Sarah your wife shall have a son." And Sarah was listening at the tent door behind him.

The guests, referred to in the plural, first express familiarity with the human question, "Where is Sarah your wife?" Then suddenly one of them is spotlighted and declares in the first person, "I will surely return to you next year, and Sarah shall have a son."

In Hebrew, the verb "to return" is emphasized by a double use of the future perfect, stressing the absolute certainty of fulfillment.

Moreover, the flow of speech that had been in the third-person plural suddenly shifts to the singular "I," prompting the reader to intuit that "this speaker is the Lord Himself."

Thus, a scene that had seemed like ordinary conversation among travelers instantly transforms into a divine declaration of promise.

Gen. 18:11 Now Abraham and Sarah were old, advanced in years. The way of women had ceased to be with Sarah.

In fact, Abraham was said to be 99 years old, and Sarah was 85. The Bible explicitly states that "the way of women had ceased to be with Sarah."

In other words, as we saw last week, Abraham's age of 99 would have been seen, even by the standards of the time, as a point at which reproductive capability was entirely lost.

And for Sarah as well, it was a stage at which pregnancy was absolutely impossible from a physical standpoint.

Here, Scripture emphasizes the physical limitations of both of them.

Gen. 18:12 So Sarah laughed to herself, saying, "After I am worn out, and my lord is old, shall I have pleasure?"

The Lord spoke to Abraham, saying that Sarah would bear a son this time next year,

but Sarah, from behind the tent, heard these words and muttered them while laughing to herself.

First to note is the phrase, "Sarah laughed to herself" (וַתִּצְחַק שָׂרָה בְּקִרְבָּהּ).

The Hebrew word "צָחַק (tsaḥaq)" means "to laugh," but in this context, it includes a complex inner reaction of "surprise," "confusion," "disbelief," and even "sarcasm."

In other words, Sarah's laughter was not joyful but rather a kind of bitter chuckle at the absurdity, an involuntary reaction like, "That's impossible."

Moreover, the phrase "בְּקִרְבָּהּ (beqirbah)" means "within her," and should be translated as "in her heart," "secretly," or "as a private thought."

Thus, Sarah did not laugh out loud but reflected inwardly on the gap between her own condition and the Lord's words, feeling it was utterly impossible.

The first half of Sarah's statement---"After I am worn out, shall I have pleasure?"---in the original Hebrew is "אַחֲרֵי בְלֹתִי הָיְתָה־לִּי עֶדְנָה (acharei veloti hayetah-li ednah)."

The word "בְלֹתִי (veloti)" is derived from a verb meaning "to be worn out" or "decayed," carrying the nuance of "I, who am worn and wasted with age."

Sarah perceives herself as someone who has finished her role as a woman, someone who has completely ceased to be a vessel for giving life.

The word translated "pleasure" (עֶדְנָה ednah) is also very important.

It shares the same root as "Eden," the garden of delight, and here, many commentators interpret it as including "sexual pleasure," "womanly joy," or "the hope of childbearing."

That is, Sarah acknowledges that she has long lost the joy of being a woman,

and based on that, she concludes that the promise of a child is, in reality, impossible.

"And my lord is old" is expressed in the original as "וַאדֹנִי זָקֵן (va'adoni zaken)."

By using the respectful term "adoni (my lord)" to refer to Abraham,

this phrase is quoted in 1 Peter 3:6 as a sign of Sarah's respect for her husband Abraham.

However, by clearly stating "zaken" (old),

she also acknowledges that Abraham, too, no longer has the capacity for sexuality or reproduction.

This short verse expresses Sarah's deep sense of reality, self-awareness, and her bewilderment at God's word.

In a situation where believing might lead to pain,

Sarah involuntarily "laughed."

It was not an insult toward God's word,

but a thoroughly realistic and honest reaction from someone faced with human limitations.

In response to this reaction from Sarah, the next verse (verse 13) shows God responding, revealing the biblical truth that "God knows even the thoughts of the heart."

And instead of rebuking the laughter, God replies, "Is anything too hard for the Lord?"

and leads Sarah into faith. Later, Sarah truly bears a son and names him Isaac---"he laughs."

This was the sign that her laugh of disbelief had been turned into a laugh of joy through the grace of God.

Gen. 18:13 The Lord said to Abraham, "Why did Sarah laugh and say, 'Shall I indeed bear a child, now that I am old?'

Gen. 18:14 Is anything too hard for the Lord? At the appointed time I will return to you, about this time next year, and Sarah shall have a son."

In Genesis 18:13--14, the Lord addresses Abraham regarding Sarah's laughter in her heart.

He says, "Why did Sarah laugh and say, 'Shall I indeed bear a child, now that I am old?'"

This is a solemn rebuke of Sarah's doubt,

and at the same time, an introduction to the certainty of God's promise.

The Lord's following words, "Is anything too hard for the Lord?" are written in Hebrew as "הֲיִפָּלֵא מֵיְהוָה דָּבָר (ha-yippālē' me-YHWH dābār)," and the verb "יִפָּלֵא (yippālē'),"

means "to be too wonderful," "too high," "separated," or "beyond reach."

In other words, it is a declaration that there is nothing too unreachable, too distant, or impossible for God.

This expression is repeatedly used throughout the Old Testament.

Thus, the word "yippālē'" contains not just the surprise of a miracle, but

the certainty and sovereignty of God's promise---"If God has spoken, it will certainly be fulfilled."

Furthermore, this expression echoes in the New Testament:

Luke 1:34 And Mary said to the angel, "How will this be, since I am a virgin?"

Luke 1:35 And the angel answered her, "The Holy Spirit will come upon you, and the power of the Most High will overshadow you; therefore the child to be born will be called holy---the Son of God.

Luke 1:36 And behold, your relative Elizabeth in her old age has also conceived a son, and this is the sixth month with her who was called barren.

Luke 1:37 For nothing will be impossible with God."

Luke 1:38 And Mary said, "Behold, I am the servant of the Lord; let it be to me according to your word." And the angel departed from her.

This part clearly reflects the wording of verse 14, highlighting the continuity between the Old and New Testaments.

Sarah's unbelief symbolizes our human tendency to be bound by experiential reality.

However, the core of faith shown here is that God is the one who fulfills His promises beyond the limits of that reality.

God's promises are not influenced by human ability or circumstances,

but are realized through His own power and will.

This event becomes a foreshadowing of God's redemptive plan that will be fully accomplished in the birth of Jesus Christ,

and leads us to the confession of faith that "nothing is impossible for God."

Gen. 18:15 But Sarah denied it, saying, "I did not laugh," for she was afraid. He said, "No, but you did laugh."

The interaction between Sarah and the Lord in verse 15 is deeply symbolic and theologically profound.

Sarah denies her inner reaction---her laughter. Saying, "I did not laugh,"

was likely because she did not want to admit her unbelief toward God's word or the agitation of her heart before Him.

But the Lord immediately responds, "No, but you did laugh," cutting off the conversation.

Within this exchange lies the tension and power of truth in the relationship between God and humanity.

At the heart of this dialogue is the Hebrew expression "כִּי צָחָקְתְּ (ki ṣāḥāqt),"

which directly translates as "Indeed, you did laugh,"

a strong affirmation from God's side, presenting the truth unavoidably.

This mode of speech is a manifestation of God's educational intervention---gently but unmistakably revealing human self-deception.

To Sarah, the laughter may have been a momentary emotion, a small whisper in her heart.

Yet God did not overlook even that fleeting moment, and brings her face to face with it.

I do not think Sarah was mocking God.

Rather, it was that she could not fully believe something she had not yet seen.

And in this regard, I believe not a single one of us can point fingers or rebuke her.

Indeed, as faith matures, one becomes less skeptical about many things.

Still, if we look deep, deep, deep into our hearts, there is no doubt that questions and anxieties remain---"But will it really be okay?" "Why not yet?" "When will it happen?" and so on.

What the Lord is doing here is drawing attention even to those small stirrings.

We tend to brush it off, thinking, "I just happened to feel that way for a moment, so it's not a big deal,"

and we go on living that way. But God does not approve of that.

Why is that so? It is because when we feel doubt about God's ability, that very place contains the key to faith.

Then, why is that the key?

It is because God's power truly works. God is the One who is faithful and truly exists.

He is not a fantasy nor a figment of human cognitive projection.

If that is the case, how will our doubt about God manifest in our reality? It will appear in the form of that doubt being overturned.

Why? Because God loves us and is trying with all His might to protect us.

Those who think, "That's not true," should try to look back over their lives. How often have we feared? How often have we worried? --- Even so, we are here now, receiving worship and offering praise together. In other words, our doubts have already been sufficiently overturned across time.

But, sadly, human beings can sometimes think of those resolutions as something "natural" or "coincidental,"

and not as the work of God's power.

Therefore, rather, when we have doubts, it is good to keep them firmly in our hearts.

By doing so, we begin to realize, "Ah, the very fact that I'm alive like this now means that God's power was working all along."

Of course, the same is true for prayer. It is precisely because we prayed that we recognize that the prayer was answered.

And this is something that only the one who prayed can truly know.

The one who was prayed for may not even notice it.

But the one who prayed knows: "I definitely prayed."

That repetition strengthens faith.

So we don't need to fear the things we feel doubtful about---

even the ones we think, "No way, not something like this."

Even the boldest doubts we have, we must face them.

I want you to remember that.

Indeed, sometimes the answer comes back in a form different from what we expected.

For example, in this case, the promise to Sarah was "within the next year"---a relatively short time.

But actually, there had been thirteen years before that.

It was because of those thirteen years that doubt was born.

And in the following year, that doubt was overturned.

This sense of time may visit us as well. We don't know.

But, if when something happens, we can say, "Ah, God moved,"

then that is a wonderful thing.

Because that connects directly to faith.

So then, what is faith?

Faith is the power by which we can live with the belief that "God is working."

Faith is power.

The key to gaining that power begins with honestly looking at the doubts we now carry.

I want you to remember that.

And here, Sarah "laughed."

This "laughter" is by no means something praiseworthy.

Yet, even so, by laughing---and then facing that laughter---

it later becomes "true laughter."

It becomes genuine joy.

We come to sense that the failures of the past are what have produced today's success.

In other words, the self who gave a cynical laugh at that time---

that very self is later affirmed.

Yes, it was a failure.

To put it extremely, Sarah mocked God in that moment.

But even so, because of what happened afterward,

she could say, "Ah, I can believe in God."

Because she could come to the conclusion, "Now I truly believe from my heart,"

all the past becomes "good" in the end.

I want us to remember that.

And I want us to move toward being able to actually live it out.

Certainly, hard things are hard. Painful things are painful.

There are times when our desires are not fulfilled.

But even in the midst of that, we must face our hearts firmly.

We must offer those doubts honestly before God.

Especially like Sarah, if God were to speak directly to us, we might say,

"Ah, I see now."

But I think there are times when that doesn't happen.

If so, then we should see this Scripture itself as giving us that opportunity now.

We ought to think that way.

So let us look at the doubts and difficult problems we currently carry---problems in our churches, in our individual lives.

Questions may arise such as, "Can this really not be done?" "Is it truly impossible?"

Let us face those things first---and then discover.

Discover the problem points.

And bring them to God in prayer.

By doing so, we will ultimately be able to gain "the power to believe."

Let us walk forward, trusting in that.
