**July 6, 2025 --- Genesis 17:15--27 "Abraham Laughed"**

Let us read Genesis again today.

Continuing from last week, this is taking place thirteen years after the birth of Ishmael.

At this point, the covenant is presented anew.

First, God appeared as the Almighty and renamed Abram to Abraham. Then, He promised the inheritance of many nations and the everlasting possession of Canaan, commanded circumcision on the eighth day for every male in his household as a sign of the covenant, and declared that anyone who refused it would be cut off from the people.

This regulation of circumcision made it possible for Abraham's descendants to automatically pass on their relationship as God's people to the next generation.

Immediately following these words is today's passage.

Truly, it would be easier to understand if we read all of it at once, but because it seemed like it would become lengthy, we have divided it into two parts.

Gen. 17:15  And God said to Abraham, "As for Sarai your wife, you shall not call her name Sarai, but Sarah shall be her name.

Gen. 17:16  I will bless her, and moreover, I will give you a son by her. I will bless her, and she shall become nations; kings of peoples shall come from her."

"Sarai" in Hebrew means "my princess," with the "-ai" suffix denoting "my."

In other words, it carries the nuance of "princess of my household." In contrast, "Sarah," with the suffix removed, simply means "princess."

The limitation is removed, and it becomes evident through her new name that she will bring blessing not only to her family but to the world.

And to this Sarah, a blessing is promised. It is repeated twice---

This is a classical rhetorical device that expresses a definitive will,

meaning that this is a word of God declaring that He will absolutely do it.

And what is that blessing? It is that Sarah will conceive and give birth.

Gen. 17:17  Then Abraham fell on his face and laughed and said to himself, "Shall a child be born to a man who is a hundred years old? Shall Sarah, who is ninety years old, bear a child?"

Now, this is Abraham's reaction to the word of God.

Abraham laughed at the word of God and questioned it.

This is a scene that could even be taken as if he snorted in disbelief and mocked God.

So first, what kind of nuance does this word "laughed" carry?

The Hebrew word *tsachaq* is a verb that covers all kinds of laughter,

ranging from the joy of celebration (21:6) to mockery (43:32), and its meaning is determined by context.

So what is the context here?

It is a response to God's declaration that something humanly impossible will happen.

Since Abraham, immediately after laughing, questions God's word,

it does not seem to be a particularly positive kind of laughter.

However, theologically, this is sometimes described as "the coexistence of faith and doubt" (Kidner).

In simple terms,

"Could this really happen?" --- surprise,

"But God is Almighty" --- trust,

"Yet I'm ninety-nine" --- realism---

these all erupted at once as a kind of "complex laughter," which is the general interpretation.

And the words that were spoken at the moment he was tilted toward the negative in that inner turmoil were:

Gen. 17:18  And Abraham said to God, "Oh that Ishmael might live before you!"

This is in response to God's astonishing, unbelievable word---something that was not visible before his eyes.

For Abraham, Ishmael is right there before him:

someone he can tangibly touch, a beloved son with whom he has shared thirteen years, a wonderful blessing.

The contrast is very stark.

It is understandable that he would naturally focus on what was in front of him.

Likely, rather than disbelieving God,

Abraham added his own interpretation to God's word and responded accordingly.

Here is God's word to Abraham:

Gen. 17:19  God said, "No, but Sarah your wife shall bear you a son, and you shall call his name Isaac. I will establish my covenant with him as an everlasting covenant for his offspring after him.

Here, God clearly denies Abraham's words. And He says that the impossible---Sarah giving birth---will happen, and that this is, in fact, the main flow of all things.

Abraham's feelings at this time are not recorded in Scripture,

but we can imagine that there must have been great confusion.

To have the God he believed in deny something he believed God had given---

this must have been very difficult to accept.

Was he happy, or was he not happy? It may be that he could not even tell at that moment.

To such an Abraham, the word of the Lord comes.

Gen. 17:20  As for Ishmael, I have heard you; behold, I have blessed him and will make him fruitful and multiply him greatly. He shall father twelve princes, and I will make him into a great nation.

Here, God declares to Abraham that He will bless Ishmael's life. At first glance, the content of this blessing appears not much different from what is given to Abraham himself or to Isaac. In fact, for Abraham, this blessing being granted to his son must have been a great assurance---enough to make him feel satisfied.

The prosperity of descendants was considered a complete and excellent blessing for a patriarch of that time. However, upon closer inspection, there is a significant difference between this and the covenant that was given to Abraham just prior.

Gen. 17:1  When Abram was ninety-nine years old the LORD appeared to Abram and said to him, "I am God Almighty; walk before me, and be blameless,

Gen. 17:2  that I may make my covenant between me and you, and may multiply you greatly."

Gen. 17:3  Then Abram fell on his face. And God said to him,

Up to this point, one could say it is the same. However,

Gen. 17:4  "Behold, my covenant is with you, and you shall be the father of a multitude of nations.

Gen. 17:5  No longer shall your name be called Abram, but your name shall be Abraham, for I have made you the father of a multitude of nations.

Gen. 17:6  I will make you exceedingly fruitful, and I will make you into nations, and kings shall come from you.

Gen. 17:7  And I will establish my covenant between me and you and your offspring after you throughout their generations for an everlasting covenant, to be God to you and to your offspring after you.

Gen. 17:8  And I will give to you and to your offspring after you the land of your sojournings, all the land of Canaan, for an everlasting possession, and I will be their God."

In other words, Abraham is not only made into a "great nation," but is declared to be "the father of many nations" and the head of a royal lineage that will rule over all.

On the other hand, from Ishmael's perspective, he is positioned as one tribe within this great stream of Abraham's lineage.

This difference is clearly a large one in terms of the scale and purpose of the blessing.

What happened to Ishmael afterward?

So then, what became of Abraham's wish that "Ishmael might live before You"? God did not reject this wish, but gave Ishmael the blessing that "twelve princes shall come from him." The number twelve, which parallels the twelve tribes of Israel, was a symbolic number in the ancient Near East representing a complete tribal structure. In reality, the descendants of Ishmael formed nomadic tribes in northern Arabia, their names appearing in Assyrian inscriptions, and by the 4th century BC, they had established the Nabataean kingdom centered in Petra.

However, God clearly stated, "I will establish my covenant with Isaac." This is what Wenham refers to as the "hierarchy of election," and according to Moltmann, while God's love is universal, the history of salvation advances through a specific lineage. Election is not for exclusion, but for service. The chosen are entrusted with the mission of drawing even the unchosen into blessing.

In Islamic tradition, Ishmael settled in Mecca with his mother Hagar, and under God's guidance, the well of Zamzam sprang forth. Later, with his father Abraham, he is said to have rebuilt the Kaaba. From his descendants came the Quraysh tribe, and eventually the prophet Muhammad emerged. The genealogy of Ishmael became the foundation for the legitimacy of the Arab people, and even today, the Saudi royal family claims descent from it. In the pilgrimage (Hajj), the ritual reenactment of Hagar and Ishmael's search for water continues, making him a symbolic figure linking faith and culture even now.

Indeed, the Arab nations regarded as descended from Ishmael, particularly the Gulf states like Saudi Arabia,

have built vast wealth backed by abundant oil reserves, wielding enormous national economic power and influence.

It seems as though the blessing "I will make him into a great nation" has been realized in geopolitical and economic terms.

Meanwhile, what about the Jewish people, descendants of Isaac?

Although they were scattered over a long history, they established global networks in fields like commerce, finance, and entertainment,

coming to hold intellectual, cultural, and economic influence.

It is a well-known fact that Jewish individuals and organizations wield immense power in modern financial markets, the film industry, music, and media.

It could be said that they have struck oil in the human heart's field of desire and now hold sway over it.

In this way, the Ishmaelite Arab world holds the material oil fields---"energy"---

while the Isaac-descended Jewish world holds the "oil fields of human desire."

These two axes of power form a major underlying structure in modern society,

and this is a profoundly fascinating reality.

Why does God insist on Sarah?

Why must the child be born through Sarah?

The reason God strongly insisted that the child must be born through Sarah

was not merely a matter of family rank or bloodline, but something deeply tied to the very history of salvation.

Ishmael, born between Abraham and Hagar, was the product of their own human solution.

God, in order to make clear that His promise is not established by human effort but by grace,

intentionally connected it through Sarah---who was old and barren.

Through this event, it was made evident to all that the covenant is not given through manipulation of lineage or merit,

but purely by the grace of God.

Rom. 9:7  And not all are children of Abraham because they are his offspring, but "Through Isaac shall your offspring be named."

Rom. 9:8  This means that it is not the children of the flesh who are the children of God, but the children of the promise are counted as offspring.

Furthermore, the event in which life came from Sarah's womb---considered as "as good as dead"---was a testimony to the creative power of God once again being at work.

Just as in the beginning of creation, life was given through the breath of God,

this powerfully testifies that what is impossible for man is possible with God.

This pattern reappears later in the case of Elizabeth, the mother of John the Baptist,

and ultimately in the virgin Mary,

repeated throughout Scripture as a mode of divine intervention that transcends human limitations.

This covenant, demonstrated through the "impossible event" of the barren woman Sarah conceiving and giving birth, clearly reveals God's power and sovereignty.

And what this event points forward to---needless to say---is Jesus Christ.

The atonement of sin through the birth, crucifixion, and resurrection of Jesus

is grounded upon the absolute work of God's plan, and that foundation is laid here.

Of course, God could have made the child of Hagar the heir to the covenant.

But human beings are prone to misunderstanding.

That is why God showed His plan in a way that is visible and undeniable to all,

so that everyone must acknowledge, "This is the power of God."

When something happens that is impossible by common sense,

we perceive God's power all the more clearly.

In that sense, this divine approach can be said to have been exceedingly effective for us humans.

And by working through Sarah,

the covenant became not a matter of human calculation but of divine grace,

God's creative sovereignty was made evident,

and the theological framework of "election and blessing" that runs through salvation history was established.

Gen. 17:22  When he had finished talking with him, God went up from Abraham.

Thus ends the word of God that had continued from last week, and God went up from Abraham.

It is not clear in what form or by what manner this occurred,

but Abraham must have perceived it as an ascending into heaven.

One would like to experience such a thing someday.

Gen. 17:23  Then Abraham took Ishmael his son and all those born in his house or bought with his money, every male among the men of Abraham's house, and he circumcised the flesh of their foreskins that very day, as God had said to him.

Gen. 17:24  Abraham was ninety-nine years old when he was circumcised in the flesh of his foreskin.

Gen. 17:25  And Ishmael his son was thirteen years old when he was circumcised in the flesh of his foreskin.

Gen. 17:26  That very day Abraham and his son Ishmael were circumcised.

"On that very day," Abraham, at ninety-nine years old, received circumcision.

By stepping into this act of physical pain himself, the promise was transformed from something merely understood in the mind into a memory engraved in the flesh.

So first, how many people received circumcision that day?

When Lot was rescued earlier, there were 318 trained men.

Based on that number, it is said there may have been around seven hundred.

We can see that "that day" was a very significant event.

Because Abraham obediently fulfilled his role as leader,

they and their families received the sign and were welcomed as the people of God.

Now about the two ages---each carries great significance.

Ninety-nine years old --- renewal at the endpoint of human possibility

Extreme age: Even in the ancient Near East, ninety-nine was regarded as the age at which reproductive ability was fully exhausted.

God intentionally marked the flesh with the sign of the covenant at this timing,

making Abraham a symbol of "new creation" at the point where fleshly potential reached zero.

On the threshold of a hundred: In Jewish tradition, "one hundred" is the number of completion, and ninety-nine is one step short.

Abraham, realizing that by his own effort he could not reach one hundred, becomes one who waits for the "one" that God supplies.

Thirteen years old --- the heir standing at the point of self-choice

Turning point of adolescence: In later Jewish society, thirteen was the age of Bar Mitzvah (bearing religious responsibility),

and already in ancient Semitic culture it was recognized as the age of decision-making capacity.

That Ishmael received circumcision at this age suggests not merely his father's command,

but a "conscious participation" with his own consent.

In fact, within Ishmael's blessing, there is a phrase not included in Abraham's covenant.

That phrase is: "I will be your God and the God of your descendants after you."

However, by receiving circumcision here, he and his tribe were able to become the people of God.

Thus, in consideration of all these elements, this timing of circumcision can be said to be the best possible one.

This may well be a scene where we catch a glimpse of the delicate touch of the God who guides the destiny of His people.

Now then, let us conclude by looking at the faith of Abraham and applying it to ourselves.

Gen. 17:18  And Abraham said to God, "Oh that Ishmael might live before you!"

This statement is by no means the words of one who does not believe in God.

However, it can be said that it was spoken as a result of interpreting God's word in his own way,

being unable to fully believe in the promise that a child would be given to Sarah.

We too, like Abraham, though we have faith, may find our hearts captured by the situations we see before our eyes.

It is not that we lack faith---rather, because we believe, we tend to say, "This must be God's guidance,"

and we end up matching our expectations and hopes to the events of reality.

Also, we often end up distorting and misapplying the true meaning of the Word to fit our own situations.

It is important to apply the Word of God to ourselves.

Because the words of Scripture are not someone else's concern but should be grasped as our own.

So, this balance is a difficult one. That is why we are given the opportunity in worship to hear the Word spoken---

that is, to encounter the Word as spoken from a perspective other than our own.

We tend to jump to conclusions when something good happens, thinking, "Now I'm safe. Everything will go well."

Or, when something bad happens, we think, "It's over. Everything has collapsed."

Even though God's plan is already over us!

Why?

This kind of perception often arises because we focus too much on a single event,

and treat that event's end as the conclusion of everything.

However, God's plan is far greater, deeper, and more complex than the individual moments we can see.

Rather than hastily deciding whether a single event is "good" or "bad,"

what we need is the perspective of how it will be used within God's plan.

Our lives and each of our problems are never complete in and of themselves,

but should be seen within the grand event of life itself---

and even more, they are embedded within the entire history of humanity's salvation.

Therefore, we ought not to be swayed emotionally by our present circumstances,

but rather, as we continue to trust in God to the very end of life,

we should wait for the true intent of God to be revealed at last.

Even if we are currently facing hardship,

when we believe that God's plan has a purpose

and that He will turn all things to good,

we will be able to follow Him in peace.

Perhaps the most difficult time is when a path that seemed to be going well is suddenly closed.

When everything seemed to be going right, and then it is suddenly lost,

we are deeply confused.

Even though we, as believers, trusted that it was a blessing given by God.

But it is precisely in such times that we must believe

that God's guidance is leading us toward a higher plan.

We must not make final judgments based on the "good" or "bad" things before our eyes,

but walk trusting in the Lord,

who uses all of our journey to bring about His glory and salvation.

All things are within God's plan.

And that plan is of the kind that turns all things for good at a timing we cannot understand.

To live believing this completely---this is what is required of us.

And when we respond in this way, it is evident that wonderful grace is prepared there.

Let us believe in God. That means not being captured by the events before our eyes,

but believing in Jesus Christ, who has already used His life to make atonement for us.

And to believe in the fact that we are saved by that,

and that even the trials we face can be overcome---

this is the wonderful grace that has been given to us.

Let our hearts fully receive this,

and may we be prepared to remember the power of God, especially in the difficult moments of life.
