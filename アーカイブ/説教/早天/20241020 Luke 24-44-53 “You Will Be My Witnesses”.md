**20241020 Luke 24:44-53 “You Will Be My Witnesses”**

Let us read from the Gospel of Luke once again.

**Luke 24:44**

Then He said to them, “These are My words that I spoke to you while I was still with you, that everything written about Me in the Law of Moses, the Prophets, and the Psalms must be fulfilled.”

Today, we come to the conclusion of the Gospel of Luke. Following this, Luke will continue with the Acts of the Apostles. Even in that account, we will see how the things written in the Law of Moses, the Prophets, and the Psalms are fulfilled through Jesus Christ and the apostles. Naturally, the fulfillment of God’s promises—specifically, the fulfillment of the Old Testament prophecies—is of great importance. Beginning with Pentecost, many events occur in the Acts of the Apostles, and each time, Old Testament passages are cited as proof of those events.

Based on Jesus’ words in **Luke 24:44**, here is a list of Messianic prophecies from the “Law of Moses, the Prophets, and the Psalms” found in the Old Testament.

**1. Messianic Prophecies in the Law of Moses (Torah)**

**Genesis 3:15**

“I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and her offspring; he shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel.”

This refers to the “offspring of the woman,” foretelling the Messiah’s confrontation with Satan (the serpent).

**Genesis 12:3**

“All the families of the earth shall be blessed through you (Abraham).”

This prophecy speaks of the blessing that will come to all the nations through the Messiah, a descendant of Abraham.

**Deuteronomy 18:15**

“The Lord your God will raise up for you a prophet like me from among you, from your brothers—it is to him you shall listen.”

This “prophet” is later applied to the Messiah, Jesus.

**2. Messianic Prophecies in the Prophets**

**Isaiah 7:14**

“Therefore the Lord himself will give you a sign. Behold, the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel.”

This prophecy points to the virgin birth of Jesus.

**Isaiah 9:6-7**

“For to us a child is born, to us a son is given; and the government shall be upon his shoulder, and his name shall be called Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace.”

This describes the birth of the Messiah and His titles.

**Isaiah 53:3-5**

“He was despised and rejected by men, a man of sorrows and acquainted with grief… But he was wounded for our transgressions; he was crushed for our iniquities; upon him was the chastisement that brought us peace, and with his stripes we are healed.”

The entire chapter portrays the suffering and redemption of the Messiah as a Suffering Servant, matching the events of Jesus’ crucifixion.

**Micah 5:2**

“But you, O Bethlehem Ephrathah, who are too little to be among the clans of Judah, from you shall come forth for me one who is to be ruler in Israel.”

This foretells the birth of the Messiah in Bethlehem.

**Zechariah 9:9**

“Rejoice greatly, O daughter of Zion\! Shout aloud, O daughter of Jerusalem\! Behold, your king is coming to you; righteous and having salvation is he, humble and mounted on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey.”

This matches the scene of Jesus’ triumphal entry into Jerusalem.

**Zechariah 12:10**

“When they look on me, on him whom they have pierced, they shall mourn for him, as one mourns for an only child.”

A prophecy about the piercing of the Messiah, corresponding to the crucifixion of Jesus.

**3. Messianic Prophecies in the Psalms**

**Psalm 2:7**

“The Lord said to me, ‘You are my Son; today I have begotten you.’”

This speaks to the Messiah fulfilling the role of the Son of God.

**Psalm 22:1**

“My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?”

These words, which Jesus cried out on the cross, are seen as a prophecy of the suffering Messiah.

**Psalm 22:16**

“For dogs encompass me; a company of evildoers encircles me; they have pierced my hands and feet.”

This describes the suffering Jesus endured on the cross.

**Psalm 16:10**

“For you will not abandon my soul to Sheol, or let your holy one see corruption.”

This hints at the resurrection of the Messiah.

**Hosea 6:2**

“After two days he will revive us; on the third day he will raise us up, that we may live before him.”

Here, the phrase “raise us up on the third day” is seen as a symbolic reference to the resurrection of the Messiah.

**Psalm 110:1**

“The Lord says to my Lord: ‘Sit at my right hand, until I make your enemies your footstool.’”

This foretells the ascension of Jesus and His position at God’s right hand.

**Summary**

As Jesus stated in **Luke 24:44**, “everything written about Me in the Law of Moses, the Prophets, and the Psalms must be fulfilled,” these prophecies were fulfilled in Jesus’ life, suffering, resurrection, and ascension. Understanding these scriptures within the context of the Old Testament and relating them to Jesus’ works in the New Testament was a crucial theological theme in the early church.

The understanding of truth revealed through the Old Testament might be a bit difficult for us to grasp today. However, for the believers of that time—devout Jews who read and memorized the scriptures to the extent of creating oral traditions—they would have likely experienced a sense of realization, saying, “Indeed, this is true\! This man, Jesus, is truly the Messiah\! He is exactly the person spoken of in the scriptures we were taught since childhood.” The full revelation of Jesus could not have been completely understood simply by reading backward from the New Testament, but His life and actions brought a sense of shock and deep realization to the Jewish community of that time.

**Luke 24:45**

“Then He opened their minds to understand the Scriptures.”

What occurred here is that Jesus enabled them to understand the Scriptures. Although this might seem to contradict the earlier statement about understanding the Scriptures, the truth is that the immense nature of what was happening was overwhelming. It was like a sudden, vast image appearing before them, and they couldn’t focus clearly. Jesus, knowing this, brought clarity, improving their vision, so to speak, so they could see clearly. How did He do this specifically? The answer is found in the Gospel of John.

**John 20:22**

“And when he had said this, he breathed on them and said to them, ‘Receive the Holy Spirit.’”

What is the Holy Spirit?

**John 14:26**

“But the Helper, the Holy Spirit, whom the Father will send in my name, he will teach you all things and bring to your remembrance all that I have said to you.”

Receiving the Holy Spirit means that their hearts were opened, and they could clearly understand Jesus’ perspective. Their previously blurry vision became clear through the Holy Spirit.

*Note: The giving of the Holy Spirit in John is a complex theological issue. Scholars debate how this event relates to Pentecost. Did the outpouring of the Spirit happen here, or was this a preparatory act, with the full outpouring at Pentecost? These are various interpretations.*

Personally, I believe that the giving of the Spirit to the apostles happened here, opening their hearts to understand the Scriptures. They then approached Pentecost with this understanding, and through the Spirit-filled words at Pentecost, many believers received the Holy Spirit.

**Luke 24:46-47**

“And said to them, ‘Thus it is written, that the Christ should suffer and on the third day rise from the dead, and that repentance for the forgiveness of sins should be proclaimed in his name to all nations, beginning from Jerusalem.’”

What was it that they needed to understand with their opened minds? It was repentance and forgiveness.

Were the disciples, who constantly failed, about to be severely rebuked here? Indeed, Jesus’ teachings were strict, and they were not always easy to follow. However, it is essential to remember that the call to repentance is not about condemning and judging them for not living up to the teachings. To know sin is, in other words, to know the right and good way to live. Such a way brings good things in life, and repentance is about turning in that direction. Even if there are sins, they have already been atoned for by Jesus.

Thus, it is repentance for the sake of forgiveness, a necessary step for peace.

When the disciples, who were Jews, heard this, they likely found the phrase “to all nations” to be uncomfortable. Originally, this was included in the promise to Abraham, so it should not have been surprising. However, in their time, there was a prevalent notion that Gentiles were impure.

Therefore, Jesus needed to ensure that the disciples, who would become apostles, fully understood the fulfillment of the Old Testament promises.

**Luke 24:48**

“You are witnesses of these things.”

**Luke 24:49**

“And behold, I am sending the promise of my Father upon you. But stay in the city until you are clothed with power from on high.”

This can be seen as a prelude to Pentecost. At Pentecost, they would be given the ability to speak many languages to proclaim the gospel, establishing the foundation of the church as a base for mission work, and then they would set out into the world as apostles.

**Luke 24:50**

“Then he led them out as far as Bethany, and lifting up his hands he blessed them.”

The name “Bethany” is derived from meanings like “House of Affliction” or “House of Poverty,” but it is also symbolically connected to healing, resurrection, and rest. In Jesus’ ministry, Bethany played a special role, being the place where Lazarus was raised, where Mary anointed Jesus with oil, and where Jesus gave His final blessing before His ascension. It stands as a spiritually significant location.

**Luke 24:51-53**

“While he blessed them, he parted from them and was carried up into heaven. And they worshiped him and returned to Jerusalem with great joy, and were continually in the temple blessing God.”

These verses are further detailed in the Book of Acts, so let’s look at them together.

**Acts 1:6-11**

“So when they had come together, they asked him, ‘Lord, will you at this time restore the kingdom to Israel?’ He said to them, ‘It is not for you to know times or seasons that the Father has fixed by his own authority. But you will receive power when the Holy Spirit has come upon you, and you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the end of the earth.’ And when he had said these things, as they were looking on, he was lifted up, and a cloud took him out of their sight. And while they were gazing into heaven as he went, behold, two men stood by them in white robes, and said, ‘Men of Galilee, why do you stand looking into heaven? This Jesus, who was taken up from you into heaven, will come in the same way as you saw him go into heaven.’”

Firstly, this passage has strong connections with eschatology. It aligns with the prophecy in the Book of Daniel.

**Daniel 7:13**

“I saw in the night visions, and behold, with the clouds of heaven there came one like a son of man, and he came to the Ancient of Days and was presented before him.”

Verse 9 says, “He was lifted up, and a cloud took him out of their sight.” The angels then say that He will return in the same way (verse 11). Indeed, this matches the imagery in Daniel. This entire sequence of the ascension can be seen as a preview of the fulfillment of the end times.

The disciples, having their hearts opened to understand that the Law, the Prophets, and the Psalms were fulfilled in Jesus Christ, now had even greater assurance in the fulfillment of the end times prophesied in the Old Testament, including the Book of Daniel.

That’s why the apostles say in Acts 3:21:

“whom heaven must receive until the time for restoring all the things about which God spoke by the mouth of his holy prophets long ago.”

They understood that the next time Jesus would come would be at the time of the renewal of all things—namely, the end times. This was made clear to them by the angels.

Belief in the resurrection and ascension of Jesus is also a belief in the events of the end times. And why does Jesus call us to repentance? It is for forgiveness. The purpose of that forgiveness is so that we can be with Him when the end comes, showing God’s love for us.

What we must always remember is that what God tells us, although sometimes it may seem strict, is actually all for our benefit. God’s love is reflected in the many words left for us. Faith is not for someone else but for ourselves. Let’s keep this in mind as we read the Word.

Now, let’s focus on the angels’ words: “Why do you stand looking into heaven?”

The angels, two young men in white robes, perhaps the same ones who were at the tomb after the resurrection, appear again. They always appear briefly, say a few words, and then disappear without any detailed explanation, which makes one curious.

But what do their words mean? First, they reflect the attitude of the apostles at that moment. They were looking up at the sky, staring in awe as Jesus was taken up. Naturally, they were amazed, perhaps thinking, “Wow\!” But\!

The important point was that Jesus would not return until the end times, His second coming. They didn’t yet grasp that this was final until the end.

They may have thought, “Maybe He’ll come back for dinner and tell us another great story,” which wouldn’t have been surprising. Jesus had risen from the dead and returned, so the apostles could have believed that He would always come back. Forty days of such occurrences might lead to such thinking.

However, the angels’ words were, “Why do you stand looking into heaven?”

The angels often provide explanations after Jesus has departed. Just as they did at the empty tomb, they prompted the disciples to move forward with the next step.

They were essentially saying, “It’s time for you to act\!” God has already given you plenty of evidence, His Word, and prepared your hearts. They were yet to receive the ultimate gift of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost, but Jesus would no longer be visibly among them.

They were to be His witnesses to “Jerusalem, Judea, Samaria, and to the ends of the earth.” This was something they would have to do on their own.

The apostles might have hoped to walk with Jesus to the ends of the earth, dining with Him, sometimes being rebuked, always walking by His side. But they needed to understand that until the end times, the day of final judgment, that would not happen again. Now, they had to take action, moving forward by themselves. Their training was complete, and the time had come for the real mission. It was daunting, but they had to move forward.

This concept of evangelizing by their own efforts is one of the mysteries of Christianity. Why does God not just do everything? We would be happy if we could always be with God. But reality includes daily misunderstandings and persecution. It feels frustrating\!

The reason is not explicitly given, but what we know is that in the end, when God’s kingdom is fully established, we will always live in joy with God. That is clearly stated. We must move toward completing God’s kingdom, and part of that journey involves our efforts to get there.

The act of evangelizing, of bearing witness to Jesus, also strengthens our own faith. When you explain to others, you need to understand it yourself. Modern education emphasizes the importance of output, and explaining something helps clarify your understanding. Since Jesus Christ is the truth, the more you examine it, the more your conviction deepens.

For a convincing witness, mere words aren’t enough. You must live a holy life, examining where your actions align with Jesus’ teachings and what steps you need to take. What should guide your actions—the ways of the world, or the way written in the Bible? Thoughtful, intentional living is necessary.

The primary purpose of being a witness is to tell others about Jesus’ goodness, but walking that path also deepens your own faith.

Jesus often emphasized personal action. Many of His miracles involved individuals taking a step themselves: “Go, wash,” “Get up, take your mat,” and so forth.

People often say that Christianity relies on God, but choosing to entrust everything to Jesus is a decision you make. The critical point is whether you can let go of yourself. If you can do that, God will be with you. He will protect you in perfect ways, though:

“You are not to know times or seasons that the Father has fixed by his own authority.”

We may not understand the timing or methods, but let’s trust that the solution will come.

However, if our hearts are full of sin—meaning, full of self-righteousness—then we may obstruct the work of the Holy Spirit ourselves. This is why repentance is absolutely necessary. We need to decide to entrust our lives to Jesus. To be protected by Jesus, we must first work on our own hearts. This is something only we can do.

Repent, recognize your sins, and continue to align yourself with the right path. By doing this, we allow the Holy Spirit to fill us with grace and joy.

**Luke 24:48**

“You are witnesses of these things.”

Let us pray that we can bear witness to Jesus throughout our God-given lives, trusting in Him daily and finding joy in our walk of faith.
