**20250504 Genesis 13:1-18 "Abram and Lot"**

Today, let's continue reading Genesis.

Last week, we saw the scene where Abram and his group arrived at the Promised Land, but famine struck, and they went down to Egypt. There, Abram called his wife his sister, causing Sarai to be taken as Pharaoh's wife. At that time, Pharaoh's palace was struck by God, leading Abram and his group to leave Egypt. This is the portion we saw last week.

Gen. 13:1  So Abram went up from Egypt, he and his wife and all that he had, and Lot with him, into the Negeb.

Gen. 13:2  Now Abram was very rich in livestock, in silver, and in gold.

Abram, having left Egypt, was very rich in livestock, silver, and gold. This passage clearly portrays this fact. Furthermore, this wealth was something added to his original possessions through the events in Egypt.

Gen. 12:5  And Abram took Sarai his wife, and Lot his brother's son, and all their possessions that they had gathered, and the people that they had acquired in Haran, and they set out to go to the land of Canaan. When they came to the land of Canaan,

Gen. 12:16  And for her sake he dealt well with Abram; and he had sheep, oxen, male donkeys, male servants, female servants, female donkeys, and camels.

Here, we must be careful to note that this wealth was simultaneously a blessing from God and also a test from God.

The phrase "very rich," literally translated, means "very heavy." Thus, it conveys both material abundance and a sense of "burden."

We live in a modern society where we easily fall under the illusion that "if we only have money, anything is possible." However, that is a great mistake, and from a standpoint of faith, it should clearly be understood as "one of the trials."

This "wealth as a trial" was also given to the Israelites during the Exodus. However, they spectacularly failed this trial. Exodus 32 describes the details of this failure clearly.

Ex. 32:1  When the people saw that Moses delayed to come down from the mountain, the people gathered themselves together to Aaron and said to him, "Up, make us gods who shall go before us. As for this Moses, the man who brought us up out of the land of Egypt, we do not know what has become of him."

Ex. 32:2  So Aaron said to them, "Take off the rings of gold that are in the ears of your wives, your sons, and your daughters, and bring them to me."

Ex. 32:3  So all the people took off the rings of gold that were in their ears and brought them to Aaron.

Ex. 32:4  And he received the gold from their hand and fashioned it with a graving tool and made a golden calf. And they said, "These are your gods, O Israel, who brought you up out of the land of Egypt!"

Ex. 32:5  When Aaron saw this, he built an altar before it. And Aaron made a proclamation and said, "Tomorrow shall be a feast to the LORD."

Ex. 32:6  And they rose up early the next day and offered burnt offerings and brought peace offerings. And the people sat down to eat and drink and rose up to play.

This passage is very terrifying to those who faithfully read the Bible and believe in God. Yet, this is exactly what human beings do.

We especially must note that the "gold" they used was originally a gift from God, yet it was used as material to create an idol. God's blessing had turned into a tool for human corruption without anyone realizing it.

Indeed, the feeling of "wealth" or "having possessions" is a tricky domain for us, because the standards we hold naturally change as we gain more. This makes it very difficult for us to notice our own shifts clearly. Without realizing it, these blessings might dangerously turn into idols.

Therefore, especially when we are blessed with abundance, we must examine our hearts more deeply. We must acknowledge that it is a blessing and at the same time a trial, and continually turn our eyes toward the Lord.

When examining ourselves, the important point is to shift our perspective from our own viewpoint and strive to see ourselves from God's perspective. When we look from our own perspective, no matter how closely we examine ourselves, we find ourselves filled with our own logic and our own excuses, making real discoveries very difficult.

We become trapped in defensive strategies we ourselves have constructed, preventing us from uncovering truths that are uncomfortable for us to accept, thus making it impossible to discover anything genuinely valuable.

However, once we shift our viewpoint and attempt to view ourselves from God's perspective, we can start to discover even those traps we've set up to deceive ourselves.

And then, pushing through these barriers, we come to realize, "Ah, this is how I was deceiving myself, this is how I was trying to defend myself." As we identify these things, we begin to find the most crucial truths about ourselves---whether those truths indicate a good or bad state depends on each individual---but we begin to see clearly how we stand before God.

Let us become capable of doing this.

Wealth itself is not wrong, but regarding that wealth, the important point is how we relate to it. Let us make every effort to re-examine ourselves from God's perspective.

Gen. 13:3 And he journeyed on from the Negeb as far as Bethel to the place where his tent had been at the beginning, between Bethel and Ai,

Gen. 13:4 to the place where he had made an altar at the first. And there Abram called upon the name of the LORD.

Abram continued his journey from the Negeb to Bethel, and finally returned to the place where he had previously pitched his tent, that is, the place between Bethel and Ai. That was the very place where he had built an altar to the Lord before, and there Abram called upon the name of the Lord once again.

Within these two short verses, a very deep spiritual meaning is contained.

First, the place Abram headed toward was not merely an ordinary location. It was the very place where, upon entering Canaan by faith, he first built an altar; in other words, the "starting point of his faith," where he began his fellowship with the Lord. Abram had once gone down to Egypt, and although he increased his wealth there, he could not be said to have experienced peace. In the matter concerning his wife Sarai, he was filled with anxiety and fear, even momentarily departing from God's will.

However, now he traveled once again and returned. This was not just a geographical move; it was a spiritual return, something that could be called a resetting of his faith. He once again called upon the name of the Lord.

Here we clearly see a characteristic of Abram's faith. He did not remain stuck in past failures or confusion. He returned before God, and stood again at the original place of worship. This is something truly wonderful.

In our lives too, there are times when our faith dulls or we feel distant from God. This can happen not only during times when things are going poorly but perhaps even more so during times of wealth and success, when our hearts drift away from God. At such times, what is required of us, just as with Abram, is to return once again to the place of the altar.

Moreover, the heart contained in this action is precisely the essence of worship. Abram's return was for the purpose of "calling upon the name of the Lord." Worship is not merely a religious activity, but an act of restoring our relationship with God and confessing Him once again as Lord. Abram, having gone through his experience in Egypt, would have keenly felt this necessity. Thus, he set his heart firmly once again and turned his face toward the Lord.

From this we receive the message that, in our journey of faith, "we can always return." God desires us to return once again to the altar. Thus, whenever we call upon the name of the Lord, God will surely hear us.

On our earthly journey, we will pass through many different places. There will be times of prosperity and times of adversity. Yet the most important thing is to continue restoring our relationship with the Lord no matter where we are. In other words, it is to continually worship. Abram demonstrated this through his own life.

Therefore, like Abram, let us also continue to worship. Let us call upon the name of the Lord, renew our faith, and realign our walk. The Lord will surely respond to that intention and make straight the path we walk.

Gen. 13:5 And Lot, who went with Abram, also had flocks and herds and tents,

Gen. 13:6 so that the land could not support both of them dwelling together; for their possessions were so great that they could not dwell together,

Gen. 13:7 and there was strife between the herdsmen of Abram's livestock and the herdsmen of Lot's livestock. At that time the Canaanites and the Perizzites were dwelling in the land.

Now, in today's passage, the person named Lot comes into focus.

Lot was the son of Abram's brother Haran; he was Abram's nephew. He left Ur of the Chaldeans with Abram, journeying toward the land of Canaan. Even after Abram's father Terah remained in Haran, when Abram departed in obedience to the word of the Lord, Lot accompanied him. From this, we understand that Lot was very close to Abram, and they journeyed together, following the Lord's guidance.

After they entered the land of Canaan, a famine arose, and Abram temporarily went down to Egypt, and at that time as well, Lot stayed with him. When Abram departed Egypt and went up to the Negeb, Lot also remained with him.

However, because of their increased possessions, it became difficult for them to continue living together. The phrase "the land could not support them both" indicates a shortage of resources such as wells and pasture.

Gen. 13:8 Then Abram said to Lot, "Let there be no strife between you and me, and between your herdsmen and my herdsmen, for we are kinsmen.

Gen. 13:9 Is not the whole land before you? Separate yourself from me. If you take the left hand, then I will go to the right, or if you take the right hand, then I will go to the left."

Faced with this situation, Abram proposed to Lot, "Choose whichever direction you wish to go." By doing this, they would divide the resources of the land and each could provide for their own livestock and people.

Here, we can say we see a significant change in Abram.

Previously, when entering Egypt, what did he say to his wife Sarai? He said something like, "Please say you are my sister, that my life may be spared," showing that his thinking was fundamentally self-centered. However, here with Lot, he says, "Do as you desire; I will be fine afterward." In other words, he shows a stance of concession, essentially saying, "We will manage with whatever remains." This change of attitude is something we must remember.

Indeed, this scene should be taken as evidence that Abram repented before God for his previous failure. Although the Bible does not explicitly record Abram's internal thought processes, making this somewhat difficult to discern clearly, assuming this repentance occurred allows us to understand the subsequent story more smoothly.

Throughout the Old Testament, there repeatedly appears the motif that things proceed better when people yield to one another or do something for the sake of others.

Furthermore, it is fundamental to biblical teaching, and something we well understand, that improvement in our relationships with others is the true foundation of happiness.

The fact that Abram could now naturally act in this manner shows that through his failure in Egypt, he had grown spiritually before God.

Gen. 13:10 And Lot lifted up his eyes and saw that the Jordan Valley was well watered everywhere like the garden of the LORD, like the land of Egypt, in the direction of Zoar. (This was before the LORD destroyed Sodom and Gomorrah.)

Gen. 13:11 So Lot chose for himself all the Jordan Valley, and Lot journeyed east. Thus they separated from each other.

Gen. 13:12 Abram settled in the land of Canaan, while Lot settled among the cities of the valley and moved his tent as far as Sodom.

Gen. 13:13 Now the men of Sodom were wicked, great sinners against the LORD.

In response to Abram's suggestion, Lot chose the Jordan Valley.

Low areas have water and are well-watered. In other words, it was also a place where life was easy.

However, located there was a city named Sodom.

The city of Sodom appears in the story of Genesis as one of the cities that received the most severe judgment. Indeed, when Lot lifted up his eyes and saw the Jordan Valley, it was a well-watered place, like the garden of the Lord. However, located there, the cities of Sodom and Gomorrah were exceedingly sinful before the Lord.

Their sin was not merely one kind, but multifaceted, and the entire society had fallen into profound corruption that rotted it from within.

Genesis chapter nineteen describes a scene where two angels visited Lot's house. At that time, the men of the city as a whole unitedly attempted to perpetrate violent sexual humiliation upon the guests. We cannot even imagine how one would desire to do such a thing, but it reveals that the city itself had become thoroughly corrupted and had collapsed morally and spiritually.

Additionally, Ezekiel chapter sixteen records the sin of Sodom as follows: "She was proud, had excess of food, and prosperous ease, but did not aid the poor and needy." That is, Sodom's sin was not merely sexual perversion, but also arrogance, luxury, and indifference toward the socially weak---attitudes that were detestable before God. Despite enjoying wealth, they showed cold-heartedness by not caring for people in trouble. This coldness could not withstand God's righteousness, and eventually, fire of judgment descended from heaven.

Ezek. 16:49 Behold, this was the guilt of your sister Sodom: she and her daughters had pride, excess of food, and prosperous ease, but did not aid the poor and needy.

Ezek. 16:50 They were haughty and did an abomination before me. So I removed them, when I saw it.

Such specific images of sin are detailed further in the oral Jewish traditions---the Talmud and the Midrash. For example, according to the Midrash, in Sodom, it was forbidden to welcome travelers. The city's laws even reportedly included rules like, "Whoever gives bread to a hungry person shall be burned alive." Another story tells of someone who gave bread to a stranger and was judged, tortured, and put to death. They built a society in which kindness toward others was illegal, not allowing even mutual assistance.

Another tradition speaks of how the people of Sodom had fallen into extreme individualism and selfishness. If someone attempted to host a poor person, that household was plundered by townspeople and punished. Furthermore, there is a cruel tradition where the women of Sodom gave a small piece of bread to a poor traveler who came to draw water, were caught, and punished by being stung continuously by bees until death. These stories indicate that their wickedness had become institutionalized, embedding evil deeply within their laws and culture.

In the pseudepigraphal Book of Jubilees, Sodom's sins are also recorded as violence and idolatry, indicating not merely ethical corruption but a spirit of rebellion that rejected God's sovereign rule. In other words, their way of life was not only against humanity, but openly defied the Creator God Himself.

Thus, the city of Sodom was a city completely opposed to God's will.

Then, why did such a rebellious spirit arise?

It can be said that it was because of prosperity.

When life goes smoothly without difficulty, people tend to misunderstand the meaning of God's will. Rather, they might perceive God merely as a source of increased troubles, someone who brings inconvenience, or as a being who restricts their present happiness.

In such a prosperous place, it becomes easier to live in rebellion against God rather than following Him, and that rebellion is regarded as righteousness.

We must remember that prosperity is always closely connected to such dangers.

There is another important point here.

It concerns through what means Lot chose this worst city---Sodom.

He chose it by "seeing."

Here, the word "see" carries significant meaning.

In fact, even during the incident in Egypt, the word "see" was used.

It was shown there that using "seeing" as a criterion for making worldly judgments is a very low-level standard.

Nevertheless, here again, we see Lot acting based on that low-level standard of judgment.

Gen. 3:6  So when the woman saw that the tree was good for food, and that it was a delight to the eyes, and that the tree was to be desired to make one wise, she took of its fruit and ate, and she also gave some to her husband who was with her, and he ate.

"Seeing" means judging based on what is visible; in other words, it means acting according to one's own unilateral perspective. It results in being easily deceived by beauty and such things. And that kind of judgment is likely to greatly diverge from a standard of faith, which is based on the invisible wisdom of God.

Here, one additional note should be made.

Regarding "beauty" or "good appearance," the issue itself is not inherently sinful. Rather, these too are wonderful blessings given by God. However, the problem is that such beauty simultaneously possesses a power to attract sin.

Therefore, we must not deny or attack all beautiful things.

I want to briefly remind you of this point so that we do not forget.

Gen. 13:14  The LORD said to Abram, after Lot had separated from him, "Lift up your eyes and look from the place where you are, northward and southward and eastward and westward,

Gen. 13:15  for all the land that you see I will give to you and to your offspring forever.

The phrase "Lift up your eyes" can also be translated as "Now, lift up your eyes," emphasizing immediacy. It refers specifically to this timing.

And this timing is precisely the moment when Abram did not adopt visible prosperity or criteria that stimulate desire as a basis for choice, but rather surrendered such choices to others, receiving only what remained.

In other words, it was exactly the moment when he understood that God's will flowed through all things, and entrusted himself to that flow.

"Lift up your eyes and look from the place where you are, northward and southward and eastward and westward."

What must be seen at this timing of placing trust in God? It is the place of God's promise.

Abram also failed due to emotions such as fear and anxiety.

But overcoming that, he worshiped again, bowing before God.

And as a result of returning to God, his actions were improved as well.

At precisely such a timing, God once again showed Abram what He would do---

this was indeed the significance of this event.

The four directions symbolize the inheritance of the entire land. And an incredibly large promise, physically and temporally, was made: "forever"---to you and to your offspring.

12:7  "To your offspring (לְזַרְעֲךָ) I will give this land."

Only "offspring" are mentioned here.

13:15  "To you (לְךָ) and to your offspring."

Actually, this is the first explicit mention that the Promised Land will be given to Abram himself.

It is deeply significant that this promise was declared immediately after Abraham himself gave something up to someone else. We sense great biblical depth here. Rather than receiving when demanding and desiring, one receives when letting go. This is a complete reversal of worldly thinking. God sees and answers.

Gen. 13:16  I will make your offspring as the dust of the earth, so that if one can count the dust of the earth, your offspring also can be counted.

The Lord promised Abram that his offspring would be "as numerous as the dust of the earth." The Hebrew word ʿāfar, meaning "dust," refers to dry soil, dust that is not only innumerable but pervasive across the entire earth.

In other words, this metaphor does not merely indicate quantitative infinity, but also suggests a universality of blessing that spreads and permeates the whole world. Although Abram still had no child at this time, the Lord purposely used a rhetorical form to emphasize the certainty of His promise, saying, "if one could count the dust, then your offspring also could be counted."

The Lord's plans do not always match the future we imagine. Even if events occur on a scale beyond our imagination, this simply shows God's power and should be seen as nothing extraordinary.

Gen. 13:17  Arise, walk through the length and the breadth of the land, for I will give it to you.

The phrase translated as "walk through" carries the nuance of continually moving throughout every corner of the land. In the ancient Near East, when a king bestowed territory upon a subject, there was a ritual of actually traversing the borders. To tread with the soles of one's feet symbolized acquiring rights over the territory. Here, the Lord encouraged Abram to confirm the Promised Land by stepping upon it with his feet.

Gen. 13:18  So Abram moved his tent and came and settled by the oaks of Mamre, which are at Hebron, and there he built an altar to the LORD.

Reading verse 18, one might wonder: did Abram settle in Hebron, and what happened to the action of walking around the land? Let me explain the situation.

Hebron is centrally located in the highlands of Canaan, a strategic point from which it is easy to move north, south, east, and west. Abram built an altar there, strengthening it as a base for worship and encampment, and from there, he adopted a nomadic lifestyle, continuously moving and grazing around the area. The fact that he pitched his tent there should be understood not as "stopping his feet," but rather as "expanding his life of faith from this base."

Finally:

Gen. 13:17  Arise, walk through the length and the breadth of the land, for I will give it to you.

What God is saying to Abram here can also be understood as: "Trust and move forward in the good things God has given."

In this life, we have been given much hope by God. It is a certain fact that wonderful promises are made to us through Scripture.

So, how do we interact with God's Word in our daily lives? Do we confidently proceed, believing, "This word is being fulfilled in my life now; I am walking this path now"?

At times, we might think, "This promise is indeed wonderful, but it's not suitable for me now. I am currently only at this level, so I cannot receive such things."

Have you ever thought this way?

In fact, this promise to Abram itself did not seem realistic even to Abram. Because he saw no possibility that his wife Sarai could conceive. In such a situation, the promise was made: "I will give you offspring as numerous as the dust of the earth."

Moreover, Abram was told, "Believe that promise and start walking now." If Abram had thought, "I'm not qualified," we would also agree that indeed he was not qualified.

We might say, "What kind of dreamlike talk is that man saying?" Abram was someone who could have been dismissed that way.

Certainly, he had wealth. He certainly had many livestock. He was indeed prosperous. But the most crucial thing---he had no child. That was Abram's reality.

Yet what God promised to Abram was precisely, "I will give you the very thing you desire most," even though Abram had no clue how that would happen. And it was fulfilled in an amazing way, and through experiencing that, Abraham's faith became truly wonderful.

Let us, too, entrust our journey to God. And let us have the courage to actually go to the places God calls us through His Word.

When we persist in following God's Word, without giving in to worldly methods or our own thinking, we will come to know God's wonderful plan, far beyond our imagination, and will actually experience it firsthand. Through this experience, the fog in our lives will clear, and all paths will become straight. On this clear, wonderful path prepared by God, everything will be arranged, and we will walk with peace in our hearts.

For this, only one thing is necessary:

to "trust in God," to "completely believe in Jesus Christ our Lord."

If we believe, we can move forward.

Let us pray and seek this earnestly today.
