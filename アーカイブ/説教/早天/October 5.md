**October 5, 2025 -- Genesis 22:1--14 "On the Mount of the Lord It Shall Be Provided."**

Let us continue reading Genesis today.

If we read only this passage suddenly, it may even seem like a scene of madness of faith.

However, let us learn that its content actually leads to a very rich blessing.

First of all, this passage is deeply connected with the death and resurrection of Jesus Christ on the cross.

Hebrews 11:17  By faith Abraham, when he was tested, offered up Isaac, and he who had received the promises was in the act of offering up his only son,

Hebrews 11:18  of whom it was said, "Through Isaac shall your offspring be named."

Hebrews 11:19  He considered that God was able even to raise him from the dead, from which, figuratively speaking, he did receive him back.

Let us first briefly summarize.

"The only son, the beloved son" (Gen. 22:2) → The Father's declaration, "This is my beloved Son" (Matt. 3:17; 17:5), and "God gave His only Son" (John 3:16).

"The Lord will provide" (22:8,14) → God publicly displayed Christ as a propitiation (Rom. 3:25; 8:32).

The substitution of the ram (22:13) = The type of substitutionary atonement (Mark 10:45; 2 Cor. 5:21; 1 Pet. 3:18).

Isaac carrying the wood (22:6) → The Lord carrying the cross (John 19:17) and the silent Servant (Isa. 53:7) foreshadowed.

"We will come back," "God will provide" (22:5,8) → Faith in the God who gives life to the dead (Heb. 11:19).

The third day (22:4) → The type of the resurrection on the third day (1 Cor. 15:4).

"He did not withhold" (LXX Gen. 22:12,16) → God who did not spare His own Son (Rom. 8:32).

In this way, this passage becomes a pattern for our present faith.

Genesis 22:1  After these things God tested Abraham and said to him, "Abraham!" And he said, "Here I am."

It refers to the series of events from Abraham's calling to the fulfillment of the promise.

The Lord called him out, promised him descendants and land, and established the covenant.

Through the birth of Isaac, the departure of Hagar and Ishmael, and the covenant with Abimelech, Abraham lived in Beersheba and called upon the name of the Lord.

After the order of family and land had been established in this way, God tested his faith.

The first thing we must clearly understand is that

this "test" is not like the kind of "exam" we think of.

Because God is Almighty, and He does not need to test people for His own sake.

If so, then what is this for?

It is for Abraham himself, the man of faith, to examine his own faith through this trial.

And also, through the experience gained from this trial,

it is for Abraham to receive an even greater blessing.

In such a trial, Abraham answers, "Here I am."

This phrase does not merely mean "I am here."

It includes the nuance of pouring out his whole being before God at this very moment.

If that is so, there is a very important point.

Before he knows what kind of challenge God is about to give,

he is already expressing the attitude of "total obedience."

To such an obedient Abraham, an astonishing challenge is given.

Genesis 22:2  He said, "Take your son, your only son Isaac, whom you love, and go to the land of Moriah, and offer him there as a burnt offering on one of the mountains of which I shall tell you."

In this fourfold expression---"your son, your only son, whom you love, Isaac"---

the deep affection Abraham had for Isaac is clearly revealed.

And God spoke these words fully knowing Abraham's heart.

And what is important is precisely this point: "therefore he must offer him."

If it had been someone else's child, even if beloved,

it might not have been such a great trial.

But Abraham believed in faith that this son was "given by God,"

and more than anything, loved him as "his only son."

And yet God said, "Offer him."

Moreover, this was not a matter of letting go temporarily or sending him on a journey.

It was a "burnt offering"---that is, to take his life, burn it completely,

and offer it up as a total sacrifice leaving nothing behind.

It was an extremely severe challenge.

Here we find the connection with the truth that Jesus was truly the only begotten Son loved by the Father.

"The only son, the beloved son" (Gen. 22:2) → The Father's declaration, "This is my beloved Son" (Matt. 3:17; 17:5), and "God gave His only Son" (John 3:16).

Matthew 3:16  And when Jesus was baptized, immediately he went up from the water, and behold, the heavens were opened to him, and he saw the Spirit of God descending like a dove and coming to rest on him.

Matthew 3:17  And behold, a voice from heaven said, "This is my beloved Son, with whom I am well pleased."

Genesis 22:3  So Abraham rose early in the morning, saddled his donkey, and took two of his young men with him and his son Isaac. And he cut the wood for the burnt offering and arose and went to the place of which God had told him.

This phrase "rose early in the morning" shows that, in response to this severe challenge,

he completed all the preparations without hesitation and departed immediately.

And his preparation was thorough---he even cut the wood for the burnt offering.

In other words, this shows that he did not try to get through the situation with some symbolic offering,

but that he had the will to carry out completely the very word of God.

Genesis 22:4  On the third day Abraham lifted up his eyes and saw the place from afar.

The third day → It is said to be a type of the resurrection on the third day.

Genesis 22:5  Then Abraham said to his young men, "Stay here with the donkey; I and the boy will go over there and worship and come again to you."

Taking "two young men" was the normal and customary practice at that time.

It was also a sufficient number to serve as witnesses that the journey truly took place.

Here we see the scene where they separate. What lay ahead was a mountain,

and it was a heavy load to climb with all the equipment needed for a three-day journey.

Therefore, leaving the donkey and the luggage behind and climbing the mountain with only the two of them

was a standard and natural course of action.

However, being just the two of them must have had a great impact on Abraham's heart.

Ordinarily, a father and son climbing a mountain together toward the same goal would be a beautiful scene,

but here, each step was taking them closer to the son's death,

and they proceeded toward it together, just the two of them.

"I and the boy will go over there and worship and come again to you."

This becomes a type of the resurrection.

Genesis 22:6  And Abraham took the wood of the burnt offering and laid it on Isaac his son. And he took in his hand the fire and the knife. So they went both of them together.

The phrase "he took the fire" likely refers to carrying either live coals or tools to kindle the fire.

And the fact that he placed the wood on Isaac's back

is a foreshadowing of Jesus Christ carrying His own cross as He walked,

a foretaste of that image.

Incidentally, regarding Isaac's age, there are various views---thirty-seven, twenty-five, thirteen, or in his late teens.

However, since it says that he "carried the wood," it is certain that he was of an age

with sufficient strength and discernment to do so.

Genesis 22:7  And Isaac said to his father Abraham, "My father!" And he said, "Here I am, my son." He said, "Behold, the fire and the wood, but where is the lamb for a burnt offering?"

Genesis 22:8  Abraham said, "God will provide for himself the lamb for a burnt offering, my son." So they went both of them together.

If we carefully consider Abraham's feelings in this scene,

we can see that within him coexisted both trust and fear, hope and anguish.

When Isaac asked, "Where is the lamb for the burnt offering?"

Abraham answered, "God will provide for himself."

In these words, his complete trust in God is already expressed.

He believed that God would open a way in some form,

though he did not know how it would happen. That is to say, rather than having the certainty that Isaac would be spared,

it was the faith that "God's promise will surely be fulfilled" that sustained him.

Through his past experiences, Abraham knew that God never breaks His promises.

In his intercession for Sodom, in the fulfillment of the promise to Sarah, and in the birth of Isaac---

even when all seemed impossible, God accomplished it.

Since that same God had promised that "through Isaac shall your offspring be named,"

Abraham believed that even if Isaac died,

God would in some way bring His promise to pass.

Hebrews 11:19 speaks precisely of this: "He considered that God was able even to raise him from the dead."

This faith supported Abraham's heart.

He responded to God's word "God will provide" not with reasoning, but with trust.

The expression "God will provide" (יִרְאֶה, *yir'eh*) carries the nuance of "God sees" or "God foresees and provides."

Abraham stood on the faith that God's eyes see everything and that He prepares beforehand.

Therefore, his words were not meant as comfort or excuse, but as a confession of faith.

Genesis 22:9  When they came to the place of which God had told him, Abraham built the altar there and laid the wood in order and bound Isaac his son and laid him on the altar, on top of the wood.

Genesis 22:10  Then Abraham reached out his hand and took the knife to slaughter his son.

We cannot fathom what was going on in Abraham's heart at this moment. The one thing certain is the fact that he walked by faith (Heb. 11:17--19). It was not understanding that went first; under the confession "the LORD will provide," he proceeded step by step in obedience (Gen. 22:8). There must have coexisted pain and trembling, and yet trust in the God of promise.

This taut, strained relationship between father and son will eventually overlap with the event of the Father and the Son, Jesus Christ.

However, the difference is decisive. Abraham was stopped, whereas God "did not spare His own Son"---in other words, He carried it out. In Abraham's scene a ram became the substitute,

but in the New Testament the very Son of God Himself became the substitute for us.

Then why did God go so far as to carry it out? Scripture answers plainly: because of love.

"For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life" (John 3:16). "But God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us" (Rom. 5:8). "In this the love of God was made manifest among us, that God sent his only Son into the world, so that we might live through him. In this is love, not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins" (1 John 4:9--10).

Genesis 22:11  But the angel of the LORD called to him from heaven and said, "Abraham, Abraham!" And he said, "Here I am."

By this answer, "Here I am," we can see that Abraham's state of faith had not changed,

and that he was prioritizing obedience before God above all else.

Genesis 22:12  He said, "Do not lay your hand on the boy or do anything to him, for now I know that you fear God, seeing you have not withheld your son, your only son, from me."

Here, when he was about to offer Isaac as a burnt offering,

a command to halt that act is given.

In other words, this trial ended here.

And through this trial it became clear that Abraham was one who fears God.

Genesis 22:13  And Abraham lifted up his eyes and looked, and behold, behind him was a ram, caught in a thicket by his horns. And Abraham went and took the ram and offered it up as a burnt offering instead of his son.

And here again we can see the connection to Jesus Christ:

"The LORD will provide" (22:8, 14) → God publicly displayed Christ as the offering (Rom. 3:25; 8:32).

The substitution of the ram (22:13) = a type of substitutionary atonement (Mark 10:45; 2 Cor. 5:21; 1 Pet. 3:18).

Jesus Christ died to redeem our sins.

Here there is a common point with the ram that was offered in place of Isaac's life.

And most important is this:

the One who did it was God Himself.

God, because of His love, for us,

offered His beloved only Son as the sacrifice.

Through Abraham's act we are able

to understand a little of the human psychology within it---suffering and sorrow.

And we surely also felt relief that it was not actually carried out.

However, the Lord did not halt but actually carried out

the offering of His beloved Son Jesus Christ as the sacrifice.

We must never forget this.

Let us keep deeply in our hearts

how great a love we have received,

and let us walk with gratitude for the grace of God.

Genesis 22:14  So Abraham called the name of that place, "The LORD will provide"; as it is said to this day, "On the mount of the LORD it shall be provided."

This verse is a phrase well known to us.

"On the mount of the LORD it shall be provided"---that is, there is provision with God.

However, there is one thing we must bear in mind.

"Provision"

does not mean that if we simply wait, it will be given automatically.

In the nuance of this phrase, the right way to grasp it is: "when one goes there and sets out to carry something out, the preparation is in place."

In other words, it does not mean that everything is satisfied while doing nothing,

nor that life is guaranteed to be provided for while remaining inactive.

This challenge, as we have seen, was by no means an easy one.

If I myself had received the same words as Abraham, I might have made many excuses---"It must be a misunderstanding," "This is only my imagination," "God would never want such a thing"---and perhaps pretended not to have heard them.

To obey the word of God is that severe.

However, the Lord's provision is something that can never be obtained unless we go there.

Because only through the process of reaching that place can we truly understand its importance.

If one thinks that he has no sin, he will not understand the meaning of the cross of Jesus Christ.

But when we look into our hearts and find sin within, we first sink into despair.

Yet, in the very midst of that despair, we realize that there is a "provision"---Jesus Christ, who has already paid the price for our sin.

This "provision of the Lord" applies not only to matters of faith but also to our daily lives.

We always pray to God: "I want it to be this way," "I wish that would happen."

But God says, "Then take one step forward from there."

When we respond to that challenge, only then do we begin to see the "provision."

Jesus, who says, "I am the way,"---if we merely remain still, many things cannot be seen. That is the reality of faith.

Now finally, let us consider once more, through Abraham's heart, what this trial was for.

Abraham received an extremely severe, unimaginable challenge, and he was able to respond to it.

Abraham, who did not refuse to pay an unbelievable price---this is undoubtedly a wonderful image of faith.

Then what should we learn from this?

Should we learn how to offer sacrifices?

Is God the one who always looks upon our sacrifices and delights in them?

The more we offer, the deeper we become as believers, and He gives us greater blessings?

If that were so, should we not then willingly offer many sacrifices---our money, our time, even the most precious things---so that good things will be given to us?

That is not correct.

We must go one step deeper and look into what is within this passage.

It is what Abraham ultimately obtained.

It was the trust that "when one walks in the way of God, God will surely respond."

The conviction that God, in His own way, will lead His own word to the right place.

We believe in the word of God. But the word of God is at times very difficult.

"You will be opposed by your family," "Love your enemies"---these are truly hard words.

Then, may we choose not to obey them?

The answer is, "We must obey."

Because when we turn from Jesus Christ and go back to our family,

the family moves further away from Jesus Christ.

If, for the sake of the family we love, we draw near to Jesus Christ even if we must leave them for a time,

then the family too will be able to draw near to Christ.

If we do not love our enemies and follow the thoughts of the flesh, there will only be deepening strife.

There is no true solution there.

If that is so, we must obey the word of God.

However, we often fail to obey, being pulled in many directions, drifting from God,

and risking making void the precious sacrifice of Jesus Christ.

If we, like Abraham, faithfully respond to the challenges given through the Word,

there will surely be provision.

And what we gain through actually receiving and experiencing that provision---

that is "deep trust in God" itself. It is to give us that trust that this trial exists.

We must remember this.

When we say, "Why am I in such a situation?" "Why can I not go to worship?"

"Why do I keep criticizing others?"---

these are trials given by God.

When we recognize them as from God and understand them as "challenges," how should we respond?

With the words, "Here I am."

That is, with the resolve to say, "In regard to my present situation, I will take the next step as one who obeys God."

We must have that posture.

And when we truly hold that attitude, the word of God truly shines.

"This word was true." "The love of God who gave this word was genuine."

We will be assured of this.

Because we will know that there is provision and that within it lies a better solution than we could ever imagine.

Let us entrust our daily lives to the Lord and walk with Him.

This life is full of difficult challenges, but the Lord already knows us.

And the fact that this challenge has been given is itself the assurance that we can overcome it.

1 Corinthians 10:13  No temptation has overtaken you that is not common to man. God is faithful, and he will not let you be tempted beyond your ability, but with the temptation he will also provide the way of escape, that you may be able to endure it.

Let us believe this, face the Lord each day, and walk through our daily lives as worship.
