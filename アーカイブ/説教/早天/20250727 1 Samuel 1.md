**20250727 1 Samuel 1:20--28 "A Child Given to the Lord"**

Today, I would like to reflect on "giving a child to the Lord" from 1 Samuel.

Giving a child to God---how should we understand this?

To state the conclusion, it is a wonderful thing.

Because it means handing over to the child a wonderful gift: a life lived in unity with God.

In today's passage, we see the scene in which a woman named Hannah entrusts her child Samuel to the priest Eli.

This child Samuel later anoints Saul as Israel's first king,

and afterward also anoints David, becoming an extremely important figure.

Today's passage can be seen as a crucial moment that shows how Samuel, who fulfills such an important role, lived with God.

Samuel, through his parents, was firmly given a connection with God. Because of that, he was able to walk with the Lord and faithfully carry out God's work while sharing a deep resonance with Him.

Let's briefly review the story up to this point.

Elkanah of the land of Ephraim had two wives, Hannah and Peninnah.

Peninnah had children, but Hannah had none,

and every year when she went up to the house of the Lord in Shiloh, she was mocked and wept.

Elkanah comforted her, saying, "Am I not more to you than ten sons?"

and gave her a double portion, but her heart found no healing.

One year, Hannah was deeply distressed and prayed to the Lord,

saying, "If you will indeed give me a son, I will give him to the Lord all the days of his life, and no razor shall touch his head."

"To give him to the Lord all the days of his life, and no razor shall touch his head" means offering the child as the Lord's possession for life,

and not cutting his hair is an outward sign connected to the Nazirite vow (as seen in Numbers 6), a vow of consecration.

Usually this vow includes abstaining from wine and avoiding contact with the dead,

but in 1 Samuel, special emphasis is placed on "no razor shall touch his head,"

indicating a lifelong dedication, not a temporary one.

That is, it is a vow based on the premise that the child, once grown, will serve the Lord continually at the tabernacle (and later the temple)---a lifelong dedication.

Seeing her lips move, the priest Eli mistook her for being drunk,

but after hearing her explanation, he blessed her, saying, "Go in peace." Hannah returned to her seat, ate, and her countenance was no longer sad.

Eventually, the family returned to Ramah, and the Lord remembered Hannah.

She conceived and gave birth to a boy and named him Samuel (meaning "I asked for him from the Lord, and the Lord heard").

1 Samuel 1:21

The man Elkanah and all his house went up to offer to the Lord the yearly sacrifice and to pay his vow.

1 Samuel 1:22

But Hannah did not go up, for she said to her husband, "As soon as the child is weaned, I will bring him, so that he may appear in the presence of the Lord and dwell there forever."

Considering the background of that time, Israelite heads of households customarily went up yearly to the sanctuary (then at Shiloh),

offered sacrifices, and promptly fulfilled their vows to God.

In verse 1:21, Elkanah intended to offer the annual sacrifice with his household and fulfill his vow.

This was the conduct of a model head of household---in other words, normal.

Also, the husband bore the responsibility of confirming and affirming the vows of his wife.

However, Hannah did not go with him this time and instead said, "When the child is weaned, I will bring him, so that he may stay there before the Lord forever."

Here, it becomes clear that her purpose was "lifelong dedication for permanent residence,"

not temporary service.

Therefore, not bringing him immediately was not a postponement of the vow,

but rather necessary to fulfill the vow---"to dedicate him for life"---in the best possible way, exactly as she had said.

"The mother raises the child by her side until he is weaned."

This overlaps with the image of Moses' mother raising her own child as a wet nurse (Exodus 2:7--10).

1 Samuel 1:23

Elkanah her husband said to her, "Do what seems best to you; wait until you have weaned him; only, may the Lord establish his word."

So the woman remained and nursed her son until she weaned him.

Here, the husband's approval is given. We can see that everything is proceeding properly and smoothly.

The law that records this is found in the following passage:

Numbers 30:10

And if she vowed in her husband's house or bound herself by a pledge with an oath,

Numbers 30:11

and her husband heard of it and said nothing to her and did not oppose her, then all her vows shall stand, and every pledge by which she has bound herself shall stand.

1 Samuel 1:24

And when she had weaned him, she took him up with her, along with a three-year-old bull, an ephah of flour, and a skin of wine,

and she brought him to the house of the Lord at Shiloh. And the child was young.

1 Samuel 1:25

Then they slaughtered the bull, and they brought the child to Eli.

Here, we see the scene where Hannah officially fulfills her vow through worship.

The bull offered here signifies, first, a burnt offering (Leviticus 1), expressing "complete surrender to God,"

and at the same time, as a peace offering accompanying a vow (Leviticus 7:16), it represents "restored fellowship with God, family, and community."

The accompanying flour and wine (Numbers 15:8--10) follow the regulations accompanying a bull offering.

In other words, Hannah fulfilled her vow before the Lord, not out of emotional excitement,

but in accordance with the order of the Law, and in a legitimate form.

1 Samuel 1:26

And she said, "Oh, my lord! As you live, my lord, I am the woman who was standing here in your presence, praying to the Lord.

1 Samuel 1:27

For this child I prayed, and the Lord has granted me my petition that I made to him.

1 Samuel 1:28

Therefore I have lent him to the Lord. As long as he lives, he is lent to the Lord." And he worshiped the Lord there.

Thus, Samuel was entrusted to the priest Eli. At that time, he was three years old.

In reality, a child at the age of three has already reached an age where he can understand many things.

Therefore, when he was entrusted at this point, there was certainly a possibility that he might have felt that his mother had abandoned him.

However, conversely, this also means that the determination of the parent was clearly communicated.

If it had been immediately after birth, before emotional awareness had developed, he would have been placed in that environment without understanding what was happening.

But for a child who has been raised for three years---quite a long time---in a home full of love,

to have everything in that environment suddenly change is no small thing.

Precisely because of that, he would likely remember the appearance of his parents at that time vividly.

And he should also be able to understand why that happened.

What is important here is that the standard for the parent's actions was this: "Because I love him, I entrust him to the Lord."

That was the foundation of her faith.

Let us reflect on the heart of the parent.

To let go of a three-year-old child---this is certainly no easy matter, and I personally feel that deeply,

considering the time I cried just from sending my own daughter to daycare.

To let go of a child at exactly the time when their mind is developing,

when personal communication becomes possible, a time of such rich interaction---

in truth, we can say there is a great theological significance in this.

It is the image of "letting go of that which is hardest to let go."

In other words, it is the image of what God always asks of us:

"Let go of that which you cling to more than God, that to which you are attached."

In Hannah's case, we can easily imagine how earnestly she desired this child.

And when raising him, how great her joy must have been, and how adorable he must have been.

But what happens if this goes too far?

It is that the relationship with the child becomes centered on the self.

That is, there arises a moment in which the child becomes "more precious than God."

In the choice between "living with God at the center" or "living in self-centeredness,"

loving the child becomes a gravitational force pulling toward self-centeredness.

Therefore, when Hannah gave her child to God, it was not merely a factual act of "entrusting the child" or "letting go,"

but rather a deep act of faith: "surrendering the attachment within her heart before the Lord."

Because he was given by God, she returned him to God---

this attitude becomes an important model of faith for us.

Everyone is prone to take what has been given and claim it as their own.

That which was received as a blessing turns, before we know it, into "something to control."

And from there, the seed of self-centeredness begins to sprout.

But Hannah did not do that. She returned to God the child she had received from Him.

And she did so at the very time she loved him most, when it was hardest to part---she did it then.

This clearly shows us the difference between worldly values and heavenly values.

In the values of the world, "what you obtain is to be used for yourself"---that is considered natural.

But in the world of faith, "what is given is to be used for the glory of God."

And this difference in values becomes a crucial point in parenting.

How should we raise our children? What do we value? We love our children. We want to raise them well.

That is natural, of course, but along with that, or perhaps deep within our hearts,

there are things that will influence our parenting.

Is it to fulfill our own dreams and ideals?

Is it to prove our ability as parents?

Or is it to respond to the will of God?

This difference will significantly change the direction of parenting.

In any case, as those who believe in God, it is truly important that we raise our children by entrusting them to the Lord.

The specifics will differ for each person,

but what is important is to show our children "the resolve that we are raising them with that mindset."

Now here, what Hannah did became a clear demonstration of her resolve to Samuel.

Precisely because he was three years old, he could understand the resolve of his mother---and that this resolve was made by faith in God, that fact and that experience. Samuel was able to firmly imprint this in his heart.

If we are not able to show this kind of resolve to our children,

what will happen to them? They will likely be easily swept away by the currents of the world.

If we are to live as those who believe in the Lord, we must show that clearly to our children.

And this is also the responsibility of a parent who believes in the Lord.

Without the resolve of a parent, how can children come to know the Lord?

Seeing parents who do not attend worship, who prioritize worldly affairs,

how can a child etch the greatness of the Lord Jesus Christ into their heart?

To think that they can is far too naïve.

It is not a simple matter.

The very reason this passage in Scripture exists is to show how difficult it is.

And it is also revealed in the scene where Abraham was about to offer his own child as a sacrifice.

Above all, we must remember the resolve of God the Father, who gave His only Son, Jesus Christ, as a sacrifice.

The resolve of a parent can be said to be an image that runs through the entire Bible.

If that is the case, then the responsibility of a parent who believes in the Lord is of utmost importance.

The appearance that a parent ought to show---that is, the daily life lived with resolve toward faith. That is where it is found.

Here, there is one thing we must be careful of:

If raising children in a "Christian way" is simply used as a means for self-realization within the Christian community,

then it may be even more sinful than the way of the world.

Now hold on a moment.

Up to this point, we have only talked about the parent's side,

but in reality, is the child happy in all of this?---it would not be strange to have such a question.

We live in a modern reality where many have been raised with an education that teaches:

"Those who do not live a good life in worldly terms are somehow inferior as human beings."

In such a context, to live with "resolve in faith" may appear far too uncertain,

far too dependent on others, and far too fragile.

Then, let us look at the children.

When we ask children today, "What do you want?"

the impression is that about 80% immediately say, "Money."

And when asked, "What will you use it for?"

they often have no answer. At best, they might say, "To buy a game,"

but beyond that, they have no clear purpose.

This looks just like blind faith in money.

In other words, it is a form of false faith---idolatry, to be precise---faith in money has taken root in the hearts of children.

So then, what does such idolatry bring into the heart?

It brings "eternal lack."

When one becomes dependent on the pleasure gained through money as a means,

no matter how much is obtained, there arises a sense of perpetual insufficiency.

The moment one possesses it, they envy someone who possesses more.

In biblical terms, it is the "spirit of covetousness" that is stirred by such idolatry.

If we hand over our children to such a value system,

their hearts will be consumed by this idol.

They will constantly feel that "something is lacking,"

and they will end up spending their entire life trying to fill that lack.

They will be forced to live with that kind of value system.

Why? Because they know nothing else.

That is why it is so important that we clearly understand

what we are handing over to our children by showing them our faith-filled resolve as parents.

If we misunderstand this point, we will take the wrong actions.

If our hearts are bound by worldly things,

if we deem "winning the competition of this world" to be good,

if we regard "success in this world" as the highest value,

and if we take "being praised in this world" as the greatest honor,

then if we make those the standard for raising our children,

we will not be able to show them this "resolve of faith."

And as a result, we will end up pushing our children toward the emptiness of this world.

So then, what should children come to know?

It is this: "God loves them."

This love is not given because they are better than someone else.

And another common misconception is this: "Even though I am worse than others, God still loves me."

That too is incorrect.

God does not see each of us as "relative beings."

He does not evaluate us as higher or lower in comparison to others.

God sees each of us as an "absolute being."

He loves me as me. He loves you as you.

He loves us wholeheartedly just as we are---

and it is this truth that children most need to know.

If we know this fact, then we no longer need to compare ourselves with others.

Because the Almighty God pours all His thoughts upon each one of us.

There is this fact: "He knows me, and therefore, He loves me."

This is a very important part, so I will say it without fear of being misunderstood.

For example──even if one is not smart, God "loves them because they are not smart."

Even if one has a disability──for instance, one hand does not move.

In that case, God "loves them precisely because one hand does not move."

It is not "He loves them despite that."

It is "because of that, He loves them."

From God's perspective, that person is not "pitiful."

God created them that way.

And "you just as you are" is an irreplaceable existence to God.

If each of us could truly grasp this, how much our hearts would be set free.

Comparing ourselves with others.

Being conscious of our differences when looking at someone else.

All of these have their origin in eating the fruit of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, which is called "the beginning of sin" in the Bible.

God never fails.

God is Almighty.

If that is the case, the fact that we are born in this way now is itself within God's plan.

God does not think, "I made a mistake."

Rather, He is saying, "You are wonderful just as you are."

Why do we fail to notice that and end up comparing ourselves to others?

That is "sin."

"Believe in Me. You are wonderful."──this is the message from God.

Children who grasp the essence of this faith will truly make their lives abundant.

Their lives will be filled with overflowing grace.

If we do not hand this over, then what does it mean to be a Christian?

Naturally, this is not an easy thing. That is why "resolve" is necessary.

At this time, Hannah made the decision to let go of Samuel during a very difficult period of age---three years old.

However, through that, Samuel walked "the best path of walking with God."

A life walking with God, moving forward with the truth that he is loved by God engraved in his heart──

that is the life he was able to live.

As a result, he found David and even bore the great work of connecting the way to Jesus Christ.

In other words, in the end, everything was connected to "the good path."

This can be called a "testimony" that richness of heart and love for God bring forth truly wonderful outcomes even in this real world.

Those who hunger in their hearts by comparing themselves to others──

no matter how much money they gain, no matter how high a position they obtain,

in the end, it will all lead to "destruction."

However, a life that carries God in the heart and walks with God──

that leads straight to "eternal life."

That is to say, the entire walk of that life brings about "blessing" as a tangible result.

This is not merely a concept.

We must clearly remember that this is a blessing that appears in visible form.

So then, what should we now hand over to the children?

That is──determined by our own "resolve."

Let us pray earnestly before the Lord,

so that we may be able to hand over the "right things" to the children.
