**20250720 Genesis 18:16--33 "Ten Righteous Ones"**

Let us continue reading Genesis today.

Up to this point, the narrative has followed the theme of Abraham's descendants, but here, the tone begins to shift.

In this verse, Abraham is depicted seeing off the guests from the heights of Mamre,

Gen. 18:19 For I have chosen him, that he may command his children and his household after him to keep the way of the Lord by doing righteousness and justice, so that the Lord may bring to Abraham what he has promised him."

God declares that He has chosen Abraham so that he may cause his descendants to carry out "righteousness and justice" (tzedakah and mishpat).

What does this mean, and how is it connected?

It is very simple. Two things.

First: It is to make known how sinful mankind truly is. (Not even ten are found.)

Second: It is to impress upon the mind the pattern that because of that sin, destruction results. (An entire city perishes.)

To carry out this powerful instruction, God proceeds with all of this directly in front of Abraham, through dialogue.

Gen. 18:20 Then the Lord said, "Because the outcry against Sodom and Gomorrah is great and their sin is very grave,

Gen. 18:21 I will go down to see whether they have done altogether according to the outcry that has come to me. And if not, I will know."

The expression that the "outcry (tsa'akah)" has reached God appears here for the first time since the incident with Cain in Genesis 4.

Gen. 4:10 And the Lord said, "What have you done? The voice of your brother's blood is crying to me from the ground.

In fact, from the archaeological remains at the proposed sites of Sodom, large quantities of children's bones and layers indicating social instability have been reported,

raising the possibility that the city was crying out in anguish.

It had likely become a city far removed from keeping the way of the Lord, from doing righteousness and justice.

However, is it because God lacks the power to know unless He goes there Himself? Of course not.

Then why?

When God declares, "I will go down and see," it is not because He lacks omniscience,

but rather to make His justice and relational dealings visible within the framework of human understanding.

This becomes effective on several points:

Public judicial process (Ancient Near Eastern "performance of justice")

In the courts of ancient cities, the king's personal on-site investigation and hearing of testimonies were regarded as the most solemn legal process.

Even the preamble to the Babylonian law codes proclaims, "I listen to the cries of the people, and judge after seeing the facts."

When God says He will "go down," He takes the same form as the highest judge of the human world,

making His justice visible to all.

Formal recognition of the cry of the victim

The Hebrew word "tsa'akah (cry)" is legal vocabulary referring to the plea of the socially oppressed (cf. Exodus 2:23).

When God says He will not merely hear but see and verify, it expresses His refusal to overlook the cry of the afflicted.

The God of justice examines the plea and proceeds with a public process.

Ex. 2:23 During those many days the king of Egypt died, and the people of Israel groaned because of their slavery and cried out for help. Their cry for rescue from slavery came up to God.

Opening the window for Abraham's intercession

By declaring an investigation, God created a temporal opportunity and left a gap for Abraham to intervene.

As we will see shortly, this leads to persistent negotiations from fifty down to ten.

From this exchange and its outcome, Abraham learns something immense.

Some have called this "God's pedagogical dialogue experiment with His covenant partner."

Anthropomorphism in accordance with the human viewpoint

Barth said, "God's self-revelation always entails consideration for human language."

When the Infinite One speaks to the finite, He makes His intent comprehensible through the act of investigation.

New Testament foreshadowing

When we think carefully, this coming in human form

is exactly a prototype of Jesus' earthly life.

In Luke 19:41, where Jesus weeps upon seeing Jerusalem,

we see a reenactment of God's act of verifying human sin with His own eyes.

Therefore, God already knows the truth.

However, in order to demonstrate a process of justice, to provide an opportunity to educate His covenant partner,

and to give formal attention to the cry of the oppressed, He deliberately declares, "I will go down to see."

This is a hallmark of the Lord's way of working: though all-knowing, He never lacks consideration for humanity.

We, too, must gratefully receive the words of the Lord,

who always works in this way.

Abraham negotiates the number down from fifty to ten.

We may be a little startled by this attitude before God,

but when we understand the cultural context of the time, we see that it was not so extreme.

At that time, price negotiation functioned as a social mechanism (institutionalized behavior pattern)

and fulfilled at least the following four roles:

Guaranteeing fairness and trust through "procedure"

In the preambles of the Code of Hammurabi and the letters of the Kingdom of Mari,

phrases such as "The king listened to the complaints, weighed the value, and corrected the measure" frequently appear.

The same principle applied to buying and selling---by negotiating prices out loud in public,

transparency was maintained to prevent later complaints of being overcharged or underpaid.

Confirming status and relational positioning

Accepting a price without negotiation was seen as behavior befitting a "slave or foreigner,"

while haggling too much was considered "undignified."

A moderate back-and-forth served as a signal: "I am a free person and I respect you,"

a ritual process used to gauge social hierarchy and alliances.

In the Amarna Letters' royal gift exchanges, even the back-and-forth over return gifts functioned as a measure of intimacy.

Not violating the "fixed price" of god or king

Some marketplace inscriptions (e.g., the Nabonidus inscription from Harran) record "This price must not be exceeded."

Negotiation worked as a process to adjust the margin without violating the official ceiling price.

Economic education and community formation

In Mesopotamian scribal schools (edubba), practice tablets survive with imagined dialogues like,

"How much for my bag of wool? --- Three shekels too high. --- Lower it by two shekels."

Such exercises taught young scribes both arithmetic and negotiation skills.

● Visualization of fair procedure --- public negotiation prevents claims of "overcharging" or "underpaying"

● Confirmation of status/relationship --- appropriate haggling marks one as a free person or ally

● Adjustment to official price --- ensures profit without violating state/temple price limits

● Site for economic education --- embedded in school dialogues to teach math and market behavior

These mechanisms served as the cultural soil for narrative metaphor.

Because price negotiation was embedded in daily life,

readers of Genesis 18 who heard Abraham's negotiation with God about the number of righteous would intuitively associate it with marketplace haggling,

and easily grasp the theological message of "bargaining over the value of the righteous."

From this we understand that to pray like Abraham's intercession is not presumptuous or rude before God,

but a legitimate act that is received with favor.

Gen. 18:33 And the Lord went his way, when he had finished speaking to Abraham, and Abraham returned to his place.

Here, there is no mention of "ascending" or any other such expression---the conversation ends plainly.

Considering what unfolds next, it seems as if God, though silent, was already speaking the conclusion He knew.

Now then, today, from this conclusion of "ten righteous,"

let us consider what it means to be "righteous."

As we will read next, Sodom is destroyed.

That means the condition was not met---there were not even ten righteous people.

What we speak today will become the foundation for next week.

We are very accustomed to the idea that "God is a God of love," and

so the intense aspect of "a God who destroys"

is not something we easily accept.

However, we must also not forget the strength of God as Judge,

who is faithful and always does what is right.

Now, looking at what follows in the biblical passage after Sodom,

it is clear that the situation was indeed dreadful.

As a result, it was destroyed.

The final judgment came so precisely that nothing remained.

Then, what about today's world?

That is, in today's society, how many righteous people are there? This is the point.

We, as a basic premise, are under sin. And where did this sin begin?

It began when we ate from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil.

This touches exactly on today's heart theme: the concept of being "righteous."

Why was eating from the knowledge of good and evil sin?

Because it meant claiming the standard of "righteousness" for oneself.

And that is the very essence of sin.

In other words, it is saying, "Righteousness does not belong to God, but to me."

That act---of calling that self-declared righteousness "right"---

this is what we do.

And this belief in one's own righteousness inevitably causes conflict.

Differences in values arise, and each party makes their own claims.

What is problematic here is that from each one's perspective,

they are not wrong.

Both parties believe they are "right," and so there is no answer to be found.

Some may say, "Well, that's what laws are for."

However, those laws are also merely someone's---or some society's---"justice."

In other words, concepts like "righteousness," "correctness," or "truth"

are revealed to be very human constructs.

We misunderstand and think of them as if they were "truth,"

but in fact, this is precisely the most human part of "sin."

You may say, "Still, without a concept of righteousness, society couldn't function.

And many things are actually resolved through it."

That is certainly true.

There are indeed problems that can be solved through it.

Therefore, I'm not saying that societal rules or social justice are always wrong.

However, if someone is completely convinced that "this is absolute,"

then that belief is very dangerous.

Especially in human relationships, it becomes highly problematic.

We feel this in our daily lives.

"How can that person not understand this?" "Why would they say something like that?" "Why would they do that?"

In many situations, we may feel puzzled by others, sometimes angry, sometimes even contemptuous.

Why is that?---At the root is the belief, "I am right."

"I am right, so the person who does that is strange."

"I am right, so I can't understand what that person is doing."

"I am right."

What does this strength of "rightness" cause?

It causes "conflict and hatred."

Even if not visible on the surface, it is something that stirs at the depths of our hearts.

It is the very nature of our "sin and guilt." Therefore, we cannot deny it.

Here is one very common pitfall.

Someone might say, "I'm aware of my mistakes. So I don't think of myself as righteous."

However, even in that,

there is a hidden attitude of "I rightly know that I am wrong."

In other words, the very knowledge that "I am wrong"

is still rooted in "I am right."

It is a deeply troublesome issue.

Sadly, this is exactly what the Bible reveals as the origin lying deep within our sin.

So, going forward, we will examine the people of Sodom.

Their actions are extremely vile and truly evoke disgust from the depths of our hearts.

But within them, they likely thought, "I am right."

And among them were probably those who thought,

"Yes, we are sexually depraved---but we know that."

By recognizing their error,

they used a kind of trick to affirm themselves through denial,

deceiving their own hearts and continuing to live.

What these people showed was "the sin of Sodom."

Indeed, this issue is truly deeply rooted, and the one speaking these things---myself---is by no means free from it, but rather lives in it daily.

In reality, moments always arise when we must face it.

Then what should we do?

First, we must begin to let go of the belief that "I am right."

Let me say at the outset---this is an impossible task.

However, moving in that direction is itself what "faith" is.

That is, we must come to grasp the truth that "it is God who is right, not me."

But again, this is tricky.

Because even thinking "I am right because I believe in God"

already contains within it the very idea "I am right."

This is truly difficult.

Because it is so deeply rooted, it is a mission close to impossible.

However, it can be approached gradually.

It can be improved step by step.

We little by little let go of ourselves.

By not relying on our own righteousness but trusting in the righteousness of God, we are able to walk forward.

That is the path of "sanctification," the path we must walk, and it is faith itself.

At this point, we may wonder:

"But if I let go of my own sense of being right, what joy is left in this world?"

"If I'm not right, then nothing is enjoyable, and everything I do is meaningless."

This is a difficult problem.

But one thing can be said---

God surely has grace prepared for us beyond that point.

When we restrain ourselves or let go of our pride, it places great pressure on us.

But once we let go, we begin to realize just how much burden we were placing on our hearts

by clinging to the idea that "I am right."

"How much energy does it take to prove that I am right?"

There's absolutely no point to it, and the other person will never change.

Why? Because the notion "I am right" won't allow it.

When we realize how heavy that burden was---

only then can we truly understand the other person.

We will then be able to obey, from the heart, God's command to "love your neighbor."
