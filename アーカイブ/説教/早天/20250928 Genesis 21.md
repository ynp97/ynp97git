20250928 Genesis 21:22--34 "It Was Called Beersheba."

Let us read Genesis again today.

This passage feels somewhat as if it is floating out of the flow, but it is told here as a record that the fulfillment of the covenant between Abraham and God was certain.

This is because, of the two pillars of the Abraham narrative---offspring and land---just before this, with the birth of Isaac, the axis of "offspring" was settled, and here, in order to establish the side of "land" publicly and legally.

Here, the foreign king Abimelech publicly declares, "God is with you in all that you do" (v.22), and the right to a well (the essential of life in those days) is acknowledged by means of visible "evidence (seven ewe lambs)."

This is also the early realization of the blessing of the nations through the patriarch.

And at the same time, it shows the way of the believer who, as a sojourner, builds peace with neighbors with righteousness and faithfulness.

And this record resonates with the later story of Isaac in Gerar.

Genesis 21:22 At that time Abimelech and Phicol the commander of his army said to Abraham, "God is with you in all that you do." (ESV)

This name of the king Abimelech, as we saw before, is said to be more like a title than a personal name, like Pharaoh. In chapter 26, Isaac also makes a covenant with the same king Abimelech.

Abimelech is "king of Gerar," and his residence belongs to "the land of the Philistines." Then, where is Philistia?

It is said not to be a "single country" corresponding one-to-one with modern borders, but rather a region spanning Palestine (the Gaza area) and Israel. Incidentally, the designation "Philistia (land of the Philistines)" later became the etymological source of the word "Palestine."

Phicol: Just as the royal name Abimelech is highly possible to be a title, so also Phicol has been discussed as possibly being a title, but the main dictionaries treat it as a proper name.

Genesis 26:26 When Abimelech went to him from Gerar with Ahuzzath his adviser and Phicol the commander of his army,

Genesis 26:27 Isaac said to them, "Why have you come to me, seeing that you hate me and have sent me away from you?"

Genesis 26:28 They said, "We see plainly that the Lord has been with you. So we said, let there be a sworn pact between us, between you and us, and let us make a covenant with you." (ESV)

Isaac also makes a similar treaty with the same king Abimelech, and to him also it is said, "The Lord has been with you."

I think you can see that this is a scene with much continuity.

This word, "God is with you in all that you do," is clearly a very important word.

The reason is that living with God does not end in self-satisfaction, but is proof that it bears fruit in visible form. It is not just a feeling, but the grace of God, the protection of God, which is evident to anyone, upon the believer.

Genesis 21:23 Now therefore swear to me here by God that you will not deal falsely with me or with my descendants or with my posterity, but as I have dealt kindly with you, so you will deal with me and with the land where you have sojourned."

Genesis 21:24 And Abraham said, "I will swear." (ESV)

In the ancient Near East, an "oath" was a public promise that gave legal weight to one's words by setting God as witness.

Based on this, neighboring powers, on an equal footing, agreed "not to attack, not to seize, not to deceive," and defined even the method of handling disputes, which is what is called a parity treaty.

The promise did not end in words, but was visualized by a gift or commemorative sign, documented and stored in the temple, or inscribed on boundary stones to publicly confirm rights of water and land.

The virtue that supports that promise is faithfulness (ḥesed). It is a little different from the meaning of "faithfulness" in Japanese. It is not kindness according to mood, but loyalty that "continues" the appropriate faithfulness to the relationship, even at cost. In short, oath and ḥesed were not merely matters of personal inner life, but the essentials of society that sustained peace, trade, and resource management between towns and towns, tribes and tribes.

Now, the question here is: were they not believing in the same God in the first place?

In antiquity, the essence of an "oath" was less "to share the same God," and more that "each party would set as witness the being he feared most." Treaty documents often lined up the names of both gods (including the other's god), and were arranged so that whichever side broke it would "receive a curse from his own god."

Genesis 21:25 Then Abraham reproved Abimelech about a well of water that Abimelech's servants had seized.

Genesis 21:26 Abimelech said, "I do not know who has done this thing; you did not tell me, and I have not heard of it until today." (ESV)

The very official occasion when Abimelech brought up, "Let us make a covenant now," was the best moment to raise the unresolved issue of the well. If he swore first, he could not include it as a condition, so before the oath, he protested and demanded correction, putting it on the agenda of the treaty.

In deserts and semi-arid lands, wells were lifelines.

The most basic rule was "the house (clan) that dug and maintained the well had priority to use it."

However, because water was a matter of life and death, there was also a tacit understanding not to refuse the minimal supply for travelers or livestock to quench their thirst.

The rights were not left as verbal promises, but made "visible" through oaths before God, witnesses, exchanges of gifts (such as livestock), boundary markers, or the naming of wells.

Use of wells:

At the well site, order was kept---humans before livestock, or timetables were set, and in the dry season distribution was made strict.

If someone seized, filled up, or polluted a well, it was regarded as a grave hostile act, and one would protest before elders or lords, settling it by return, compensation, or renewed oath.

With neighboring clans or towns, equal agreements (treaties) were made---"not to attack, not to seize, not to deceive"---and the ownership and usage conditions of wells and procedures in case of trouble were written down.

In short, the three pillars of preventing water disputes and protecting the life and trade of the region were: priority of the one who dug, sharing in emergencies, and visible promises and procedures.

And now, there is this flow that this protest is made and formally acknowledged.

In arid regions, well rights are directly linked to survival. The careful exchange over legal protest and the duty of notification suggests the context of lawsuit--mediation.

Genesis 21:27 So Abraham took sheep and oxen and gave them to Abimelech, and the two men made a covenant.

Genesis 21:28 Abraham set seven ewe lambs of the flock apart.

Genesis 21:29 And Abimelech said to Abraham, "What is the meaning of these seven ewe lambs that you have set apart?"

Genesis 21:30 He said, "These seven ewe lambs you will take from my hand, that this may be a witness for me that I dug this well."

Genesis 21:31 Therefore that place was called Beersheba, because there both of them swore an oath.

Genesis 21:32 So they made a covenant at Beersheba. Then Abimelech and Phicol the commander of his army rose up and returned to the land of the Philistines. (ESV)

Abraham gave livestock as a sign of the treaty, and in addition, he deliberately set aside only "seven (shevaʿ)" ewe lambs. When Abimelech asked the reason, Abraham answered, "Receive them as proof that I dug this well." Then the two swore before God ("nishbaʿ"). Therefore the place was called "Beʾer-shevaʿ."

In other words, "seven (shevaʿ)" → "swear (nishbaʿ)" → "Shevaʿ (place name)," a triple chain of the same sound is arranged.

This means that the number seven was not just matching numbers, but a device to make people remember the "sign" of the covenant by ear. People would remember by wordplay, "Because he offered seven and swore, this is the 'well of Shevaʿ,'" so that they would not forget the event where the well's rights were publicly confirmed.

Genesis 21:33 Abraham planted a tamarisk tree in Beersheba and called there on the name of the Lord, the Everlasting God.

Genesis 21:34 And Abraham sojourned many days in the land of the Philistines. (ESV)

Abraham planted the tamarisk in order to leave as a "living marker" the well rights and mutual non-aggression peace confirmed by the covenant with Abimelech, rather than a stone monument.

At the same time, there was the aim of creating there a worship center to continually call on the name of the Lord. In a life with much movement, he placed a marker of the place where water, the source of life, and the Everlasting God who rules life, meet.

The tamarisk (Hebrew 'eshel) is resistant to drought and salt, has deep roots reaching groundwater, shields wind, and makes shade as an evergreen. It is ideal beside a well, grows slowly but lives long, and thus becomes a symbol enduring time, fitting to the confession of calling on "the Everlasting God (El Olam)."

Further, in rabbinic tradition, the 'eshel is also understood as a sign of "hospitality," overlapping the image of a small inn with food, drink, and lodging prepared.

In other words, this planting had multiple purposes: a place of prayer, a memorial of peace, and a sign of practice in welcoming travelers.

Thus, the covenant with God that was in Abraham is now fulfilled in two aspects, offspring and land.

And we will see in the next part how this fulfilled promise influences Abraham.

That is, it is the figure of obeying the command to offer before God the life of Isaac, the most precious. We will look at it in detail next week.
