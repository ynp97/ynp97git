June 22, 2025 --- Genesis 16:7--16 "Where have you come from, and where are you going?"

Let us read Genesis again today.

This passage records the event in which Hagar, having conceived as a surrogate, falls into a broken relationship with Sarai, and is eventually mistreated and runs away.

And what happened in the place where she fled to is what is written in today's portion.

Gen. 16:7  The angel of the LORD found her by a spring of water in the wilderness, the spring on the way to Shur.

The place she fled to was a desert area---she was traveling along "the way to Shur," which stretches between Canaan and Egypt.

In other words, it is very likely that she was trying to escape and return to her homeland, Egypt.　

This image of "wilderness" reminds us of the path Israel took during the Exodus from Egypt.

As a prototype, here we can see Hagar's flight, her encounter with God, and the various disciplines imposed upon her---

I believe this is a passage that shows such elements.

→ The place where Jesus was tempted (Matthew chapter 4)

The encounter by the spring also → reminds us of Jesus and the Samaritan woman by the well (John chapter 4).

Here it is written that "the angel of the LORD appeared."

In fact, this is the first appearance of "the angel of the LORD" in the Bible.

The fact that this first appearance was before Hagar, a female slave,

seems to express well the character of the Bible.

So to speak, it gives the image of appearing before "the one most unworthy."

And this is the important point: it was not Hagar who sought God,

but God who sought Hagar and made His angel appear to her.

This verb "found her" (וַיִּמְצָאָהּ) expresses intentional intervention.

That is, it carries the strong meaning of "came searching and discovered," not by chance.

As we touched on last week, Hagar likely did not have faith in the LORD.

Basically, she must have held to a religion rooted in Egyptian idolatry.

Yet even so, in the form of the angel of the LORD, the LORD appeared before Hagar.

Gen. 16:8  And he said, "Hagar, servant of Sarai, where have you come from and where are you going?" She said, "I am fleeing from my mistress Sarai."

Here, the words of the angel of God are written.

The question, "Where have you come from and where are you going?"---this reminds us of a certain famous scene in the Bible.

It is the words God spoke to Adam, who had sinned and was hiding:

"Where are you?" (Genesis 3:9).

Gen. 3:8  And they heard the sound of the LORD God walking in the garden in the cool of the day, and the man and his wife hid themselves from the presence of the LORD God among the trees of the garden.

Gen. 3:9  But the LORD God called to the man and said to him, "Where are you?"

This question is not because God does not know.

It is certainly not a question for the purpose of gathering information.

That is made clear by the way the angel speaks here.

He said, "Hagar, servant of Sarai," knowing exactly her status, her name, and everything about her.

Even so, he deliberately asks, "Where have you come from and where are you going?"

Why is that?

It is because Hagar is being given a chance to look again at herself.

There are things one cannot see unless one questions oneself.

For that reason, God deliberately "gives a question."

If hypothetically, it had been said to her, "You are now coming from Abram and trying to return to Egypt,"

Hagar would have justified herself right then.

---"I am leaving the place where I was mistreated and trying to return to a place of freedom"---

that alone would have filled her heart as the only thing to be considered.

However, if she were to receive the question "Where have you come from and where are you going?" in her own heart,

and look back from the beginning, Hagar might have noticed a more essential perspective.

For example, "Where have you come from?"

It is not simply "from my mistress Sarai who mistreated me,"

but "from Abram, a great figure who longed for his first child and sought to believe in the promise of God"---

such a shift in perspective might have occurred.

And "why did I have to leave from there?"

She might have realized that it was not only Sarai's unreasonableness,

but also her own pride and arrogance that strained the relationship.

Originally, even though she was a surrogate mother, serving her mistress was within the bounds of societal norms.

However, within that relationship, Hagar harbored feelings of superiority and hostility in her heart, and she crossed the line.

Also, "Where are you going?"

She may have thought she was heading toward a "land of freedom," but in reality, it was nothing more than an escape toward an uncertain future---

a future where she had no relatives, no one to protect her, and no stability.

If she had fled from Abram's household, her position would have been that of an unmarried pregnant woman,

a woman with no social protection and no place to go---that was her reality.

This question, "Where have you come from and where are you going?"

was a God-given opportunity for Hagar to ask herself that same question.

Through this question, she must have been able to reevaluate her situation, the inclinations of her heart,

and the meaning of the choices she had made.

And there is one more important perspective.

That is: how should one understand what happened in Abram's household?

It was that very household where God's promise was present, and where His plan was about to bear fruit.

How much Hagar herself understood this is not clearly stated in Scripture.

However, the fact that she recognized "the angel of the LORD" as being from God (Yahweh)

suggests that she had at least some degree of understanding.

If so, then the place she came from---that is, Abram's household---was a place where God was working,

a place where God's plan was being advanced.

On the other hand, the place she was trying to go---most likely her homeland of Egypt---

can be said to have been a land full of idols, a place far from the promise of God.

In other words, Hagar, without even realizing it, was leaving the place of God's presence,

and was trying to return to a culture separated from God.

The reason the angel of the LORD deliberately spoke to her may have been to cause her to remember this,

and to draw her back into the center of God's plan.

And the LORD was urging that the faith within her heart would be transformed from the midst of idolatry

into a rich faith in the LORD.

"Where are you going?"---

What lies beyond this question is the gentle voice of invitation from God: **"Come to Me."**

Not the place to which she was fleeing, nor the place to which she would return,

but the place she was truly meant to go was to be with God---

God desired that she would realize this truth.

Gen. 16:9  The angel of the LORD said to her, "Return to your mistress and submit to her."

Here, concrete words were spoken by the angel of God.

That was the command: "Return to Sarai."

An important point here is that she was not told, "Return to Abram."

What the angel of God commanded Hagar was strictly to return to Sarai.

In other words, this means that Hagar was being asked to fulfill her original role---

that of a female slave.

This was by no means an easy thing for Hagar.

Because she was being told to willingly return to a painful situation from which she had fled because she was oppressed.

And not only to return, but also to "submit and serve."

There is no promise within this command such as "good things will happen as soon as you return."

Rather, even in the reality of suffering, she was being called to walk faithfully there.

Here is an important point we must learn.

It is that God's guidance does not always lead in the easier, clearer, or simpler direction.

Even in our own lives, there are times when we are praying to God and yet it feels as though our prayers are not being heard.

Sometimes, even immediately after praying, further hardships may come.

But that does not mean "God is not at work."

That is a great misunderstanding.

God is One who works in every place, and in every situation He loves us and guides us.

That is why, even in difficulties beyond our understanding, the hand of the LORD is extended,

and when we humbly obey that divine guidance, a new path will be opened.

Such a principle of God is clearly shown in the first narratives of the Bible, in the book of Genesis.

We must remember this well as believers.

Gen. 16:10  The angel of the LORD also said to her, "I will surely multiply your offspring so that they cannot be numbered for multitude."

Here we see what happens when Hagar walks in the path of obedience to God.

It is that many descendants will be increased from her.

This can also be taken to mean that a stable future for her clan will be granted.

In other words, Hagar, who was merely a female slave and who failed in her attempt to gain power for herself,

is here being assured that her position will be made honorable by God's way, and that she will be given great joy in life.

The words of Genesis 16:10 have a structure almost identical to the words God gave to Abram.

Therefore, we may understand that the same blessing given to Abram is also being given to Hagar.

In the culture of that time, the prosperity of one's descendants was the greatest achievement and pride in life.

Hence, the promise from God, "your offspring will be multiplied and uncountable," must have been words of immeasurable joy and honor.

Thus, it is clearly shown here that even those who seemed forsaken are promised astonishing blessings through obedience to God.

Gen. 16:11  And the angel of the LORD said to her,

"Behold, you are pregnant

and shall bear a son.

You shall call his name Ishmael,

because the LORD has listened to your affliction."

Just like verse 10, this verse 11 also has a structure similar to other parts of Scripture.

Is. 7:14  Therefore the Lord himself will give you a sign. Behold, the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel.

Matt. 1:22  All this took place to fulfill what the Lord had spoken by the prophet:

Matt. 1:23  "Behold, the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and they shall call his name Immanuel"

(which means, God with us).

That is, what is being declared here is that the child Hagar is now pregnant with is an important child within God's plan.

And this is being told directly to the mother, Hagar, by God Himself.

And the name "Ishmael" ("God has heard") evokes the idea that Hagar's suffering, which seemed to be ignored and unrecognized,

was actually heard and understood by God, and that there is indeed a recompense for that suffering.

Gen. 16:12  He shall be a wild donkey of a man,

his hand against everyone

and everyone's hand against him,

and he shall dwell over against all his kinsmen."

Here, it is written what kind of person Ishmael will become.

There are similar expressions elsewhere in Scripture, so let us look at those as well.

Luke 1:31  And behold, you will conceive in your womb and bear a son, and you shall call his name Jesus.

Luke 1:32  He will be great and will be called the Son of the Most High. And the Lord God will give to him the throne of his father David,

Luke 1:33  and he will reign over the house of Jacob forever, and of his kingdom there will be no end."

When we compare these two, though the form of the messages is similar, the content is quite different.

In Ishmael's case, it reads as if he will become very aggressive and self-centered.

Let us briefly organize how the tribes descending from Ishmael (Isma'il) historically and spiritually developed from that point onward,

in contrast with the teachings of Jesus.

**Late Old Testament Period (1st millennium BC):**

The twelve tribes of Ishmael prospered through nomadism and trade,

but repeatedly clashed with surrounding settled kingdoms,

reflecting the Genesis word "he shall dwell over against all his kinsmen."

Up to this point, unlike Jesus' gospel of enemy-love and reconciliation,

the survival principle remained "self-reliance and retaliation."

**Rise of Islam (7th century):**

Muhammad raised the banner "we are descendants of Ishmael,"

realizing monotheism and tribal unity.

Though Islam teaches charity and forgiveness,

its parallel emphasis on community defense and jihad stands apart

from the ethics of Luke's Gospel which upholds "love your enemies" and nonresistance.

**Medieval to Modern Period:**

The Arab-Ishmaelite sphere built vast empires,

interacted with the world through scholarship and commerce,

but political systems often relied on tribal honor and balance of power.

Though mercy was preached,

those who resisted authority were harshly punished,

and the Gospel teaching of "turning the other cheek" remained more of a distant ideal.

**Oil and Nation-State Era (20th century):**

In countries like Saudi Arabia and the Gulf States,

nomadic sheikhs became monarchs,

merging Islamic law with modern state law.

A "community ruled by law," distinct from the Gospel's enemy-love, was established,

and the Ishmaelite spirit of independence transformed into economic nationalism.

**Modern Polarization (21st century):**

On one side, nations like UAE and Bahrain prioritize diplomacy and coexistence;

on the other, groups like Hamas and the Houthis choose resistance and confrontation.

Both paths, grounded in "the freedom of Ishmael,"

continue to move through history in directions different

from the enemy-love and cross-shaped self-sacrifice shown by Jesus.

Overall, the development of the Ishmaelite line has shifted from nomadism to empire, and then to resource-based nation-states, with "independence and honor" at its core.

While teachings of mercy and charity have been incorporated, the Lukan ethic of thorough nonviolence---"love your enemies, bless those who curse you"---was never placed at the center of their system.

The prophecy of Genesis has, in history, become the reality of freedom, tension, and polarization, and it continues to stand in ethical contrast to the gospel of Jesus.

In this way, the image of one who is born by God's will and exists within His plan,

yet does not necessarily walk straightforwardly in the path of God, is difficult for us to understand.

It is a passage where questions inevitably arise---"For what purpose?" "Why in this way?"

However, one thing can certainly be said: the existence of such a "counter-type" makes the righteousness of Jesus all the more radiant.

One who forgives others, who does not shy away from accepting shame as shame.

The life of Jesus stands in stark contrast to those who believe "retaliation is natural," or "the fight must not stop,"

and it continues to shine as light in this world---that is a sure reality.

Gen. 16:13  So she called the name of the LORD who spoke to her, "You are a God of seeing," for she said, "Truly here I have seen him who looks after me."

After this series of conversations with God, Hagar names God.

That name is "El Roi." In Hebrew, "El" means God, and "Roi" means "one who sees."

In other words, "the God who sees me," or "the God who sees."

This word "see" does not merely mean visual recognition,

but carries the deeper meaning of understanding a person's existence, taking notice, and showing care.

Hagar was filled with astonishment and awe that there is a God who takes notice of someone like her,

who pays attention to her existence.

Also, in the Old Testament, to "see" God is regarded as something extremely fearful.

Though she conversed with God, the fact that her life was preserved

filled Hagar with deep surprise and reverence.

And what must be noted above all is that Hagar named God.

In the Bible, while God may say, "I am such-and-such,"

it is extremely rare for a human being to give God a name.

That such a special act was done by Hagar, a female slave, is significant.

Gen. 16:14  Therefore the well was called Beer-lahai-roi; it lies between Kadesh and Bered.

This place, where God appeared to Hagar---a female slave who did not believe in God and held the lowest status at the time---

came to be called "Beer-lahai-roi."

In fact, this location is later mentioned in the Bible as the place where Isaac dwells.

Because Isaac was born, Ishmael and his mother Hagar would leave Abraham's household.

Yet, the fact that Isaac later dwells at Beer-lahai-roi is a mysterious providence arranged by God.

Gen. 16:15  And Hagar bore Abram a son, and Abram called the name of his son, whom Hagar bore, Ishmael.

Gen. 16:16  Abram was eighty-six years old when Hagar bore Ishmael to Abram.

As the result of these events, Hagar returned to Abram and gave birth to the child.

And the name was given just as God had commanded: Ishmael ("God has heard").

This was truly based on Hagar's experience of her suffering and prayer being heard by God.

And for Abram as well, in the midst of his deep wrestling over whether his own thoughts and God's promises would truly be fulfilled,

this child could be seen as one answer that had been given.

It is likely that he gladly received this name and named the child.

And it is written here that Abram was eighty-six years old at that time.

The narrative of Genesis now temporarily departs from the issue of Abram's offspring, so let us add a brief note.

After this, God remains silent for thirteen years.

And after that silence, the event of Isaac's birth will occur.

What is noteworthy here is that during those thirteen years, Ishmael had grown and was nearing adulthood.

To Abram, this child was his beloved son, whom he believed to be the heir and had raised with love.

However, at that very moment, Isaac is given to him through Sarai.

In other words, Abram is then forced to make the decision

to let go of Ishmael, whom he had loved and raised for thirteen years, for the sake of God's plan.

It means that an even greater trial of faith awaits him.

Today, in closing, I would like us to learn one final point. That is the fact that it was a human being who gave God the name "the God who sees."

The gaze of the "God who sees" was originally a light of mercy,

but as time went on, the God who sees came to be misunderstood as "the God who watches,"

and people began to fear His gaze and tried to protect themselves through law and works.

The strict observances of Judaism and the external regulations of Islam both bear a strong awareness at their root:

"I must do what is right because God is watching."

The gracious gaze of God transforms into the watching eyes of obligation---

this is a universal trap that even lies hidden within Christianity.

At this point in the story, Hagar was seen by God, her life was preserved,

she returned to Him, and she was given the grace to believe. That is a fact.

And this is a beautiful passage that shows that God's character is truly poured out

even upon those of low status and those who had no faith to begin with---

a deeply gracious revelation of the essence of God.

However, there is one thing we must remember here.

It is the meaning of the word "to see."

As mentioned earlier, this does not merely mean to obtain visual information,

but includes the idea of knowing everything personally and looking with the heart.

However, human beings have a tendency to interpret such words superficially.

That is, when the phrase "the God who sees" is received as "the God who sees me,"

what happens?

It can produce the misunderstanding:

"I must act in such a way that I appear right before God,"

or "I must be approved by God through my actions."

The God who sees becomes "the God who watches,"

and people fear His gaze and try to protect themselves with law and behavior.

The strict observances of Judaism and the external regulations of Islam

both reflect a strong awareness at their root:

"I must do what is right because God is watching."

The gracious gaze of God transforms into the watching eyes of obligation---

this is a universal trap that even lies hidden within Christianity.

This is precisely the kind of thinking that leads to legalism

and is contrary to the gospel.

That God sees us is the manifestation of His grace---

that He understands, loves, and has compassion on all of us---

and it never means that He evaluates us based on our actions.

We must keep this firmly in mind.

With such important cautions in mind,

we must gladly lift our eyes to God as "the God who sees us."

God truly sees each one of us. And He is always guiding us.

As seen in the case of Hagar, there are times when God instructs us to return

to a place that may appear to be nothing but "hardship."

Such a word from God is spoken because He sees us more clearly than anyone else---

in other words, because He knows us.

Today, we may not have the experience, like Hagar,

of an angel of the LORD appearing before us and asking us such a question.

However, we are learning the word of God through the Scriptures.

And we believe that these words remain today in this form

for the very purpose of speaking to us.

If so, we must receive and accept the Bible

as the word given to us by "the God who sees us."

And on the path of walking according to this Word,

there is a future prepared by the God who sees---

a future we ourselves cannot see,

a version of ourselves that we ourselves cannot see.

We do not actually know "where we have come from or where we are going."

However, God is telling us this through His Word.

I believe this, and I want to entrust myself to the Lord and walk with Him.

That path will surely be upheld and protected by God,

and we will be able to walk it, step by step, with strength.

That is what I want to believe.
