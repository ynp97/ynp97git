May 25, 2025　Genesis 15:7--21　"A Torch Passed Through."

After rescuing his nephew Lot in the previous war, Abram received bread, wine, and blessing from Melchizedek, king and priest of Salem near Jericho, and then rejected the worldly spoils offered by the king of Sodom, thereby showing his dependence on God.

"After these things"---immediately following his victory and confession of faith---the word of the LORD came to him in a vision, saying, "Fear not, I am your shield; your reward shall be very great."

However, Abram, who had no child, lamented, "Will Eliezer of Damascus, the servant of my house, be my heir?" In response, the LORD assured him, "Your very own son shall be your heir," and pointed to the stars of the night sky, indicating that his descendants would be numerous.

Abram believed in that promise, and his faith was counted to him as righteousness by God. The establishment of this trust becomes the premise for the blood covenant ceremony and the declaration of "the boundaries of the land" in today's passage.

Gen. 15:7 And he said to him, "I am the LORD who brought you out from Ur of the Chaldeans to give you this land to possess."

Gen. 15:8 But he said, "O Lord GOD, how am I to know that I shall possess it?"

God identified Himself to Abram by saying, "I am the LORD." This self-declaration shares the same structure as the prologue to the Ten Commandments.

Ex. 20:2 "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of slavery."

Before delivering this crucial covenant of the Ten Commandments, God clearly revealed who He is.

And the verb that follows, "who brought you out of Ur of the Chaldeans," is in the Hiphil perfect form, which does not refer merely to a one-time movement, but carries the nuance of continuous intervention---"I have guarded you from the beginning of your departure up to this present moment."

In other words, what the LORD declared at this time was: I am the Almighty God who, from the very beginning of your journey, has gone before you, guarded your rear, and continued to lead you through every hardship.

And this very LORD is the one who now says He will give the land to Abram. However, this is a declaration that has been spoken several times in various forms since Abram's departure.

Gen. 12:1 Now the LORD said to Abram, "Go from your country and your kindred and your father's house to the land that I will show you.

Gen. 12:2 And I will make of you a great nation, and I will bless you and make your name great, so that you will be a blessing.

Gen. 12:3 I will bless those who bless you, and him who dishonors you I will curse, and in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed."

By withholding the name of the land and saying only, "to the land that I will show you" (אֲשֶׁר אַרְאֶךָּ), it is made clear that only God knows the destination.

In other words, Abram could make no decisions or judgments about the destination.

It was in that context that his faith was tested by the act of setting out.

Gen. 12:7 Then the LORD appeared to Abram and said, "To your offspring I will give this land." So he built there an altar to the LORD, who had appeared to him.

Here, God specifically revealed the promised land to Abram.

However, at the same time, He said that it would be "to your offspring" that the land would be given.

In other words, even at this point, it was implicitly revealed that Abram himself would not dwell in that land.

Then why was the promise given in this manner?

This too is thought to be a test of Abram's faith by God.

Because, as you all know, Abram's wife Sarai was barren and could not conceive.

It was in such circumstances that God declared, "To your offspring I will give this land."

Even if Abram had rejected the promise by saying, "Such a thing is impossible," it might have been understandable.

Yet he believed that word.

And to express that faith, Abram built an altar, worshiped God, rejoiced, and praised Him.

That is to say, he continued to hold on to hope that God would surely work in some way, even for a future that was completely unseen to him.

Gen. 13:14 The LORD said to Abram, after Lot had separated from him, "Lift up your eyes and look from the place where you are, northward and southward and eastward and westward,

Gen. 13:15 for all the land that you see I will give to you and to your offspring forever.

Gen. 13:16 I will make your offspring as the dust of the earth, so that if one can count the dust of the earth, your offspring also can be counted.

Gen. 13:17 Arise, walk through the length and the breadth of the land, for I will give it to you."

Gen. 13:18 So Abram moved his tent and came and settled by the oaks of Mamre, which are at Hebron, and there he built an altar to the LORD.

Here, for the first time, the word "forever" (עַד־עוֹלָם) is added, emphasizing the permanence of the promise.

There are two points of faith-testing shown in this passage.

First, right after Lot chose the fertile Jordan valley and left (v.14),

Abram temporarily loses visible land and resources.

At that very moment of loss, God commands, "Lift up your eyes," and promises that all the land he can see will be given to him "forever."

In other words, what Abram could see was the barren land left to him after giving the most fertile area to Lot. So the question is whether Abram can receive God's declaration---which is the exact opposite of what he sees---as true.

Can he accept the gap between the shrinking visible reality and the unseen expansion of the future? ---This is the first test.

Next comes the command, "Arise, walk through the land."

Also, "get up and walk" was an ancient custom that served both as a survey ritual and a declaration of ownership, to taste the land actually given.

If that is the case, then walking the land as though one already owns it, when one does not yet in reality,

means being asked to live in a way that expresses faith through concrete action before the promise is fulfilled.

If one does not believe, what would be the point of walking?

As a response to this challenge, Abram pitched his tent in Hebron and built an altar. In other words, he responded not by settling, but by worshiping. And he established a base of worship in Hebron,

a hub from which he could access various lands.

To summarize: (1) to continue believing in a promise that appears like an empty pledge right after having yielded blessings to others, and (2) to walk as if already given land that is not yet under his control, and to respond with worship---these two are the hidden tests of faith in this passage.

It is through this sequence that today's renewed declaration of the giving of the land comes.

Gen. 15:7 And he said to him, "I am the LORD who brought you out from Ur of the Chaldeans to give you this land to possess."

This manner of speaking resembles how ancient overlords addressed their vassals, saying,

"I have done such-and-such for you, therefore you must do this"---

a tone used as a kind of "transaction."

However, here, while maintaining His position as Lord,

God carefully tells Abram what He has done for him,

yet does not go on to make a demand of him.

In response to this declaration, Abram replied:

Gen. 15:8 But he said, "O Lord GOD, how am I to know that I shall possess it?"

While receiving such unilateral grace, Abram asks, "O Lord GOD, how am I to know that I shall possess it?"

The opening *bammāh* asks "by what means," and the suffix in *ʾîrāšennāh* is an emphatic imperfect, highlighting "will I surely inherit it?"

This is the same polite construction found in Psalm 86:17, "Show me a sign of your favor," and Isaiah 7:11, "Ask a sign of the LORD your God."

Abram asks, "What is the assurance of my possession (yāraš)?"

This is not so much unbelief as it is in keeping with the ancient Near Eastern treaty custom of seeking a sign in covenant negotiations.

Gen. 15:9 He said to him, "Bring me a heifer three years old, a female goat three years old, a ram three years old, a turtledove, and a young pigeon."

Gen. 15:10 And he brought him all these, cut them in half, and laid each half over against the other. But he did not cut the birds in half.

In Hebrew, "to make a covenant" is *kārat bĕrît*---literally "to cut a covenant."

The practice was for covenant parties to cut animals in half and walk between them,

declaring by this act, "If I break this covenant, may I become like these carcasses."

The heifer, female goat, and ram are livestock that later became major sacrifices in ritual law (all three are basic assets in a grain-based economy).

"Three years old" (*šĕlōšet*) indicates full reproductive maturity---a symbol of completeness.

By cutting these complete animals, the solemnity and irrevocability of the covenant are emphasized.

The turtledove and the young pigeon correspond to the small offerings allowed in Leviticus 1:14--17, symbolizing the covenant's accessibility even to the poor.

As for why the birds were not cut, it is generally understood that this was simply due to the physical impracticality of arranging such small creatures as symmetrical halves.

Gen. 15:11 And when birds of prey came down on the carcasses, Abram drove them away.

The "birds of prey" in Genesis 15:11, on the surface, are a natural depiction of animals gathering around carcasses. However, since the verb is in the perfect tense, it conveys the image that they had already come down---they arrived in an instant.

Symbolically interpreted, they can be seen as representing "hostility, confusion, and destruction aimed at God's covenant."

At the very place of covenant with God, something arises immediately to defile it.

This is something that often happens in our own lives as well.

At the very moment we act out of devotion to God, something worldly interrupts.

But what did Abram do? He immediately drove them away. And thus, the covenant was established.

Without offering the excuse, "I couldn't do it because such-and-such happened," he carried out what was right before God. In this sense, Abram's act of driving away the birds of prey can be seen as the model posture of a believer who "guards the covenant with God."

Gen. 15:12 As the sun was going down, a deep sleep fell on Abram. And behold, dreadful and great darkness fell upon him.

Now, in this part, we see "sleep," and within that sleep, revelation is given through the voice of God. This may initially seem similar to the "vision" we saw last week, but in fact, it is quite different.

As we can see from the beginning of today's passage, the vision occurred while Abram was awake---it was a form of contact with God that allowed conversation. But in this "sleep," and within the revelation that takes place in it,

there is no such space for interaction.

Instead, what we see is the presence of great fear.

A vision received while awake, and a deep sleep that robs consciousness, are spoken of in succession within just a few verses---a very rare occurrence.

This shows how God first gives assurance through a conscious vision, and then silences human activity completely,

so that all movement happens under God's complete sovereignty.

This deep sleep actually appears elsewhere in Scripture.

The most striking example is the sleep that came upon Adam.

Gen. 2:21 So the LORD God caused a deep sleep to fall upon the man, and while he slept took one of his ribs and closed up its place with flesh.

The verb for "caused to fall into sleep" comes from the sense of "to lower consciousness, to induce stupor,"

and expresses a forced, passive state. It is, essentially, a sleep like general anesthesia.

That is, the one who falls asleep is rendered completely inactive.

This point is important.

"Coma-level cessation of self-control." In such a state, the person loses all right to speak,

and having lost all strength, must entrust all things to God.

In other words, the actions that follow are performed with the human as a complete recipient.

There is an image here of God placing His overwhelming power into the hands of one made entirely powerless.

That overwhelming force is what is referred to here as the dread and darkness.

Gen. 15:13 Then the LORD said to Abram, "Know for certain that your offspring will be sojourners in a land that is not theirs and will be servants there, and they will be afflicted for four hundred years.

Gen. 15:14 But I will bring judgment on the nation that they serve, and afterward they shall come out with great possessions."

Here, in the midst of darkness and dread, something is spoken.

It is about what will happen to Abram's descendants in the future.

God's answer to Abram's question, "O Lord GOD, how am I to know that I shall possess it?"

is ultimately given in the scene where the covenant is enacted.

But before that, God reveals to Abram what this covenant actually means.

And the content is precisely the history of Israel that we know well.

That they would suffer as slaves in Egypt for 400 years, then depart from there, form the nation of Israel, and increase in number---that is the flow revealed here.

It can be said that the image of God's promise, previously spoken to Abram multiple times, is now described in more concrete and detailed form.

And Abram's personal end is also clearly foretold:

Gen. 15:15 As for you, you shall go to your fathers in peace; you shall be buried in a good old age.

It was exactly as this, as recorded in Scripture.

Gen. 25:7 These are the days of the years of Abraham's life, 175 years.

Gen. 25:8 Abraham breathed his last and died in a good old age, an old man and full of years, and was gathered to his people.

What is shown here can be said to be the magnificent development of Israel and the blessed life of Abram.

It is this glorious future that God is now trying to reveal to Abram through "a vision" and "deep sleep."

But what must not be forgotten is the fact that God did not stop the 400 years of slavery.

God knew in advance that great suffering was to come for Israel.

And yet, He did not put a stop to it---because He did not approve of the nation of Israel simply appearing one day out of nowhere.

Why is that?

Because for the people of Israel to rise up as a people centered on faith in the LORD,

they needed first to go through a period of "preparation to become the people of God."

This "preparation," as we can see from Abram's own life, is something that proceeds in stages and with care.

And God declared in advance that the necessary duration for that preparation was 400 years.

Indeed, if we recall the flow of the Book of Numbers we are reading in the early morning, this becomes clear.

Israel's exodus from Egypt, and the 40 years in the wilderness through which they were further refined---

this very history testifies to the fact that all things are proceeding based on this covenant with Abram made today.

What we must not misunderstand is this:

God "endured" the 400 years so that Israel could become a good nation.

This was not "God being cruel for 400 years," but

a patience rooted in the truth that "it truly takes 400 years to be made ready."

We, on the other hand, often wish our desires would come true by tomorrow.

But God endures even the process of suffering

in order to bring about the fulfillment at the best possible timing.

Why is that?

Because He desires that human beings would reach the happiest state of freely choosing to love God.

And for such a people to build a "nation"---

in that, the wonderful love of God's will is filled.

God desires to reveal the beauty of people gathering centered on that love, as His glory.

In order to lead human beings to the happiest state of freely choosing to love God,

God does not excessively control people. No matter how long it takes,

He waits patiently if it is necessary.

This must not be forgotten.

Gen. 15:16 And they shall come back here in the fourth generation, for the iniquity of the Amorites is not yet complete."

What is the iniquity of the Amorites? And what is the reason God "waits" until it is full?

The Amorites were a representative group among the seven Canaanite nations inhabiting the promised land,

and in the books of Kings, they are summarized as "a people who practiced idolatry on high hills" (2 Kings 21:11).

Leviticus 18:24--28 lists their sexual and religious corruption and warns that "the land itself will vomit them out."

Lev. 18:24 Do not make yourselves unclean by any of these things, for by all these the nations I am driving out before you have become unclean,

Lev. 18:25 and the land became unclean, so that I punished its iniquity, and the land vomited out its inhabitants.

The point here is the fact that God is also thinking about the Amorites. He waits until their sin is full.

In other words, God's desire that the Amorites would become better people is expressed in this passage.

But at the same time, God sees something else.

He sees that even after 400 years, their sin will not end---rather, it will worsen,

or even spread to other regions---

and in that case, it becomes necessary to purify that land using Israel as the people of God.

Therefore, now, by God's power, a child is being given to Abram,

and the preparation is beginning for the "cleansing of Canaan" that will occur 400 years later.

And when the time is fulfilled---as we are now reading in the Book of Numbers---

the fourth generation, that is, 601,730 soldiers, a multitude of over two million people,

will pour into Canaan, spiritually prepared after 400 years.

This truly grand event will be realized.

Here, let us repeat again, this is an important point:

This does not mean that the Amorites were worthless in God's eyes.

Precisely because they were valuable, a period of 400 years of grace was given.

This is also evident in the fact that God's way of doing things was applied to Israel as well.

When Babylon destroyed Jerusalem, the very same thing happened.

At that time as well, God gave warning after warning after warning, repeatedly trying to correct Israel.

But in the end, Israel became so corrupt that even they had to be struck down.

What is shown here is that the justice of God will surely be carried out, and sometimes the method is on a very grand scale.

And that God always desires to save all people,

and watches the movement of each heart.

However, if there is evil and sin in the heart of man, and it is left unrepented,

God knows it, and if necessary, He will strike.

We must not forget this.

And this means that we are not the exception.

How does the current world look?

Is it worshiping God, knowing His will, overflowing with love, and centered on God in its structure?

From the perspective of those who know the Bible, one must say that the world is doing the exact opposite.

There is no way God is not seeing this reality.

If that is the case---what will happen?

That is something we must learn from Scripture.

Now that Jesus has already come, the next judgment will be the final judgment.

And the fact is, we are living in a time where it would not be strange at all if that judgment came even now.

If so, what should we do?

We must urgently proclaim this Jesus Christ.

It has been prophesied beforehand that the day will come when God strikes this world.

Because it is important, let me say it again: the reason for this act of "striking" is so that He may lead us to a better place---it is because of His love for us. We must remember this.

The Amorites were given 400 years. How much time is left for us?

Gen. 15:17 When the sun had gone down and it was dark, behold, a smoking fire pot and a flaming torch passed between these pieces.

The smoking fire pot (*tannûr*) and the flaming torch (*lapîd*) are both elements of fire, recalling the cloud and pillar of fire at Sinai, and the protection on the night of Passover in Exodus 12.

What is important is that only God passes between the carcasses.

In a covenant made in such a way, where the participants pass through between slain animals,

both parties would be declaring: "If I break this covenant, may I be torn apart like these animals."

But here, only God passes through. Abram, who is in a deep sleep, does not pass through---in fact, he cannot, because he has been forcibly put to sleep.

This indicates that the covenant is a unilateral, unconditional covenant of grace made by God.

It is also a type of Christ, who bore sin on the cross.

God clearly demonstrates that He is the one who makes the covenant and keeps it at the cost of His own life.

Thus, the question,

Gen. 15:8 But he said, "O Lord GOD, how am I to know that I shall possess it?"

is answered in a vivid and overwhelming way.

Gen. 15:18 On that day the LORD made a covenant with Abram, saying, "To your offspring I give this land, from the river of Egypt to the great river, the river Euphrates,

Gen. 15:19 the land of the Kenites, the Kenizzites, the Kadmonites,

Gen. 15:20 the Hittites, the Perizzites, the Rephaim,

Gen. 15:21 the Amorites, the Canaanites, the Girgashites and the Jebusites."

What is important here is that it is God Himself who declares that He will give this land to the descendants of Abram.

And this is not just a declaration---it is a covenant.

The image here is not of them conquering the land of enemies by their own strength, but rather, that it is given by the hand of God

for the purpose of rebuilding the kingdom of God.

If the covenant were to be broken, then the LORD Himself would have to be torn in two. (Which is, of course, impossible.)

Today we have seen that a unilateral covenant of grace was made between God and Abraham.

As we saw earlier, this covenant is a type of the atonement of Jesus Christ---

that is, it is the pattern of the covenant we Christians have made with God through Jesus Christ.

So then, what is the covenant we have made?

It is to believe in Jesus Christ.

And by that, our lives become God's.

However, whenever we seek to renew our faith, or make a decision to take a step forward, almost without exception,

the temptations of this world attack us. It happens just as we are about to step out,

as if to block our walk of faith.

Just as Abram, right before the covenant with God, was attacked by birds of prey that tried to devour the sacrifice,

in our own lives too, there are hindrances that arise just as something is about to happen between us and God.

The values of this world, the opinions of others, fear, past failures, the desire for pleasure or stability---

these become "birds of prey" that try to steal the offering of faith.

However, it is precisely at such times that we must fight. And the fight of faith is not merely about wishing something in our hearts,

but about actually resisting temptation, standing on the Word of God, and moving forward.

Whether or not things go well, or whether or not we are making a mistake---

these are not, in truth, the essential issues. What matters most is whether we sincerely sought to be right before God.

We become stronger by repeatedly taking this forward-leaning posture of faith.

In other words, we can grow step by step, gradually.

God is the One who teaches us His will little by little for that very purpose.

At times, because of our own immaturity, the meaning of a passage of Scripture that had not been clear to us may suddenly shine in our hearts one day.

We may come to clearly understand what it is that we truly ought to do.

That is because God has prepared that moment.

He is the One who watches over us and prepares us for our growth step by step. God is the One who does this.

And we must trust in His timing.

If that is the case, then the opposite is also true:

We ought to respond with all our strength to the Word that has been given to us now, to the truth we can understand now.

With our actions, we believe and walk forward with everything we can do now.

Without hesitation, without fear. We do what we believe is right.

The rest, God will give.

It is not something we seize by our own power---God will give it.

Therefore, we must not shrink back.

Let us cling to the Word of God, pray, and stand against temptation.

And at the end of that path will be the time when we can truly believe in God with all our hearts,

and the wonderful time when that faith will be rewarded.

Let us lay hold of this true blessing and the assurance of faith.
