September 14, 2025 -- Genesis 21:1--7 "The Given Smile"

Let us read Genesis again today. Today's passage is where Isaac is born.

It is not merely that a child was born; this scene carries a very great meaning. First, let us read Romans, which speaks about this.

**Romans 4:16--25**

That is why it depends on faith, in order that the promise may rest on grace and be guaranteed to all his offspring---not only to the adherent of the law but also to the one who shares the faith of Abraham, who is the father of us all, as it is written, "I have made you the father of many nations"---in the presence of the God in whom he believed, who gives life to the dead and calls into existence the things that do not exist. In hope he believed against hope, that he should become the father of many nations, as he had been told, "So shall your offspring be." He did not weaken in faith when he considered his own body, which was as good as dead (since he was about a hundred years old), or when he considered the barrenness of Sarah's womb. No unbelief made him waver concerning the promise of God, but he grew strong in his faith as he gave glory to God, fully convinced that God was able to do what he had promised. That is why his faith was "counted to him as righteousness." But the words "it was counted to him" were not written for his sake alone, but for ours also. It will be counted to us who believe in him who raised from the dead Jesus our Lord, who was delivered up for our trespasses and raised for our justification.

What I want you to draw from this passage in Romans is the theme of this event---the main theme. It is not that what one wanted and desired was granted, but that it was proven that God is the One who creates life. This is an extremely important point.

Abraham at this time did not have what he wanted merely given to him; he witnessed that "God truly is the One who handles life." And what this experience leads to is that later, when Isaac grew, and when he was given the command to offer that Isaac as a sacrifice, to take his life and give him to God, he obeyed then.

If one looks at the scene of the offering of Isaac without understanding this part, Abraham may look like a man crazed by faith. But we come to understand that God did everything in order and sequence so that this could be done---it is the result of that. God is the One who handles life; if one obeys what He says, there is no mistake. That is what this means.

And why is it such a very great theme that He is "the One who handles life"?

It is because it connects to the great theme of the resurrection of Jesus Christ and to the One who raised Jesus Christ from the dead. It also becomes a great proof that we can escape from the dominion of sin and live anew.

Therefore, here in a couple in whom life could by no means be born, life was in fact brought forth---and this event was inscribed in history.

**Genesis 21:1--2**

The LORD visited Sarah as he had said, and the LORD did to Sarah as he had promised. And Sarah conceived and bore Abraham a son in his old age at the time of which God had spoken to him.

Now, this "creation of life from the impossible" was done to prove that God is, in the truest sense, the One who handles life---I said this. But there is actually another aspect.

It is the aspect of "it was done for Sarah." God visited Sarah, and it was done for her.

What position was Sarah in? As I have said many times, at that time, for a woman to be unable to have children was thought of in a way that endangered her status as a woman. We do not know who blamed her or whether anyone did. But it is certain that there was great anguish within herself.

But here, an event occurs in which she is released from that anguish that had tormented her for a very long time.

Truly, God's work is wondrous. While He deals with a great theme, He also provides personal grace for each individual person. → To be explored at the end: the grace called faith.

**Genesis 21:3**

Abraham called the name of his son who was born to him, whom Sarah bore him, Isaac.

"Isaac" is the Hebrew יִצְחָק (*Yitzḥaq*). The root צחק means "to laugh." The imperfect verb form "(he) laughs" became the name. Pronunciation is "Its-**ḥ**ak" (ts and the guttural **ḥ**). Greek Ἰσαάκ → English *Isaac*. The intensive stem of the root extends to "mock," but in Genesis 21:6, "laugh to/with me" (לִי) has the sense of "rejoice with me."

That is, centered on the birth of this Isaac, the motif of "laughing" is developed in various forms.

**Genesis 21:4**

And Abraham circumcised his son Isaac when he was eight days old, as God had commanded him.

Here the matter of circumcision appears.

**Genesis 17:10--12**

You shall be circumcised in the flesh of your foreskins, and it shall be a sign of the covenant between me and you. He who is eight days old among you shall be circumcised. Every male throughout your generations, whether born in your house or bought with your money from any foreigner who is not of your offspring,

**Genesis 17:25**

And Ishmael his son was thirteen years old when he was circumcised in the flesh of his foreskin.

**Genesis 21:4**

And Abraham circumcised his son Isaac when he was eight days old, as God had commanded him.

That the matter of circumcision is inserted here seems to have the intention of making clear the difference between Isaac and Ishmael.

In other words, Isaac was the one who kept the command that one must be circumcised on the eighth day, while Ishmael, because of the reason for his birth, could not keep it.

By this explicit statement, we can see that there is a difference. Then what does this difference represent? It means that Isaac is the one who, in the true sense, is connected as Abraham's child. One could also say that he bears it engraved in his body as a sign.

Why is this emphasized here? It is because it is necessary to make clear that the line flowing from Abraham stands upon the covenant between God and man that culminates in Jesus Christ.

If this were to become blurred, the possibility would arise that someone from Ishmael could be the Messiah. In order to exclude this, it connects to the point that Jesus Christ is indeed the Messiah---why? Because on the eighth day he was circumcised; the one who kept that covenant is Isaac.

However, this does not at all mean that God does not love Ishmael; rather, it is a "sign" necessary so that later generations may rightly understand the truth of the statement that the Savior is Jesus. If we think that God did this for that purpose, it is easy to understand.

Of course, Ishmael is also loved. And we who are uncircumcised are also loved. That is entirely all right.

Only, when later someone should say, "No, it is not the descendants of Abraham but the descendants of Ishmael,"---then: "At that time, it was promised that one must keep it on the eighth day. Under that promise stands Isaac."

Therefore, it proceeds logically that Jesus Christ, who is connected from there, is the Messiah.

This plan of God is consistent even in various legal and covenantal aspects. If we think that this is guaranteed in the matter of circumcision, it is easy to understand.

**Genesis 21:5**

Abraham was a hundred years old when his son Isaac was born to him.

Once again the time of one hundred years is confirmed. What sort of age was this one hundred years? It was, in the eyes of the whole world, an age at which one had completely lost sexual ability and at which it was impossible as a human being to have a child.

At that time God gave new life. This is an extremely important theme.

As I said at the beginning: that God gives life---that is, that He is the God who governs life itself. If we can place our trust there, then when our relationship with God is set right---that is, when we are righteous before God---death is no longer something to be feared.

And this not fearing death is an extremely great theme for Christianity.

Because we must first once die to ourselves and then pass through the process of being raised to Christ. If, at that time, we do not have the assurance that we will receive new life, we will not be able to renounce ourselves---that is, to quit a way of life ruled by self-centeredness and to move toward a new way of life.

In other words, in order for us to trust completely in Jesus Christ as the One who handles this life, who brings resurrection, and who makes us new, we must trust that this One truly is the God who handles life.

In order to give us that trust, Scripture deals with matters of life in a most striking way. It is certain that even in the birth of Jesus Christ, such a theme is at work.

We, when it comes to life, cannot help but cling to it.

But if we truly trust God as the One who handles life, then we can say that the greatest enemy, death, has been overcome, and that all things in this world are not worth anxiety.

And to arrive there is the maximizing of grace within us. Therefore, in this way, already in the very first part of Scripture, God's nature is being proven. Certainly, even in what is written in Genesis, there are images that show God's great power.

However, by a miracle occurring to this one man Abraham, I think we are able to have a more concrete image.

We want to rejoice that this Scripture bears this kind of divine consideration that draws near to our hearts.

**Genesis 21:6**

And Sarah said, "God has made laughter for me; everyone who hears will laugh over me."

Now, let us first resolve the part that gives a sense of strangeness in a simple way. It is the expression "will laugh over me." Reading this in Japanese, one cannot help but associate it with the nuance of "being laughed at." Therefore, it may be somewhat difficult to grasp.

However, in fact, when one reads the original language, it is clear that it does not mean that. The meaning here is rightly understood as "they will smile at me." That is, a very good situation has visited even her, and that blessing is also rejoiced in by those around her, and the blessing spreads. This is what it means.

Then why is the translation "laugh" used? It is because the name Isaac is deeply bound up with the word "laugh." If one were to change it to another expression, this nuance would collapse. If we take it that the word "laugh" is unified throughout the whole narrative, it becomes easier to understand.

**Genesis 21:7**

And she said, "Who would have said to Abraham that Sarah would nurse children? Yet I have borne him a son in his old age."

Truly, how good this is. Who would have said to Abraham, "Sarah would nurse children"? Certainly, people of the world would not have said it could be done. But God Himself is the One who declares He will do it, and He is the One who surely brings it to pass.

Sarah's first laughter was a sign of "not believing God to the end." First, that laughter is clearly defined as a failure. That is, even when faced with God's promise, people still harbor doubt and have the weakness of judging by their own experience and reason.

But God did not leave that failure as it was. Sarah's "laughter" was eventually changed into "laughter of joy." This is not by human effort, but it is the work of God Himself. The laughter that began from unbelief was painted over by God into the laughter of blessing.

The will of God expressed in the difference between these laughters is only one: it is "to lead us." He does not end by condemning human failure but uses that failure to lead us into the assurance of faith. This is truly the will of God.

Today's passage has a great theme. God is the One who creates life out of nothing. And this connects to the cross and resurrection of Jesus Christ, and to our eternal life. It is a marvelous proof. We must firmly hold onto this and live.

But it is not only the great theme. Here we must also pay attention to the fact that God touched Sarah's difficult heart and resolved it. Certainly, Abraham had no child with Sarah. But the fact that there was Ishmael, who had grown up well, is true.

If we think of it this way, it can be said that Sarah was the one who was more deeply wounded at that time. Sarah, who bore suffering that could not be told to anyone, had that suffering taken away by God Himself at the end of her life---this is truly the event that occurred here.

It can be said that this was a very great grace of God. But we need to grasp this event one step more deeply.

If one takes it simply, one may think that grace is that "what was wanted was obtained," "a desire was satisfied." But that is a dangerous direction.

If only that is at the center of the heart, the heart is weak, and it begins to seek more and more things. Wanting this, wanting that. Eventually, it may even become a heart in which the demands to God outweigh gratitude to God.

Therefore, when we see such a miracle, we must not look at "what was obtained," but rather look at "what it connects to."

Everything given is not for satisfying our physical and bodily desires. All is for giving us the foundation and clue for trusting God to the end---this is the understanding we need.

What about Sarah until now? Abraham had various events, and she must have heard of them. She did not not know of God's greatness.

But when she was directly told, "You will bear a child one year from now," she could only laugh in disbelief.

In her life, good things may not have happened, and by choosing to have a child by substitution through her own plan, she was hurt more deeply and may have walked a life of resignation. "God works for Abraham, but perhaps not for me"---she may have thought that.

But what happened here happened for Sarah. It happened directly to Sarah. She was able to gain as experience the assurance that God is the One who works for me. This is what happened to Sarah here.

We too make many prayers to God. That is not a bad thing. Rather, it is fundamentally what we should do, and it is good.

But we must not forget that God, because He loves us greatly and thinks of us, is the One who gives us what is needed at the best time, at the needed time, and what is needed.

Concerning the fact that a child was given when Sarah was ninety and Abraham one hundred, it can be said that it was given as fitting for the time when true trust in God was gained.

Therefore, even if we now think we want something, it will not necessarily be as we wish. There are elements necessary from God's viewpoint that we do not know, and perhaps it is being prolonged.

It may not be something easy. We may worry, suffer, even turn back. But what we must know is that God truly loves us. Surely it may be painful, surely it may be difficult, surely the future may not be visible. But God will surely give us a beautiful solution in His time. We are to believe that.

If we have this strength of faith, then even toward our perceived lack and suffering now, we will be able to endure. Why? Because there is always hope in our hearts.

And that hope is something tremendous---that it triumphs over death. We are given the privilege to live, raising our eyes to God, with this hope in our hearts.

This state---that we can believe in Jesus Christ---let us polish it up, make it more beautiful. And let us, with our spiritual beauty, be able to lead our beloved families.

Let us give thanks that the Lord looks at each of our hearts and, through the problems we bear, leads us.
