Genesis 22:15--24 "Because You Have Obeyed My Voice"

Last week, we read about Abraham, who, being commanded by the Lord, was about to offer Isaac as a sacrifice; about God, who stopped him after seeing his faith; and about the ram that was provided in Isaac's place --- the provision of the Lord on the mountain.

Today's passage follows immediately after that.

In other words, after Abraham worshiped the Lord by offering the sacrifice in place of Isaac, this passage begins.

The main themes today are twofold:

First, that our obedience to God always connects to what follows.

Second, that God's preparations are steadily progressing, even in places unseen --- meaning, the path we walk is entirely known to God.

Genesis 22:15--19 (ESV)

15 And the angel of the Lord called to Abraham a second time from heaven

16 and said, "By myself I have sworn, declares the Lord, because you have done this and have not withheld your son, your only son,

17 I will surely bless you, and I will surely multiply your offspring as the stars of heaven and as the sand that is on the seashore. And your offspring shall possess the gate of his enemies,

18 and in your offspring shall all the nations of the earth be blessed, because you have obeyed my voice."

19 So Abraham returned to his young men, and they arose and went together to Beersheba. And Abraham lived at Beersheba.

At first glance, this seems like a simple repetition of the covenant that had already appeared many times before, but there are several differences.

First, in form. Until now, God had said "I will promise," but here God says, "By myself I have sworn."

In other words, it has shifted from *covenant* to *oath*. This is not a written contract, but an unchangeable declaration backed by God's very being --- an expression of His irrevocable will.

Next, in timing. Previous promises were given at Abraham's calling, as assurance amid uncertainty, or when the sign of circumcision was instituted.

But this one is given right after Abraham has followed through a great test in obedience.

In other words, God saw Abraham's wholehearted obedience and reaffirmed the promise as an oath.

Also, the intensity of the language increases. No longer just "as the stars," but, in Hebrew expression, *"blessing I will bless you, multiplying I will multiply you"* --- a double emphasis --- and now adding "sand," further amplifying the scope of number and blessing.

A newly added element is the phrase, "Your offspring shall possess the gate of his enemies."

In the ancient world, the gate of a city symbolized authority, security, and governance.

This, therefore, is a guarantee of future dominion and divine protection.

To "possess the gate of enemies" is a metaphor for taking control of the city's power and judicial seat (cf. Deut. 21:19).

Joshua captured Jericho, Ai, and the Canaanite cities (Josh. 6--12).

David took the Jebusite stronghold of Zion and made it his capital (2 Sam. 5:6--9).

During David's and Solomon's reigns, Moab, Edom, Aram, and the Philistines were subdued (2 Sam. 8; 1 Kgs. 4).

After the return from exile, Nehemiah rebuilt the city gates, restoring the sovereignty of the community (Neh. 3).

Thus, this promise was concretely fulfilled at key points in history.

Furthermore, in regard to universal blessing, the phrase that was previously "in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" now becomes "in your offspring all nations shall be blessed."

The means --- the "through whom" --- becomes clearer.

Whereas before, it referred collectively to Abraham's descendants, now it focuses on *"offspring" (seed)* in the singular.

This clarifies that the channel through which blessing spreads is a single line --- and in Christian understanding, that "offspring" is Jesus Christ Himself.

The earlier phrase "your offspring shall possess the gate of his enemies" also points prophetically to the victory of Christ over the dominion of Satan in this world.

On the human side, while earlier it was said, "He believed, and it was counted to him as righteousness,"

here it is stated, "because you have obeyed my voice."

This does not mean that he earned the covenant through works,

but that his obedience in Genesis 22:1--12 demonstrated the authenticity of his faith,

and therefore God confirmed His original promise by oath as unshakable.

James 2:19--24 (ESV)

19 You believe that God is one; you do well. Even the demons believe---and shudder!

20 Do you want to be shown, you foolish person, that faith apart from works is useless?

21 Was not Abraham our father justified by works when he offered up his son Isaac on the altar?

22 You see that faith was active along with his works, and faith was completed by his works;

23 and the Scripture was fulfilled that says, "Abraham believed God, and it was counted to him as righteousness"---and he was called a friend of God.

24 You see that a person is justified by works and not by faith alone.

And concerning the reference to the land, the promise of inheriting the land, which had been repeated up to this point, does not appear here. The focus has shifted from Abraham's personal matters to a universal, human level.

In other words, this new promise can be seen as the moment when God, having seen Abraham's faith, confirmed all His previous promises and reestablished them as the foundation for blessing all humankind.

This powerful covenant is also quoted in the book of Hebrews:

Hebrews 6:13--18 (ESV)

13 For when God made a promise to Abraham, since he had no one greater by whom to swear, he swore by himself,

14 saying, "Surely I will bless you and multiply you."

15 And thus Abraham, having patiently waited, obtained the promise.

16 For people swear by something greater than themselves, and in all their disputes an oath is final for confirmation.

17 So when God desired to show more convincingly to the heirs of the promise the unchangeable character of his purpose, he guaranteed it with an oath,

18 so that by two unchangeable things, in which it is impossible for God to lie, we who have fled for refuge might have strong encouragement to hold fast to the hope set before us.

This oath---God's own oath---marks the moment when the visible promises given to Abraham---land and offspring---shifted into something spiritual.

And what brought this about was his act of obedience.

Then why did he obey? This is the central point: he obeyed because he believed.

That is, the depth of his faith had grown so greatly that this oath was given on account of that faith.

What did he believe? He believed that the act of slaughtering Isaac with his own hand---something that in human terms appeared cruel, tragic, and filled with sorrow---would be transformed by God into something righteous and good.

Through the utterly impossible event of Sarah's giving birth, Abraham had come to believe deeply that God is the One who governs life itself.

If that is so, then even when Isaac seemed about to be slain and lose his life, Abraham knew that God Himself was able to deal even with that.

He came to understand that God is truly transcendent---beyond all human reason---

and yet that this transcendent God personally involves Himself in human affairs.

It was precisely because he fully trusted in this truth that he was able to act.

Thus, we can see that the content of this oath does not bring Abraham any immediate, tangible benefit in his present life.

In reality, he already had a son, and he already possessed land.

What more could he possibly desire? And yet it is to this Abraham that God grants this oath as an act of grace.

This shows that through the test involving Isaac, God was leading Abraham from visible, worldly blessings to a deeper kind of blessing.

Or rather, all the steps of his journey up to this point had been leading him toward this profound grace.

And that grace is the blessing of being able to *truly believe in God from the heart*.

What does it mean, then, to be blessed?

Is it to possess things? Is it to experience good fortune in this world?

If that were the case, then as we approach death, we would realize that all such things perish with our bodies---

we would face the fact that we cannot take any of it with us, and we would lose our lives with a sense of emptiness and loss.

But according to the faith we hold, what awaits at the end of life is not loss,

but eternity---its very opposite---the opening of the door to something new and far better.

In other words, the moment we lay aside our limited body and mind,

we are transformed into something new. It is a moment to rejoice in.

Those who have this hope and joy can remain at peace even amid severe trials.

Even martyrdom---which to the faithless seems sheer madness---

becomes, in the end, an act filled with thanksgiving and joy.

We, including myself, may not yet have that kind of faith so easily.

However, we can be sure that through each event we experience, we are being led toward that faith even now.

As believers in Christ, let us remember that we are being nurtured into those who can truly believe from the heart

that death---the thing we most fear---is not terror, but hope itself.

And there is yet another great grace found here.

Though Abraham opened many doors toward the future, one might wonder---did he himself gain nothing from it, except for the deepening of his faith?

That is not the case. He gained the joy of *becoming a giver*. And what did he give?

He became one who gave to the whole world the precious gift of *faith*.

In modern terms, we sometimes hear the word *giver* in self-help literature,

but here Abraham became the ultimate giver.

And in fact, the greatest joy is *to give*. This is a fundamental concept of the Christian faith,

and here we see its global dimension---Abraham now fulfills his role as the greatest giver of all.

This joy, this incomparable joy, is the joy that has now been given to Abraham.

Yet even this joy is possible only through sincere faith---

the conviction that God is true and that He always reveals the right path.

We, too, must become such "givers," because there is great joy in giving.

At last week's baptism service, I was truly thankful to God for what took place.

As was shared in the testimonies, there was clearly *one who shared the message*,

and through the love and faith present in that messenger, hearts were opened, and the Holy Spirit surely touched them.

If that messenger had not truly believed from the heart, the message could not have been conveyed.

It was by seeing the daily conduct of the one who shared, a life guided by God,

that hearts were opened, the Spirit entered, and transformation occurred.

This also came about because each person in this church sought to live rightly before God.

Though the essence of the work is entirely God's and not our achievement,

it is also true that if our lives had not reflected God's presence, this would not have happened.

The joy of having been able to help pass on faith to another remains fresh in our memory.

It truly filled our hearts with gladness.

May we continue joyfully to take up our role as those who hand down this faith.

Genesis 22:20--24 (ESV)

20 Now after these things it was told to Abraham, "Behold, Milcah also has borne children to your brother Nahor:

21 Uz his firstborn, Buz his brother, Kemuel the father of Aram,

22 Chesed, Hazo, Pildash, Jidlaph, and Bethuel."

23 (Bethuel fathered Rebekah.) These eight Milcah bore to Nahor, Abraham's brother.

24 Moreover, his concubine, whose name was Reumah, bore Tebah, Gaham, Tahash, and Maacah.

This brief genealogy is inserted not to close the tension of Mount Moriah (the offering of Isaac),

but to move the story forward---to prepare the way for what will happen next.

The key lies in the phrases "after these things" and "Bethuel fathered Rebekah."

In other words, to the Isaac whose life had just been spared,

a *future wife*---one to carry on the covenant line---has already been provided.

This connects directly to Genesis 24, where Isaac and Rebekah meet and marry.

If we read carefully, this does not mean that these children were born afterward,

but that Abraham was *told* about relatives who had already been born and who were continuing the family line far away.

The relationships connect as follows:

Nahor was Abraham's brother, and Milcah---Nahor's wife---was also Abraham's niece.

Their son Bethuel became the father of Rebekah and Laban.

Here, for the first time, Rebekah's name appears---the woman who will become Isaac's bride

and the one through whom the covenant of descendants and blessing will be handed to the next generation.

Kemuel is described as "the father of Aram," indicating a link to the later Arameans (the Aramaic-speaking peoples).

Uz and Buz correspond to names of eastern tribes or regions ("the land of Uz" in Job; Elihu the Buzite),

showing how Abraham's wider family network extended toward Mesopotamia.

Chesed, Hazo, Pildash, and Jidlaph, though less known, serve to portray a branching family line,

implying a broad kinship circle rather than a single household.

Bethuel, as mentioned, is the father of Rebekah, whose name here prepares readers for the coming bride narrative in chapter 24.

The final verse lists Nahor's concubine Reumah and her four sons---Tebah, Gaham, Tahash, and Maacah---

emphasizing that Nahor's household formed a wide clan network. This shows that when Abraham's servant later goes to seek a bride,

he is not approaching an isolated family but a larger kinship community.

Maacah, notably, would later become the name of a minor kingdom or region,

hinting that the biblical narrative is intertwined with real geographic and ethnic networks.

In summary, this genealogy serves three purposes:

\(1\) It introduces Rebekah ahead of time, smoothly connecting the story to Isaac's forthcoming marriage.

\(2\) It demonstrates how the covenant would be continued within Abraham's own extended family,

showing that the promise was to be preserved "as a household," not lost to local customs.

\(3\) By listing names that map the kinship and geography, it prepares the reader for future journeys involving Rebekah, Laban, and Haran.

Immediately after the moment when life was restored on the mountain,

God reveals that *a future partner to carry that life forward has already been prepared.*

Genesis 25:20 (ESV)

And Isaac was forty years old when he took Rebekah, the daughter of Bethuel the Aramean of Paddan-aram, the sister of Laban the Aramean, to be his wife.

Thus, we see that God, in order to bring to fulfillment His covenant with Abraham,

had already prepared even the wife who would continue the promise.

What we must remember here is that God prepares things for us in places we do not know and cannot see. We believe that God is the One who can see beyond time itself. Therefore, He is able to prepare, in unseen moments, the things that are yet to come in our lives.

There are moments when we say, "This must have been destiny," yet we should remember that it is precisely because God had prepared everything beforehand so that it would happen at that very time.

If we truly believe this, then we can trust that whatever happens to us is part of God's preparation, and that all things will come together beautifully in His timing.

If we see everything from a self-centered perspective, we might only think, "Something is happening somewhere." But from God's greater perspective, we can believe that each of our actions, one by one, serves a purpose and is being woven into His plan.

We should hold this as a foundation for our lives.

Indeed, our lives are full of failures. Many times we say, "I shouldn't have done that," "Why did I say that?" "What was I thinking?"

Yet when we ask whether those failures are truly meaningless, we can know they are not.

Abraham was able to learn many things precisely because he failed.

And it is through those failures that we can read the words of Scripture as our own.

If Abraham had been a perfect man, we would have no hope when we look at ourselves.

But like Abraham, we can reach a deeper faith through our stumbling and mistakes.

We learn not through his successes, but through his failures.

This is the consistent tone throughout the Bible. How many times did God's people, Israel, receive rebuke in Scripture? The Bible is full of their failures.

David and Moses, too, had their own failures.

Especially their failures in their later years are deeply relatable for us.

Even Peter and Paul---Scripture clearly records their failings.

Therefore, we too should not grieve over our failures excessively.

God knows them all.

And they are never in vain. When we rise again and walk forward, those failures will surely be joined to something good.

If we think carefully, the times when we most clearly demonstrate our faith in the Lord are precisely when we are in difficult situations.

If, when we fail or suffer, we only look back in regret, the light of the Lord will not be revealed.

But if, after a great failure, we can still look up to the Lord and continue to walk, how great a testimony that becomes to His glory!

It is not easy, but it is important that we view every situation through the lens of Jesus Christ.

When we can do that, what we find there is never despair, but hope.

If something has been lost, new life will grow.

We should see it this way: what has been buried in the soil is not wasted, but becomes nourishment for that new life to grow.

When we believe that this new life is something given by God,

then our past failures, our hardships, our sorrows---all become nourishment.

Through that nourishment, what God newly gives us will grow and flourish.

Let us once again see our lives through the lens of Jesus Christ.

And at all times, let us live with faith that we are loved by God---

and because of that love, He knows us and causes all things to happen for our good.
